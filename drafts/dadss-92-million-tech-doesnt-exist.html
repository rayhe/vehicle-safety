Sobriety Report

$92 Million and 18 Years Later, the Technology That Was Supposed to End Drunk Driving Doesn't Exist

Dale Impactor III • Clautomotive Desk
June 2, 2026

According to the toxicology reports, and there are a lot of them, 13,524 people were killed in alcohol-impaired crashes in 2022.[1] One every 39 minutes, thirty-seven per day. Congress looked at those numbers in November 2021, passed the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, and buried inside Section 24220 a mandate that should have changed everything: every new vehicle sold in America must include passive technology capable of detecting whether a driver's blood alcohol concentration exceeds 0.08 g/dL and preventing the car from moving if it does.[2] No breathalyzer tube, no ignition interlock; the car just knows.

November 15, 2024 was the deadline. It passed without a rule, without a prototype, without a production vehicle, and without much public comment from the agency responsible for meeting it.

$91,687,500
Federal funds awarded to a single nonprofit, the Automotive Coalition for Traffic Safety, for alcohol detection R&D since 2008

In February 2026, NHTSA told Congress the quiet part out loud: "There are no in-vehicle technologies in production that can measure BAC or BrAC above 0.08 g/dL passively."[3] Its current breath sensor requires a directed puff of breath toward the sensor, which disqualifies it as passive under the statute. Its touch-based sensor, which attempts to read BAC through the skin via near-infrared spectroscopy on the start button, has never been validated at production scale. Eighteen years of research produced zero deployable technology.

Where did the money go? USAspending.gov tells a remarkably simple story. Two cooperative agreements, 22 transactions, one recipient.[4] Award DTNH2213H00433 delivered $46,687,500 between March 2014 and September 2023. Award 693JJ92350015 added another $45,000,000 beginning February 2023 and running through February 2028. Both awards went to a single entity: the Automotive Coalition for Traffic Safety, a 501(c)(3) based in Leesburg, Virginia.

ACTS is a consortium of 15 automakers, a detail worth letting settle for a moment.

Every company that will be regulated by the eventual FMVSS standard is also a member of the trade group that controls the research, sets the timeline, and receives the federal funding to develop the technology they will eventually be required to install. NHTSA issues an Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking in January 2024, collects 18,000 public comments, and has not issued a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking since.[5] The extension clause in Section 24220 allows up to three additional years, pushing the theoretical deadline to November 2027, but NHTSA's own language is deliberately indefinite: they will "continue to provide annual reports to Congress" until the technology is ready.[3]

No timeline, no milestones, just a promise to send annual reports.

726,000,000
Estimated daily vehicle-start events across 227 million licensed U.S. drivers

Grant the most generous possible assumption: that a production-ready passive BAC sensor will eventually exist. Consider the false positive mathematics, which alone should sober up any regulator who has spent more time with policy briefs than probability distributions. FHWA data counts approximately 227 million licensed drivers in the United States, averaging roughly 3.2 trips per day based on the National Household Travel Survey.[6][7] That produces an estimated 726 million vehicle-start events daily. At 99.9% accuracy, which sounds extraordinarily precise until you multiply it by the denominator, 726,000 sober drivers are locked out of their own cars every day. At 99.99% accuracy, that number is still 72,600. At five nines, the gold standard for mission-critical systems, 7,260 sober Americans are stranded daily by a system designed to stop approximately 37 drunk driving deaths. The technology would need to reach six nines of accuracy, 99.9999%, to produce fewer daily false lockouts than daily drunk driving fatalities. No passive biometric sensing system in any field has achieved that accuracy at scale.

Simple arithmetic produces those numbers: 227 million drivers multiplied by 3.2 trips divided by false positive rate. That 3.2 figure comes from the 2022 NHTS; actual individual trip frequencies vary enormously by age, region, and employment status, introducing roughly plus or minus 20% uncertainty. But the order of magnitude holds. Even at the low end, five-nines accuracy still produces thousands of daily lockouts.

Rep. Thomas Massie has twice tried to defund Section 24220 implementation. H.Amdt.641 to H.R.4820 failed 201-229 in November 2023. H.Amdt.155 to H.R.7148 failed again in January 2026.[8] Massie's objections center on government overreach and consumer freedom, but the false positive math hands him an engineering argument far stronger than any ideological one.

Contrast the DADSS program's $91.7 million for zero deployed units with ignition interlocks, the technology that already exists and already works. An interlock costs roughly $100 to install. Studies consistently show a 67% reduction in repeat DUI offenses while the device is active.[9] Approximately 80% of convicted DUI offenders never install one, either because their state doesn't mandate it, because enforcement is lax, or because the offender simply drives on a suspended license.[9] The DADSS funding could have subsidized interlock installation for every convicted offender in America for several years running.

The strongest case for the DADSS program is that ignition interlocks are reactive by design, only reaching drivers after arrest and conviction, while passive detection would prevent first offenses. That distinction is real and meaningful. An estimated 300,000 people drive drunk in the United States every day, and only about 4,000 are arrested.[10] Passive detection would theoretically intercept the 296,000 who evade enforcement entirely. If the technology worked, and worked at the required accuracy threshold, it would be the most significant automotive safety advance since the seatbelt. What went wrong is not the aspiration but the execution: 18 years and $91.7 million have produced an admission that the goal remains out of reach, delivered by the agency responsible for reaching it, to the Congress that mandated it, on a timeline that nobody will commit to.

Meanwhile, 37 people die every day from alcohol-impaired crashes, not in some projected future scenario but right now, while Congress waits for technology that its own agency says does not exist.

FARS only captures fatal crashes; the roughly 290,000 annual alcohol-related injuries and 1.5 million DUI arrests represent a much larger impaired-driving population that this analysis does not address.[1] The $91.7 million in cooperative agreements does not include NHTSA's internal program costs, contractor support, or testing infrastructure, so the true federal investment is likely higher. And ACTS's annual reports detail genuine engineering progress on the breath and touch sensors; the claim here is not that the research is fraudulent, but that the structural incentives of having the regulated industry manage the research timeline have predictably produced an indefinite timeline.

If you have been convicted of DUI in any state, call your DMV and ask whether your state offers an interlock subsidy program. Sixteen states now provide financial assistance for low-income offenders, and most will credit the interlock period against your license suspension.[9] If you are a state legislator, look at the 80% non-compliance rate and ask whether your enforcement mechanism has teeth. If you are a voter, ask your representative why Congress mandated a technology that NHTSA says does not exist, funded the effort through a trade group controlled by the companies it regulates, and has no public plan for what happens when the extension expires in November 2027.

Passive alcohol detection was supposed to be in your next car, but it isn't, nobody can say when it will be, and the tab stands at $91,687,500 and counting.
