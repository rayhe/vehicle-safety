# Research: "7,314 Pedestrians Died Last Year. America's Roads Were Designed to Kill Them."

## Thesis
America's pedestrian death crisis is not a behavioral problem — it's an infrastructure problem with three compounding blades: darkness (76% of fatal crashes happen after dark), missing sidewalks (65% occur where no sidewalk exists), and vehicle size (SUVs/pickups now account for 54% of pedestrian fatalities, up from parity with cars a decade ago). When all three converge — a dark arterial with no sidewalk and an SUV traveling 40+ mph — pedestrian survival probability drops below 10-15%. The safety technology that was supposed to help (pedestrian AEB) literally doesn't work at night, which is when most pedestrians die.

## Novel Contribution: The Three-Blade Cross-Tab
Nobody has combined these three FARS/IIHS/GHSA datasets into a single framework:

**Blade 1: Darkness**
- 76% of pedestrian fatalities occur in dark conditions (NHTSA/GHSA 2023)
- Fatal pedestrian crashes at night DOUBLED from 3,030 (2010) to 5,578 (2023) — 84% increase
- Daytime fatalities rose only 28% in the same period (1,092 → 1,396)
- The nighttime share of pedestrian deaths is actively GROWING

**Blade 2: No Sidewalk**
- 65% of pedestrian deaths in 2023 occurred in locations without a sidewalk (GHSA 2025 report)
- Since 2017, pedestrian fatalities in places without a sidewalk rose by 1,164, vs only 167 increase in locations WITH a sidewalk
- 76% of deaths occur away from intersections (mid-block)
- "Stroads" — hybrid street/road designs with 45-55 mph speeds — account for ~60% of all pedestrian fatalities (Strong Towns/FHWA)

**Blade 3: Vehicle Size**
- Light trucks (SUVs, pickups, vans) accounted for 54% of pedestrian fatalities in 2023 where vehicle type was known
- A decade ago, cars and light trucks were roughly equal
- Vehicles with hood height >40 inches are 45% more likely to cause pedestrian fatalities (IIHS 2023)
- Already covered in our hood-height article — this piece focuses on the SYSTEM, not the vehicle

**The Convergence Math:**
- Dark + no sidewalk + SUV at 40 mph = survival probability ~10-15%
- At 20 mph: ~2.5% fatality probability
- At 30 mph: ~10% fatality probability
- At 40 mph: ~50% fatality probability (20x the 20 mph rate)
- At 50 mph: ~90% fatality probability

## The AEB Failure
- Pedestrian AEB reduces crashes by 27% overall (IIHS, Cicchino 2022)
- BUT on dark roads, the effect is NEGLIGIBLE (IIHS 2024/2025)
- IIHS: "On dark roads, the effect of pedestrian AEB on crash risk is negligible"
- High-visibility clothing actually CONFUSES AEB sensors (IIHS 2025)
- This means the single most important safety technology for pedestrians doesn't work when 76% of deaths occur

## Racial Disparity (Strengthens systemic argument)
- Black Americans have a pedestrian death rate 118% higher than non-Hispanic white (SafeTREC 2025)
- American Indian/Alaska Native: 3.42x the rate of White pedestrians (NHTSA 2022)
- Low-income neighborhoods are statistically less likely to have sidewalks, marked crosswalks, slower speed road designs
- This isn't just infrastructure — it's infrastructure inequality

## Hit-and-Run Trend
- 25% of pedestrian deaths in 2023 were hit-and-run (up from ~20% pre-2020)
- In 94% of fatal hit-and-runs, the striking vehicle was the fleeing vehicle
- 1,818 people killed by someone who drove away

## Freeway Deaths: The Hidden Pedestrian
- ~1,200 people died on freeways/interstates in 2023 — roads not designed for pedestrians
- Includes stranded motorists, first responders, tow truck drivers
- All states have Move Over laws; they're nearly unenforceable

## Strongest Counterargument
"Pedestrians share responsibility — jaywalking, impairment, inattention." 
Counter: 29% of pedestrians killed had BAC ≥0.08. That's real. But:
1. Infrastructure created the exposure — no sidewalk, no crosswalk for half a mile, dark road = people WILL cross
2. The 65% without sidewalks didn't choose to walk where there's no sidewalk — there IS no sidewalk
3. 70% of dead pedestrians are male (unchanged since 1975) — behavioral patterns exist, but roads amplify them
4. Even the "jaywalking" framing is an automotive industry invention from the 1920s
5. Driver behavior matters too: 16% of fatal pedestrian crashes involved a drunk driver
