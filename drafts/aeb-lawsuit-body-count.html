Every Automaker in America Is Suing to Kill a Rule That Would Save 360 Lives a Year

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
April 6, 2026

In April 2024, NHTSA finalized FMVSS 127, a rule requiring automatic emergency braking in every new car and light truck sold in the United States by September 2029. The agency's own regulatory impact analysis projected the mandate would prevent approximately 360 deaths and 24,000 injuries per year once fully phased in.[1] Fourteen months later, the Alliance for Automotive Innovation, representing virtually every major automaker selling vehicles in the U.S., filed suit in the D.C. Circuit to kill it.[2]

360
Estimated lives saved per year by the AEB mandate, per NHTSA's regulatory impact analysis

Their complaint does not say AEB is bad. They love AEB. They spent a billion dollars developing it. They voluntarily agreed in 2016 to make it standard on all models by 2025, and they delivered.[3] What they object to is the specific performance threshold: NHTSA's rule requires the system to stop the vehicle entirely, with zero contact, behind a lead vehicle at speeds up to 62 mph. Alliance CEO John Bozzella wrote to Congress that the standard is "practically impossible with available technology" and that NHTSA's own testing showed only one vehicle met the stopping-distance requirement.[2]

That is not a frivolous objection. But it has a body count.

What AEB actually prevents

IIHS studied police-reported crashes across 22 states from 2010 to 2014 and found that vehicles with AEB experienced 50% fewer rear-end crashes and 56% fewer rear-end injury crashes compared to identical models without the technology.[4] Forward collision warning alone, without automatic braking, cut rear-end crashes by 27%. IIHS estimated that if every vehicle in 2013 had been equipped with autobrake, more than 700,000 police-reported rear-end crashes would not have occurred. That is 13% of all police-reported crashes in a single year.

Rear-end collisions are not the only crash type, but they are the one AEB is designed to address. In our FARS dataset covering 2014 through 2023, we tallied 332,000 fatal crash involvements across all vehicle classes.[5] FARS does not classify crash type consistently enough to isolate rear-end fatals, but applying IIHS's 13% figure yields roughly 43,000 AEB-addressable fatal crash events over the decade. Cut that by half and you get approximately 21,500 fatal crashes that a fully AEB-equipped fleet might have prevented. That is 2,150 per year.

21,500
Estimated AEB-preventable fatal crashes, 2014-2023, if the entire fleet had been equipped

NHTSA's official estimate of 360 saved lives is considerably lower. The gap reflects three constraints: the mandate only applies to new vehicles, fleet turnover takes 12 to 15 years, and not every AEB-addressable crash would have been prevented even with the system active. The 360 figure is a steady-state estimate once fleet penetration reaches a mature level. In the interim years, the number ramps up as AEB-equipped vehicles replace older ones.

The cost of delay, in bodies

The mandate's compliance deadline is September 2029. If the lawsuit delays implementation by two years, to 2031, NHTSA's own math suggests roughly 720 additional people will die who otherwise would not have. Three years of delay: 1,080. If the rule is killed entirely and AEB adoption plateaus at current voluntary levels, approximately 95% of new vehicles but only an estimated 30 to 40% of the total registered fleet, the deficit compounds indefinitely.[6]

S&P Global Mobility reported the average age of a vehicle on U.S. roads hit 12.6 years in 2024.[7] Under the original mandate timeline, the fleet would approach full AEB saturation by approximately 2042. Push that to 2045 and you've added three more years of a mixed fleet where an Altima with AEB brakes automatically while the 2009 Civic behind it does not.

Which vehicles need it most

FARS data lets us identify which vehicles are involved in the highest volume of fatal crashes. Sedans account for 137,137 fatal crash involvements in our dataset, more than any other class.[5] Pickups follow at 85,127, SUVs at 86,564. But the critical variable is not which class crashes the most. It is which class kills others the most when it does.

In pickup crashes, the truck's own occupant dies only 48.9% of the time. The other 51.1% of fatalities are someone else: a sedan driver, a pedestrian, a cyclist.[5] For sedans, the split inverts: the sedan's occupant dies 64.5% of the time. AEB on a pickup prevents the pickup from rear-ending the sedan. AEB on the sedan prevents the sedan from rear-ending the vehicle ahead. Both save lives, but AEB on the heavier vehicle carries an outsized benefit because the heavier vehicle is more likely to be the surviving aggressor in a two-vehicle crash.

The automakers' argument, stated at full strength

The Alliance is not arguing against AEB. They are arguing that NHTSA set the bar at a level current brake hardware cannot reliably clear. A hydraulic brake system that can decelerate a 5,500-pound pickup from 62 mph to a complete stop before making any contact with a lead vehicle requires stopping distances that exceed the physical limits of most production brake systems. Europe's equivalent regulation, UN Regulation 152, requires speed reduction and collision mitigation, not zero contact.[2] If NHTSA had adopted the European standard, the Alliance argues, the rule would already be implementable. Instead, automakers face a deadline they may not be able to meet, which could result in production delays, CAFE penalty exposure, or legal liability for systems that function but don't hit zero contact.

This is a real engineering constraint, not theater. If the no-contact standard forces automakers to delay model launches or de-content vehicles, the downstream effect could perversely slow the rollout of AEB in the fleet. An achievable standard adopted on time might save more lives than a perfect standard adopted late.

Where the argument breaks down

The Alliance's proposed remedy is repeal, not amendment. They are not petitioning for a modified standard with a higher-but-achievable contact threshold. They are asking the D.C. Circuit to vacate the rule entirely. If they win, the U.S. reverts to having no federal AEB mandate at all. The voluntary commitment already expired. Automakers already met it. There is no backstop.

They also filed a post-election letter to the incoming Trump administration urging it to "re-open" the rule, a move that suggests their endgame is not a technical fix but a political one: run out the clock under a friendlier administration and hope the issue dies of natural causes. In the meantime, 360 people per year don't die of natural causes. They die in frontal crashes that a $200 radar module and a software update could have mitigated.

What you can do

Check whether your current vehicle has AEB. If you bought a 2018 or newer model from a major brand, it probably does, thanks to the 2016 voluntary agreement. If you drive anything older, it almost certainly does not. When shopping for your next vehicle, confirm AEB is standard, not optional. Ask the dealer about IIHS front crash prevention ratings for the specific trim you're considering, which are available at iihs.org/ratings. Check your VIN for open recalls at nhtsa.gov/recalls. And if the policy dimension matters to you, the docket for FMVSS 127 is public.

Methodology

Fatal crash counts from NHTSA FARS bulk data, 2014-2023 (332,000 crash involvements across 337 models with 50+ deaths). AEB effectiveness from IIHS 2016 study (updated) covering 22-state police-reported crash data. AEB-addressable crash share (13%) from IIHS calculation of rear-end proportion of all police-reported crashes. NHTSA's 360 deaths/year estimate from the FMVSS 127 Final Regulatory Impact Analysis. Fleet age from S&P Global Mobility 2024 report. The 21,500 figure is our own estimate: (332,000 crashes × 13% rear-end share × 50% AEB reduction) = 21,580, rounded. This is an upper bound assuming full fleet AEB penetration throughout the decade, which did not exist.

Limitations

FARS does not classify crash type (rear-end vs. side-impact vs. head-on) in a format amenable to bulk model-level analysis, so our AEB-addressable crash estimate uses IIHS's 13% rear-end share rather than a direct FARS calculation. The 360 deaths/year figure is NHTSA's projection, which incorporates fleet penetration curves and partial-effectiveness adjustments we have not independently replicated. Fleet turnover math assumes linear scrappage, which oversimplifies the actual age distribution. AEB effectiveness varies by speed, weather, system calibration, and whether the system includes pedestrian detection. The 50% figure is a statistical average from one IIHS study period and may not generalize to all future conditions.
