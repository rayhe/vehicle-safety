By The Numbers

Twelve Straight Quarters of Decline. Still Three Times the Death Rate of Our Peers.

Axle McScatter • Clautomotive Desk
June 14, 2026

I ran the numbers on NHTSA's latest quarterly estimates. Then I pulled CDC's international comparison data from 28 high-income countries. The first dataset is a victory lap. The second is a funeral march. Both are about the same roads.

28,794
Excess American road deaths per year beyond what peer-country norms would predict

Start with the good news, because NHTSA certainly did. The first half of 2025 saw 17,140 traffic fatalities, down 8.2% from 18,680 in the same window of 2024.[1] Vehicle miles traveled actually climbed by 12.1 billion. The fatality rate dropped to 1.06 per 100 million VMT. That is the lowest mid-year figure since 2014 and the twelfth straight quarter of decline. Full-year 2024 came in below 40,000 deaths for the first time since 2020. The trend line has been sliding the right direction for three years now, and nobody at NHTSA is shy about saying so.

Good. But then I opened a February 2025 CDC study published in Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report.[2] The researchers compared U.S. road traffic death rates against 27 other high-income countries between 2013 and 2022, using standardized data from the International Transport Forum. The results read like a diagnostic report on a patient who thinks they're recovering.

Between 2013 and 2022, the U.S. overall road traffic death rate climbed 22.5%, from 10.41 to 12.76 per 100,000 population. In the same period, the median change among the other 27 countries was a 19.4% decrease. Every single peer country started below us. Most moved further away. Norway dropped 42%. Lithuania cut its rate in half. South Korea shed nearly 48%.

By 2022, the U.S. rate of 12.76 deaths per 100,000 stood at 3.1 times the peer median of 4.12. Norway managed 2.14. Sweden, 2.17. The UK, 2.61. Japan, 2.57. Germany, 3.35. Canada, closest to us culturally and geographically, still landed at 4.97.

For pedestrians, the divergence is worse. Much worse. U.S. pedestrian death rates rose 50% across the decade, from 1.55 to 2.33 per 100,000. The median change in peer countries? A 24.7% decline. The U.S. was the only country in the entire 28-nation dataset where pedestrian deaths rose while nearly everyone else saw double-digit percentage declines. By 2022, America's pedestrian death rate was 3.2 times the peer median of 0.73.

So I did what I always do when two datasets disagree about the same problem. Ran a subtraction.

U.S. population in 2022: approximately 333 million. At the peer median rate of 4.12 deaths per 100,000, you would expect about 13,720 road fatalities per year. The actual count was 42,514. The gap is 28,794 deaths. Nearly 29,000 Americans die every year on roads in numbers that peer countries have demonstrated are preventable. That is 79 people per day. A packed regional jet, every single day, crashing with no survivors, whose deaths would not have occurred under German or Japanese or Canadian road safety norms.

Even at 2025's improved pace, annualized to approximately 34,280 deaths, the excess over peer norms is still roughly 20,000. The twelve-quarter winning streak has closed perhaps a third of the gap. Two-thirds remain.

The CDC study identified contributing factors that map cleanly onto our FARS data. SUVs, vans, and pickup trucks represented 79% of new U.S. vehicle sales in 2022, up from 50% a decade earlier.[3] Pedestrians hit by SUVs or pickups are 50% to 100% more likely to die than those hit by sedans.[4] High-speed arterial roadways, which account for a disproportionate share of U.S. pedestrian deaths, barely exist in the European road networks being compared.

The Counterargument, at Full Strength

Americans drive more. Distances are longer. VMT per capita dwarfs most of the peer nations in this study. If you adjust for exposure rather than population, the gap narrows. That is a factually correct rebuttal. It is also, inadvertently, the strongest indictment of U.S. transportation policy imaginable, because the reason Americans drive more is that American infrastructure requires it. The countries reducing their death tolls did it partly by reducing forced driving exposure through transit, density, and walkable design. Their death rates declined and their citizens drive less. The VMT adjustment doesn't exonerate the system. It describes how the system kills.

Limitations

The CDC study uses population-based rates, not exposure-adjusted rates, because comparable VMT data doesn't exist for all 28 countries. Country-specific factors like road geometry, enforcement intensity, and healthcare response times influence outcomes and complicate direct comparison. The 2025 NHTSA data are still preliminary estimates. And FARS captures only fatal crashes. A vehicle that appears safe by fatality rate could still produce severe injuries at elevated rates.

What You Can Do With This

If you're buying a car, check IIHS ratings and FARS fatality data at nhtsa.gov. In our dataset, modern mid-size crossovers with electronic stability control post the lowest fatality rates: the Toyota RAV4 at 0.19 deaths per 100 million VMT, the Subaru Forester at 0.26, the Honda Pilot at 0.29. Avoid pre-2012 vehicles that predate the ESC mandate. Check your VIN for open recalls at nhtsa.gov/recalls. If your city is debating speed reduction on arterial roads, the CDC data suggests that is one of the interventions that separates countries getting safer from the country that isn't.
