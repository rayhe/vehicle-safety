FARS Says 9% of Fatal Crashes Involve Drugs. The Real Number Is Closer to 55%.

Dale Impactor III • Clautomotive Desk
March 26, 2026
☕ 4 min read

According to the toxicology reports — and there are not nearly enough of them — our FARS data shows that 8.7% of drivers in fatal crashes test positive for nonalcohol drugs. Alcohol positive: 15.1%. Total impaired: 20%. Those numbers feel manageable. Depressing, but manageable.

They are also, according to a peer-reviewed study published this month in Accident Analysis & Prevention, off by a factor of six.[1]

55%
of fatally injured drivers tested positive for nonalcohol drugs (2018–2022)

Researchers analyzed 138,505 fatally injured drivers from FARS data spanning 2013 to 2022, divided into pre- and post-enhancement periods. In the first half (2013–2017), 39.9% of fatally injured drivers tested positive for at least one nonalcohol drug. In the second half (2018–2022), after FARS expanded its toxicology data collection, that number jumped to 55.0%.[1] Alcohol, meanwhile, barely moved: 37.1% to 37.9%.

Drugs passed alcohol years ago. Nobody updated the scoreboard.

Where the 6x gap comes from

Our database at The Crash Report uses FARS toxicology data aggregated across 490,736 drivers in fatal crashes between 2014 and 2023. We report 8.7% drug-positive. The published research reports 55%. Both use FARS. Both are technically correct. The gap comes from three compounding failures in how drug data gets collected, recorded, and reported.

Failure one: not everyone gets tested. On average, only about 60% of fatally injured drivers in the United States receive drug testing.[3] But that average hides enormous state-level variation. Some states test more than 90% of fatally injured drivers. Others test fewer than 10%. NHTSA, the GAO, and the NTSB have all flagged this inconsistency as a fundamental data quality problem.[3] If your state is one of the ones barely testing, your contribution to the national drug tox dataset is mostly zeros — not because your drivers are sober, but because nobody looked.

Failure two: stop-limit testing. This one should make you angry. In many forensic toxicology labs, if a driver’s blood alcohol concentration hits 0.08% or above, the lab stops testing.[2] No drug panel. No additional analysis. The reasoning: impairment is “already explained” by the alcohol, budgets are limited, and most states don’t impose enhanced penalties for poly-substance use. An NIJ-funded study of 2,514 suspected impaired driving cases found that 23% of those cases involved both alcohol and drugs.[2] Stop-limit testing would have caught the alcohol in those cases and missed the drugs entirely.

Failure three: FARS had a data cap. Before 2018, FARS limited recording to three nonalcohol drugs per driver.[1] The enhancement that year removed that cap. The result? The odds of detecting any nonalcohol drug jumped 42%. For three or more drugs, detection tripled (adjusted odds ratio: 3.01).[1] The drugs were always there. The form just didn’t have enough lines.

What’s actually in the blood

The breakdown from the 2018–2022 period is bleak. Marijuana positivity among fatally injured drivers: 26.4%, up from 14.4% in the earlier period. Stimulants — primarily methamphetamine and fentanyl — rose from 9.3% to 25.1%. That is a near-tripling. Depressants climbed from 9.1% to 13.7%. Drivers testing positive for two or more nonalcohol drugs went from 13.6% to 19.2%.[1]

One in five fatally injured drivers now has multiple nonalcohol drugs in their system. The poly-substance era arrived while the measurement infrastructure was still arguing about how to count single substances.

What this means for vehicle-level data

Every per-vehicle impairment statistic on this site — every Charger bar-car percentage, every Corvette alcohol rate, every Prius sobriety badge — is a floor, not a ceiling. When we report that the Dodge Charger has a 9.1% drug-positive rate from 4,339 drivers, the true rate is almost certainly multiples of that. The same goes for every vehicle in our database.

Our data captures what the states bothered to test for, what the labs bothered to analyze after clearing the alcohol threshold, and what FARS bothered to record before it ran out of fields. It is a document of institutional attention, not pharmacological truth.

Limitations

The 55% figure from the published study reflects drivers who died in crashes — not all drivers involved. Our 8.7% aggregates across all drivers in fatal crashes, including surviving drivers who may not have been tested. Comparing the two requires acknowledging different denominators. The 6x ratio is illustrative, not exact. Additionally, drug presence does not equal drug impairment: a positive THC test can reflect use days prior, and FARS makes no causal attribution between substance presence and crash causation.

Strongest counterargument

The jump from 39.9% to 55.0% may be partly a measurement artifact, not just a real increase in drugged driving. FARS’s 2018 enhancement improved detection by 42%, meaning some of the apparent rise reflects better counting, not worse behavior. This is a legitimate interpretation — and the study authors acknowledge it. But it actually strengthens the core point: if better counting reveals dramatically higher numbers, the old numbers were wrong. The question is not whether drugged driving increased. It is how bad the undercount was all along.
