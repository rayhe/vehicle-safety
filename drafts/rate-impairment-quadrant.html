The Drunkest Drivers Have the Lowest Death Rates. Physics Doesn’t Care About Your BAC.

Vin Wreckage • Clautomotive Desk
March 21, 2026

Plot every vehicle in FARS on two axes — fatality rate per 100 million VMT on one, percentage of drivers who tested positive for alcohol or drugs on the other — and a pattern emerges that should bother every traffic safety official in America.

31
vehicles with >20% impaired drivers and below-median death rates

I expected two clusters: dangerous cars with dangerous drivers (top-right) and safe cars with sober drivers (bottom-left). The intuitive story. Instead, FARS data for 2014–2023 across 117 vehicle models with 300+ fatalities produces four distinct quadrants, and the two counterintuitive ones hold 51 of the 117 vehicles.

The Quadrant Nobody Talks About

Set the medians at 1.5 deaths per 100M VMT and 20% impairment. The top-right quadrant — “Predictable Killers,” high rate plus high impairment — holds 27 vehicles. Mustangs, Camaros, aging GM sedans. Both the vehicle and the driver are the problem. Nobody needs a cross-tabulation to explain a Cobalt with a 5.1 rate and 22.4% impairment.

The biggest cluster — 39 vehicles — is “Safe + Sober” at bottom-left. Modern crossovers: RAV4 (0.19 rate, 18.4% impaired), Equinox (0.36, 19.4%), Rogue (0.35, 19%).[1] No surprises there either.

But the second-largest quadrant, with 31 vehicles, is bottom-right: high impairment, low death rate. Dodge owns this corner. The Charger — 22.7% of its fatal-crash drivers tested positive for substances, yet its fatality rate is just 0.75 per 100M VMT.[2] The Challenger sits at 22.5% impaired, rate 1.0. The Durango: 22.5% impaired, 0.54 rate. Even the Ford Crown Victoria — the cop car — shows 22.3% impairment but a rate of only 1.46.

These aren’t vehicles with low impairment rates that happen to be safe. These are vehicles whose drivers are more intoxicated than average and still dying at below-average rates.

The Physics Explanation

Mass. That’s the entire explanation. IIHS research on vehicle size and weight confirmed what Newton implied: for vehicles below fleet average weight, every 500 additional pounds meaningfully reduces occupant death risk.[3] The Dodge Charger weighs 4,200 lbs. The Durango tops 5,400. The Crown Victoria, built on a body-on-frame platform, came in at 4,100. These vehicles absorb impact energy that lighter vehicles transfer directly to occupants.

Meanwhile, the “Sober Deathtraps” quadrant (top-left: 20 vehicles) tells the inverse story. The Hyundai Veloster — 8.54 deaths per 100M VMT with only 17.4% driver impairment — is killing sober people at a rate that would be scandalous if anyone were tracking it this way.[1] The Chevrolet Tracker: 7.83 rate, 12.7% impaired. These drivers aren’t drunk. They’re just in cars that can’t protect them.

The Toyota Solara is the most extreme case. A fatality rate of 4.25 with only 4.1% driver impairment — the soberest vehicle in the entire dataset, and one of the deadliest. A mid-2000s Camry coupe with less structural rigidity than the sedan it was based on, killing attentive, clearheaded drivers at four times the rate of a Toyota RAV4 whose drivers are buzzed at nearly five times the Solara’s impairment rate.

What This Actually Means

The calculation: FARS fatality rate = deaths per 100M vehicle-miles-traveled, estimated from fleet size × NHTS annual mileage averages.[4] Impairment = percentage of drivers in FARS fatal crashes who tested positive for BAC > 0 or any drug. Cross-tabulation is original analysis of the three FARS data arrays (BY_MODEL, TOXICOLOGY, MODEL_YEAR) covering 117 vehicles with 300+ fatalities in the 2014–2023 FARS window.

The implication is ugly: decades of anti-drunk-driving campaigns targeted behavior, but FARS data suggests vehicle mass may be a larger determinant of whether you survive a fatal crash than whether you’re sober. An impaired driver in a 5,000-lb Durango (0.54 rate) is statistically 15.8 times less likely to die per mile than a sober driver in a 2,800-lb Veloster (8.54 rate).

Limitations

Three important caveats. First, FARS only records fatal crashes — the roughly 40,000 annual deaths, not the 6.7 million total crashes.[1] A vehicle with low fatality rates might still cause enormous numbers of injuries and property-damage crashes. Second, toxicology testing rates vary by state and crash type. Not every driver in a fatal crash is tested for drugs, which likely depresses impairment percentages for some vehicles more than others. Third, VMT estimates use NHTS averages by vehicle type, not actual odometer data, introducing ±15% uncertainty for low-volume models.

The Counterargument, at Full Strength

The strongest objection: this analysis flatters heavy vehicles by design. FARS captures deaths. A drunk driver in a 5,500-lb truck who causes a fatal crash that kills the other driver appears in the data as a vehicle with low occupant death risk. The heavy vehicle isn’t safer in any aggregate sense — it just exports its danger to smaller vehicles. The IIHS research confirms this: adding 500 lbs to an already-heavy vehicle increases risk to crash partners without meaningfully improving occupant protection.[3] The “Drunk Survivors” quadrant might more accurately be called “Drunk Exporters” — vehicles whose impaired drivers survive because someone in a lighter car didn’t.

That counterargument is partially supported by the data. The average deaths-per-crash ratio for “Drunk Survivors” is 0.47, meaning in over half of fatal crashes involving these vehicles, the person who died wasn’t inside the heavy vehicle. The “Sober Deathtraps” average 0.74 — when you crash one of those, you die.

Physics protects. Physics also kills. It just depends which seat you’re in.
