The IIHS Spent 60 Years Testing Crashes. Now It Wants to Stop the Driver.

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
May 3, 2026

The Insurance Institute for Highway Safety has been slamming cars into walls since 1959, measuring how well sheet metal and airbags protect the people inside when physics goes wrong. For six decades, "safety" meant one question: when you crash, how badly do you break? Starting in 2027, the IIHS will ask a different question entirely: why did you crash in the first place?

IIHS President David Harkey announced that Top Safety Pick+ winners must include intelligent speed assistance by 2027 and impaired-driving detection by 2030.[1] That second requirement means the car must sense when its driver is drunk, high, or dangerously drowsy and intervene before the crash happens. Not after. Before.

Sixty years of crash testing have produced remarkable engineering. Frontal overlap tests gave us crumple zones that fold precisely. Side impact standards prompted curtain airbags. Roof strength ratings reduced rollover fatalities. But all of that engineering activates in the final half-second, the 150 milliseconds between contact and rest, and the improvements are flattening out. The IIHS just proved this itself: it tightened its 2026 criteria, demanding good rear-seat protection and better pedestrian crash avoidance, and 21 vehicles that earned the top award in 2025 lost it in 2026, dropping the winner count from 66 to 45.[2] The low-hanging fruit in passive safety is gone. What remains is the driver.

FARS data across 490,736 tested drivers in fatal crashes between 2014 and 2023 reveals the scale of what structural engineering cannot fix: 98,348 of them, a clean 20%, tested positive for alcohol, drugs, or both.[3] Sports car drivers led at 22.5%. Van drivers came in lowest at 18.1%. The gap between the worst and best vehicle class was barely four percentage points, because impairment does not respect body style, price bracket, or marketing demographic. It is the one constant FARS measures that crumple zone geometry has zero influence over.

Speed tells a similar story. NHTSA data shows speeding contributed to 29% of all crash fatalities in 2023, killing roughly 11,000 people a year.[4] Intelligent speed assistance systems, the technology IIHS will require by 2027, use GPS and camera-read speed limit signs to warn drivers or limit throttle response when they exceed posted limits. Europe has already mandated ISA in all new vehicles since July 2024. The American timeline, characteristically, is five years behind and voluntary through an insurance consortium rather than federal regulation.

Run the math on the combined addressable envelope. If impaired-driving detection prevented even half of the roughly 3,800 annual impairment-linked fatalities visible in our FARS dataset, that saves 1,900 lives per year. If ISA cut half of the 11,000 speed-related deaths, that saves 5,500 more. The combined theoretical ceiling approaches 7,400 lives per year, and that estimate uses deliberately conservative 50% effectiveness assumptions, because both technologies are immature and drivers will find ways to defeat them. For comparison, the federal AEB mandate finalized in FMVSS 127 projects saving 360 lives annually.[5] The IIHS behavioral safety requirements address an envelope roughly 20 times larger than the single biggest federal vehicle safety rule of the decade.

The strongest counterargument writes itself and deserves full-strength airing: ISA systems might kill people they are designed to save. A driver merging onto a highway where the GPS database shows 45 mph but traffic flows at 65 could be trapped at a dangerous speed differential. A sober driver locked out by a false-positive impairment sensor in a remote area at 2 AM faces a different, non-trivial risk, and if breathalyzer-grade false positive rates of 0.2% scale to 280 million registered vehicles, that is 560,000 wrongful lockouts per year. The IIHS is not mandating these technologies through law. It is using its award program as leverage, which means automakers can choose not to comply and simply forfeit the safety rating. Whether that distinction protects consumers or insulates the IIHS from liability for bad implementations is an open question.

What the IIHS is admitting, without using these words, is that the crashworthiness ceiling is close. Building a car that survives a 40 mph frontal overlap has been solved; the 2026 Toyota Camry, Subaru Forester, and Hyundai Tucson all earn Good ratings in every structural test. But 20% of the people driving those cars into fatal crashes are impaired, and 29% are speeding, and no crumple zone in the world fixes that. The next chapter of vehicle safety is not about the car absorbing the crash. It is about the car refusing to let the crash start.

What this means for you

If you are buying a car in 2027 or later, check whether it includes intelligent speed assistance. By 2030, look for impaired-driving detection, likely through steering-pattern analysis or infrared driver-facing cameras. These features will be required for the IIHS top safety award, which means vehicles without them will carry lower safety ratings, which means your insurance premiums will eventually reflect the gap. In the meantime, the single most effective thing you can do has not changed: do not drive impaired, and do not drive 20 over the limit. Technology that prevents those two behaviors could save 7,400 people a year. Your own discipline already can.
