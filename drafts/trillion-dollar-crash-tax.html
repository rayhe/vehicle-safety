By The Numbers

Every American Pays $724 a Year for Other People's Car Crashes. Two Trucks Account for $71.

Axle McScatter • Clautomotive Desk
June 6, 2026

Avian Law Group released a report this week calculating the total societal cost of motor vehicle crashes in the United States.[1] $1.77 trillion. That one figure absorbs everything: fatalities, injuries, property damage, emergency services, lost productivity, legal and insurance overhead, and the quality-of-life deficit that follows 2.42 million injured people home from the hospital every year, often into a life they no longer recognize. Divide it by population. Every American pays more than $1,000 annually for a transportation system that kills 107 people per day. I did something the report didn't. I split that bill by vehicle.

$23.5B
Annual fatality cost of just two vehicles: the Chevrolet Silverado and the Ford F-150. More than all 17 sports cars in the FARS database combined.

The methodology is simple and verifiable. The Department of Transportation assigns a Value of a Statistical Life of $12.5 million per fatality.[2] FARS recorded 191,193 deaths across 337 vehicle models over ten years (2014 to 2023).[3] Multiply each model's annual death count by $12.5 million and you get a per-model fatality cost. This captures only deaths, and in that sense dramatically underestimates the true cost, which includes hundreds of billions in injury, property, and administrative expenses. But fatality cost alone reveals which vehicles are generating the most societal damage, because the DOT's VSL is standardized, reproducible, and the same for a Porsche Macan as for a Nissan Versa.

The Silverado leads. At 9,591 deaths over the decade, it generates $11.99 billion in annual fatality cost, or $36.33 per American per year. The F-150 follows at $11.49 billion ($34.83 per American). Together, two pickup truck nameplates from two manufacturers generate $23.5 billion annually. That exceeds the combined fatality cost of every sports car in the database: every Corvette, every Mustang, every Camaro, every Porsche 911 and Challenger and MX-5 Miata, all 17 models, totaling $7.7 billion. America's most popular trucks cost three times more in human lives than the cars designed to be driven recklessly.

But volume doesn't explain everything, and this is where the numbers complicate the intuition. The five most expensive models on an absolute basis are the Silverado ($12.0B), F-150 ($11.5B), Accord ($8.9B), Civic ($8.2B), and Camry ($7.9B). Those are America's best-sellers. They generate the most fatality cost because there are millions of them on the road, not because they kill at unusual rates. The Silverado's death rate per 100 million VMT is 1.25, close to the overall national average. The Honda Accord's rate is 3.07, more than double the Silverado's, but the Accord generates $2 billion less per year in absolute cost because its fleet is smaller. Which number matters more? If you're a policymaker budgeting ambulance services, the absolute cost is the relevant metric: a dead body costs the same amount regardless of what caused the crash. But if you're a consumer choosing between two cars at a dealership, the rate tells you your personal risk. Both numbers are real, and neither obsoletes the other.

The class breakdown illuminates a structural fact about American driving. Sedans generate $111.4 billion in annual fatality cost, making them the most expensive vehicle class at $337.60 per American. SUVs follow at $58.1 billion ($175.92), then pickups at $52.0 billion ($157.55), vans at $9.9 billion ($29.91), and sports cars at $7.7 billion ($23.23). Sedans dominate because the FARS dataset spans a decade during which sedans still constituted the majority of the American fleet. As crossovers continue replacing sedans in showrooms (median SUV death rate: 0.35 per 100M VMT versus 0.68 for sedans), the sedan's share of this bill will shrink. Whether total cost shrinks with it depends on how much the vehicle weight arms race in SUVs and pickups raises costs for everyone else.

There is a strongest-possible objection to this entire framing and it deserves full-strength articulation. Calculating a "crash tax" per model and dividing by the U.S. population implies collective cost distribution, but crashes are not an externality equally imposed on all Americans. A Silverado fatality in rural Montana is not billed to a subway commuter in Manhattan. Insurance premiums, healthcare costs, and infrastructure spending are distributed through mechanisms that loosely correlate with driving exposure, not uniformly across the population. The "$724 per American" figure is directionally useful but mechanically fictional. The real distribution is wildly uneven: drivers subsidize pedestrians, high-mileage commuters subsidize remote workers, and residents of high-fatality states subsidize residents of low-fatality states. Any honest accounting of "your" crash tax requires knowing where you live, how much you drive, and what your state spends on road infrastructure.

What you can do with this information is straightforward. If you're shopping for a vehicle and want to minimize your household's contribution to the crash-cost ledger, buy newer and buy heavier. The Tesla Model Y (rate: 0.03, annual fatality cost: $712,500, or $0.002 per American), the Kia Telluride (0.04, $3.9M), and the Mazda CX-30 (0.12, $6.5M) have fatality costs three orders of magnitude smaller than the top ten. Every model built after 2018 with standard AEB, ESC, and side curtain airbags belongs to a different actuarial universe than the 2006 Impala still rolling around on bald tires with a cracked windshield. The single most effective thing NHTSA could do to lower the $1.77 trillion bill is not a new regulation. It's getting old cars off the road.
