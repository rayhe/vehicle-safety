# Research: FARS Can't Detect Tired — The 10x Drowsy Driving Death Gap

## Angle
FARS officially records 633 drowsy-driving deaths in 2023. The Governors Highway Safety Association (GHSA), using multiple data sources including FARS itself and the Crash Investigation Sampling System (CISS), estimates the real number is approximately 6,326 — a 10x gap. The fundamental problem: unlike alcohol (BAC test) or drugs (toxicology screen), there is no post-crash test for fatigue. The counting system is structurally blind to one of the top killers on American roads.

## Primary Sources (3+)

### 1. GHSA "Wake Up Call" 2026 Update
- URL: https://www.ghsa.org/resource-hub/wake-up-call-update
- PDF: https://www.ghsa.org/sites/default/files/2026-02/Wake_Up_Call_2026_Update.pdf
- Author: Jim Hedlund (Highway Safety North), commissioned by GHSA with GM
- Key findings:
  - 17.6% of all fatal crashes (2017-2021) involved a drowsy driver
  - Estimated 6,326 drowsy driving deaths in 2023
  - FARS only records 633 drowsy driving deaths in 2023
  - 1.57% of all driving at any given time is drowsy (~3 of every 200 drivers)

### 2. NHTSA FARS Database
- URL: https://www.nhtsa.gov/research-data/fatality-analysis-reporting-system-fars
- Official 2023 drowsy driving death count: 633
- Total traffic deaths 2023: ~40,990
- FARS codes drowsiness as a driver-related factor, but it depends on police officer judgment at scene

### 3. AAA Foundation for Traffic Safety
- URL: https://aaafoundation.org
- Nearly 20% of Americans admit to driving drowsy in the previous month
- Prior studies established the sleep-deprivation crash risk curve

### 4. NHTSA Crash Investigation Sampling System (CISS)
- URL: https://www.nhtsa.gov/crash-data-systems/crash-investigation-sampling-system
- Used by GHSA to cross-reference FARS and develop adjusted estimates

## Key Data Points
- 633 vs 6,326: the official vs estimated drowsy driving death count (2023)
- 10x multiplier gap
- 17.6% of all fatal crashes involved drowsy drivers (GHSA analysis, 2017-2021)
- 17-19 hours awake = impairment comparable to 0.05 BAC
- 70% of parents with children under 17 report driving drowsy
- Higher risk: ages 16-24, males, college students, long-haul truckers, night-shift nurses
- No physical evidence left after a crash — no "tired test"
- Drowsy driving is the "4th D" after drunk, drugged, and distracted
