# Research: The Luxury Impairment Paradox

## Angle
America's drunkest drivers don't drive beaters — they drive luxury cars. FARS toxicology data shows luxury and performance brands have systematically higher impairment rates in fatal crashes than domestic/economy brands.

## Kill Test
- **Genuinely surprising?** YES — reverses the "drunk redneck in a pickup" stereotype
- **Novel data cross-tab?** YES — FARS toxicology aggregated by brand (not done in 127 existing articles)
- **External validation?** YES — LendingTree 2024 study + UC Berkeley crosswalk study
- **Would a reader share this?** Absolutely — class/wealth angle is inherently provocative

### Key Finding
Top 3 impairment brands: Infiniti (24.4%), Pontiac (22.4%), Audi (22.0%)
Bottom 3 impairment brands: Toyota (19.1%), Lincoln (19.0%), Mercury (19.2%)
Spread: 5.4 percentage points — narrow but consistent luxury > economy pattern

## Novel Contribution
1. First brand-level aggregation of FARS toxicology data (490K+ drivers)
2. Discovery that within-brand model spread (up to 21 pts) exceeds between-brand spread (5.4 pts)
3. Quantitative evidence that the model you choose within a brand predicts impairment risk more than brand choice itself

## Limitations
- FARS only captures fatal crashes — does NOT represent all DUI incidents
- Impairment testing rates vary by jurisdiction (some states test more than others)
- Brand demographics are confounded (age, income, geography)
- Small sample sizes for some luxury brands (Volvo: 535 drivers)
- "Impaired" includes BAC > 0 OR drug-positive, which includes trace amounts

## Strongest Counterargument
The luxury-impairment correlation might be driven by demographics rather than wealth itself. Luxury brand drivers skew younger in fatal crashes (performance models attract younger buyers). Younger drivers across ALL brands have higher impairment rates. The brand is a proxy for age, not wealth. If you controlled for driver age, the brand effect might disappear.
