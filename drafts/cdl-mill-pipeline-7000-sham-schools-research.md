# Research: CDL Mill Pipeline — 7,000 Sham Schools, Five Dead on I-95

## Angle
The CDL training pipeline is a body count factory. FMCSA has removed 7,000+ sham driving schools from its registry since 2025, but the graduates are already on the road. The I-95 bus crash (May 30, 2026) — which killed 5 people including two children — was caused by a driver who got his CDL from a school now under federal investigation. This is the consumer-safety angle: when you share a highway with a commercial vehicle, you're betting your family's lives that the driver was properly trained. Seven thousand sham schools say that bet is getting worse.

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: I-95 Bus Crash (May 30, 2026)
- Location: I-95 South near Quantico exit, Stafford County, Virginia
- 5 killed, including a 13-year-old and a 7-year-old (all from Massachusetts)
- 34+ patients transported, 3 critical
- Driver: Jing Sheng Dong, 48, Staten Island, NY
- Charged with 5 counts involuntary manslaughter + reckless driving (indicted by Stafford County grand jury)
- Bus plowed into slowed work zone traffic, rear-ended a Suburban which was forced into an Acura
- The Acura caught fire — 4 of the 5 fatalities were in the Acura (including both children)
- Fifth death in the Suburban
- Driver reportedly does not speak English
- Got CDL in New York in 2024, per Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy
- CDL linked to "7 CDL Driving School" — under federal investigation
- Sources: Reuters, Washington Examiner

### Source 2: DOT Subpoena to New York State
- DOT/FMCSA subpoenaed New York State DMV for records on Dong's CDL
- Accused NY of "failing to provide records" / allegedly obstructing inquiry
- Seeking: CDL records, entry-level driver training docs, investigation docs on "7 CDL Driving School"
- FMCSA said it couldn't get records "through other reasonable means"
- Compliance deadline: 10 AM Wednesday (June 4, 2026)
- Warned of criminal or civil contempt penalties for non-compliance
- Source: Washington Examiner (obtained subpoena)

### Source 3: FMCSA CDL Mill Crackdown (Feb 2026)
- 550+ sham CDL schools found in violation during 5-day enforcement blitz
- 300+ investigators across 50 states
- 1,426 on-site sting operations
- 448 notices of proposed removal for failing basic safety standards
- 109 schools VOLUNTARILY WITHDREW when they learned investigators were coming
- 97 additional schools still under investigation
- Since 2025: FMCSA has removed 7,000+ CDL schools from Training Provider Registry
  - ~3,800 in January (mostly non-responsive)
  - 550+ in February sting
  - Rest in ongoing enforcement
- Violations found:
  - Unqualified instructors (didn't hold correct licenses for vehicles they taught)
  - Fake addresses
  - Wrong vehicles (didn't match training being offered)
  - Failed to test students on basic requirements
  - Failed to train on hazmat transport
  - One removed school had previously trained school bus drivers
- Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy: "For too long, the trucking industry has operated like the Wild, Wild West"
- Sources: FreightWaves, CCJ, Overdrive, CDL Life, Trucking Dive

## Novel Contribution
Cross-referencing the timeline: FMCSA removed 7,000+ sham schools but the I-95 crash driver got his CDL in 2024 — meaning he was trained and licensed through the same pipeline the feds were supposedly cleaning up. The enforcement lag between removing schools and revoking the licenses of their graduates creates a rolling body count. 7,000 schools removed, unknown number of graduates still driving with licenses earned from those schools.

## Limitations
- FARS data doesn't break out deaths by driver training quality — no way to quantify how many of the 5,340 annual deaths involve CDL mill graduates specifically
- The I-95 crash investigation is ongoing; driver's CDL training details may change as more facts emerge
- The 7,000 school number includes non-responsive/inactive schools, not all were actively fraudulent
- Can't know how many CDL mill graduates are currently on the road

## Strongest Counterargument
The vast majority of CDL holders are properly trained and the industry's fatality rate per mile has been relatively stable. The CDL mill crackdown is evidence the system self-corrects. And the I-95 crash may have been caused by fatigue or distraction, not training deficiency — we don't yet know.
