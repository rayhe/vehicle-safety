Money Buys Airbags, Not Sobriety: Luxury Cars Match Muscle Cars in DUI Rates

Dale Impactor III • Clautomotive Desk
March 29, 2026
★ 3 min read

Audi A3 drivers involved in fatal crashes tested alcohol-positive 22.7% of the time.[1] For reference, the Ford Mustang sits at 17.5%. The Dodge Charger, which we already crowned America's favorite bar car, manages 22.7%. The Audi A3 ties it. Across 63 luxury vehicle models in FARS data from 2014 to 2023, the assumption that expensive cars carry responsible drivers is empirically false.

Audi A3 drivers alcohol-positive in fatal crashes — higher than any muscle car

The full leaderboard reads like a valet ticket at a steakhouse. BMW 7 Series: 21.3% alcohol-positive across 230 drivers. Cadillac CTS: 20.6% across 931. Infiniti G37: 19.6% across 959. Porsche 911: 19.3%. Tesla Model S: 19.1%.[1] For comparison, the overall sedan average in FARS is 15.4%. Sports cars average 17.1%. Luxury sedans blow past both.

A 2024 LendingTree analysis of DUI rates by brand corroborates this pattern from the opposite direction: BMW topped the national rankings at 3.1 DUIs per 1,000 drivers. Acura placed second at 2.7. Audi third at 2.4.[2] That is not a coincidence emerging from two unrelated datasets. It is the same signal measured two different ways.

The Sedan-SUV Split

Within every luxury brand that sells both body styles, sedans post higher alcohol rates than SUVs. Not sometimes. Every single time.

BMW 7 Series: 21.3% alcohol. BMW X5: 14.1%. Gap: 7.2 percentage points. Mercedes E-Class: 18.0%. Mercedes GL-Class: 10.0%. Gap: 8.0 points. Audi A3: 22.7%. Audi Q7: 16.9%. Gap: 5.8 points. Cadillac CTS: 20.6%. Cadillac Escalade: 15.9%. Gap: 4.7 points. Lexus LS: 18.0%. Lexus GX: 12.7%. Gap: 5.3 points.[1]

Average gap across six brands: 5.7 percentage points. The sedan buyer and the SUV buyer within the same brand, at the same price point, are statistically different drivers. The sedan cohort skews toward solo evening driving. The SUV cohort skews toward families. Same showroom. Different toxicology profiles.

The M5 Anomaly

Then there is the BMW M5. Among 109 drivers in fatal crashes, 15.6% tested drug-positive, the highest rate of any luxury vehicle in the dataset. Combined with its 17.4% alcohol rate, 22.9% of M5 drivers in fatal crashes were impaired.[1] A $110,000 performance sedan with the impairment profile of a vehicle half its price. The M5 occupies a peculiar space: wealthy enough for the best crash protection money can buy, reckless enough to need it.

Why Wealth Correlates With Impaired Driving

A 2012 UC Berkeley study by Paul Piff at the Institute of Personality and Social Research observed 152 drivers approaching pedestrian crosswalks. While 80% of cars stopped, drivers of expensive vehicles were significantly less likely to yield.[3] Crosswalk noncompliance and drunk driving are different offenses, but both reflect the same underlying variable: risk tolerance that increases with perceived invulnerability.

Luxury cars deliver that perception honestly. German engineering really does save lives in crashes. BMW X3 owners die at 28.3 per 100,000 fleet. Chevrolet Cobalt owners die at 586.7 per 100,000 fleet.[1] But structural survival advantage and driver sobriety are orthogonal. The data argues that knowing your car will protect you does not make you less likely to drink before driving. It may make you more likely.

The Counterargument

The strongest objection: luxury cars are driven disproportionately in urban areas near bars and restaurants, inflating alcohol exposure per mile relative to a Silverado commuting 40 miles of interstate. Geography and use patterns rather than any wealth-impairment link could account for the elevated rates. That is plausible and partially true. But it does not explain the sedan-SUV split within brands, where both body styles share the same urban geography. A BMW 7 Series and a BMW X5 are both parked in the same driveways and the same restaurant valet lots. Only one has a 21.3% alcohol rate.

Limitations

FARS captures only fatal crashes. The impairment profile of luxury drivers in non-fatal crashes may differ. Sample sizes for some models are modest (BMW M5: 109 drivers, Audi A3: 203). Drug positivity in FARS includes prescription medications, not exclusively illicit substances. Toxicology testing rates vary by jurisdiction. And critically, these are rates among drivers already in fatal crashes, not the DUI rate of the general driving population. A vehicle that survives more crashes filters its FARS sample toward higher-severity events, which may independently correlate with impairment.
