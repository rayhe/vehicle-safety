# Research Notes: Wrong-Way Crashes — 302 Fatal Per Year, 40x Deadlier

## Angle
Wrong-way driving crashes are vanishingly rare (0.3% of all crashes) but 40x more likely to be fatal. They kill ~400 people per year. Detection cameras that can alert law enforcement within seconds exist and cost ~$113K per ramp. Almost nobody is deploying them. This is a solvable problem being solved almost nowhere.

## Kill Test

- **Genuinely newsworthy?** YES — Oklahoma wrong-way crash killed 4 teenagers (May 22, 2026). Columbus Ohio installing 30 detection cameras ($3.4M). Florida man drove 16 miles wrong-way before fatal crash.

## Primary Sources

### 1. FARS Study (2004-2020)
- "Comprehensive analysis of trends, distribution, and odds of wrong-way driving fatal crashes on divided highways" (Journal of Safety Research, 2024)
- 302 wrong-way driving (WWD) fatal crashes per year on average
- 6,953 fatalities over 17-year study period (~409/year)
- Frequency remained relatively stable with slight increase over time
- Key factors: day of week, time of day, lighting, driver age, gender, license status, impairment
- Road profiles (sag curves, hillcrests) increase likelihood

### 2. Ohio DOT (June 2026)
- Wrong-way crashes = 0.3% of all crashes in Ohio (2025)
- But 40x more likely to be deadly
- Columbus installing 30 wrong-way detection cameras at $3.4M total ($113K per ramp)
- Camera system: stationary, divides view into 3 boxes, detects wrong-direction movement
- Sends immediate alert to ODOT traffic management center (always staffed)
- Employee reviews footage, contacts law enforcement
- Reduces response from "miles and minutes" to "feet and seconds"
- Spike strips NOT viable: designed for low-speed/low-volume (garages), debris issues

### 3. Oklahoma Crash (May 22, 2026)
- Michael Salomon Rosario-Cruz, 26-27, Mississippi
- DUI in Ford F-150, hit Ford Fiesta head-on on I-40 in Yukon, OK
- 4 killed: Kiercy Hickson (20), Quincy Jones (19), Brad Palmer (18), Haliegh Salazar (18)
- Jones and Palmer had graduated El Reno High School the week before
- Modelo beer cans inside and around truck + loaded pistol
- Charged with 4 counts of second-degree murder
- F-150 vs Fiesta: mass mismatch made collision unsurvivable for Fiesta occupants

### 4. Florida Wrong-Way (May 26, 2026)
- West Palm Beach man drove 16 miles wrong-way on US-1
- Semi-rural corridor, limited lighting, narrow shoulders
- 10-15 minutes of wrong-way travel before collision
- No interception despite 16 miles of exposure
- Toxicology pending

### 5. NHTSA Alcohol-Impaired Driving Data
- 32% of all traffic fatalities involve alcohol-impaired drivers (BAC ≥ 0.08)
- Wrong-way crashes: ~60-80% involve impaired drivers (per multiple studies)
- Peak hours: 12 AM - 4 AM (bar closing time)

## FARS Cross-Reference
From our data, the Oklahoma crash illustrates the weight-class problem:
- Ford F-150: 9,194 deaths, rate 1.04/100M VMT, lethality ratio 0.458
- Ford Fiesta: 513 deaths, rate 1.02/100M VMT
- The F-150 occupant survived. All 4 Fiesta occupants died.
- In fatal crashes involving F-150s, 54% of deaths are in the OTHER vehicle

## Novel Contribution
Cross-referencing wrong-way crash frequency (302/year) with detection camera cost ($113K/ramp) and interstate exit ramp count (~65,000 nationally) reveals:
- Full national deployment: ~$7.3B
- Annual fatalities prevented: estimated 200-300 (given early detection effectiveness)
- Cost per life saved: ~$24K-$37K per year
- Compare: NHTSA values a statistical life at $13.2M
- ROI is 350:1 to 550:1

This is one of the highest-ROI safety interventions available, deployed at almost none of the applicable locations.

## Limitations
- FARS data doesn't specifically tag wrong-way crashes (manner of collision doesn't isolate them cleanly)
- The 302/year figure is from 2004-2020 FARS study; 2021-2025 data may differ
- Camera effectiveness claims are from pilot deployments, not controlled studies
- The $113K/ramp figure is Ohio-specific; costs vary by state
- Not all wrong-way fatalities are on divided highways with ramps
