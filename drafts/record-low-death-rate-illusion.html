Existential Dread

36,640 Americans Died on the Road Last Year. NHTSA Called It a Record.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
May 14, 2026

NHTSA dropped its 2025 preliminary numbers last month with the enthusiasm of a school that just raised its GPA from a D+ to a C-. 36,640 traffic fatalities, a 6.7% decline from 2024, the fewest since 2019, fifteen consecutive quarters of improvement, and a fatality rate of 1.10 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled. Press releases called it historic, headlines said record-low.[1]

1.08
The actual U.S. record-low fatality rate per 100M VMT, set in 2014 with 3,965 fewer deaths

In 2014, the United States recorded 32,675 traffic deaths and a fatality rate of 1.08 per 100 million VMT. That was eleven years ago, before mandatory automatic emergency braking, before every new car had a rearview camera, before lane-keeping and blind-spot monitors became standard equipment on a Corolla. Back then, 32,675 dead was treated as a crisis, and NHTSA launched Vision Zero while advocates demanded reform. A rate of 1.08 was acknowledged as the lowest in U.S. history, and nobody threw a parade.[2][3]

Eleven years later, with a decade of mandated safety technology layered onto the fleet, the death count is 12% higher and the rate is worse. We added backup cameras to every new vehicle, electronic stability control to every platform, automatic high beams and adaptive cruise and collision warnings that scream at you through the steering wheel, and we managed to kill an additional 3,965 people per year compared to the baseline we were already ashamed of.

What happened between 2014 and 2025 was not a safety plateau but a spike. American traffic deaths jumped from 32,675 in 2014 to 35,092 in 2015, crept upward through 2019 at 36,096, then detonated during the pandemic: 42,939 dead in 2021, the worst toll since 2005. Year by year the count clawed back, quarter by quarter, until 2025 landed at 36,640, and the federal government declared victory because the number was lower than the catastrophe that preceded it.[4]

The per-VMT rate makes it look even more reasonable if you don't examine the denominator. Americans drove roughly 3.33 trillion miles in 2025, about 29.8 billion more than in 2024 and roughly 300 billion more than in 2014. More driving inflates the bottom of the fraction, pressing the rate down even when the numerator, which represents corpses, goes up. This is denominator math dressed as progress. Divide the same tragedy by a bigger number and the quotient looks better.[1]

The international comparison makes the denominator trick harder to ignore. In 2024, the European Union recorded 19,800 road fatalities across 450 million people, a rate of roughly 44 deaths per million population. The United States, with 340 million people and 36,640 deaths, runs at approximately 108 per million, which is 2.5 times the EU average. Sweden, which treats traffic death as a public health emergency and not a cost of freedom, posted 22 per million in 2024. The U.S. rate is nearly five times Sweden's.[5][6]

And the gap is not closing. In 2014, the EU recorded about 25,300 road deaths. By 2024, that fell to 19,800, a 22% reduction across a decade in which European roads carried more traffic, more electric scooters, and more delivery vans than ever. The United States, over the same period, went from 32,675 to 36,640, a 12% increase. Same decade, same access to the same crash-avoidance technology, opposite trajectories.[5]

A 2016 CDC analysis of 20 high-income countries found the U.S. had the highest crash death rate per 100,000 population, the highest rate per 10,000 registered vehicles, and the fifth-highest rate per 100 million miles traveled among the group. Per-VMT in that study, using 2013 data: 1.10. Same number NHTSA announced last month as a triumph, already an embarrassment thirteen years earlier. CDC calculated that if the U.S. matched even Belgium, the second-worst performer in the study, it would save 12,000 lives annually.[7]

Thirty-nine states reported year-over-year declines in 2025, and pedestrian fatalities dropped 11%. These are real improvements, and the people who worked on speed management, intersection design, and impaired-driving enforcement deserve credit for bending the curve during the worst sustained spike in two decades. Fifteen consecutive quarters of decline represent genuine progress against a genuine crisis.[1]

But progress from the peak of a catastrophe is not the same as progress from the floor. America has not improved its traffic safety since 2014. It broke itself, badly, and is now reassembling the pieces in approximately the same configuration they were in before, while adding more safety equipment to every vehicle it sells. If mandatory AEB, rearview cameras, ESC, and a decade of lane-keeping systems delivered net zero improvement over 2014 levels, something structural is overwhelming the technology, and it is probably the roads themselves: wider, faster, built around throughput instead of survival, running through communities designed so that walking across the street requires the same risk calculus as merging onto an interstate.

If you are shopping for a vehicle: check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls before anything else. Buy the newest model year you can afford, pick an IIHS Top Safety Pick+, and remember that driver-assist technology only works when the road was designed for human error, which most American roads were not. Your car's safest feature is the brake pedal. Its second-safest feature is everyone else using theirs.
