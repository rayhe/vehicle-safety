# Research: The GMC Sierra — The Silverado's Other Half

## Angle
FARS tracks deaths by nameplate, not platform. The Chevrolet Silverado sits at #1 with 9,591 deaths. The GMC Sierra sits at #7 with 3,337. Nobody connects them because they wear different badges. But they share the same GMT/T1XX platform, same frame, same powertrain, same safety structure. Combined: **12,928 deaths over 2014–2023** — the single deadliest vehicle platform in American transportation.

## Kill Test: Is This Genuinely Newsworthy?
YES. No FARS analysis I've found aggregates by platform rather than nameplate. This is the kind of cross-tabulation that changes how you read every vehicle safety ranking. The nameplate system literally hides 25.8% of GM's full-size truck body count behind a different badge.

## Novel Contributions
1. **Platform-level death aggregation**: GM full-size truck = 12,928 deaths vs Ford F-Series (F-150 + F-250) = 10,103 vs Ram = 5,121. GM leads by 28%.
2. **Same platform, different death rate**: Sierra rate 1.01/100M VMT vs Silverado rate 1.25/100M VMT — a 24% gap for mechanically identical trucks. Hypothesis: Sierra buyers skew wealthier (GMC "Professional Grade" marketing), buying higher trims with more safety features.
3. **Combined self-fatality ratio**: 0.482 — meaning 51.8% of people who die in GM full-size truck fatal crashes are NOT the truck's occupant. At 12,928 total deaths, that's ~6,700 other-party deaths.
4. **IIHS 2023 study**: Trucks with hood heights >40" are 45% more deadly to pedestrians. Both Sierra and Silverado qualify.

## Strongest Counterargument
The Silverado's higher rate could reflect fleet/commercial use (higher mileage, more exposure, more base-model work trucks with fewer safety features) rather than a meaningful safety difference. The Sierra's lower rate may simply reflect that its buyers — who pay $5–10K more for essentially the same truck — get higher trim levels with better safety tech as standard.

## Limitations
- FARS only captures fatal crashes, not all crashes. Fleet composition (work truck vs personal) isn't tracked.
- Our VMT estimates use average annual miles, not actual odometer data. Commercial Silverados likely drive more miles than our estimate.
- We can't directly measure buyer demographics or trim-level distribution from FARS.
- The "platform aggregation" assumes shared engineering = shared safety profile, which is mostly but not perfectly true (slightly different cab structures, bumper heights in some years).
