# Research: Telluride Fire Recall × FARS Blind Spot

## Angle
The Kia Telluride is tied for the 2nd-lowest crash death rate in FARS (0.04/100M VMT, 31 deaths in 10 years, fleet of 612,500). Only the Tesla Model Y at 0.03 is lower. By every traditional crash safety metric, it's one of the safest vehicles in America.

Today (July 9, 2026), NHTSA announced Kia is recalling 462,869 Tellurides — virtually the entire fleet — because the front power seat motor can overheat and catch fire while the vehicle is parked or being driven. The defect is a stuck power seat slide knob or an improper recall repair. 100% of vehicles estimated to have the defect.

The novel cross-reference: FARS only captures crash deaths. Fire risk from component defects is invisible to every fatality rate we publish. The Telluride's 0.04 crash death rate might be meaningless if the thing burns down in your garage.

## Key Data Points

### FARS (crash deaths)
- Telluride: 31 deaths, 86 fatal crashes, fleet 612,500, VMT 7,656M, rate 0.04
- Death/crash ratio: 31/86 = 0.360 (when it IS in a fatal crash, occupant dies 36% of the time)
- Annual crash deaths: 3.1/year
- For context, the average vehicle death rate across FARS is ~1.0/100M VMT
- Telluride is 25× safer than the average by this metric

### Fire recall
- NHTSA recall announced July 9, 2026
- 462,869 vehicles (virtually all Tellurides ever sold)
- Defect: front power seat motor overheat → fire
- Cause: stuck slide knob or improper prior recall repair
- Risk: fire while parked or driving
- Remedy: electronic fuse assembly, free
- Source: Reuters, NHTSA

### The blind spot
- FARS = Fatality Analysis Reporting System = crash deaths only
- Vehicle fires kill ~500 people annually in the US (NFPA data)
- Non-crash vehicle fires are NOT in FARS
- NHTSA's recall database captures fire risk, but it doesn't feed into any death RATE metric
- A vehicle can have a 0.00 crash death rate and still burn down with occupants inside
- IIHS ratings don't test for fire risk either
- Consumer Reports doesn't test for fire risk
- The only signal is the recall database itself

## Novel contribution
Cross-referencing the crash death rate (0.04, near-zero) with the fire recall scope (463K, near-total) to expose a measurement blind spot. FARS-based safety rankings can't see this threat.

## Strongest counterargument
The fire defect has a remedy (electronic fuse assembly), no deaths have been reported from the defect, and the recall system WORKED — Kia identified the problem and is fixing it. The Telluride is still genuinely safe in crashes. Fire risk from component defects exists for all vehicles, and the recall system is the correct safety net for this category. FARS wasn't designed to measure this and shouldn't be criticized for not doing so.

## Limitations
- We don't know how many Telluride fires have actually occurred from this defect
- NFPA vehicle fire fatality data is aggregate, not per-model
- The 462,869 number represents total Tellurides potentially affected, not vehicles that will actually catch fire
- The defect may be low-probability per-vehicle (but affecting the entire fleet)
