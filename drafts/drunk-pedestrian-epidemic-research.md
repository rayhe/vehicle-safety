# Research: The Drunk Pedestrian Epidemic

## Angle
Nearly 1 in 3 pedestrians killed in fatal crashes were legally intoxicated (BAC ≥ .08). The pedestrian safety conversation is almost entirely about vehicle design (hood height, AEB, A-pillar blind zones) and road infrastructure (stroads, crosswalks, lighting). But the FARS data shows the single largest behavioral factor in pedestrian deaths is the pedestrian's own impairment — and nobody talks about it.

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES. This flips the dominant narrative. Every major pedestrian safety organization frames the crisis as a vehicle/infrastructure problem. The data says the biggest behavioral factor is on the other side of the equation. This isn't victim-blaming — it's acknowledging a variable that nobody's engineering a solution for.

## Primary Sources

### 1. NHTSA FARS 2023 Data
- 7,215 pedestrian fatality crashes in 2023
- Alcohol involved in 46% of all fatal pedestrian crashes (either party BAC ≥ .01)
- 30% of fatal pedestrian crashes involved a pedestrian with BAC ≥ .08 (legally intoxicated) — 2,165 crashes
- 16% involved a driver with BAC ≥ .08 — 1,129 crashes
- Pedestrian intoxication rate nearly DOUBLE the driver intoxication rate
- Source: NHTSA FARS database, Premier Law Group analysis of 2023 data

### 2. NHTSA Countermeasures Report
- Pedestrian fatalities hit low of 4,109 in 2009
- Rose to 5-year average of 6,502 (2017-2021)
- Pedestrians account for 16-17% of total traffic fatalities
- Source: NHTSA Countermeasures That Work, 10th Ed

### 3. GHSA 2024 Preliminary Data
- Pedestrian traffic fatalities remain ~50% higher than a decade ago
- 2024 preliminary data report available
- Source: Governors Highway Safety Association

### 4. Age Demographics (FARS 2023)
- Ages 21-24: 40% of killed pedestrians had BAC ≥ .08 (highest rate)
- Ages 35-44: 37% intoxication rate (1,328 fatalities)
- Ages 25-34: 36% intoxication rate (1,199 deaths)
- Ages 45-54: 35% intoxication rate (1,104 fatalities)
- For adults 21-54: roughly 1 in 3 to 2 in 5 killed pedestrians were legally drunk
- Older adults: substantially lower rates

## Novel Cross-Tabulation
The article combines:
1. FARS pedestrian impairment data (30% pedestrians drunk, 16% drivers drunk)
2. The infrastructure/vehicle design narrative (stroads, hood height, AEB)
3. The spending gap: billions on vehicle safety tech, near-zero on pedestrian impairment intervention

## Original Contribution
- Calculation: if you could eliminate pedestrian intoxication from the equation, 2,165 fatal crashes per year would be affected — potentially saving more lives than AEB deployment will save in the next decade
- Cross-reference with existing VS articles on vehicle-centric pedestrian safety (hood-height, A-pillar, AEB) to show the data supports BOTH interventions simultaneously
- The "intervention gap": we have roadside alcohol checkpoints for drivers, but zero equivalent for pedestrians walking on high-speed roads at 2 AM

## Strongest Counterargument
- This could be read as victim-blaming. The pedestrian didn't choose to be hit. Vehicle design and road design created the lethality of the impact.
- Intoxicated pedestrians are MORE vulnerable, not more culpable — impairment makes them less able to avoid danger, not more deserving of death
- Infrastructure changes (better lighting, median barriers, sidewalks) would protect drunk and sober pedestrians alike
- State at full strength: pedestrians should be able to walk home drunk without dying, and the fact that they can't is a design failure

## Limitations
- FARS alcohol testing rates vary by state (9% to 81% BAC testing rate for surviving drivers in 2021 — down from 52% in 2012)
- Pedestrian BAC testing may be more complete (deceased are always tested) but still subject to jurisdiction
- "Alcohol involved" ≠ "alcohol caused" — a legally drunk pedestrian in a crosswalk with the right-of-way is still not at fault
- The 2,165 number is crashes where the pedestrian had BAC ≥ .08, not a direct causal attribution
