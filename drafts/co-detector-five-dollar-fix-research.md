# Research: CO Detector — The $5 Fix NHTSA Won't Mandate

## Angle
NHTSA has known since 1993 that a $5 carbon monoxide detector in vehicles could save hundreds of lives per year. They've denied every petition. On July 15, 2026, five people died in Toledo, Ohio — a grandmother and four children under 5 — because their car with a flat tire filled with CO and nobody knew. Every room in your house has a CO detector by law. Your car — the thing that actually produces CO — doesn't.

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: Toledo Incident (July 15, 2026)
- **What:** Grandmother Candace McAllister (36) and 4 children (Liberty 4, Storm 3, Legacy 4, Stone 2) died from CO poisoning while inside a car with a flat tire
- **Location:** 300 block of Phillips Ave, Toledo, Ohio
- **Details:** CO levels measured at 5x the lethal limit. Mother Adriana Coleman survived. Father arrived and found all five unconscious.
- **Source:** People magazine, WTVG/WTOL (local), Toledo Fire & Rescue

### Source 2: PEER Petition to NHTSA (March 2016, denied January 28, 2018)
- **What:** Public Employees for Environmental Responsibility petitioned NHTSA to require CO detectors in all new gas-powered vehicles + engine cut-off devices
- **Key claim:** "Most cost-effective auto safety measure since seat belt"
- **Data:** 1,500+ people die each year from CO poisoning related to motor vehicles (including suicides). Tens of thousands injured.
- **NHTSA response:** Denied with no explanation. PEER sued under FOIA.
- **Source:** PEER.org press releases, NBC News 4

### Source 3: Carnegie Mellon Research Institute Study (early 1990s, commissioned by NHTSA)
- **What:** NHTSA contracted Carnegie Mellon to evaluate metal oxide semiconductor gas sensor technology for CO monitoring in vehicle cabins
- **Findings:** Technology was "at the point where a stable selective CO monitor is within reach"
- **Recommendation:** NHTSA should undertake further research
- **Cited 500 accidental CO deaths per year**
- **Timeline:** This was ~1993. NHTSA did nothing.
- **Source:** Safety Research & Strategies (safetyresearch.net)

### Source 4: Senator Tina Smith Letter to NHTSA
- **What:** Sen. Smith pressed NHTSA on keyless ignition CO deaths
- **Data:** Since 2006, at least 28 deaths and 45 injuries from CO related to keyless ignition vehicles
- **Key quote:** NHTSA referred to keyless ignitions as a "clear safety problem" but has not acted
- **More than half of 17 million new vehicles sold annually use keyless ignition**
- **Source:** senate.gov

### Source 5: Consumer Reports / Toyota
- **What:** Safety Research & Strategies tracked 37 CO deaths linked to keyless ignitions; 17 were Toyota owners
- **Cost:** Engine cut-off capability costs ~$5 per vehicle; most vehicles already have the hardware
- **Timeline:** Ford and GM added auto-shutoff in 2012. Toyota took until 2018+ (13 years after first death).
- **NHTSA proposed a warning rule in 2011, backed off after automaker criticism**
- **Source:** Consumer Reports

### Source 6: CDC
- **What:** ~500 accidental CO deaths per year in the US
- **57% of unintentional CO deaths (1979-1988) were caused by motor vehicle exhaust
- **83% of those were associated with stationary vehicles**
- **Source:** CDC MMWR

## Novel Contribution / Original Analysis
**The 33-Year Gap:** NHTSA commissioned the Carnegie Mellon study in ~1993. It's now 2026. That's 33 years of knowing the technology was ready and not acting. At even a conservative estimate of 200 preventable accidental vehicle-CO deaths per year (not counting suicides), that's roughly 6,600 people. A $5 detector. In a vehicle that costs $48,000+ on average.

**The Home vs. Car Paradox:** Every state either requires or strongly incentivizes CO detectors in homes. The Consumer Product Safety Commission estimates home CO detectors have saved thousands of lives since becoming common in the 2000s. But the device that PRODUCES the CO — your car — has never been required to detect it. Your $30 smoke/CO alarm at Home Depot has a CO sensor. Your $50,000 SUV doesn't.

**The 750,000 Explorer Problem:** Ford Explorer SUVs were alleged in class-action litigation to funnel exhaust fumes containing deadly CO levels into the passenger cabin. Passengers in the rear — often children — were exposed to the highest levels. NHTSA issued CO-related recalls for ~100,000 vehicles between 2013-2015. Still no detector mandate.

## Actionable Takeaways
1. Buy a battery-powered portable CO detector for your car (they exist, ~$30-40 on Amazon)
2. Never idle a vehicle in an enclosed space — even with windows cracked
3. If you have a keyless ignition, confirm your car has auto-shutoff after extended idle
4. Check if your vehicle model has had CO-related recalls at nhtsa.gov/recalls
5. If you have a flat tire or breakdown, turn the engine OFF and exit the vehicle if safe
