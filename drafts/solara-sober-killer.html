Existential Dread

96% of Toyota Solara Fatal Crashes Involved a Sober Driver. It Still Kills at 5x the National Average.

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
April 7, 2026

Of the 195 drivers in fatal Toyota Solara crashes recorded in FARS toxicology data, only 8 tested positive for any impairment. That is 4.1%. Fleet average: 20.3%. No other vehicle in the database comes close to that sobriety rate. And the Solara has the 9th highest fatality rate of any vehicle in America.

4.1%
Impairment rate in fatal Solara crashes, vs. 20.3% fleet average

The Solara posts a fatality rate of 4.25 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled. Fleet average: 0.90. That puts this discontinued Toyota coupe in the company of the Chevy Cobalt (5.10), the Chevy Impala (5.00), and the Ford Mustang (6.02). Except every one of those vehicles has impairment rates between 21% and 26%. Their drivers are drunk, drugged, or both. Solara drivers are stone sober, and they are dying at nearly the same rate.

Same Platform. Double the Death Rate.

The Solara is a Camry. Mechanically, it shares the XV20 and XV30 platform, the same engine options, the same transmission choices. Toyota assembled both in Cambridge, Ontario. The Camry sedan posts a death rate of 2.03 with 19.2% impairment. The Solara posts 4.25 with 4.1%.[1]

Take that in. Same bones. Same powertrain. The coupe variant kills at 2.1 times the sedan's rate while its drivers are nearly five times more sober. Whatever is killing Solara occupants, it is not what they drank.

What the Data Points To

Three factors converge. First, the Solara was produced from 1998 to 2008. Every surviving unit predates mandatory ESC (2012), AEB, blind-spot monitoring, and modern crash structures. It lacks every active safety system that modern crossovers use to prevent the crash from happening in the first place.[2]

Second, a significant fraction of the Solara fleet is the convertible variant, introduced in 2000. Convertibles sacrifice the fixed roof that acts as a structural member in rollovers. IIHS rated the 2004-2008 Solara convertible, and while frontal crash performance was acceptable, the missing B-pillar and roofline rigidity are physics problems that no rating can wish away.[3]

Third, demographics. A mid-size Toyota coupe with automatic transmission and a reputation for reliability attracted older, conservative buyers. That demographic is statistically less likely to be impaired but more vulnerable in crashes due to fragility and slower emergency reaction times. The sobriety is real. The vulnerability is also real.

We Built a Sober-Killer Index

To quantify how badly a vehicle's death rate exceeds what its impairment profile predicts, we created a simple composite: (vehicle rate / fleet avg rate) × (fleet avg impairment / vehicle impairment). A score of 1.0 means the vehicle kills and impairs at exactly the fleet average. The Solara scores 23.4, the highest of any vehicle with 100+ fatal crash drivers and 100+ total deaths.

For context: the Land Cruiser (rate 6.27, impairment 8.9%) scores 15.9. The Chevy Tracker (7.83, 12.7%) scores 13.9. The Ford Mustang (6.02, 21.9%) scores 6.2, because its drivers are almost as impaired as the fleet average. The Solara is in a category by itself because its drivers cannot be blamed for their deaths.

Strongest Counterargument

The Solara's FARS sample is 195 drivers. That is large enough for statistical significance but small enough that a handful of multi-fatality crashes could skew the impairment percentage. If three of those eight impaired drivers were in high-casualty events, the impairment rate might underrepresent individual risky behavior. And the low-mileage problem is real: Solaras are old and driven infrequently, which inflates per-VMT death rates. A per-registration analysis (as we published yesterday) might soften the gap. But even a 50% correction leaves the Solara with a death rate of 2.1, matching the Camry while still carrying one-fifth its impairment load. The sobriety gap survives adjustment.

Limitations

FARS captures fatal crashes only. The Solara may perform differently in non-fatal injury crashes, which outnumber fatalities roughly 100:1. Toxicology data depends on testing rates, which vary by state and crash circumstances. Fleet size estimates carry ±15% uncertainty for discontinued models. We cannot isolate coupe versus convertible fatalities within FARS model codes, so the convertible's contribution remains estimated, not measured.

What You Can Do

If you own a Solara: check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls. Model years 2004-2008 were included in the 2009-2011 Toyota unintended acceleration recall covering sticky throttle pedals and floor mat entrapment.[4] Confirm the recall was completed. The vehicle lacks ESC, AEB, and every post-2012 active safety standard. Drive accordingly. And if you are shopping for a used mid-size coupe: the Solara's Toyota badge is not a safety guarantee. It is a 20-year-old car wearing a trusted name.
