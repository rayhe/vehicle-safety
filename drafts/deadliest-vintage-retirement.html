37,810: Traffic Deaths Drop 12% as America’s Deadliest Cars Finally Hit the Junkyard

Axle McScatter • Clautomotive Desk
April 23, 2026

I ran the numbers. Then I ran them again. They actually got better. The National Safety Council estimates 37,810 people died on American roads in 2025, down 12% from 42,789 the year before.[1] Mileage went up 0.9%. Deaths cratered. The mileage death rate fell to 1.14 per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, its lowest level in over a decade.

62,254
Deaths from model year 2002–2007 vehicles in FARS 2014–2023 data

Everyone wants credit. ADAS advocates point to automatic emergency braking. Enforcement agencies cite seatbelt compliance campaigns. Infrastructure people talk about road diets and rumble strips. All of those matter. But there is a quieter, more mechanical explanation hiding in the FARS model year data, and it involves a junkyard.

When you aggregate every fatal crash in the NHTSA Fatality Analysis Reporting System from 2014 through 2023 and sort by the model year of the vehicle involved, a mountain emerges. Model year 2005 sits at the peak: 11,363 deaths. Model year 2004 is right behind at 11,221. The six-year span from 2002 through 2007 accounts for 62,254 deaths across all tracked models, which is roughly one-third of the entire dataset from a window that covers vehicles built between 1980 and 2023.[2] No other six-year span comes close. Model years 2015 through 2020 produced 29,838 deaths over the same FARS window, which is less than half the carnage from the earlier vintage despite comparable or higher sales volumes.

Why? These were the last vehicles built before electronic stability control became mandatory in 2012. Most lacked side curtain airbags, pedestrian-detection AEB, or any form of lane departure warning. They were sold during the SUV and pickup boom of the early 2000s, when body-on-frame trucks outsold safety innovation by three-to-one, and they stayed on American roads for two decades because trucks and body-on-frame SUVs are notoriously durable. A 2004 Chevrolet Trailblazer has a design philosophy that predates the iPhone. It is also, per our FARS data, responsible for 663 of that model's 2,457 total deaths, concentrated in just two neighboring model years.

By 2025, a model year 2005 vehicle is twenty years old. The median scrappage age in the United States sits around sixteen years. The math is straightforward: the single deadliest vintage in American automotive history is aging past its expiration date, and every crushed chassis in a Pull-A-Part yard is one fewer pre-ESC, pre-AEB, pre-everything vehicle on the highway. NHTSA's own early estimate for the first half of 2025 showed 17,140 deaths, down 8.2% from the same period in 2024.[3] The trend accelerated in the second half.

None of this diminishes the contribution of AEB. The voluntary commitment by twenty automakers in 2016 put automatic emergency braking on the vast majority of new vehicles by 2022, and NHTSA's finalized FMVSS 127 mandate will require it on every new car and light truck by September 2029.[4] AEB reduces rear-end crashes by roughly 50%, per IIHS estimates.[5] But AEB only protects people inside new vehicles. Fleet turnover protects everyone, because it removes the vehicles most likely to be in a fatal crash from the road entirely.

The counterargument deserves its full weight: vehicle age is not the same as vehicle danger. Older vehicles are disproportionately driven by lower-income owners, who may drive more miles on worse roads with fewer safety nets. The model year death curve partially reflects socioeconomic exposure, not just engineering. A 2003 Cavalier does not kill because it is a 2003 Cavalier. It kills because it is a 2003 Cavalier being driven by someone who cannot afford a 2023 anything, on a two-lane county road with no median barrier, at 11 p.m. Disentangling vehicle design from driver circumstance is the entire problem with FARS analysis, and I will not pretend we have solved it here.

What the data does show unambiguously: the model year death curve is a mountain, and we are past the peak. Model years 2018 through 2023 contribute progressively fewer deaths per vintage, a decline that reflects both better engineering and less time on the road. As the 2002-2007 cohort continues to exit the fleet at roughly 5-8% per year, the structural ceiling on traffic deaths will keep dropping even if driver behavior does not improve at all.

What this means for you: If you are driving a vehicle built before 2012, check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls. If your vehicle lacks electronic stability control (standard on all vehicles from MY 2012+), understand that IIHS estimates ESC reduces fatal single-vehicle crash risk by 56%.[6] The safest upgrade you can make is not a dashcam or a tire pressure monitor. It is the next car.

Limitations: FARS data captures only fatal crashes. The 37,810 deaths are a fraction of the roughly 6.7 million annual crashes. Our model year aggregation uses counts from the 323 models tracked in our dataset, not the complete FARS universe. Scrappage rate estimates are based on industry-average age distributions, not actual registration counts by model year. The causal relationship between fleet turnover and the 2025 decline is correlational; confounders include economic conditions, fuel prices, and enforcement patterns. The 2025 NSC figure is preliminary and may be revised.
