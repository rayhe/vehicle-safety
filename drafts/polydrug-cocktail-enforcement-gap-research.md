# Research: Polydrug Cocktail Enforcement Gap

## Angle
America's breathalyzer can't detect the fastest-growing impairment type in fatal crashes. Polysubstance impairment (alcohol + drugs simultaneously) grew 38% in 4 years. 1 in 5 impaired drivers who die in crashes are on both alcohol AND drugs. The enforcement infrastructure is fighting yesterday's war.

## Kill Test
- Is this genuinely surprising? YES — most people think DUI = drunk driving. The polydrug angle is counterintuitive and underreported.
- Novel data contribution? YES — our FARS cross-tabulation showing polysubstance rates vary 8x by vehicle model is original analysis nobody else has published.
- 3+ primary sources? YES — see below.

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: Accident Analysis & Prevention (peer-reviewed, 2026)
"Polysubstance impairment detected in fatally injured drivers, United States, 2018–2022"
- DOI: 10.1016/j.aap.2026.108506
- n = 60,741 fatally injured drivers with tox testing within 1 hour of crash
- Key findings:
  - Polysubstance prevalence: 14.9% (2018) → 18.8% (2022), p < 0.0001
  - Cannabis most common non-alcohol drug (25.0%), then stimulants (19.2%), narcotics (8.3%)
  - Nighttime poly drivers 3x more likely than daytime (aOR: 2.94)
  - Odds of polysubstance increased 38% from 2018 to 2022 (aOR: 1.38)

### Source 2: FARS Toxicology Data (our database, 2014-2023)
Our cross-tabulation (original analysis):
- 490,736 drivers in fatal crash tox data
- 98,348 impaired (20.0% of all)
- Of those impaired: 56.8% alcohol-only, 24.6% drug-only, 18.6% BOTH
- 18,336 polysubstance drivers total
- By vehicle model (150+ drivers, 20+ impaired):
  - HIGHEST poly ratio: Pontiac Bonneville 40.5%, Acura TLX 31.5%, Mercedes GLK 30.8%
  - LOWEST: Mercury Sable 5.0%, Tesla Model S 6.1%, Fiat 500 7.1%
  - Range: 5% to 40.5% — an 8x difference by vehicle model
- By vehicle class: Sports Car 4.2%, Sedan 3.8%, Pickup 3.7%, SUV 3.6%, Van 3.4%

### Source 3: Gaize.ai / DRE Statistics
- ~10,000 Drug Recognition Experts (DREs) nationwide
- Washington DC: only 6 DREs
- DRE accuracy: up to 81% for cannabis
- Most small/mid-size cities have only a few DRE officers
- Few officers want to become DREs (challenging curriculum, poor career incentive)

### Source 4: NTSB
- NTSB has recommended lowering BAC limit to 0.05 (from 0.08)
- But this still only addresses alcohol — doesn't touch drug component
- Even if BAC limit lowered, breathalyzers still can't detect polydrug cocktails

### Source 5: ACS Clinical Congress 2025 (Wright State University)
- Montgomery County, OH: 246 deceased drivers tested, 41.9% THC-positive
- Average THC level: 30.7 ng/mL (legal limits 2-5 ng/mL)
- Legalization didn't change prevalence (42.1% before vs 45.2% after)

## Original Contribution
Cross-referencing FARS toxicology data to compute polysubstance rates per vehicle model. The 8x variation between vehicle models (5% to 40.5%) is a novel finding. The enforcement gap — breathalyzer-centric DUI enforcement vs. polydrug reality — has data support but hasn't been framed with per-vehicle data.

## Strongest Counterargument
FARS toxicology data measures presence of substances, not necessarily impairment. Someone who used cannabis a week ago might still test positive. The "polysubstance" label in FARS counts any drug + any alcohol, which could include subtherapeutic levels. The published study addressed this by using BAC ≥ 0.08 threshold, but our FARS data uses any alcohol-positive.

## Limitations
- FARS data (2014-2023) doesn't separate by year, so we can't show the growth trend ourselves
- Our polydrug calculation (alc + drug - any) assumes "any" is the union, which should be correct
- Small sample sizes on some vehicles (using 150+ driver threshold to mitigate)
- Toxicology testing rates vary by jurisdiction — not all deceased drivers are tested
- Per-vehicle model analysis reflects driver demographics as much as the vehicle itself
