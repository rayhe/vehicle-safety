# Research Notes: The Vintage Kill List — Model-Year Pareto Analysis

## News Hook
- NHTSA 2025 report: 36,640 deaths (6.7% decline, lowest since 2019)
- 15 consecutive quarterly declines
- Rate: 1.10/100M VMT (lowest since 2014)
- Feb 2026 preliminary: 1.01/100M VMT (accelerating)
- Sources: AASHTO Journal, NSC Injury Facts, NHTSA

## Original FARS Contribution
Model-year-level Pareto analysis: just 42 make-model-year combos (the top 1% of 4,144 total) account for 10.4% of all traffic deaths (19,524 of 187,058).

Key findings:
- **26 of the 42 are Silverados or F-150s** from 1997-2008
- Every single top-42 entry is from 1997-2015
- The 2001 F-150 alone: 672 deaths (the single deadliest vintage in America)
- The 2004 Silverado: 663 deaths (#2)
- Top 10 are all trucks, all pre-2010

Broader Pareto:
- 24 models (7.1% of 337) account for 50% of all deaths
- 79 models (23.4%) account for 80%
- 450 model-year combos (10.9% of 4,144) account for 50%

## The Policy Implication
If you could scrap just these 42 vintage trucks/sedans, you'd prevent ~10% of all road deaths. That's ~3,800 of 2025's 36,640 deaths. No law enforcement campaign, no recall, no speed camera comes close.

Cash for Clunkers (2009) retired ~677,000 vehicles but prioritized fuel economy, not safety. A safety-targeted scrappage program focusing on these 42 specific vintages would be orders of magnitude more effective per dollar.

## Counter-arguments (required)
1. **Survivorship bias:** These vehicles appear deadliest partly because millions are still on the road. Their per-mile rates may not be the worst — pure volume drives the count.
2. **Demographic confounds:** Early-2000s trucks are disproportionately owned by rural, lower-income, higher-risk demographics. The vehicle isn't the only variable.
3. **Fleet economics:** Many owners can't afford to scrap a running truck. A scrappage program without adequate replacement funding just removes transportation from people who need it.

## Limitations
- FARS captures fatal crashes only (subset of all crashes)
- Model year data shows deaths in FARS 2014-2023 window; a 2001 F-150 appearing = it was 13-22 years old during crashes
- VMT estimates use average driving assumptions, not actual odometer data
- Can't separate vehicle design from driver demographics from operating conditions
