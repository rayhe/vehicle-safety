# Research Notes: Cannabis Impaired Driving — America's Measurement Crisis

## Thesis
America counts 12,429 alcohol-impaired driving deaths per year with high confidence because BAC testing is standardized, enforceable, and scientifically correlated with crash risk. For cannabis — which now shows up in 42% of fatally injured drivers who are tested — the country literally cannot produce a reliable count because drug testing rates vary from <10% to >90% by state, THC blood concentration doesn't correlate with impairment, and there is no roadside equivalent of a breathalyzer. The nation is fighting its fastest-growing impairment crisis with a broken speedometer.

## Novel Contribution
Nobody has published the three-failure framework that makes drug-impaired driving data fundamentally unreliable:

1. **The Measurement Failure** — Only ~60% of fatally injured drivers are tested for drugs (state average per NHTSA Report to Congress, 2024). State variation: <10% to >90%. Compare: alcohol BAC testing is ~80%+ with imputation models for unknowns. For drugs, NHTSA doesn't even try to impute — the data is too inconsistent.

2. **The Science Failure** — NHTSA's own 2024 TOPIC report states: "drug concentration in blood does not correlate to driving impairment" for cannabis. Unlike alcohol's linear BAC-to-impairment curve, THC blood levels can be 30+ ng/mL in chronic users who aren't impaired, or <5 ng/mL in occasional users who are. The 5 per se states (IL, MT, NV, OH, WA) have THC limits that NHTSA itself says lack scientific support.

3. **The Enforcement Failure** — There is no roadside breathalyzer for THC. Oral fluid devices (SoToxa) detect presence but not impairment level. THC drops 80% in blood within 90 minutes of smoking, creating urgency that the system can't match. DRE (Drug Recognition Expert) evaluations take 45+ minutes and are subjective.

**Cross-calculation:** If 42% of the ~60% of tested drivers are THC-positive (ACS Clinical Congress 2025, Montgomery County OH study, N=246), and the 40% untested have similar or higher rates (since undertesting is worst in states with high cannabis prevalence)... the TRUE THC-positive rate in fatal crashes could be 45-55%. At 40,901 deaths in 2023 with ~28,000 drivers, that's potentially 12,600-15,400 THC-involved driver fatalities — a number comparable to alcohol's 12,429. But we literally cannot confirm or deny this because the data infrastructure doesn't exist.

**The alcohol comparison that crystallizes it:**
- Alcohol: 0.08 BAC = impaired (scientific consensus since 1990s)
- Alcohol: breathalyzer → result in 2 minutes → admissible in court
- Alcohol: 80%+ testing rate in fatal crashes → NHTSA imputes the rest
- Alcohol: 12,429 deaths in 2023 (high-confidence number)

- Cannabis: No concentration = impaired (NHTSA says "little evidence" for per se limits)  
- Cannabis: No roadside equivalent → blood draw → hours to process → THC drops 80% in 90 min
- Cannabis: ~60% testing rate in fatal crashes (10-90% by state) → no imputation
- Cannabis: ??? deaths in 2023 (literally unknown)

## Key Data Points

### THC Prevalence in Fatal Crashes
- **42% of fatally injured drivers tested positive for active THC** (delta-9-THC) — ACS Clinical Congress 2025, Montgomery County OH, N=246, 2019-2024
- Average THC level: **30.7 ng/mL** — 6-15x above state per se limits (2-5 ng/mL)
- Rate was consistent over 6 years and DID NOT change after Ohio's 2023 recreational legalization (42.1% pre vs 45.2% post, not statistically significant)
- Range by year: 25.7% to 48.9%

### Drug Testing Gap
- Average state drug testing completion for fatally injured drivers: **~60%** (NHTSA Report to Congress, 2024, citing 2019 FARS data)
- State range: **<10% to >90%** — massive variation
- Multiple federal agencies (GAO, NTSB) have called this data "unreliable"
- NHTSA added new drug testing fields to FARS in 2022/2023 to try to improve completeness
- North Dakota oral fluid vs blood comparison: 94% accuracy for cannabis

### Alcohol vs Drug Infrastructure
- Alcohol BAC testing: standardized since 1960s, imputation models since 1990s
- BAC 0.08 = impaired: scientific consensus, all 50 states + DC
- Drug testing: no standardization, no imputation, no reliable national count
- Only 5 states have per se THC limits (IL 5ng, MT 5ng, NV 2ng, OH 2ng, WA 5ng)
- NHTSA TOPIC report (2024): "relatively little research supports [per se THC limits'] relationship to crash risk"

### Legalization and Crash Rates
- IIHS: states that legalized recreational cannabis saw **6% increase in injury crash rates** and **4% increase in fatal crash rates** (CA, CO, NV, OR, WA vs control states)
- But: traffic death rates in legalization states actually fell 11.6% from 2016-2019 (vs 10.6% nationally) — Quartz Advisor study
- Contradictory findings are themselves evidence of measurement inadequacy

### Poly-Substance Use (Alcohol + Cannabis)
- **33% of simultaneous alcohol+cannabis users drove within 2 hours** — IIHS 2024 survey (N=3,000+, 6 states)
- 11% of drivers reported past-year simultaneous use of both substances
- Experimental studies show combined use worsens driving MORE than either substance alone
- IIHS hospital ER study: increased crash risk from marijuana ONLY when combined with alcohol

### Enforcement
- No THC breathalyzer equivalent exists
- SoToxa oral fluid device: detects presence, not impairment level. 94% accuracy for cannabis (ND study)
- IMMAD-VR (Colorado): 4-minute VR goggle test, measures impairment not substance — promising but early
- Drug Recognition Expert (DRE) evaluations: 45+ minutes, subjective, not scalable
- THC drops >80% in blood within 90 minutes of smoking — timing urgency vs testing delay

### NHTSA Spending Context
- ~$800M/year on impaired driving enforcement (primarily alcohol-focused)
- Alcohol deaths stuck at ~30% of total for 30 years (10,196 in 2019 → 13,617 in 2021 → 12,429 in 2023)
- Drug-impaired deaths: unknown number, inadequate data, fragmented enforcement

## Strongest Counterargument
"THC presence doesn't equal impairment — chronic users metabolize THC differently, and detection in postmortem toxicology doesn't prove the cannabis caused the crash." 

This is 100% correct and actually STRENGTHENS the article's thesis. The fact that THC presence ≠ impairment is exactly WHY per se limits don't work and why the data is unreliable. Unlike alcohol, where 0.08 BAC reliably predicts impairment, a driver with 30 ng/mL THC might be a chronic user who consumed hours ago or might have smoked 20 minutes before driving. The science can't distinguish. This means:
- The 42% positivity rate overstates impairment (some tested drivers weren't impaired by cannabis at time of crash)
- But the 60% testing rate understates prevalence (many cannabis-involved crashes aren't captured)
- The two biases push in opposite directions, and we have no way to net them out
- This is the core argument: America is trying to solve a problem it can't measure
