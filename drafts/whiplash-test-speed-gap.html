The Gap

Every Small SUV Aced the Old Whiplash Test. Then IIHS Added 10 MPH.

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
April 19, 2026

The IIHS killed its own whiplash test in 2022. Not because the problem was solved. Because the test was too easy. Virtually every vehicle on sale earned a "good" rating at the single test speed of 20 mph, and the institute quietly shelved the evaluation rather than continue rubber-stamping seats that weren't actually protecting people.[1]

4 of 18
Small SUVs earned "good" in the new whiplash test

Four years later, the replacement test arrived. Same concept, same sled, same dummy with an articulated spine. One difference: a second acceleration pulse simulating a 30 mph rear impact alongside the original 20 mph hit.[1] Ten additional miles per hour. That was enough to expose most of America's best-selling small SUVs as neck-injury machines.

Of the 18 small SUVs subjected to the new test, four earned "good" ratings: the Audi Q3, Hyundai Ioniq 5, Subaru Forester, and Toyota RAV4. Nine scraped by with "acceptable." Two landed at "marginal." And three failed outright: the Ford Bronco Sport, Hyundai Tucson, and Mazda CX-50.[1]

The failure modes were specific and damning. In the Tucson, the head restraint shoved the dummy's chin down toward its chest. The CX-50 let the head slide backward and upward, straightening the cervical vertebrae in a way that's visible on the test footage. The Bronco Sport was worst of all: IIHS called it "particularly poor support for the head and spine," with a long delay before the dummy's head even contacted the restraint and a dangerous velocity gap between the pelvis and skull.[2]

Neck sprains and strains are the single most commonly reported injury in U.S. auto insurance claims, per IIHS President David Harkey.[1] Rear-end crashes account for roughly 30% of all collisions. Not the spectacular multi-fatality pileups. The 25 mph fender-benders at stoplights that put you in a cervical collar for six weeks.

FARS data adds context. The Tucson has accumulated 669 occupant deaths across 1,472 fatal crashes in the 2014-2023 period, giving it a lethality ratio of 0.454. The RAV4, which passed the new whiplash test, recorded 914 deaths across 1,834 fatal crashes for a lethality ratio of 0.498. These numbers track total crash lethality, not rear-end-specific outcomes, but they illustrate how two vehicles in the same showroom segment can diverge in occupant protection.[3]

The Mazda CX-5, the predecessor platform to the failed CX-50, actually has one of the lowest lethality ratios in the compact SUV segment at 0.432. Good structural engineering that still can't protect your neck at 30 mph. Front crash protection and rear-impact whiplash prevention are separate engineering problems, and automakers have been solving one while largely ignoring the other.

Marcy Edwards, the IIHS senior research engineer who built the new evaluation, was blunt: "We don't yet fully understand the mechanisms that cause whiplash injuries. But our research makes us confident that reducing these types of movements and stresses should cut down on whiplash injuries in the real world."[1]

Translation: seat design in 2026 is still operating on incomplete science, and the old test let automakers optimize for a benchmark that didn't capture real-world injury patterns.

What You Can Do

If you're shopping for a small SUV and rear-end protection matters to you, four models earned "good" in the new IIHS whiplash test: the Audi Q3, Hyundai Ioniq 5, Subaru Forester, and Toyota RAV4. The Bronco Sport, Tucson, and CX-50 failed. Check the full ratings at iihs.org/ratings before signing anything. And adjust your head restraint: the top should reach at least the top of your ears, positioned as close to the back of your head as possible. Most people never touch it after buying the car. Most people also assume 20 mph and 30 mph are basically the same crash. They are not.
