### 🔬 Research Rigor — 8.5 ✅ (was 7.5 — IMPROVED)

This was the failing dimension. The two blocking issues from round 0 were:

**Issue #1 (was Critical): The article's central thesis had a factual gap — NHTSA 2025 data showing 1.10 rate was already below baseline.**

**Resolution:** The article now includes a dedicated paragraph (paragraph 5) that directly addresses this:

> "At the annual level, the crossing had already happened. NHTSA's preliminary full-year 2025 estimate put the rate at 1.10 per 100 million VMT across an estimated 36,640 deaths, technically below the pre-COVID floor for the first time on a yearly basis. But annual averages compress summer spikes and winter dips into a single number. The NSC's month-by-month estimates reveal the texture inside that average, and March 2026's 1.07 is the lowest individual monthly reading in the post-pandemic series."

This is well executed. It:
- Acknowledges the 2025 data plainly ("the crossing had already happened") — no burying or minimizing ✅
- Cites the specific numbers (1.10 rate, 36,640 deaths) with proper ref-2 attribution ✅
- Explains *why the monthly data still matters* ("annual averages compress summer spikes and winter dips") — this is the key analytical move that reframes the thesis from "first time ever" to "monthly texture inside the annual average" ✅
- Properly repositions March 2026 as "the lowest individual monthly reading in the post-pandemic series" rather than the unqualified "first time" ✅

The lede was also revised to scope the claim: "In the National Safety Council's monthly tracking, a single month's estimated fatality rate dipped below..." This properly attributes the finding to the NSC monthly data product specifically, which is defensible — the NSC's March 2025 monthly estimate was 1.15, above the 1.13 baseline, so March 2026 at 1.07 *is* the first NSC monthly reading below the floor. ✅

**Remaining gap:** The article doesn't mention NHTSA's Q1 2025 rate of 1.05 or H1 2025 rate of 1.06, which were even lower than March 2026's 1.07. A thorough reader familiar with NHTSA's quarterly estimates might wonder why the article focuses on NSC monthly data when NHTSA's own quarterly data for Q1 2025 was already lower. The article implicitly answers this (NSC monthly tracking is a different data product with different methodology) but doesn't address the quarterly estimates explicitly. This is a minor gap, not a blocking one — the article chose NSC monthly data as its frame and is consistent within that frame.

**Issue #2 (was Critical): Pre-COVID baseline derivation not shown.**

**Resolution:** The baseline is now shown inline:

> "subtract the pre-COVID average of 1.13 (the mean of six annual FARS rates, 2014 through 2019: 1.08, 1.13, 1.17, 1.16, 1.13, 1.10)"

This is placed in the excess death calculation section, where methodological transparency matters most. The six annual rates are listed, the method is stated ("mean"), and the reader can verify the arithmetic (sum = 6.77, ÷ 6 = 1.128, ≈ 1.13). ✅

**Strictly speaking, 1.128 rounds to 1.13 to two decimal places, but this is a coin-flip rounding (1.1283…).** The margin between 1.07 and 1.13 is only 5.3%, so a baseline of 1.13 vs. 1.12 could matter. The article uses "approximately 1.13," which is correct and honest. No deduction.

**Issue #3 from R0 (non-blocking): Fleet mechanism hypothesis lacks state-level fleet-age data.**

**Status:** Not addressed in this round. Still a valid weakness but remains properly hedged ("even if the data can't prove it yet"). Not blocking at 8.5.

**Updated "COVID debt" language:**

> "and with 2025's full-year rate landing at 1.10, the 'COVID debt' appears finally paid in full."

This is a stronger claim than R0's "may finally be paid" — but it's now supported by the 2025 data (1.10 < 1.13). The excess calculation covers 2020–2024; the 2025 rate of 1.10 confirms the rate is below baseline, so the "debt" framing is coherent. ✅

**Score justification:** The two blocking issues from R0 are both resolved. The article now acknowledges the 2025 annual data, properly scopes the "first time" claim to NSC monthly estimates, shows the baseline derivation, and integrates the 2025 context into the narrative arc. The remaining gap (NHTSA Q1 2025 quarterly data not mentioned) is minor — it doesn't undermine any claim the article makes, and the article's chosen frame (NSC monthly data) is internally consistent. 7.5 → 8.5.
