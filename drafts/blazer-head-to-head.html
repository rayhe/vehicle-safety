IIHS Crashed a 1996 Blazer Into a 2026 Blazer. The Dummy Lost Its Head.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
June 25, 2026

IIHS celebrated its 30th anniversary this week by ramming a 1996 Chevrolet Blazer into a 2026 Blazer at 40 mph in a moderate overlap front crash test.[1] One driver walks away sore. One gets decapitated.

48,352
Lives saved by IIHS crash test improvements, 1999–2024

Literally. Dashboard slammed into the dummy's lap while the airbag caught its chin and snapped the neck's nodding joint clean through. IIHS noted that a human driver wouldn't lose their head in quite the same way, but the underlying message was unambiguous: the 1996 Blazer would have killed you. Thirty-three centimeters of footwell intrusion, twenty centimeters of instrument panel shoved backward, and the steering column rammed upward into the driver's chest. A Poor rating from IIHS, and they meant it.

We ran the FARS numbers, and they confirm what the crash lab demonstrated in fiberglass and steel.

GM's old Blazer platform and its siblings accumulated 3,698 fatalities across a decade of FARS data: 369 under the Blazer name, 2,473 as the Trailblazer that replaced it, and another 856 under the Tracker badge that shared the same lightweight-SUV-as-coffin philosophy. At 7.83 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, the Tracker ranks among the 10 deadliest vehicles in the entire FARS dataset. Peak carnage hit in 2003, when the Trailblazer alone killed 430 people in a single model year.

The 2019-and-newer Blazer carries a rate of 0.56. That is a 14-fold reduction from the Trailblazer it spiritually replaced, and it reflects everything IIHS quantified in its anniversary study: improvements across moderate overlap, small overlap, side impact, and roof strength tests that together saved 48,352 lives and generated $538 billion in societal value on just $600 million in IIHS funding. A 900-to-1 return on investment.[1]

A counterargument matters here. Old Blazers still on the road skew toward rural use, lower-income owners, and deferred maintenance. Survivorship bias inflates their fatality rate beyond what structural deficiencies alone would produce. IIHS tests isolate engineering; FARS captures everything, including driver demographics and road conditions. Fourteen times deadlier is real, but not all of it is the crumple zone.

Still: the dummy's head came off, and that part wasn't demographics.

What to do: If you're driving any 1995–2004 Blazer, Trailblazer, Jimmy, Envoy, or Bravada, understand that your vehicle earned a Poor frontal crash rating with 33 cm of cabin crush. Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls for outstanding recalls. If you're shopping for a midsize SUV, the current Blazer earns Good ratings across all IIHS evaluations, as do the Hyundai Tucson and Kia Sportage, but run your candidate through iihs.org/ratings before you sign anything.
