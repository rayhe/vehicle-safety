# Research Notes: 100 Deadliest Days — Teen Fatalities Diverge

## Core Story
Summer 2024 saw 10,438 road deaths across June-August, averaging 113/day — an 8.1% improvement over two years. But fatal crashes involving teen drivers (ages 15-20) went the opposite direction: 542 → 635 fatalities, a 17.2% increase. Teens are now 6.1% of summer deaths, up from 4.8%. This is the ONLY age group that worsened in both years of the study period.

## Primary Sources

### 1. MoneyGeek "100 Deadliest Driving Days" Analysis (2026)
- Analyzed FARS data for June-August 2022, 2023, 2024 across all 50 states + DC
- Normalized by FHWA Traffic Volume Trends monthly VMT data
- **Key finding:** 17.2% increase in teen driver summer fatalities while overall dropped 8.1%
- Teen summer fatalities: 542 (2022) → 635 (2024)
- Texas led all states: 68 teen summer fatalities
- Arizona nearly doubled: 12 → 23
- Teen share of summer deaths: 4.8% → 6.1%
- Source: moneygeek.com/living/driving/100-deadliest-days-summer-traffic/
- Underlying data: FARS 2022 Final, 2023 Final, 2024 Annual Report File

### 2. NHTSA Full-Year Data (for context)
- 2024 FARS: 39,254 total deaths, rate 1.19/100M VMT
- 2025 preliminary: 36,640 deaths, rate 1.10/100M VMT, 6.7% decline
- 15 consecutive quarterly declines starting Q2 2022
- Source: NHTSA DOT HS 813 800, April 1, 2026

### 3. Johns Hopkins / AAA Foundation Research
- Teens 16-19: fatal crash rate nearly 3x higher than drivers 20+ per mile
- Summer teen fatal crashes increase ~30% between Memorial Day and Labor Day
- Risk factors: nighttime driving, multiple passengers, inexperience, seatbelt non-use
- Source: Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health

### 4. State-Level Summer Rates (2024)
- Mississippi: 1.88 deaths/100M VMT (worst, +11.9% over 2 years)
- Massachusetts: 0.67 (best)
- Spread: 2.8:1 ratio
- 10 states worsened, 41 improved
- Only 22 states improved in BOTH consecutive years
- Georgia biggest single-state drop: -25.2% (1.31 → 0.98)

### 5. FARS Vehicle Data (Cross-Reference)
- Teens typically drive older, cheaper vehicles (8-15 years old)
- Top-death-rate sedans in FARS are exactly the cars teens inherit:
  - Honda Civic: 6,553 deaths, rate 2.25 (2006-2008 model years: 320-402 deaths each)
  - Honda Accord: 7,102 deaths, rate 3.07
  - Toyota Camry: 6,328 deaths, rate 2.03
  - Nissan Altima: 4,787 deaths, rate 2.88
  - Hyundai Elantra: 2,407 deaths, rate 1.50
- Pre-2015 model years of these vehicles mostly lack AEB, FCW
- 2008 Honda Civic (peak deaths: 402) would now be 16 years old — prime teen hand-me-down age

## Novel Contribution
The cross-tabulation of the teen summer fatality divergence with vehicle-age fleet composition. Nobody has connected:
1. Teens are the only age group getting MORE dangerous in summer
2. The vehicles teens drive (cheap, old sedans) are exactly the ones with highest death rates in FARS
3. The safety technology in new cars (AEB, FCW) is irrelevant to teens because they don't drive new cars
4. The fleet-age gap IS the teen death gap

## Strongest Counterargument
The 17.2% increase could be partially driven by more teens driving post-pandemic (driver's license backlogs, more summer activities). If teen VMT rose faster than the fatality count, the per-mile risk may not have worsened as dramatically. MoneyGeek's data doesn't normalize teen deaths by teen-specific VMT.

## Limitations
- FARS data uses 15-20 age bracket, which includes some non-teen adults (19-20)
- MoneyGeek uses June-August, not exactly Memorial Day-Labor Day
- No vehicle-specific breakdown of teen fatal crashes in this dataset (we infer from fleet age, not direct match)
- Small state counts (DC: 18, South Dakota: 50) show volatile percentages

## Actionable Takeaways
- Parents buying a teen's first car: prioritize AEB and FCW over price, look at IIHS recommended vehicles for teens list
- Check your teen's vehicle VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls
- If your teen drives a pre-2015 sedan, it likely lacks AEB — consider upgrading
- Teen summer driving curfews and passenger limits (GDL laws) reduce nighttime risk
