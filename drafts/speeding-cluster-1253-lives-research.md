# Research: Speeding Deaths Down 1,253 in 2025 — The Behavioral Cluster

## Angle
NHTSA released fresh data (July 2026): 2025 speeding-related fatalities estimated at 10,035, down 11% from 2024's 11,288. That's 1,253 fewer deaths — 3.4 lives per day — from a single cause category. Speed limits didn't change. The cars didn't change. Fleet turnover is ~12 years. So what did?

## Original Contribution
Cross-referencing NHTSA's newly released speeding-behavior correlation data with FARS impairment rates by vehicle class reveals that "speeding" is not a standalone behavior — it's the visible tip of a behavioral cluster. NHTSA's own 2024 data shows:
- 50% of speeding drivers in fatal crashes were **unbuckled** (vs 21% non-speeding)
- 37% of speeding drivers had BAC ≥ 0.08 (vs 17% non-speeding)
- 15-20 male drivers: 39% speeding, highest of any age group

When speeding declines, it likely drags drunk driving and unbuckling down with it. The 1,253 lives saved may not be a speed story at all — it's a behavioral-cluster story.

## FARS Cross-Reference
Vehicle classes with highest FARS impairment rates (anyPct):
- Sports Car: 9.5% drug, 17.1% alcohol → most impaired class
- Sedan: 8.9% drug, 15.4% alcohol
- Pickup: 8.6% drug, 15.2% alcohol
- SUV: 8.4% drug, 14.7% alcohol
- Van: 8.1% drug, 13.4% alcohol → least impaired class

Sports cars have the highest impairment AND the highest average death rate (1.9 per 100M VMT). This aligns with the "everything-wrong driver" hypothesis — the same person who speeds also drinks, also doesn't buckle up.

## Data Points for Article
- 1,253 fewer speeding deaths (2025 vs 2024)
- 3.4 lives saved per day from speeding decline alone
- 50% of speeding fatal crash drivers unbuckled
- 37% of speeding fatal crash drivers drunk (BAC ≥ 0.08)
- Speeding is 29% of all traffic fatalities (2024)
- Total 2024 traffic fatalities: ~38,924 (11,288 / 0.29)
- Q1 2026 rate: 0.99/100M VMT (second-lowest quarterly ever)

## Limitations
- FARS impairment data is 2014-2023; 2025 impairment breakdown not yet available
- Correlation between speeding and impairment doesn't prove causal linkage in decline
- Speed limit changes can happen at local level without federal tracking
- Remote work persistence is a hypothesis, not measured
- The "behavioral cluster" theory is our inference from NHTSA's correlation data, not NHTSA's stated conclusion

## Strongest Counterargument
The 11% decline could be statistical regression to the mean after pandemic-era highs (2020-2022 saw dramatic increases). The 10,035 figure merely returns to 2019 levels. This may not reflect genuine behavioral change — just the fever breaking.
