IIHS Finally Rated Commercial Vehicles. The Body Count Explains the Delay.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
June 16, 2026

On June 9, the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety released its first-ever safety evaluations of commercial vehicles.[1] Cargo vans. Class 3 pickups. The vehicles that deliver your packages, haul construction materials, and account for 16% of all U.S. roadway fatalities. The test? Whether they come with airbags and a seat belt reminder that lasts longer than eight seconds.

6,535
People killed in 2023 in crashes involving heavy/medium trucks and light vans

That is not a typo. In 2026, the nation's most prominent vehicle safety organization is checking whether a Ford Transit has a functioning seat belt chime. Federal standards for commercial vehicles only require the audible warning to last 4 to 8 seconds. Four. Your microwave beeps longer when your burrito is done.

Five of the nine vehicles IIHS evaluated lack effective seat belt reminders: the Ford Transit T250, Mercedes Sprinter 2500, Rivian Delivery 500, Chevy Express 2500, and the Ram 3500. The Express goes further, also skipping standard force limiters on the driver's belt.[1] That means the belt doesn't adjust its tension during a crash. A technology that has been standard in $22,000 Corollas for over a decade.

Congratulations to the Chevy BrightDrop 400 and Ram ProMaster 2500 for clearing this extremely low bar.

The real problem isn't who's inside the van

IIHS describes its initial focus as "driver protection." That framing deserves scrutiny. Because FARS data from 2014 to 2023 tells a very specific story about who actually dies when commercial vehicles crash: it is overwhelmingly not the driver.

We calculated what we're calling the "other-kill rate" for every vehicle in the FARS dataset: the percentage of a vehicle's fatal crash involvements where its own occupant survives. When a sedan is in a fatal crash, the sedan's occupant dies 64.5% of the time. When a commercial van or heavy pickup is in a fatal crash, the calculus inverts.[2]

79.5%
Fatal crash involvements where the Ram 2500 occupant survived and someone else died

The Ford Transit's other-kill rate is 69.2%. In 577 fatal crash involvements over a decade, 178 Transit occupants died. The remaining 399 crashes killed other people: occupants of smaller vehicles, pedestrians, cyclists. The Transit Connect sits at 62.3%. The Ford E-350, the fleet workhorse of FedEx and ambulance services, at 59.0%.[2]

For heavy pickups, the asymmetry is even starker. The Ram 2500 was involved in 748 fatal crashes from 2014 through 2023. Just 153 of those killed a Ram occupant. Run that ratio and you get what we'll call the asymmetric lethality index: 4.9 fatal crash involvements for every occupant death. In plain language, for every Ram 2500 driver who dies, the truck participates in roughly four other fatal crashes where someone in a smaller vehicle, on a bicycle, or on foot does not survive. The Ford F-250 comes in at 2.7. Even the Chevy Express, a relatively modest cargo van, sits at 2.1.

Compare that to a Chevy Cavalier at 1.17, meaning nearly every fatal crash involving a Cavalier kills its own driver. The Cavalier occupant absorbs the violence. The Ram 2500 occupant exports it.

A regulatory blind spot measured in bodies

IIHS president David Harkey acknowledged as much in the announcement. "These vehicles have been an afterthought when it comes to regulations and safety information," he said. In the same release, IIHS noted that AEB and headlight evaluations for road user protection are planned but haven't started yet.[1]

The sequencing matters. Driver protection came first. External lethality waits. NHTSA's forthcoming AEB mandate (FMVSS No. 127, effective September 2029) applies to light vehicles under 10,000 pounds GVWR.[3] Class 3 pickups weigh 10,001 to 14,000 pounds. They are literally one pound over the line. The vehicles with the highest asymmetric lethality in the FARS dataset sit in a regulatory gap specifically sized to exclude them.

To be fair, commercial vehicles serve essential economic functions, and equipping them with advanced safety systems adds costs that ripple through supply chains. Fleet managers optimize for uptime, payload, and total cost of ownership. The IIHS is starting where the industry can follow: basic airbags, belt technology, reminders. Build the program. Toughen it later. That is the pragmatic argument, and it has merit.

But "pragmatic" is a strange word to use when 6,535 people died last year and the first evaluation checks whether a delivery van beeps long enough to annoy its driver into buckling up. The Ram 2500's 4.9:1 asymmetric lethality ratio means the driver protection checklist is optimizing for the one person in these crashes who already has the best odds of walking away.

What you can actually do

If you manage a commercial fleet: check whether your vehicles made the IIHS driver protection list. The Chevy BrightDrop 400, Ram ProMaster 2500, Chevy Silverado 3500HD, and Ford F350 SuperCrew passed all five criteria. If your fleet runs Ford Transits or Sprinters, the belt reminder systems do not meet IIHS thresholds. Push your procurement team to demand AEB and automatic braking as standard when ordering next year's vehicles. Don't wait for IIHS to test it.

If you share the road with these vehicles: distance is your only defense. The physics have not changed. A 14,000-pound Class 3 pickup meeting a 3,200-pound sedan at combined closing speeds of 60 mph produces a mass ratio of 4.4:1. That ratio shows up in the FARS data as a survival rate that runs in one direction. The seat belt reminder on the truck is not going to help you.
