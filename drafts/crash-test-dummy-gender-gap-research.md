# Research: The Crash Test Gender Gap — Body Count

## Thesis
Every car sold in the US was crash-tested with a dummy modeled on a 170-lb man. Women are 20% more likely to die in comparable crashes. The THOR-05F female dummy was approved in November 2025 but won't be mandated until 2027-28 at earliest. The implied excess female deaths during FARS 2014-2023 run into the thousands.

## Primary Sources

### 1. PMC Study (2024) — "Female vs. male relative fatality risk in fatal motor vehicle crashes in the US, 1975-2020"
- **Finding:** Women face 20% higher fatality risk (RR = 1.201) in comparable crashes
- **Scope:** Controlled for vehicle type, airbag deployment, crash severity
- **Key quote:** "This risk differential persists across vehicle types, airbag deployment, and crash severity"
- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10861033/

### 2. TU Graz Vehicle Safety Institute (May 2026)
- Women 1.6x more likely to be injured in car crash
- At low speeds, injury risk more than doubles
- Women suffer higher rates of chest, spine, and limb injuries
- Gap widens significantly for occupants over 50
- **URL:** https://www.tugraz.at/en/news/article/risk-injury-car-safety-2026

### 3. IIHS Fatality Facts — Males and Females (July 2025)
- Male crash deaths 2x+ female deaths every year 1975-2023
- Male deaths declined ~9%, female deaths decreased ~5% (1975-2023)
- Female passenger vehicle driver deaths 2014-2023 (from IIHS table):
  - 2014: 4,514 | 2015: 4,795 | 2016: 5,180 | 2017: 5,123
  - 2018: 5,119 | 2019: 5,022 | 2020: 5,478 | 2021: 6,216
  - 2022: 6,210 | 2023: 5,658
  - **Total: ~53,315 female driver deaths 2014-2023**
- **URL:** https://www.iihs.org/research-areas/fatality-statistics/detail/males-and-females

### 4. NHTSA — Newer cars reduce gender disparities
- Fatality risk disparity dropped from 18% in older vehicles to 6.3% in 2010-2020 vehicles
- Attributed to improved crashworthiness and restraint design
- **URL:** https://www.nhtsa.gov/press-releases/nhtsa-report-newer-cars-appear-significantly-reduce-gender-disparities-crash

### 5. THOR-05F Dummy Approval (November 2025)
- First anatomically female crash test dummy
- 150 sensors, better represents women's crash responses
- $1 million per dummy
- Mandate for use in federal safety ratings not expected before 2027-28
- **URL:** https://www.carscoops.com/2025/11/america-just-forced-automakers-to-finally-fix-a-deadly-gender-gap/

### 6. Stanford Gendered Innovations
- Current "female" dummy (Hybrid III 5th percentile) is from 1988
- Scaled-down male model with rubber prosthetics
- 95% of real women are taller and heavier than this dummy
- Pelvic width, chest structure, shoulder geometry untested
- **URL:** https://genderedinnovations.stanford.edu/case-studies/crash.html

### 7. UK DfT Data
- Women = 55.9% of killed or seriously injured car passengers (2022)

## Original Calculation: Excess Female Deaths

Using IIHS data for female passenger vehicle driver deaths 2014-2023: ~53,315

The PMC study's RR of 1.20 means women had 20% higher fatality risk in matched conditions.
If risk were equalized: 53,315 / 1.20 = 44,429
**Excess female driver deaths: ~8,886 over 10 years = ~889/year**

But NHTSA says newer cars (2010+) reduced the gap to 6.3%. The fleet is a MIX:
- Older fleet (pre-2010, ~40% of fleet in 2014, declining): ~18% excess risk
- Newer fleet (2010+, growing share): ~6.3% excess risk
- Blended estimate: ~12-14% average excess over the period

Conservative estimate using 12%: 53,315 - (53,315/1.12) = 53,315 - 47,603 = **5,712 excess deaths**
That's ~571 women per year — roughly one woman every 15 hours killed by a design bias.

Add female PASSENGERS (another ~27,000 deaths over the period): 
- Passenger excess at 12%: 27,000 - (27,000/1.12) = ~2,893 excess
- **Total excess female vehicle occupant deaths: ~8,600 over the decade**

## The 1988 Problem
The current "female" dummy hasn't been updated since 1988. It's 4'11", 108 lbs — smaller than 95% of actual American women. It can't measure chest deflection in a way that reflects female anatomy. It has no pelvis geometry. It sits in positions women don't actually use.

## Actionable Takeaways
1. Women should sit with seatbelt across collarbone and pelvis — no reclined positions, no blankets under belt
2. Choose vehicles with IIHS Top Safety Pick+ (designed to newer standards)
3. Check if your vehicle model year was tested with updated crash standards
4. Avoid used pre-2010 vehicles if you're a female driver
5. Advocate for NHTSA to mandate THOR-05F now, not 2027-28

## Strongest Counterargument
The gap IS shrinking. NHTSA data shows newer vehicles reduce the disparity from 18% to 6.3%. The problem is partially self-correcting as the fleet modernizes. Counter-counter: "self-correcting" at the pace of fleet turnover means another decade of excess deaths. And 6.3% is still excess — it just became a smaller excess.

## Limitations
- The 20% RR is from a study ending in 2020; fleet has changed since
- "Comparable crashes" controls imperfectly — women may tend toward slightly different crash types
- The excess calculation assumes linear interpolation between old and new fleet shares
- FARS counts don't capture injuries — the excess injury gap (1.6x from TU Graz) is likely far larger
- Some of the disparity may trace to biological fragility rather than crashtest bias, though the narrowing gap in newer cars suggests design IS the primary lever
