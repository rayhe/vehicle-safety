# Research Notes: TSP+ vs FARS Death Rates — 2026

## News Hook
IIHS released 2026 Top Safety Pick+ results (July 2026). 49+ vehicles awarded TSP+, 5 vehicles tested but FAILED to earn any award: Audi A3, Cadillac CT5 (no FARS), Lexus IS, Nissan Kicks, Toyota Tacoma Crew Cab.

## Original Finding
Cross-tabulating the 2026 TSP+ winner list against fleet-wide FARS death rates (2014–2023) reveals an inversion: **29% of TSP+ winners (5 of 17 with FARS data) have above-average death rates, while 100% of TSP failures (4 of 4 with FARS data) have below-average rates.**

## Key Insight
IIHS tests TODAY'S design. FARS measures the fleet over its full lifespan. A Camry that passes every 2026 lab test still shares a nameplate — and sales pipeline — with 20 years of older Camrys on the road. The award goes to the engineering. The body count goes to the fleet.

## Strongest Counterargument
Fleet age confound: the Camry has been sold since 1982. Its 6,328 FARS deaths include ancient models that would fail every modern test. The Kona debuted in 2018 — its fleet is inherently newer and safer. Comparing fleet-wide rates to current-year lab results is apples to oranges.

## Rebuttal
Yes — and that's the point. When a consumer sees "Top Safety Pick+," they don't think "this badge only applies to the 2026 model year." They think "safe car." But the road is a time machine: the average vehicle in a fatal crash was built in 2007. The award system creates a false present tense for safety.

## Limitations
- FARS rate uses estimated VMT, introducing ±15% uncertainty for lower-volume models
- Fleet composition (model years on road) differs radically by nameplate age
- TSP+ tests apply to current model year only; FARS captures all model years
- Some TSP+ winners (Rivian R1S, Volvo EX90, Tesla Cybertruck, Kia K4) have no FARS data yet (too new)
