# Research: The Deadliest Speed Limit in America Is 45 MPH

## Thesis
45 mph is the most common posted speed limit where drivers strike and kill pedestrians and cyclists in America. It's not a coincidence — it's a design failure. These "stroads" (street-road hybrids) combine highway-speed traffic with pedestrian-generating land uses like strip malls, bus stops, and residential driveways. The result: 60% of all pedestrian fatalities happen on arterial roads, and the single deadliest speed limit is the one posted on nearly every suburban boulevard in the country.

## Novel Contribution
Cross-tabulation of three independent datasets:
1. NHTSA FARS striking-vehicle speed limit data → 45 mph is the #1 speed for pedestrian/cyclist deaths in urban areas
2. IIHS pedestrian injury risk curves (2024) → at 45 mph, fatality risk exceeds 50%; at 25 mph, it's ~1%
3. Smart Growth America / Strong Towns arterial road analysis → 60% of pedestrian fatalities on stroads, 54% on state-owned roads

Nobody has combined these three to build the lethality multiplier: a pedestrian hit at 45 mph is 50× more likely to die than one hit at 20 mph.

## Key Data Points

### NHTSA / League of American Bicyclists (2022 FARS)
- 45 mph is the **most common posted speed limit** where a driver strikes and kills a pedestrian or cyclist
- Urban areas: 45 mph is the deadliest speed limit for both pedestrians AND cyclists
- Rural areas: 55 mph is #1 (interstates), but 45 mph is still top-3
- 65% of pedestrian deaths with reported speed limits were on roads posted 40+ mph
- 62% of cyclist deaths with reported speed limits were on roads posted 40+ mph

### IIHS Pedestrian Injury Risk Curves (Monfort et al., 2024)
- At 20 mph: 1% fatality risk, 46% moderate injury risk
- At 35 mph: 19% fatality risk, 86% moderate injury, 67% serious injury
- At 50 mph: 80%+ fatality risk
- USDOT: 42 mph = 50% chance of death for pedestrians
- Vehicle height COMPOUNDS the effect: median pickup at 35 mph → 91% serious injury vs 52% for a median car
- U.S. pedestrians are injured at lower speeds than German pedestrians — because U.S. vehicles are taller

### Smart Growth America — Dangerous by Design 2024
- Pedestrian deaths increased 75% since 2010
- 54% of all pedestrian deaths occur on state-owned roads (states own only 20% of roads)
- Arterial roads (stroads) account for approximately 60% of all pedestrian fatalities
- Black and American Indian/Alaska Native people killed at disproportionately higher rates
- Only 8 states saw any improvement in pedestrian death rates from 2013-2022

### Strong Towns / Tennessee Study
- Tennessee's 117% increase in pedestrian deaths (2009-2019) correlated with crashes on "straight, multi-lane roads with speed limits over 35 mph in urban and suburban areas"
- These are textbook stroads

### FARS General (2022)
- 42,721 total motor vehicle fatalities
- 7,593 pedestrian deaths (18%)
- 1,096 cyclist deaths (3%)
- Pedestrians + cyclists = 8,689 deaths = 20% of all traffic fatalities
- Pedestrian deaths: 7,516 (1975) → 7,593 (2022) — virtually unchanged in absolute terms despite massive vehicle safety improvements

### USDOT Speed-Fatality Threshold
- 32 mph = 25% risk of pedestrian death
- 42 mph = 50% risk of pedestrian death
- The jump from 35 to 45 mph crosses the lethality threshold

## The "Stroad" Argument

A stroad is a road that tries to be both a street (for people) and a road (for cars). Characteristics:
- Posted 35-55 mph (usually 40-50)
- Multiple lanes in each direction (4-6+)
- No median barrier or only a painted center turn lane
- Driveways every 50-200 feet (strip malls, gas stations, fast food)
- Bus stops directly on the shoulder
- Sidewalks present but set back only a few feet from 45+ mph traffic
- Crosswalks spaced 1/4-mile or more apart
- Traffic signals every 500-1500 feet (contradicting the "speed" design)
- No dedicated bike infrastructure

The design sends contradictory signals: the road geometry says "go 50," the speed limit says "go 45," the land use says "people cross here constantly."

## Strongest Counterargument
"Lowering speed limits doesn't change driver behavior — drivers will go whatever speed the road is designed for." This is actually an argument FOR the thesis: the road design IS the problem, not just the speed limit number. But lowering the limit alone (without road redesign) has modest effects. The real intervention is road dieting: removing lanes, adding medians, narrowing lanes, adding bike lanes.

**Counter to the counter:** Cities that have implemented Vision Zero with road diets show 20-40% reductions in fatalities (NYC, Hoboken, etc.). Hoboken went from 4 pedestrian deaths per year to zero for 7+ years running.
