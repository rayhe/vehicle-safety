# Research: IIHS Shifts from Crash Survival to Crash Prevention

## Angle
The IIHS just announced it will require intelligent speed assistance (ISA) for TSP+ by 2027 and impaired driving detection by 2030. This is the most radical shift in automotive safety testing in 60 years: from testing what happens WHEN you crash to testing whether the car stops you from crashing at all. FARS data quantifies the addressable gap.

## Journalist
Mia Crumplezone — Safety Engineering Editor. Beat: Vehicle design analysis, safety tech.

## Kill Test
**Is this genuinely newsworthy?** YES. The IIHS moving from passive to active behavioral intervention is a fundamental paradigm change. Nobody has cross-tabulated FARS impairment/speed data against the specific IIHS technology requirements.

**Novel contribution:** Calculating how many FARS deaths fall into the addressable envelope of the new IIHS tech requirements (ISA + DUI detection). Our data says ~20% of 490,736 tested drivers in fatal crashes were impaired. Speed is linked to 29% of fatalities per NHTSA. Combined, these two factors touch ~40-50% of all fatal crashes. The IIHS is implicitly admitting that better crumple zones hit a ceiling.

## Primary Sources (4+)

### 1. NHTSA FARS 2014-2023 (our dataset)
- 191,193 total deaths across 337 vehicle models
- 490,736 drivers tested for impairment
- 98,348 (20.0%) tested positive for ANY impairment
- 74,188 (15.1%) alcohol-positive
- 42,496 (8.7%) drug-positive
- Sports cars highest impairment: 22.5%
- Vans lowest: 18.1%
- Annual impairment-linked deaths: ~3,832

### 2. IIHS ISA + DUI Detection Announcement
- Source: Jalopnik, Automotive World, CarBuzz, FenderBender (multiple reports)
- ISA (Intelligent Speed Assistance) required for TSP+ by 2027
- Impaired driving detection required for TSP+ by 2030
- IIHS President David Harkey: aims to reduce road deaths by 30% by 2030
- 29% of 2023 crash fatalities linked to speed-related incidents (IIHS data)

### 3. NHTSA AEB Mandate (FMVSS 127)
- AEB required for all light vehicles by September 2029
- Must stop at speeds up to 62 mph
- Must brake automatically at 90 mph when collision imminent
- Projected: save 360+ lives and prevent 24,000+ injuries annually
- Must detect pedestrians in daylight AND at night
- Some delay/reconsideration under current administration

### 4. IIHS 2026 TSP+ Criteria Changes
- 45 vehicles won TSP+ in 2026, down from 66 in 2025 (32% drop)
- New: Good rating in rear-seat safety required
- New: Acceptable in pedestrian crash avoidance required
- New: All safety systems must be standard (no optional-only)
- Zero minivans, only 2 large pickups won
- Coming: driver attention monitoring standards

### 5. NSC Preliminary Data
- 2025 fatalities dropped 6.7% to ~37,810
- Pedestrian deaths dropped 11% (largest since 2010)
- Still above 2019 pre-pandemic levels

## Key Data Points for Article
- FARS: 20% of drivers in fatal crashes are impaired → ~3,800 deaths/year in our dataset
- Speed involved in 29% of fatalities nationally → ~11,000/year
- If ISA prevented 50% of speed-related fatal crashes: ~5,500 saved
- If DUI detection prevented 50% of impaired crashes: ~1,900 saved
- Combined theoretical ceiling: ~7,400 lives/year
- For context: the AEB mandate projects saving 360 lives/year
- ISA + DUI detection addressable envelope is 20x larger than AEB alone

## Strongest Counterargument
ISA systems may increase fatalities by making drivers complacent or by restricting speed in emergencies where acceleration is the correct evasive maneuver. DUI detection false positives could lock sober drivers out of their vehicles in dangerous situations (remote areas, fleeing danger). The technology may not work well enough — breathalyzers have 0.2% false positive rates; scale that to 280 million registered vehicles and you get 560,000 false lockouts.

## Limitations
- FARS only captures fatal crashes (a fraction of ~6.7M total crashes)
- FARS impairment is tested post-crash; presence ≠ causation
- Speed data not available per-vehicle in our FARS dataset (29% is aggregate NHTSA)
- ISA effectiveness depends on speed limit accuracy (which is poor on many roads)
- DUI detection technology is nascent — no commercial in-vehicle system exists at scale
