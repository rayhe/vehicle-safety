The Gap

GM Killed Its SUVs and Accidentally Saved 14,000 Lives

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
March 31, 2026

Between 2002 and 2009, General Motors built two mid-size SUVs on the GMT360 body-on-frame platform: the Chevrolet Trailblazer and the GMC Envoy. Together, they killed 3,461 occupants across 5,086 fatal crashes in the FARS database. When GM discontinued both and pivoted to unibody crossovers, the combined occupant death rate dropped roughly eightfold.[1]

8×
Approximate drop in occupant death rate from GMT360 SUVs to their unibody crossover replacements

The Trailblazer posted a death rate of 2.83 per 100 million vehicle-miles traveled. The Envoy clocked 2.26. Their replacements tell a different story: the Chevrolet Equinox lands at 0.36, the GMC Terrain at 0.29, the Traverse at 0.20.[1] Same dealership network, same brand badges, same customer walking through the door. Radically different odds of walking away from a wreck.

The lethality numbers are equally damning. In 68% of fatal crashes involving a GMT360, the truck's own occupant died. For the unibody replacements, that figure dropped to 48%. The Traverse and Acadia specifically pushed below 40%, meaning most of their fatal-crash involvement resulted in a death outside the vehicle, not inside it.[1] Crumple zones work. Unibody construction absorbs energy across the entire structure rather than channeling it through rigid frame rails into the cabin.[2]

Run the counterfactual and the scale becomes grotesque. The five unibody replacements accumulated an estimated 6.3 billion vehicle-miles over the study period. Apply the GMT360's death rate to that mileage and you get roughly 16,500 predicted occupant fatalities. The actual count was 2,006. That gap represents an estimated 14,500 lives that weren't lost, largely because GM stopped welding body-on-frame trucks and started stamping unibody crossovers.

Credit where it's complicated, though. This wasn't a single-variable experiment. The GMT360 vehicles in the FARS window were 15 to 22 years old, running on degraded safety equipment with aging tires and worn brake components. The crossovers were newer, equipped with electronic stability control mandated since 2012[3], standard side-curtain airbags, and modern crash structures tested against updated IIHS and NCAP protocols.[2] Hand-me-down demographics also skew the GMT360 data: older, cheaper vehicles migrate to younger, higher-risk drivers.

So was it the unibody switch specifically? Partially. Compare the GMT360 to other body-on-frame SUVs of similar vintage: the Toyota 4Runner posted a 1.0 rate, the Chevy Tahoe a 2.49. The GMT360 was bad even by body-on-frame standards. And the unibody crossovers aren't exceptional in their class; the Toyota RAV4 manages 0.19, the Honda CR-V 0.53. GM didn't build miracles. They just stopped building a platform that was measurably, fatally worse than the competition, and started building one that was average. Average, in vehicle safety, saves thousands of lives.

GM never marketed this as a public health victory. The crossover boom was sold as fuel economy, ride comfort, and cargo versatility. But the FARS data turns a consumer preference story into a mortality story. Every Equinox that replaced a Trailblazer on a suburban driveway quietly reduced that household's odds of a fatal outcome by a factor of eight. Sometimes the biggest safety interventions arrive disguised as marketing strategy.
