The Nissan Rogue Killed 127 People in Its Worst Model Year. The RAV4 Never Broke 88.

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
April 18, 2026

A single model year of the Nissan Rogue produced 127 fatalities in FARS data, which is 44% more than any vintage of the Toyota RAV4 ever recorded. Both vehicles compete for the same buyer in the same showroom aisle at roughly the same price, yet one sustained triple-digit death counts for four consecutive model years while the other never crossed 90.

127
Deaths from model year 2017 Nissan Rogues in FARS fatal crashes, 2014–2023

Nissan's second-generation Rogue, built on its T32 platform from model year 2014 through 2020, traces a trajectory that should concern anyone shopping the compact SUV segment. Early T32 vintages looked reasonable enough at 43 deaths for MY2014 and 68 for MY2015, numbers that tracked roughly with the vehicle's growing sales volume. Then the curve bent upward and locked into position: MY2016 hit 109, MY2017 peaked at 127, and MY2018 and MY2019 held at 109 and 107 respectively.[1] Four years running above 100 fatalities from a single nameplate is a pattern that demands explanation, particularly when every major competitor in the segment was doing something fundamentally different during the same period.

Toyota's RAV4, riding the TNGA architecture from MY2019 onward, peaked at 88 deaths in its launch year and then dropped to 55 and 57 for subsequent vintages.[1] Honda redesigned the CR-V for MY2017 and watched deaths fall from 101 to 75 in a single year, continuing down to 66 and then 30 in subsequent model years. Ford's Escape spiked to a segment-worst 165 deaths in MY2017 but immediately collapsed to 87 the following year, suggesting a one-vintage anomaly rather than a structural deficiency. Nissan's Rogue didn't spike and recover; it spiked and plateaued, sustaining elevated fatalities across four complete model-year cohorts before the third-generation CMF-C platform brought numbers back down to 49 for MY2021.

Nissan will point to fleet growth, and they would have a point worth taking seriously. Sales of the Rogue roughly doubled between the first and second generations, making it Nissan's best-selling model around 2016, and significantly more T32-generation Rogues hit American roads than T31 units in any given year. More vehicles means more exposure to crashes of every kind, which should mechanically increase raw death counts even at an identical per-vehicle risk rate. But Toyota sold even more RAV4s during the same period, building a fleet of 3.76 million versus the Rogue's 2.19 million, and the RAV4's per-vehicle death rate lands at 0.24 per thousand fleet vehicles while the Rogue sits at 0.44.[1] Nearly double, from a smaller fleet. Mazda's CX-5, occupying the same price bracket with a fleet of 1.05 million, peaked at just 26 deaths in its worst model year and runs 0.15 per thousand fleet vehicles, which means you would need a magnifying glass to find its deaths if plotted on the same chart as the Rogue.

What the math shows

Peak model-year deaths across the compact SUV segment, ranked worst to best: Ford Escape MY2017 at 165, Nissan Rogue MY2017 at 127, Honda CR-V MY2014 at 124, Chevrolet Equinox MY2018 at 120, Toyota RAV4 MY2019 at 88, Mazda CX-5 MY2018 at 26.[1] Ford's spike was a one-year anomaly that corrected itself, and Honda's peak came on an older platform that improved dramatically when the 2017 redesign arrived, while GM's Equinox spiked with its own redesign and partially recovered. Only the Rogue sustained triple-digit deaths across four model years without meaningful correction.

A reasonable counterargument deserves its full weight here: FARS captures only fatal crashes, and per-model-year fleet registration data isn't publicly available at the granularity needed to compute true normalized rates per vintage. Nissan's 2016-2019 sales boom may have been large enough to fully explain the death count increase without any change in underlying vehicle safety. Without precise cohort sizes, this analysis relies on aggregate fleet-adjusted rates and cross-competitive comparison rather than vintage-specific normalization, and if Nissan sold 2.5 times more MY2017 Rogues than MY2013 Rogues, the death count doubling could reflect exposure alone rather than engineering deficiency.

Three facts complicate that defense, however. First, Toyota's RAV4 experienced comparable sales growth and did not produce a comparable death plateau.[2] Second, the Rogue's aggregate lethality ratio of 54.5% puts it above the compact SUV class average, meaning that when Rogues appear in fatal crashes, a disproportionate share of fatalities are Rogue occupants rather than people in other vehicles.[1] Third, impairment rates among Rogue drivers in fatal crashes sit at 19%, squarely in the normal range for the segment, which eliminates the hypothesis that the vehicle simply attracts reckless drivers who inflate its numbers.[1]

What to do with this

If you own a 2016-2019 Rogue, check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls for outstanding recall work, and verify that all airbag and AEB-related service campaigns have been completed. Buyers shopping the segment today should note that Nissan's 2021+ Rogue on the newer CMF-C platform shows a significant death count reduction to 49 for MY2021, and the current IIHS evaluation awards it Top Safety Pick, though the same award was given to second-generation models that produced the elevated plateau. For the strongest fleet-adjusted safety records in compact SUVs, the RAV4 and CX-5 lead the segment by a wide margin.[2]
