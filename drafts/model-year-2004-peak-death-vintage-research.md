# Research: Model Year 2004 — America's Deadliest Vintage

## Original Finding (Novel Cross-Tabulation)
Analyzed FARS_MODEL_YEAR data to determine which model year has the most vehicles at their PEAK death toll. Found that **Model Year 2004 is the deadliest vintage**, with **23 vehicles** reaching their maximum FARS death count at this model year — nearly double the next-highest vintage (MY 2005 with 15 vehicles).

## Why 2004?
1. **Peak pre-recession production volume** — 2003-2005 were boom years for US auto sales. More units → more still on road → more FARS exposure.
2. **Last vintage before ESC spread** — Federal ESC mandate announced Sept 2007, phased 2008-2012. MY 2004 vehicles almost universally lacked ESC. IIHS estimates ESC reduces fatal single-vehicle crash risk by 49% for cars, 73% for SUVs.
3. **End-of-generation designs** — Many MY 2004 vehicles were final years of aging platforms: Cavalier (→ Cobalt 2005), Grand Am (→ G6 2005), Envoy (→ Acadia 2007), Venture (→ Uplander 2005), LeSabre (→ Lucerne 2006). End-of-run vehicles get minimal safety investment.
4. **10-19 years old during data window** — FARS data spans 2014-2023. MY 2004 vehicles were aging into the "second owner" demographic during the EXACT window being measured — worn brakes, bald tires, deferred maintenance, lower-income buyers who can't afford newer cars.
5. **Pre-AEB, pre-side-curtain mandate** — Side curtain airbags didn't become standard until 2013-ish. AEB came even later. MY 2004 had none of these.

## Counterarguments (steel-man):
- The spike may be primarily a fleet-size artifact: 2003-2005 were high-sales years, so more vehicles = more deaths mechanically
- The FARS data window (2014-2023) creates a "sweet spot" bias: vehicles need to be old enough to have accumulated crashes but young enough to still be on the road in significant numbers. MY 2004 hits this sweet spot perfectly.
- Without per-model-year registration data, we can't calculate death RATES per vehicle — only absolute counts.

## Limitations:
- FARS tracks FATAL crashes only — not total crashes. A vehicle could have high fatal crash counts but also high total crash survival rates.
- Deaths per model year doesn't account for miles driven per model year — older vehicles are driven fewer miles.
- Some peak years may reflect single-incident mass-casualty events rather than systematic patterns.
- The FARS data aggregates 2014-2023; year-to-year trends within that window are not visible.
