Existential Dread

A Porsche 911 Is Safer Than Your Camry

Vin Wreckage • Clautomotive Desk
March 21, 2026

Somewhere right now a parent is telling their teenager they can’t have a sports car because it’s dangerous, then handing over the keys to a Honda Accord—a vehicle with a FARS fatality rate of 3.07 deaths per 100 million VMT.[1] The Porsche 911 sits at 0.69. The thing they fear is 4.5 times safer than the thing they chose. Parenting is hard. Math shouldn’t be.

9 of 17
Sports cars in FARS with lower death rates than the average family sedan

I ranked all 17 sports cars in the FARS dataset by per-VMT fatality rate, then compared them against the sedan class average of 1.02 deaths per 100M VMT.[1] Nine sports cars came in under. The BMW Z4 at 0.46. Toyota Celica at 0.63. Acura RSX at 0.68. The Porsche 911 at 0.69. Even the Mitsubishi Eclipse—a car nobody would mistake for a safety-first purchase—logged 0.81. All of them lower than a Toyota Camry (2.03), Honda Civic (2.25), or Ford Focus (2.52).

Not lower by a rounding error. Lower by multiples. The Chevrolet Impala—the car your aunt considers “practical”—kills at 5.00 per 100M VMT. Seven times the 911.[1] Your insurer thinks the Impala is sensible. The morgue data disagrees.

But the sports car class splits violently in half. Below the sedan average: the Z4, Z3, Celica, RSX, 911, Boxster, Eclipse, Tiburon, Challenger. Above it—way above it—the Nissan 350Z (1.61), Corvette (1.52), Scion tC (2.30), Camaro (3.44), Mustang (6.02), and the Hyundai Veloster at a staggering 8.54.[1] Same class designation. Twelve-fold spread in fatality rates. Calling something a “sports car” tells you almost nothing about how likely it is to kill you.

The obvious explanation is demographics. Porsche 911 buyers average around 50 years old, earn north of $200K, and disproportionately treat the car as a weekend toy.[2] Mustang buyers skew younger, with a much wider income band and higher daily-driver usage. Different humans, different outcomes, regardless of sheet metal.

Except the toxicology data wrecks that tidy narrative. The 911 has a 22.8% impairment rate in fatal crashes. Nearly one in four 911 drivers who died were drunk or drugged.[1] The Corvette: 26.2%. These are not sober, careful retirees navigating parking lots. These are wealthy people hammering fast cars after cocktails. And the overall death rate still stays low. A 911 with nearly a quarter of its fatal-crash drivers impaired kills at a lower per-mile rate than the Camry—a commuter box full of sober people going to work. Driver behavior alone cannot explain that gap. The vehicle’s engineering—rear-engine weight distribution, 350mm ceramic brakes, tires wider than some sedans’ hoods, 60 years of iterative development at the Nürburgring—is doing measurable work.[2]

Then there’s the Veloster. The existential punchline. Impairment rate: 17.4%—the lowest of any sports car in FARS.[1] Death rate: 8.54—the highest. The soberest sports car drivers in America are dying at twelve times the rate of the drunkest ones. Hyundai built a 2,600-lb asymmetric three-door that IIHS rated “Acceptable” on small overlap, priced it for young buyers on tight budgets, and called it a sports car.[3] The 911 earned “Good” on every metric. That $80,000 price gap is, among other things, a survival premium.

Original Analysis

Cross-tabulating per-VMT fatality rate against in-crash impairment rate across the sports car class reveals a four-quadrant map nobody draws. Four vehicles sit in the “high impairment, low death rate” corner (911, Boxster, Corvette, BMW Z models)—engineering compensating for behavior. The Veloster occupies the opposite corner: low impairment, catastrophic death rate. Sober drivers dying more in cheaper cars while drunk drivers survive in expensive ones. That inversion is the actual safety story hiding behind the “sports car” label.

Methodology & Limitations

Death rates per 100M VMT estimated from FARS 2014–2023 using fleet size and NHTS average annual mileage (11,500 mi/yr).[1][4] Impairment = BAC > 0 or drug-positive toxicology. Fleet sizes for several sports cars are small (Z4: 43,750; Boxster: 26,250), so a handful of additional crashes could shift rates meaningfully. VMT estimates aren’t odometer readings—if sports car owners drive fewer discretionary miles than assumed, their true per-VMT rate is even lower. FARS only captures fatal crashes (~36,000/yr of ~6.7M total); a low fatality rate doesn’t preclude high injury rates.

Strongest Counterargument

Selection effect dwarfs everything else. 911 owners are disproportionately older, wealthier, more experienced. They drive fewer miles in better conditions—weekends, dry pavement, trips they skip when it rains. Transplant the median Accord buyer into a 911 and the rate doesn’t stay at 0.69. IIHS has documented extensively how age, sex, and income confound vehicle-level safety comparisons,[2] and this segment is where confounding hits hardest. This analysis cannot separate the car from the driver. But neither can anyone else’s—and that’s exactly why the simplistic “sports cars are dangerous” narrative persists unchallenged.
