# Research: The 1.0 Floor Myth — US vs International Fatality Rates

## Angle
The US just recorded a 1.01 fatality rate per 100M VMT for February 2026 — the closest it's ever come to breaking below 1.0. But 1.0 isn't a floor. Norway operates at 0.32 per 100M VMT equivalent. Sweden at 0.48. The OECD median is 0.64. The US "record" would place it dead last among developed nations. The 22,000 lives between the US rate and Norway's aren't lost to physics — they're lost to policy.

## Key Data Points

### US Historical Rate (per 100M VMT)
- 1925: 18.0
- 1966: 5.50 (peak absolute deaths: 50,894)
- 2014: 1.08 (32,675 deaths) — all-time annual record low
- 2019: 1.10 (36,096 deaths)
- 2021: 1.37 (43,230 deaths) — pandemic peak
- 2024: 1.19 (39,254 deaths)
- 2025: 1.10 (36,640 deaths) — second-lowest annual rate ever (NHTSA)
- Q1 2025: 1.05 (8,055 deaths) — 12th consecutive quarterly decline
- Feb 2026: 1.01 (2,470 deaths) — lowest monthly rate ever recorded (NSC)

### Why the Gap Exists (CDC/MMWR analysis)
1. Higher speed limits (US: 65-85 mph vs EU: 60-70 mph / 100-120 km/h)
2. Less mass transit (fewer alternatives to driving)
3. More VMT per capita (Americans drive significantly more)
4. Road design: wider roads, fewer roundabouts, stroad infrastructure
5. Weaker enforcement: DUI thresholds (US 0.08 vs Sweden 0.02)
6. Vehicle mix: heavier trucks/SUVs vs European car fleet
7. Seat belt usage: US ~92% vs Sweden ~98%
8. No universal speed cameras or ISA (Intelligent Speed Assistance)
