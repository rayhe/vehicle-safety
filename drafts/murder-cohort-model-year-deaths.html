The Murder Cohort: 6 Model Years Produced 31.7% of America's Traffic Deaths

Axle McScatter • Clautomotive Desk
April 1, 2026

I ran a cross-tabulation on 187,058 deaths from the FARS 2014–2023 dataset, bucketed by model year of the vehicle involved. The histogram that came back has a tumor growing out of it, and that tumor has a name: model years 2000 through 2005.

59,284
Deaths involving MY 2000–2005 vehicles in FARS 2014–2023

Six production years. Out of more than four decades of model years in the dataset. Responsible for 31.7% of all fatalities. Obscene math: 9,881 deaths per model year, dwarfing the 2012–2017 cohort's 6,393 average by a factor of 1.55.

Peak model year? 2005, at 11,363 deaths. Runner-up: 2004, at 11,221. These two years alone combine for 22,584 deaths, which is more than the entire 1990–1999 decade (26,591) produces when you subtract its best three years.

Why This Generation?

Three forces converge. First, exposure: these vehicles were 9–23 years old during the study window, sitting in the sweet spot where they're cheap enough for anyone to buy but old enough for structural fatigue, worn brakes, and bald tires. Second, volume: the early 2000s were peak American auto production, with Silverados and F-150s rolling off assembly lines by the hundreds of thousands. Third, technology: none of these vehicles were required to have electronic stability control. NHTSA's ESC mandate didn't take effect until 2012.[1] The IIHS found ESC reduces fatal single-vehicle crashes by 56% and rollovers by 77–80%.[2] These vehicles predate that protection entirely.

The Class Breakdown Is Where It Gets Ugly

Comparing the murder cohort (MY 2000–2005) against the ESC generation (MY 2012–2017) by vehicle class:

Pickups: 14,942 deaths down to 5,493. A 63% drop. Body-on-frame trucks from the early 2000s lacked side curtain airbags, modern crumple zones, and stability control. The 2003 Silverado alone contributed 597 deaths.

SUVs: 15,886 to 9,195. Down 42%. This tracks with the shift from body-on-frame Explorers and Trailblazers to unibody crossovers with vastly better crash structures.

Vans: 2,845 to 1,416. Down 50%. The old Dodge Grand Caravan was a rolling death certificate.

Sedans: 24,134 to 20,720. Only 14% improvement. Sedans were already better-engineered, so the redesign dividend was smaller.

Sports cars: 1,477 to 1,534. Up 4%. The only class that got worse. The Mustang, Camaro, and Challenger sold in vastly higher numbers in the 2012–2017 window thanks to the retro muscle car revival. More horsepower sold means more horsepower crashed.

The Body Count By Name

The top seven vehicles from the murder cohort, by deaths during 2014–2023:

Chevy Silverado (3,517). Ford F-150 (3,341). Honda Accord (2,397). Ford Explorer (1,852). Dodge RAM (1,773). Chevy Trailblazer (1,742). Toyota Camry (1,734). These seven models alone account for 16,356 deaths from just six model years. That's 8.7% of the entire FARS dataset concentrated in seven nameplates from one generation.

The Fleet Is Finally Turning Over

S&P Global Mobility reported in 2024 that the average US vehicle age hit a record 12.6 years, with passenger cars averaging 14.5 years.[3] At a steady 4.5% scrappage rate across 289 million registered vehicles, roughly 13 million vehicles leave the road annually. The 2000–2005 cohort is now 21–26 years old. They're finally dying.

And so are fewer Americans. NHTSA's 2024 early estimate projects 39,345 traffic fatalities, a 3.8% decline from 2023's 40,901 and the first time below 40,000 since 2020.[4] The agency celebrated "11 consecutive quarterly decreases." They should thank the scrapyard.

Methodology

I summed deaths from FARS_MODEL_YEAR across all 337 tracked models, grouping by model year. The calculation: for each model year, total all fatalities across every make and model in the FARS 2014–2023 dataset. Class comparisons use the same grouping, filtered by vehicle class (Sedan, Pickup, SUV, Van, Sports Car). The "redesign dividend" estimate compares per-year averages between cohorts but does not normalize for fleet size or exposure years, making it illustrative rather than precise.

Limitations

Exposure asymmetry is real and significant. A 2003 Silverado had up to 10 years of presence in the 2014–2023 FARS window; a 2020 Silverado had 3. Even at identical per-mile fatality rates, older model years would accumulate more deaths through sheer time in the dataset. This analysis measures cumulative damage to the population, not per-vehicle risk.

FARS captures only fatal crashes. A vehicle with low fatality counts may still cause severe injuries at high rates. And fleet size data by model year isn't publicly available at granular enough resolution to calculate deaths-per-10,000-registered-vehicles for each cohort.

The Strongest Case Against This Framing

A skeptic would argue: of course older model years show more deaths. They had more time in the study window. A 2004 F-150 could appear in every year from 2014 through 2023. A 2022 F-150 gets two years maximum. This isn't a "murder cohort," it's a measurement artifact of how FARS windows work.

That's partially true. But the counterpoint is just as stark: if a vehicle generation remains on the road for 20+ years and kills at elevated rates because it lacks modern safety technology, the cumulative body count is the policy-relevant metric. NHTSA doesn't award partial credit for years of exposure. Dead is dead, and 59,284 of them were in vehicles that predated the ESC mandate.
