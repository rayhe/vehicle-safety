# Research: The Land-Use Tax — Why Pedestrian Deaths Rose 54% While Everything Else Got Safer

## Angle
America spent 23 years building safer vehicles. Non-pedestrian traffic deaths fell 9.7% from 2000 to 2023. Pedestrian deaths rose 53.6% in the same period. A new FAU study published in JAPA (April 2026) argues this is fundamentally a land-use problem, not a road design problem. The U.S. puts essential destinations (grocery stores, pharmacies, gas stations, fast food) on high-speed arterials. European countries restrict this. At UK pedestrian fatality rates, ~5,500 more Americans would be alive each year.

## Original Contribution — The "Land-Use Tax" Calculation

From IIHS data (2023):
- Pedestrian deaths: 7,314
- Other deaths: 33,587 (from 37,182 in 2000 = -9.7%)
- Pedestrian deaths from 4,763 in 2000 = +53.6%
- Pedestrian share: 11% (2000) → 18% (2023)

US population 2023: ~335 million
US ped rate: 7,314/335M = 2.18 per 100K

At UK rates (4x lower): 2.18/4 = 0.545/100K × 335M = 1,826 deaths
At Canadian rates (3x lower): 2.18/3 = 0.727/100K × 335M = 2,434 deaths
At Norwegian rates (13x lower): 2.18/13 = 0.168/100K × 335M = 56 deaths

**Excess deaths (the "land-use tax"):**
- vs UK: 7,314 - 1,826 = 5,488 excess deaths/year
- vs Canada: 7,314 - 2,434 = 4,880 excess deaths/year
- vs Norway: 7,314 - 56 = 7,258 excess deaths/year

These are people who would still be alive if the U.S. had European-style land-use patterns that don't force pedestrians onto arterials.

## Counterargument (required)
The strongest counterargument: road design DOES matter, and the FAU study itself found that road characteristics (speed, lanes) also contribute. Land use is a factor, not THE factor. The U.S. also has higher VMT per capita, more sprawl requiring more arterial crossings, different enforcement culture, and higher rates of distracted/impaired driving. European countries have lower pedestrian deaths for multiple reasons beyond just zoning. Attribution is complex.

## Limitations
- International comparison ratios are population-rate comparisons, not exposure-adjusted (U.S. has much higher VMT per capita)
- FARS data doesn't directly code land-use context of pedestrian crashes (we're inferring from the FAU study which only covered Florida)
- The 53.6% increase since 2000 may be partially driven by increased walking/cycling exposure (more people walking), not just increased danger
- GHSA's H1 2025 decline may partly reflect regression to mean from pandemic highs, not structural improvement
