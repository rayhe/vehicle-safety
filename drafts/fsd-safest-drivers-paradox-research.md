# Research: FSD Is Trying to Replace the Safest Drivers in America

## Angle
NHTSA opened engineering analysis EA26002 on March 18, 2026, investigating Tesla's FSD system across 3.2 million vehicles (2016-2026 Model S/X, 2017-2026 Model 3, 2020-2026 Model Y, 2023-2026 Cybertruck). Nine crashes cited including one fatal.

The paradox: FARS data shows Tesla Model 3 drivers are already the SAFEST in the entire dataset. Cross-tabulating NHTSA's FSD investigation with FARS reveals FSD isn't replacing dangerous drivers — it's replacing ones who barely crash.

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES. Nobody else has cross-tabbed the FSD investigation with the underlying FARS safety profile of the fleet. The finding challenges both Tesla bulls (FSD has marginal safety value if the baseline is already excellent) and Tesla bears (the vehicles under investigation have the lowest death rates on the road). Original contribution: first FARS-based analysis of FSD's replacement baseline.

## Key FARS Data Points

### Tesla Model 3 in FARS:
- Deaths: 92 (over 2014-2023)
- Crashes: 212
- Fleet: ~1,575,000
- Rate: 0.05 deaths per 100M VMT (LOWEST in entire dataset)
- Crash frequency: 13 per 100K fleet (LOWEST in entire dataset)
- Lethality: 0.434 (43.4% of crashes are fatal — actually middling)

### Comparison vehicles (median crash frequency: 174 per 100K):
- Honda Accord: freq 548, rate 3.07 — 42x higher death rate than Model 3
- Chevy Silverado: freq 347, rate 1.25 — 25x higher death rate
- Toyota Camry: freq 393, rate 2.03 — 41x higher death rate
- Ford F-150: freq 306, rate 1.04 — 21x higher death rate

### Safety Haven quadrant (Model 3 is extreme outlier):
- Tesla Model 3: freq 13, lethality 0.434
- Honda HR-V: freq 30, lethality 0.534
- Mazda CX-5: freq 36, lethality 0.432
- VW Tiguan: freq 36, lethality 0.472

### The paradox quantified:
- Model 3 crash frequency is 13x LOWER than median (13 vs 174)
- Model 3 death rate is 0.05 vs dataset-wide average ~1.5-2.0
- A Chevy Cobalt driver is 100x more likely to die per mile than a Model 3 driver
- FSD is attempting to improve upon the LOWEST baseline risk in the dataset

## Limitations
- FARS only captures fatal crashes — Tesla's low rate could reflect crash avoidance (AEB, collision warnings) rather than driver skill
- Tesla fleet is newer on average (2017+) vs dataset vehicles spanning 1990-2023 — newer vehicles have inherently better safety tech
- Tesla owner demographics (higher income, education) correlate with safer driving independent of vehicle
- Model 3's low VMT could be under- or over-estimated — EV mileage patterns differ from ICE
- FSD engagement data isn't in FARS — we can't separate FSD-on vs FSD-off crashes

## Strongest Counterargument
Tesla's low FARS rates likely reflect its newer fleet age and wealthy owner demographics more than any inherent vehicle advantage. A 2020 Camry might perform similarly if FARS had enough data on only 2017+ model years. The comparison is partially age-biased because FARS accumulates deaths across the full fleet lifespan, and older vehicles dominate death counts. Still — even among other new vehicles (2017+), Tesla's crash involvement rate remains notably low.
