Ford Recalled 6 Cars for Every 1 It Sold Last Year. Now It’s Telling Owners to Stop Driving.

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
June 2, 2026

On June 1, Ford told 4,653 people who bought a Bronco Sport or Maverick to park their vehicles and call a tow truck.[1] The front lower control arm ball joints may have been bolted on wrong at the assembly plant. If one separates at highway speed, the wheel disconnects from the steering knuckle. You lose the ability to steer a two-ton vehicle traveling at 70 miles per hour. Ford's language is politely clinical: "loss of vehicle control, increasing the risk of a crash." Translation: your front wheel falls off.

6.2 : 1
Ford's recall-to-sales ratio in 2025: 12.9 million vehicles recalled vs. 2.07 million sold in the U.S.

Recall 26S36 is Ford's 37th of 2026, nearly triple the count of any other manufacturer through the same period.[2] General Motors, Toyota, and Stellantis sit at roughly 12 each. But quantity understates the problem, because the critical shift is not how many recalls Ford issues but how severe they have become. Most of Ford's 153 recalls in 2025 were software patches and OTA updates: rearview camera glitches, towing software errors, dashboard display bugs, all fixable with a remote download that never requires a tow truck or a torque wrench. A ball joint installed incorrectly during vehicle assembly is not a line of bad code. It is a physical, structural failure on the factory floor, and it means Ford's quality crisis has migrated from the software layer to the hardware layer, from fixable-at-a-distance to tow-it-to-a-dealer, from predictable to random.

Run the math on Ford's 2025 recall volume and the picture clarifies into something almost absurd. Ford issued recalls covering approximately 12.9 million vehicles while selling 2.07 million new ones in the United States.[3] That ratio of 6.2 recalled vehicles for every single vehicle sold has no modern precedent at sustained scale. Through five months of 2026, the pace has worsened: roughly 9.6 million vehicles tagged across 37 campaigns against estimated sales of 850,000, which puts the 2026 ratio on track to reach 11.8:1. Ford is recalling its own fleet faster than it can build new cars.

The financial consequences have tracked the mechanical ones with uncomfortable precision. NHTSA levied a $165 million civil penalty against Ford for delayed recall disclosures.[4] A single 2025 recall of 694,000 Bronco Sports and Escapes for faulty fuel injectors cost an estimated $570 million, per SEC filings. Annual warranty and recall costs regularly exceed $4 billion. CEO Jim Farley has described the situation as "self-inflicted wounds" and launched a "test vehicles to failure" quality initiative in 2024, promising results within 18 months, a deadline that arrives mid-2026. The ball joint recall suggests the wounds are still being inflicted.

Assembly errors are qualitatively worse than design errors, and this distinction matters for the 4,653 owners currently stranded. A design defect is deterministic: every vehicle rolling off the line has the same flaw, and the fix is identical for each one. An assembly error is stochastic, meaning some vehicles were bolted together correctly, others were not, and there is no way to know which category yours falls into without physical inspection. Ford's fix requires a visual inspection of the ball joint assembly on both sides of every affected vehicle. Some will pass; others will need the entire lower control arm replaced, likely followed by a front-end alignment to prevent accelerated tire wear. There is no software update for gravity.

FARS data provides an imperfect but relevant lens on what Ford's quality trajectory means for occupant survival. The Ford Escape, which shares the C2 platform family with the Bronco Sport, logged 2,284 deaths at a rate of 0.95 per 100 million VMT between 2014 and 2023.[5] The Explorer: 3,797 deaths at 1.54 per 100 million VMT. The F-150, Ford's flagship and America's best-selling vehicle: 9,194 deaths across the same decade. These are not hypothetical statistics but the real-world environment in which Ford's quality problems operate, a fleet of millions of vehicles driven hundreds of billions of miles, where structural failures do not stay theoretical for long.

Ford claims its elevated recall numbers reflect a proactive safety culture, catching problems before they cause injuries rather than after. There is a version of this argument that is true: an automaker that issues recalls aggressively is, in a narrow sense, more transparent than one that buries known defects. GM sat on a faulty ignition switch for over a decade before recalling 30.4 million vehicles in 2014, a catastrophe that killed at least 124 people.[6] Ford has not had a cover-up of that magnitude. But proactive recall culture is supposed to be a transitional phase, a painful cleanup that precedes improvement. At 153 recalls in 2025 and 37 through five months of 2026, this is not a transition. It is a state of being.

If you own a 2021–2026 Bronco Sport or 2022–2026 Maverick, check your VIN at ford.com/support/recalls today. If your vehicle is affected by recall 26S36, do not drive it. Ford is covering towing costs and offering mobile inspection where available. The notification letters go out June 5, but NHTSA has already posted the recall, so do not wait for the letter. Steering is not optional.
