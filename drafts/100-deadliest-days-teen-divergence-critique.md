### Critic 6: 🔬 Research Rigor — 8.8/10

**Original contribution:** ✅ Two novel analytical connections:
1. **Temporal divergence framing:** MoneyGeek's raw data shows teen summer fatalities rising while overall deaths fall, but MoneyGeek didn't connect this to vehicle fleet composition or FARS per-model death rates. This article does.
2. **Fleet-age mechanism thesis:** Arguing that the teen divergence is driven by the vehicles teens drive (pre-2015 sedans without AEB) rather than teen driving behavior per se. This reframes the problem from "teen drivers are getting worse" to "teen vehicles are the ones safety progress left behind."

**Novelty vs. existing Crash Report stories:**
- `teen-driver-safe-list-fars-gap.html` — about IIHS teen list vehicles having high FARS death rates. Different hook (list quality vs. temporal trend).
- `teen-oldest-car-fatality-premium.html` — about parent-teen fleet age gap. Overlapping theme but different data source (no MoneyGeek summer trend).
- This article is sufficiently distinct: the hook is the MoneyGeek 2024 summer data showing a temporal divergence trend, not a static snapshot.

**Limitations acknowledged:** ✅ Five explicit, honest limitations:
1. FARS 15-20 age bracket includes non-teens
2. MoneyGeek uses June-August, not Memorial Day-Labor Day
3. FARS vehicle data ends 2023 vs. 2022-2024 teen trend
4. Small-state volatility (Arizona example)
5. Cannot directly cross-tab age with vehicle model — inference is indirect

**Strongest counterargument at full strength:** ✅ Post-pandemic licensing backlog thesis is presented without strawmanning. The response is honest: "even generous VMT assumptions cannot fully absorb a 17.2% fatality increase spread across two consecutive years." Does not claim to fully defeat the counterargument — says "Something structural is happening," which is an appropriate confidence level.

**Verifiability:** ✅ All FARS numbers exactly match `fars_output.js`. MoneyGeek URL provided. IIHS URLs provided.

**Methodology transparency:** ✅ The vehicle-age inference is explicitly flagged as indirect: "based on fleet composition data and the economic reality of teen car ownership, not direct measurement."

**Gap:** The article relies on a single secondary source (MoneyGeek) for its core teen fatality trend data. If MoneyGeek's analysis methodology has errors, the entire article falls. However, MoneyGeek cites FARS directly, and the methodology is described (FARS + FHWA VMT). The risk is acceptable for a journalism piece.
