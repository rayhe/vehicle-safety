## Critic 1: 🔍 General Editor — Score: 8.5/10

**Strengths:**
- Headline is outstanding — "Might Burn Down in Your Garage" is visceral and specific. OG description is equally good.
- Lede opens with a punchy number fragment ("Thirty-one people.") that immediately grounds the paradox.
- Structure is clean: crash safety → recall bombshell → FARS blind spot → counterargument → call to action.
- The "cosplay as a space heater" line is the best sentence in the piece — vivid, darkly funny, on-brand.
- Pull stat is well-chosen (the recall number, not the death rate).

**Weaknesses:**
- At 587 words the article overshoots the 300–500 target. The NFPA paragraph and the "gap between what we measure" paragraph could be tightened or merged.
- The closing question ("safest against what?") is good but the preceding sentence ("the next time someone tells you...") is a bit long for a sign-off.
- The article is slightly more essay-like than the typical Crash Report punchy feature — it reads more like Vin Wreckage's philosophical beat than Rex Driverton's noir investigation. Rex would be more cutting, less reflective.

**Revisions needed:**
1. Trim ~80–90 words from the body to hit the 500-word ceiling.
2. Sharpen the Rex Driverton voice — add one more sardonic observation, cut one reflective sentence.

## Critic 3: ⚖️ Ethics Reviewer — Score: 9.0/10

**Moral reasoning:**
- The article is fundamentally fair. It presents the paradox without demonizing Kia — the counterargument paragraph explicitly states the recall system worked and the Telluride is genuinely safe in crashes.
- No self-congratulation. The article admits its own site's tables share the blind spot ("tables we publish on this site").
- The piece does not overstate fire danger — it correctly notes no deaths have been reported from this defect.

**Potential concerns:**
- The phrase "might spontaneously combust" in the OG description is slightly inflammatory for a defect with no reported deaths. The article body is more measured, but the OG tag could cause unnecessary alarm.
- The "75% of every one ever sold" framing in the pull-label is attention-grabbing but defensible given the FARS fleet estimate (75.6%).

**Positions:**
- The article takes a clear position (safety metrics have a fire-risk blind spot) without being preachy. It doesn't tell readers what policy changes to demand or blame regulators. It simply asks "safest against what?" — which is the right question.

**No revisions needed** beyond the OG description softening (optional, minor).

## Critic 6: 🔬 Research Rigor — Score: 8.5/10

**Original contribution:** ✅
Cross-referencing the Telluride's near-zero FARS crash death rate with the scope of the fire recall is a genuine novel analysis. Nobody else has juxtaposed the two databases this way for this vehicle. The broader observation — that FARS, IIHS, and NHTSA five-star ratings are all structurally blind to non-crash fire risk — is the kind of systemic insight the guide calls for.

**Limitations acknowledgment:** ✅
- Article explicitly states FARS is crash-only and fire deaths are excluded by design (both in body and disclaimer).
- The disclaimer notes fleet/VMT estimates use registration-weighted imputation.
- Could be slightly more explicit about the NFPA figure being aggregate (not Telluride-specific), but the article doesn't claim it is.

**Strongest counterargument:** ✅
Full-strength presentation: recall system worked, no deaths, crashworthiness is real engineering. Not strawmanned. The article even says "That is real engineering, and a seat motor defect does not erase it."

**Methodology transparency:** ⚠️
- The 0.360 death-per-crash ratio is stated but not derived (31 deaths ÷ 86 crashes). Minor — the inputs are in the references.
- The "25×" claim needs correction (actual: 22.5×).

**Verifiability:** ✅
All factual claims are traced to either FARS data, the Reuters report, or NFPA. The one weak link is the NFPA URL, which may have changed.

**Revisions needed:**
1. Fix the 25× multiplier.
2. Consider briefly noting that NFPA vehicle fire deaths are aggregate, not model-specific.
