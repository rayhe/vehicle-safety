Kia Recalled 463,000 Tellurides for Fire Risk. The Fix Caught Fire.

Clara Rollover • Clautomotive Desk
July 10, 2026

In June 2024, Kia told 462,869 Telluride owners their front seats might catch fire. Park outside, they said. Bring it to a dealer. We'll install a bracket and swap out the knobs. Problem solved.

Two years later, the problem is not solved. Kia filed a second recall this week for the same vehicles, the same defect, the same fire risk. Seven underseat fires. Eleven melted seat motors. Some of those fires happened in Tellurides that had already received the first recall repair.

462,869
Tellurides recalled twice for the same fire defect

Read that again. Owners brought their SUVs in. Dealers performed the recall service. Kia closed the paperwork. And the seats kept catching fire.

Kia's own investigation into the re-failures turned up two problems. First: "sporadic dealer workmanship issues." Some technicians reinforced the seat switch cover but apparently forgot to replace the switch itself. Second, and worse: the physical design flaw survived the fix. Kia's filing with NHTSA acknowledges that "if occupant strikes the seat slide knob when entering vehicle, twisting forces may also result in a dislodged or misaligned seat switch." Translation: bumping the knob with your knee while climbing in can undo what the recall was supposed to prevent. Motor runs. Overheats. Smolders.

Kia's new remedy isn't a redesigned switch. It's an electronic fuse spliced into the seat motor circuit. If the motor runs continuously because the switch is jammed, the fuse pops before the motor can overheat. It's a circuit breaker on a problem Kia couldn't engineer away in two years and two tries.

The Compounding Exposure Problem

NHTSA's own data shows that roughly 39% of recalled vehicles never get repaired.[1] For the Telluride, that means approximately 180,000 of the original 462,869 may have never seen a dealer at all. They still have the original defective switch, the original knobs, no bracket, no fuse.

Of the roughly 283,000 that did get the first fix, an unknown fraction are still vulnerable. The fix didn't work. Kia's own words: fires in repaired vehicles, "sporadic dealer workmanship," design flaws the remedy didn't address.

So what's the real number of Tellurides at risk right now? Not 39%. Not 61%. Somewhere between most and all of 462,869 vehicles remain exposed to the original fire defect. The second recall doesn't begin until August 13. VINs won't even be searchable until July 17.

A Fuse Is Not a Fix

There's a meaningful engineering distinction between fixing a defect and limiting its consequences. Kia did not redesign the seat switch. They did not change the mechanism that allows the knob to dislodge the switch when struck. They added a fuse. If the motor runs when it shouldn't, the fuse blows, and the motor stops before it generates enough heat to ignite. Vehicles built after May 30, 2024 already have a reinforced switch, but the 462,869 recalled units get the fuse.

It'll probably work. Fuses are simple, reliable, well-understood. But the philosophy matters. The first recall said: we'll make the switch harder to dislodge. That failed. The second recall says: we accept the switch will dislodge, so we'll cut the power before it burns. It's a concession, not a correction.

The Broader Pattern

This isn't happening in isolation. In March, a two-year-old named Lucia Ayala suffocated when a power-folding third-row seatback trapped her in a 2026 Hyundai Palisade.[2] Hyundai recalled more than 60,000 Palisades. Cadillac discovered the same defect in 14,540 of its Vistiq EVs and issued its own recall.[3] Kia pulled 568 Telluride Hybrids for the same third-row issue. All separate from the front seat fire recall.

Different mechanisms, different brands. Same gap: powered seat systems are failing in ways nobody tested for, and the industry response has been reactive, serial, and in Kia's case, inadequate on the first attempt.

The Strongest Case for Kia

Fairness demands noting: zero injuries, zero deaths. Seven fires in 462,869 vehicles is a rate of 0.0015%. Mechanically, the fuse is sound. And Kia discovered the remedy failure itself, not after a crash or a federal investigation. That's better than the alternative. But "nobody burned alive yet" is a low bar for a vehicle marketed to families, and the fact that a recall remedy needed its own recall raises questions about whether the initial investigation was thorough enough.

What To Do

If you own a 2020–2024 Kia Telluride: park it outside, away from your house and other cars. Not in the garage. Not next to the neighbor's Honda. This is not overcaution. NHTSA issued the same advisory twice.

Starting July 17, check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls. Notification letters will be mailed August 13. When the fix is available, get it done. Yes, even if you already got the first one done. Especially if you already got the first one done.

And check your VIN even if you've "completed" the original recall. A completed recall on a Kia Telluride currently means you received a repair that Kia itself says didn't work.
