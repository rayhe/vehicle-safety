The Gap

NHTSA Just Built a Safety Test for Cars That Don't Need It

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
May 8, 2026

On Wednesday, NHTSA announced that the 2026 Tesla Model Y became the first vehicle to pass its brand-new advanced driver-assistance safety benchmark.[1] Four fresh tests: pedestrian automatic emergency braking, lane keeping, blind spot warning, and blind spot intervention. A real expansion of the five-star program. Progress.

One problem. The Model Y already had the lowest fatality rate of any SUV in FARS at 0.03 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled.[2] Certifying it as safe is like awarding a fire extinguisher to a building made of concrete.

92.4%
of fatal crash vehicles are from model years before 2018, predating widespread ADAS adoption

Run the FARS model-year death distributions against the ADAS adoption timeline and the gap is staggering. Of the 187,058 vehicle-occupant fatalities in the 2014-2023 dataset, 92.4% involved model years before 2018, the era before automatic emergency braking became standard equipment. Just 7.6% involved vehicles from 2018 or newer. Narrow to model years 2022 and later, where AEB is nearly universal, and you get 0.6% of deaths. The new test regime certifies 0.6% of the problem.

The ten deadliest vehicles by fatality rate tell the story bluntly. Seven are discontinued: the Hyundai Veloster (8.54 deaths per 100M VMT, killed off in 2021), the Chevy Tracker (7.83, gone since 2004), the Cobalt (5.10, axed in 2010), the Chevy S-10 (4.83, dead since 2004), the Cadillac Seville (3.89, discontinued 2004). These vehicles are still on roads, still in fatal crashes, and will never sit for NHTSA's shiny new test. Meanwhile, the five safest vehicles are all current-production, ADAS-loaded models that effectively passed before the test existed.

NHTSA deserves credit for finally testing what matters. Pedestrian AEB alone could prevent thousands of crashes a year once the full fleet turns over. But "once the fleet turns over" is doing enormous work in that sentence. The average age of a vehicle on U.S. roads hit 12.6 years in 2024, according to S&P Global Mobility.[3] Fleet turnover is slowing, not accelerating. Every year that a 2006 Impala stays registered is a year NHTSA's new benchmark cannot reach it.

The uncomfortable math: for every fatality in a 2022-or-newer vehicle, there are 143 fatalities in vehicles built before ADAS existed. No test, no rating, no five-star sticker will save the people driving a 2007 Cobalt on a rural two-lane at 11 p.m.

What this means for you

If you drive a vehicle older than 2018, you are statistically in the fleet where nearly all fatal crashes occur. NHTSA's new tests will not help you. What will: checking your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls for unfixed safety recalls. Aftermarket blind-spot mirrors cost $8. A forward-facing dashcam with collision warning runs under $100. None of these are ADAS, but they are the interventions available to the 92% of the fleet that NHTSA's testing program cannot reach.

Limitations

The 92.4% figure is cumulative across the 2014-2023 FARS window. Older model years have more exposure time in the dataset (a 2005 model accumulates crashes over all ten years; a 2022 model has one or two). This inflates the raw count for older vehicles. It does not change the core finding: the fleet currently killing people is overwhelmingly pre-ADAS, and no new-car test program addresses that retroactively. A rate-controlled analysis would narrow the gap but not close it.
