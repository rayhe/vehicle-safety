# Research: NHTSA Removed the Brake Pedal. The Software Replacing It Has Been Recalled Five Times.

## Article #412 | Journalist: Clara Rollover | Date: 2026-07-02

## Core Thesis
On June 26, 2026, NHTSA proposed eliminating the brake pedal requirement from Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards for vehicles "designed never to be operated by a human." The proposal contains no standardized emergency stop mechanism — how passengers stop the car "would likely vary by manufacturer." This article examines the regulatory leap of faith against the actual track record of the software being trusted with that responsibility.

## Key Data Points

### NHTSA Proposal (June 26, 2026)
- Eliminates FMVSS requirement for brake pedal AND emergency brake lever in ADS-only vehicles
- No standardized emergency stop button or mechanism required
- NHTSA quote: "It is NHTSA's expectation that if these controls are removed, passengers will be provided with a means to direct an ADS-operated vehicle to come to a stop, though how a passenger would indicate they wanted the ADS-operated vehicle to stop would likely vary by manufacturer."
- NHTSA argues manual controls "could pose as a safety risk through intentional or unintentional misuse by a vehicle passenger"
- Part of Transportation Secretary Sean Duffey's AV Framework (April 2025)
- NHTSA Chief Counsel Peter Simhauser: "enabling AV manufacturers to develop faster and spend less time on unnecessary process, while still advancing safety"
- NHTSA Administrator Morrison: "a vehicle designed never to be operated by a human doesn't get safer because it has a foot pedal"
- Source: Jalopnik (June 30, 2026), Reuters (June 26, 2026), Federal Register

### Waymo Recall Record (5 voluntary recalls, ~2.5 years)
1. **Feb 2024**: 444 vehicles — software couldn't predict towed vehicle movement. 2 collisions with same towed pickup in minutes.
2. **Jun 2024**: Jaguar I-Pace — collision with telephone pole
3. **May 2025**: 1,212 vehicles — collisions with gates, chains, barriers. 16 collisions (2022-2024). NHTSA: "collisions with clearly visible objects that a competent driver would be expected to avoid."
4. **Dec 2025**: 3,067 vehicles — illegally passing stopped school buses. 19+ incidents in Austin alone, one "moments after a student had crossed in front of the vehicle." Also incidents in Atlanta.
5. **May 2026**: 3,791 vehicles — driving into flooded roads despite speed. San Antonio vehicle became stuck. Waymo suspended operations in 4 cities (Atlanta, Dallas, San Antonio, Houston). THIS RECALL WAS FOR BEHAVIOR THE PREVIOUS SOFTWARE WAS SUPPOSED TO HANDLE.

### Crash Statistics
- **1,790 NHTSA-logged crashes involving Waymo vehicles since July 2021** (Witherite Law Group / NHTSA data)
- **Jan 2026**: Waymo vehicle struck a child in Santa Monica crosswalk — NHTSA and NTSB investigations opened

## Strongest Counterargument
NHTSA's position is defensible on narrow logic: a vehicle designed never to be operated by a human genuinely doesn't need a brake pedal in the traditional sense. Adding manual controls passengers aren't trained to use could introduce new failure modes. And Waymo's per-mile injury rate, by its own reporting, is lower than human drivers. The question isn't whether the logic is reasonable in theory — it's whether the software has earned this trust in practice.

## Limitations
- The 1,790 crash figure includes all types (minor, major, at-fault, not-at-fault). Not all are software failures.
- Waymo's fleet is small enough that statistical comparisons to human driving are still immature.
- The rule applies ONLY to ADS-only vehicles, not consumer cars with optional autonomy.
- "Recall" in the AV context often means an OTA software update, not a physical repair.

## Actionable Insights
1. If you're a passenger in a robotaxi, locate the emergency stop mechanism BEFORE the ride starts. There's no standard — it could be a button, a screen tap, a voice command, or nothing at all.
2. First responders: Know that no federal standard exists for how to stop an ADS vehicle externally.
3. Waymo's flood recall led to service suspension in 4 cities. If your city has robotaxi service, check whether it has been suspended for weather events.
4. Tesla Cybercab is testing without pedals in Austin RIGHT NOW. Expect to share the road with pedalless vehicles.
