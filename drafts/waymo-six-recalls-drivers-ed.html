Waymo Has Had Six Recalls in Two Years. Every One Failed Something You’d Learn in Driver’s Ed.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
June 18, 2026

Waymo filed its sixth recall with NHTSA today. Nearly 3,900 robotaxis were ignoring road-closure signs and driving into active freeway construction zones in California and Arizona, with a dozen-plus incidents since early April. Workers with hard hats and orange vests, doing their jobs next to several tons of confused Jaguar I-PACE.

6 recalls
Six NHTSA filings in 28 months, each one a basic driving skill

Last month it was floodwater. An empty Waymo robotaxi in San Antonio slowed down, considered a flooded lane, drove in anyway, and got swept away. Before that, school buses with their red lights flashing, and before that, gates and chains in parking lots. A telephone pole that the software rated as "low damage." Two separate Waymo vehicles hitting the same truck being towed backward through an intersection in Phoenix, one after the other, minutes apart, like a slapstick bit that writes itself.

Stack the six recalls in order and something remarkable emerges: every single one maps to a chapter in a standard driver’s education textbook. Not obscure edge cases requiring years of on-road experience, but the curriculum you cover before your first supervised drive around a parking lot.

The Syllabus

Recall one, February 2024. Waymo filed on 444 vehicles after the Phoenix tow-truck incident. Its software “incorrectly predicted the future motion of the towed vehicle.”[1] Driver’s ed equivalent: sharing the road with large vehicles. You know that chapter with the diagrams showing blind spots and wide turns; human solution is to look at the truck.

Recall two came in June 2024 when a robotaxi hit a telephone pole at 8 mph while en route to pick up a passenger in Phoenix.[2] The software had “assigned a low damage score” to the pole. Driver’s ed equivalent: basic vehicle control, stationary obstacle avoidance. Human solution: see the pole, which is a wooden cylinder approximately one foot in diameter and thirty feet tall, planted in the ground, not moving.

Recall three dropped in February 2025, covering 1,200 vehicles after multiple low-speed collisions with gates, chains, and barrier arms at parking lot entrances, accumulated over two years of incidents dating back to December 2022.[3] Nine additional barrier collisions were disclosed during the NHTSA review. Driver’s ed equivalent: navigating controlled-access areas, something you practice before the written test. Human solution: stop at the gate, wait for it to open.

Recall four arrived in late 2025 after Waymo vehicles in Austin and Atlanta were sailing past stopped school buses with their red lights activated. Waymo filed a voluntary recall and pushed an OTA patch.[4] Driver’s ed equivalent: chapter one, page one, paragraph one. A legal obligation so fundamental that it appears in every state’s permit test. Flashing red lights on a 40-foot yellow vehicle exist specifically to be unmissable. Waymo missed them.

Recall five, May 2026, covering the full fleet of 3,791 vehicles. On April 20, a robotaxi encountered standing water on a 40-mph road, slowed to a crawl, and then drove in. It had to be recovered.[5] NHTSA noted Waymo was still “developing the final remedy.” Driver’s ed equivalent: adverse weather driving. Human equivalent: a bumper sticker that says “Turn Around, Don’t Drown.”

Recall six, today. Nearly 3,900 vehicles blowing past ramp-closure signs into pre-planned construction zones on freeways in California and Arizona.[6] More than a dozen incidents since early April. Driver’s ed equivalent: reading the orange road signs with the words on them.

The Cadence Problem

Waymo spaced its first four recalls across 22 months. That last pair arrived 37 days apart. That acceleration matters because the fleet is also growing fast. Recall one covered 444 vehicles. Recall six covers 3,900. The fleet has multiplied ninefold while the software keeps discovering new categories of common objects and situations it cannot handle.[7]

A $126-billion company that raised $16 billion in its latest funding round and delivers 500,000 paid trips per week across 10 U.S. cities is still, in mid-2026, filing federal safety notices because its cars don’t know what a construction zone looks like. At that valuation, each of the six lessons learned works out to roughly $21 billion per skill. A driver’s education course at your local community college costs about $50 and covers all of them in a single semester.

The Strongest Case for Waymo

Credit where it’s earned: the recall mechanism itself is extraordinary, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. When Toyota discovers a defective fuel pump, the average recall reaches about 70% completion after six quarters of manufacturer reporting to NHTSA.[8] Thirty percent of affected cars never get fixed. They stay on the road, defective, until they crash or get scrapped. Waymo pushes a software patch over the air and hits 100% of its fleet in days. Every vehicle, simultaneously safer, with a paper trail to prove it.

And zero fatalities. Twenty-five million fully autonomous miles, 697 reported incidents under NHTSA’s Standing General Order, no passenger or bystander deaths attributed to the autonomous system.[9] That is a genuine safety record. A human-driven fleet of 3,900 vehicles covering the same urban miles would almost certainly have killed somebody by now.

The counterargument deserves its full weight: these recalls are the process working. Catching failures before they become fatal, patching them instantly, documenting everything. The system is learning. If you only look at outcomes, Waymo is the safest fleet operator in America.

The Catch

But outcomes alone don’t tell you how close the misses were, or how the next unknown edge case will land. A robotaxi getting swept away in San Antonio floodwater was empty. A Waymo striking a child near a Santa Monica elementary school in January caused only minor injuries. An empty car veering through an active construction zone with workers present hasn’t killed anyone yet.

Yet.

The pattern exposed by these six recalls is not that robots are stupid. They are clearly capable of navigating staggering complexity for hundreds of thousands of trips per week. The pattern is that the failure modes are basic. Not novel autonomous-vehicle challenges like ethical trolley problems or adversarial attacks on lidar. Garden-variety hazards covered in every state’s standard driving curriculum. Poles. Signs. Water. School buses. Trucks. Cones.

The gap between what the system handles brilliantly and what it fails at catastrophically is not closing with scale. It is widening, because scale means more cities, more weather, more construction seasons, more of the unstructured messiness that human drivers absorb through a lifetime of spatial reasoning and common sense.

What You Should Do

If you ride Waymo: the fleet is safer than most human-driven taxis by any statistical measure available, and the recall mechanism is genuinely best-in-class. But stay aware of conditions. Pay attention during construction zones and adverse weather, because the car may not. If you’re a pedestrian or a worker in a Waymo-operating city: the vehicle treats you as a data input, and the data models have blind spots the company keeps discovering through incident reports. Be visible. Be predictable. Don’t assume the car sees you.

If you’re an investor: six recalls in two years is either the world’s most transparent safety program or a confession that the software hits a new wall every four months. Possibly both. The question is whether the wall-hitting rate converges toward zero before a recall-worthy incident produces the fleet’s first fatality. At current cadence, there will probably be a seventh recall before the end of summer.
