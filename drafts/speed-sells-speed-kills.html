The Auto Industry Spends $9.5 Billion Selling Speed. NHTSA Spends $9.6 Million Cleaning Up the Dead.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
May 14, 2026

An IIHS study released this month found that 43% of American car advertisements promote speed or performance. Eight percent mention safety. Not 18%. Not 28%. Eight.[1]

990 : 1
Ratio of industry speed-marketing spend to NHTSA's anti-speeding budget

Run the math on that and it gets worse quickly. The U.S. auto industry spent roughly $22 billion on advertising in 2024 and 2025 combined across OEM and dealer channels.[2][3] If 43% of that messaging sells speed, that is approximately $9.5 billion per year devoted to convincing Americans that driving fast is aspirational, cinematic, and deeply tied to personal identity. NHTSA, the federal agency responsible for keeping those Americans alive afterward, spent $9.6 million on its national anti-speeding campaign.[4] The spending ratio: roughly 990 to 1.

Speeding killed 12,330 people in 2021, accounting for 29% of all traffic fatalities that year and reaching a 14-year high.[4] IIHS called it a crisis. Detroit's response has been to spend more on ads showing sedans drifting through mountain passes at twilight while a voiceover whispers about "precision engineering."

FARS data from 2014 through 2023 makes the relationship between marketing and mortality uncomfortably concrete. The Ford Mustang, arguably the most speed-marketed nameplate in American automotive history, carries a fatality rate of 6.02 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled. Sedans average 1.02; SUVs average 0.63. Six times the rate of its own class, backed by 2,739 dead across the study period.[5] The Chevrolet Camaro clocks in at 3.44. The Hyundai Veloster, marketed as an affordable entry point into the performance segment, hits 8.54, the highest rate of any vehicle in FARS with more than 500 deaths, though its smaller fleet size introduces more statistical noise.

As a vehicle class, sports cars average a fatality rate of 1.95 per 100 million VMT, against the SUV average of 0.63. Vehicles sold on speed die at triple the rate of vehicles sold on cargo space, and that is not an accident of physics, because physics does not watch Super Bowl ads.

Where this gets genuinely absurd: the same company funding both sides. General Motors published a study with the University of Michigan Transportation Research Institute in May 2026 claiming its advanced driver assistance features reduce injury crashes by 14% to 57%.[6] Noble work, backed by data from 700,000 police-reported crashes. GM also manufactures the Camaro and the Corvette. In FARS, 1,147 Corvette drivers appear in fatal crash records, with a 26.2% impairment rate to boot.[5] Ford runs advertisements for its Co-Pilot360 safety suite alongside advertisements for a Mustang that has buried 274 people per year for a decade. One division builds the guardrail; its counterpart builds the catapult.

IIHS also found, in a separate 2024 study analyzing 600,000 trips, that drivers who speed are more likely to handle their phones while doing it, with phone use climbing 12% for every 5 mph above the posted limit on freeways.[7] Speeding and distraction are not independent failure modes. They are the same driver, at the same time, in a car that was sold to them on the basis that it could outrun its own consequences.

The United States is the only major automotive market that does not regulate speed depictions in car advertising. In Brussels, ads portraying illegal speeds are restricted; Australia bans commercials showing reckless driving outright. In the U.S., a manufacturer can air a primetime ad showing a vehicle traveling 120 mph through a residential neighborhood, and the only required disclaimer is a line of eight-point text reading "professional driver, closed course."[1]

To be clear about what the data does not show: it does not prove that car ads cause speeding deaths. People who purchase Mustangs are self-selected risk-takers whose demographic profile, age distribution, and mileage patterns would inflate their death rate regardless of what they saw on television. Plenty of those 12,330 speeding fatalities were Camry and F-150 drivers who never watched a single performance ad. Correlation is not a body bag, even when the bodies are real.

But here is what the data does show, and what IIHS is too polite to say this bluntly: an industry that spends a thousand dollars promoting speed for every single dollar the government spends fighting it has no standing to express surprise when speed kills 12,000 people a year. If you are shopping for a vehicle and a manufacturer's marketing strategy makes you feel something about velocity, check the FARS death rate on that model before you sign the lease. The Mustang's 6.02 is not an abstraction. It is 2,739 funerals.
