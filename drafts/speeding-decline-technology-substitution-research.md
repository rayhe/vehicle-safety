# Research: Speeding Deaths Dropped 11% Without Speed Limit Changes

## Primary Sources

### 1. NHTSA DOT HS 813 829 (July 2026)
- **Title:** Early Estimates of Motor Vehicle Traffic Fatalities and Fatality Rate by Sub-Categories in 2025
- **Key finding:** Speeding-related fatalities decreased **11%** from 2024 to 2025
- **Context:** Overall fatalities dropped 6.7% (39,254 → 36,640), so speeding decline outpaced the average
- **Other sub-categories:**
  - Newer vehicles (<10 years): deaths down **12%**
  - Older vehicles (≥10 years): deaths down **9%**
  - Rollover crashes: down **11%**
  - Ejections: down **13%**
  - Unrestrained occupants: down **13%**
  - Urban arterials: down **12%**
  - 25-34 age group: down **13%** (largest age decline)
  - 65+: **0%** change (no improvement)
  - Rural interstates: **UP 1%** (only category that went up)
- **Source URL:** https://crashstats.nhtsa.dot.gov/Api/Public/ViewPublication/813829

### 2. IIHS 30th Anniversary Study (June 24, 2026)
- IIHS crashworthiness tests have saved ~50,000 lives since 1995
- Translates to ~$500 billion in societal cost savings
- 1996 vs 2026 Blazer head-to-head crash test: 1996 driver would have suffered serious/fatal injuries; 2026 driver walks away
- Source URL: http://www.iihs.org/news/detail/iihs-crashworthiness-tests-save-nearly-50-000-lives-since-programs-launch

### 3. NHTSA Q1 2026 Early Estimate
- Q1 2026: 7,770 deaths, down 4.3% from Q1 2025
- Fatality rate: 0.99 per 100M VMT — lowest quarterly rate since 2014
- Source: Carscoops / Reuters reporting, July 2026

### 4. FARS Data (2014-2023) — from fars_output.js
- **Sports cars (high-speed deaths):**
  - Mustang: rate 6.02, 2,739 deaths
  - Veloster: rate 8.54, 598 deaths
  - Camaro: rate 3.44, 1,204 deaths
  - Corvette: rate 1.52, 320 deaths (much lower — engineering?)
- **Modern SUVs (low death rates despite higher speeds):**
  - Model Y: rate 0.03 (!!)
  - Model 3: rate 0.05
  - RAV4: rate 0.19
  - Equinox: rate 0.36
  - Rogue: rate 0.35
- **The gap:** Mustang rate 6.02 vs RAV4 rate 0.19 = 31.7x difference
- **Corvette anomaly:** Sports car, but rate 1.52 — less than half the Camaro (3.44). The Corvette has better crash structure, more engineering investment per unit.

### 5. Speed Limit Context
- No federal speed limit changes in 2024-2025
- Several states RAISED limits (e.g., some rural roads from 70 to 75 mph)
- ISA (Intelligent Speed Assistance) NOT mandated in the US (EU mandated July 2024)
- NHTSA's proposed pedestrian protection FMVSS still in comment period

## Original Contribution / Novel Analysis

**Thesis:** The 11% decline in speeding deaths is NOT driven by behavioral change or regulatory enforcement. It's driven by vehicle technology: AEB, ESC, improved crash structures, side curtain airbags, and seatbelt pretensioners. The vehicles are absorbing crashes that would have been fatal 10 years ago.

**Evidence:**
1. Speed limits didn't decrease → behavioral explanation weak
2. Newer vehicles (<10yr) improved 12% vs older (≥10yr) improved 9% → 3pp gap = technology effect
3. IIHS credits crashworthiness for 50,000 lives since 1995 → structural improvements ongoing
4. FARS shows massive death rate gaps between old and new platforms in the same class
5. Corvette (1.52) vs Camaro (3.44) = same class, huge engineering gap

**Strongest Counterargument:**
Maybe people ARE speeding less? Possible factors:
- Pandemic driving patterns finally normalized
- Gas prices → less aggressive driving
- WFH → fewer rush-hour commutes, lower average speeds
- Increased camera/enforcement in some cities

**Rebuttal:** 
- VMT increased in 2025 (per NHTSA rate calculation), so people drove MORE not less
- Rural interstates (high-speed roads) saw deaths GO UP 1% — speeding didn't decrease there
- If behavior changed, ALL road types should improve; the rural interstate increase contradicts this
- The 3pp newer-vs-older vehicle gap specifically isolates the technology effect

**Limitations:**
- FARS 2014-2023 data predates the 2025 data by 2 years → can show structural patterns but not the 2025 decline directly
- Speeding-related classification in FARS has reporting lag and methodology changes
- Vehicle technology vs. road design vs. behavior cannot be fully disentangled
- Fleet age distribution effects: more newer vehicles on road = lower overall rate mechanically
