# Research: Speed Sells. Speed Kills. The $22 Billion Paradox.

## Angle
The U.S. auto industry spends ~$22B/year on advertising. IIHS found 43% of ads promote speed/performance; only 8% mention safety. Meanwhile, NHTSA spent $9.6M fighting speeding. The industry outspends the safety response ~1,000:1 on the exact behavior that kills 12,330 people per year.

Cross-tab this with FARS: vehicles marketed for speed (Mustang, Camaro, Corvette, Veloster) have death rates 3-8x the fleet average. The same companies fund ADAS safety studies, then run Super Bowl ads showing cars drifting through canyons.

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES. The IIHS advertising study is from May 2026 — fresh data. The 43%/8% split has never been cross-referenced with FARS death rates by marketed vehicle category. The 1,000:1 spending ratio is an original calculation nobody has published.

## Primary Sources (3+)

### 1. IIHS Car Advertising Study (May 2026)
- 43% of U.S. car ads focus on speed or traction
- Only 8% emphasize safety
- 29% rise in speed-related crash deaths
- U.S. is only major country that doesn't regulate car advertising for speed claims
- IIHS urges automakers to treat unsafe speed like drunk driving
- Sources: Autoblog, Streetsblog USA coverage

### 2. NHTSA FARS / Speeding Data
- Speeding-related fatalities in 2021: 12,330 (29% of all traffic fatalities)
- Speeding was at a 14-year high in 2021
- NHTSA launched a $9.6M anti-speeding campaign (2022)
- Source: nhtsa.gov Speeding and Speed Management page

### 3. FARS Vehicle Data (our dataset)
- Sports Car class avg death rate: 1.95 per 100M VMT (highest class)
- Sedan average: 1.02, SUV average: 0.63
- Ford Mustang: rate 6.02, 2,739 deaths — the most marketed-for-speed car in America
- Chevrolet Camaro: rate 3.44, 1,204 deaths
- Hyundai Veloster: rate 8.54, 598 deaths
- Chevrolet Corvette: 1,147 drivers in fatal crashes, 26.2% impairment rate

### 4. Auto Industry Advertising Spending
- U.S. OEM ad spending: $12B in 2024 (Statista)
- Dealer ad spending: ~$10B in 2025 (DealershipGuy)
- Total: ~$22B
- Toyota alone: $661M, Chevrolet: $603M, Ford: $315M
- Automakers spent $2.1B on linear TV alone in 2025

### 5. IIHS Speeding + Phone Use Correlation (2024)
- 600,000 trips analyzed
- Phone handling rises 12% per 5 mph over speed limit on freeways
- Speeders are simultaneously texting — dual risk compounding

## Novel Analysis (Original Contribution)
1. **The 1,000:1 Ratio:** Industry spends ~$9.5B marketing speed (43% of $22B). NHTSA spends $9.6M fighting speeding. Ratio: ~990:1.
2. **FARS Death Rate by Marketing Category:** Sports cars marketed for speed have avg death rate 1.95 vs sedan avg 1.02 vs SUV 0.63. The Mustang's 6.02 rate is literally 6x the sedan average, 10x the SUV average.
3. **Same-Company Paradox:** GM funds ADAS safety studies claiming 86% crash prevention, while advertising the Camaro (3.44 death rate) and Corvette (26.2% impairment). Ford advertises Mustang (6.02 death rate) while touting its Co-Pilot360 safety suite.
4. **International Regulatory Gap:** EU, Australia, and other countries regulate speed depictions in car ads. The U.S. does not. Speed-related deaths are 29% of the U.S. total; in the EU they're ~26%.

## Strongest Counterargument
The correlation between speed-focused ads and speed-related deaths doesn't prove causation. People who buy sports cars are self-selected risk-takers — the ads may be reflecting demand rather than creating it. And sports car buyers are a small fraction of total fatalities; the 12,330 speeding deaths include plenty of Camry and F-150 drivers.

## Limitations
- FARS sports car death rates include demographics/age effects (younger drivers buy sports cars and crash more regardless of advertising)
- The 43% figure covers all speed/traction imagery, not just dangerous speeds — a snow-driving ad showing traction control is counted alongside a canyon-drifting ad
- $22B total ad spend is a rough sum of different data sources from different years (2024-2025)
- We cannot isolate the causal pathway: ad → purchase → speeding → death
