Nissan Has 14,456 Bodies and Nobody Noticed

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
March 28, 2026

Fourteen thousand, four hundred and fifty-six people died in Nissan vehicles between 2014 and 2023.[1] That body count exceeds Hyundai, BMW, and Volkswagen combined. You probably haven't read a single headline about it.

13 of 19
Nissan models rank above their vehicle class median for death rate

We've covered individual Nissan models before on this site. The Altima's rental-fleet carnage. The Maxima's physics-defying death rate. The Sentra's entry-level body count. But isolated, those stories look like bad luck spread across a big lineup. They're not. Run the numbers across all 19 Nissan models in FARS and a pattern emerges that should terrify anyone walking into a Nissan showroom: 68% of their lineup ranks above the class median death rate. In a randomly distributed world, you'd expect 50%. Nissan beats that by 18 percentage points.

Start with the sedans. The Maxima carries a death rate of 5.11 per 100 million VMT, which makes it the single deadliest sedan in the entire FARS dataset. Not percentile. Rank one. Dead last. Class median? 0.81.[1] That's a 6.3x multiplier for choosing the wrong Nissan over a median competitor. Altima sits at 2.88, a comfortable 95th percentile. Sentra: 2.13, 86th percentile. Three sedans, all in the worst quartile of their class, collectively responsible for 9,624 deaths.

The Methodology

For each of Nissan's 19 models in FARS, we computed the death rate (fatalities per 100 million vehicle-miles traveled), then ranked it against every other model in the same vehicle class (Sedan, SUV, Pickup, Sports Car, Van). Percentile = rank ÷ total models in class. A 50th-percentile vehicle is exactly average. Higher is deadlier.

The calculation: rate = deaths ÷ (estimated_fleet × annual_vmt_per_vehicle × 10_years) × 100,000,000. Fleet size comes from annual sales data; VMT from the National Household Travel Survey.[2] Uncertainty is roughly ±15% for low-volume models, tighter for high-volume sellers like the Altima and Rogue.

Beyond Sedans

SUVs aren't safe harbor. Xterra ranks at the 87th percentile among all SUVs, Pathfinder at 78th, Kicks at 73rd. Kicks is an especially grim case: 339 deaths, 459 fatal crashes, and a 73.9% lethality ratio. That means nearly three-quarters of its fatal crashes kill someone. For context, Toyota's Highlander has a lethality of 45.4%. Honda's Pilot: 45.3%.[1] You buy a Kicks thinking you're getting SUV safety. You're getting 2002 Cavalier survivability in a crossover wrapper.

Frontier sits at the 76th percentile among pickups. Even Nissan's legacy truck, simply called "Pickup" in FARS, hits the 80th percentile.

Bright spots exist. Nissan's Rogue, their volume crossover, ranks at the 35th percentile, solidly below-average in death rate. Titan pickup: 32nd percentile. Commercial vans (NV, NV200) are fine. These prove Nissan can engineer safe vehicles when the commercial stakes demand it. Rogue is their best-seller. Apparently, you get what you pay attention to.

The Counterargument, at Full Strength

Nissan's buyer demographic skews younger, lower-income, and disproportionately urban. These are independently high-risk populations for fatal crashes regardless of what they drive. Altima is a fleet and rental staple, meaning it accumulates miles from unfamiliar, high-turnover drivers who inflate its exposure. Maxima attracted a performance-oriented buyer who drove it harder than a Camry owner. Demographic sorting, not engineering failure, could explain most of this spread.

That argument has merit. But it doesn't explain the Kicks. It doesn't explain the Pathfinder. It doesn't explain why 13 of 19 models, spanning every class Nissan competes in, all tilt the same direction. Demographic effects should produce some models below median in each class. Nissan's sedans dominate the worst quartile while Toyota, selling to overlapping demographics with the Corolla and Camry, places those models at the 63rd and 66th percentile respectively. Same buyers. Different outcomes.

What This Doesn't Prove

FARS captures fatal crashes only. Nissan might outperform in non-fatal crash survivability or injury severity, and we'd never see it in this data. Fleet and VMT estimates introduce ±15% uncertainty on rate calculations, and for smaller-volume models like the 370Z (51 deaths), the confidence interval is wide enough to drive a Titan through. We also can't isolate build quality from infrastructure effects: if Nissan owners disproportionately drive on rural two-lanes, that's a road problem, not a car problem.

IIHS's 2025 tests offered a pointed footnote: the redesigned Kicks earned a "Poor" rating in the moderate overlap front test before Nissan scrambled to fix it for 2026.[3] The brand is projecting a $4.2 billion loss this fiscal year.[4] A company bleeding money that fast doesn't typically invest more in occupant protection.

Fourteen thousand four hundred fifty-six people. One brand. Nobody called it systemic. Consider it called.
