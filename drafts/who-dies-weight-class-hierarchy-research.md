# Research: Who Dies in America's Weight-Class Hierarchy

## Angle
FARS data reveals a metric nobody reports: the occupant-death-per-fatal-crash ratio. For every fatal crash a vehicle is involved in, what percentage of the time does its OWN occupant die? This ratio exposes the weight-class hierarchy of American roads — big trucks survive their crashes by transferring lethal force to everyone else.

## Novel Contribution
Cross-tabulating FARS deaths vs crashes per model to compute "externalized lethality" — the percentage of fatal crashes where someone OTHER than the vehicle's occupant dies. This isn't just a weight story; it's a quantified, model-by-model hierarchy.

## Key Data Points (FARS 2014-2023, vehicles with 500+ fatal crashes)

### Lowest occupant death ratio (other people die):
- Ram 2500: 0.205 — 80% of its fatal crashes kill someone ELSE
- Ram 1500: 0.341
- Ford F-250: 0.364
- Toyota Tundra: 0.415
- Dodge Ram: 0.436
- Ford F-150: 0.458

### Highest occupant death ratio (own occupants die):
- Chevrolet Cavalier: 0.857
- Dodge Neon: 0.856
- Buick LeSabre: 0.823
- Chevrolet Cobalt: 0.808
- Chevrolet HHR: 0.801

### Class averages:
- Pickups: 0.485 (51.5% of fatal crashes kill someone other than the pickup occupant)
- Vans: 0.511
- SUVs: 0.526
- Sedans: 0.644
- Sports Cars: 0.672

### The gap:
- Ram 2500 (0.205) vs Cavalier (0.857) = 4.2x difference
- In absolute terms: pickups involved in 82,290 fatal crashes, only 39,897 pickup occupants died
- ~42,393 deaths were in OTHER vehicles/pedestrians (4,239/year)

## External Sources

### IIHS Study (Feb 2025) — "Supersizing vehicles offers minimal safety benefits"
- Above 4,000 lbs: every +500 lbs reduces own driver death rate by 1, increases crash-partner death rate by 7
- Below 4,000 lbs: every +500 lbs reduces own driver death rate by 17, increases partner rate by only 1
- Pickups nearly 2x as likely as cars to result in car-partner fatalities (2017-22)
- Quote: "Choosing an extra-heavy vehicle doesn't make you any safer, but it makes you a bigger danger to other people." — Sam Monfort, IIHS senior statistician
- Source: https://www.iihs.org/news/detail/supersizing-vehicles-offers-minimal-safety-benefits--but-substantial-dangers

### IIHS vehicle size/weight topic page
- Source: https://www.iihs.org/topics/vehicle-size-and-weight

### PubMed — Mass ratio and relative driver fatality risk
- A 1% lighter vehicle increases fatality risk by 2.7-4.3%
- Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/ (mass ratio study)

### NBER — Vehicle Weight and Automotive Fatalities
- Being hit by a 1,000-pound heavier vehicle increases fatality risk by ~1 in 1,000
- $93 billion annual external costs from vehicle weight
- Source: https://www.nber.org/papers/w17170

### FARS database
- Source: https://www.nhtsa.gov/research-data/fatality-analysis-reporting-system-fars

## Self-Critique Gate
**Angle:** FARS occupant-death-per-crash ratio reveals that pickups externalize over half their fatal crash deaths to other road users. The Ram 2500 is 4.2x more likely to kill someone else than its own driver.

**Challenge:** We've done pickup-body-count, pickup-size-death-inversion, and weight-plateau stories. Is this genuinely new? YES — those stories looked at total deaths and per-VMT rates. This specific metric (deaths/crashes ratio as a proxy for externalized lethality) has never been computed on our site. It reveals WHO dies, not just HOW MANY die. That's a fundamentally different question.

**Verdict:** PROCEED. Strong novel contribution, multiple external sources, actionable consumer angle.
