Sobriety Report

DUI Violations Among 66-to-90-Year-Olds Are Up 45%. Nobody Is Checking.

Dale Impactor III • Clautomotive Desk
June 7, 2026

Every sobriety checkpoint in America is designed to catch someone under 35. The platoon of neon cones, the 2 a.m. deployment window on the highway outside the college-town bar district, the public awareness campaigns with stock photos of twenty-somethings clutching car keys next to empty bottles. Meanwhile, DUI violations among Americans aged 66 to 90 have increased 44.8% since 2019, the largest surge of any age group in the country.[1] The enforcement apparatus is facing south while the problem migrated north.

44.8%
Increase in DUI violations among ages 66–90 since 2019 — the largest of any age group

That figure comes from The $1.77 Trillion Wreck, a data report released June 5, 2026, by Avian Law Group, aggregating NHTSA, NASS, and state court records.[1] For context: the 36-to-45 age group saw a 27.4% increase over the same period. Drivers 66 to 90 nearly doubled that rate. And unlike the post-pandemic speeding surge (major violations up 55.5% since 2019 despite only 1.2% more miles on the road), nobody noticed the elderly DUI spike because it doesn't fit the mental model of what a drunk driver looks like.

Our FARS toxicology data suggests this isn't new behavior finding new participants. It's existing behavior finding more of them. Cross-tabulating NHTSA's Fatality Analysis Reporting System data for 2014 through 2023 against vehicle brands that skew heavily toward buyers 65 and older, a pattern emerges that should have raised alarms a decade ago: the "grandparent fleet" already has some of the highest impairment rates in fatal crashes.

The Buick Park Avenue leads the entire FARS toxicology dataset. Not the Camaro. Not the Mustang. The Park Avenue, a car sold almost exclusively to retirees, posts a 31.7% impairment rate in fatal crashes across 259 tested drivers.[2] The Buick LeSabre sits at 23.5% across 1,209 drivers. Cadillac CTS: 25.9% across 931. Lincoln Continental: 22.2%. The overall dataset average is roughly 20%. Sports cars, the supposed impairment villain, average 22.5% as a class. Buick, the brand whose marketing budget targets AARP members, averages higher.

A study published in Traffic Injury Prevention confirmed the mechanism. Researchers at the University of Memphis analyzed nine years of FARS data covering 87,060 drivers in two-vehicle fatal crashes and found that more than one-third of drivers over 70 who crashed tested positive for substances. Impaired older drivers were two to four times likelier to be at-fault than sober ones.[3] That multiplier is not subtle. It means an impaired 75-year-old behind the wheel of a Buick Encore is carrying roughly the same actuarial risk as a drunk 22-year-old in a Camaro, except nobody is running checkpoints outside the Olive Garden at 6:30 p.m.

The body count supports the math. The National Safety Council reports that 9,727 Americans 65 and older died in motor vehicle crashes in 2024, up from 9,587 the year prior and up 33% over the last decade.[4] The 65-plus population grew 28% over that same window. Deaths outpaced demographic growth by five percentage points, which means the per-capita death rate for older drivers is climbing, not holding. Brown University's geriatric safety research quantifies the age curve: per-mile crash involvement for drivers 70 to 79 matches that of teenagers, and for drivers 80 and older, it is four times the expected baseline.[5]

Why the post-pandemic acceleration? The Avian Law Group report doesn't speculate, so we will. COVID-era isolation drove a well-documented surge in alcohol and substance use among older adults, a population already vulnerable to medication interactions and declining metabolic tolerance. What started as quarantine coping became routine. The roads emptied during lockdowns and then refilled with drivers whose habits had changed. Younger demographics got busted faster because enforcement was waiting for them. Nobody was waiting for the 72-year-old on prescription painkillers driving home from book club.

The structural invisibility goes deeper than demographics. Sobriety checkpoints are overwhelmingly deployed between 10 p.m. and 3 a.m. Elderly impaired driving peaks earlier. Ignition interlock mandates, where they exist, typically target repeat offenders; first-offense DUI adjudication for a 70-year-old driver often results in a suspended sentence and a suggestion to "be more careful." The vehicles themselves provide cover, too. A Buick Encore weaving gently across a lane line at 35 mph on a residential street does not trigger the same law enforcement response as a Dodge Charger doing 85 in a 55. One gets pulled over. The other gets a concerned look from the neighbor.

What this means for you

If you have a parent or grandparent driving regularly, their physician visit should include a medication-driving interaction review. Polypharmacy is the norm for Americans over 65: the average is four to five concurrent prescriptions, many of which carry drowsiness or impairment warnings that go unread. Ask the pharmacist directly whether the drug combination is safe for driving. Don't assume sobriety means safety. And if you're buying a car for an older family member, check the NHTSA recall database at nhtsa.gov/recalls and verify the vehicle has current-generation automatic emergency braking. AEB won't fix impairment, but it can interrupt the moment between a delayed reaction and a fatality.

Limitations

FARS captures fatal crashes only, not the full impaired-driving landscape. Our vehicle-level impairment data can't be mapped directly to driver age because a 30-year-old can drive a Buick. The 44.8% DUI violation increase could partly reflect increased enforcement scrutiny of elderly drivers rather than increased impaired driving. And the Avian Law Group report, while sourcing NHTSA and state court data, originates from a personal injury law firm. Cross-reference accordingly.

Strongest counterargument

The per-capita death rate increase for drivers 65 and older is modest: 3.5% over a decade. That could be noise in a demographic shift, not a behavioral crisis. And total U.S. traffic fatalities are declining. The February 2026 mileage death rate hit 1.01 per 100 million VMT, down from 1.32 three years earlier.[6] Maybe the system is working. Maybe vehicle technology is absorbing the behavioral deterioration without anyone noticing. If so, the right response is not more checkpoints but faster fleet turnover, so the cars that protect impaired drivers from killing themselves and others replace the ones that don't.
