Five Dead on I-95. The Safety System Saw It Coming.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
June 1, 2026

At 2:35 a.m. on May 30, a charter bus operated by E&P Travel Inc. barreled into stopped traffic on I-95 southbound in Stafford County, Virginia, near a work zone where vehicles had slowed to a crawl. It struck a Chevrolet Suburban, which was shoved into an Acura that caught fire. Five people died, including a family of four traveling to a wedding in South Carolina. An NTSB investigator who examined the scene said of the bus driver's braking effort: "If there was any braking, there wasn't much."[1]

50%
E&P Travel's inspection failure rate — violations in 3 of 6 FMCSA inspections

Dmitri Doncev, 45, and his wife Ecaterina, 44, died alongside their children Emily, 13, and Mark, 7, when their Acura was crushed and ignited. Priscilla Mafalda, 25, of Worcester, Massachusetts, was in the Suburban directly ahead of the bus. She did not survive either. Forty-four others were hospitalized, three in critical condition.[2]

The driver, Jing S. Dong, 48, has been charged with two counts of involuntary manslaughter, with additional charges pending. He is a naturalized U.S. citizen who obtained his commercial driver's license in New York in 2024. Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy said Dong does not speak English, a direct violation of 49 CFR §391.11(b)(2), which requires commercial motor vehicle drivers to read and speak English well enough to understand highway traffic signs, respond to official inquiries, and converse with the general public.[3]

That federal regulation is not a suggestion, not a best practice, not aspirational guidance but a condition of licensure, and it exists for a reason that became obvious at 2:35 in the morning on a Virginia highway: if you cannot read the signs that say WORK ZONE AHEAD, you cannot react to them.

E&P Travel Inc., based in Kings Mountain, North Carolina, operates four motorcoaches with eleven registered drivers. Department of Transportation records show the company received safety violations in three of its six roadside inspections, a 50% failure rate. Three of its violations were speed-related. And on September 9, 2025, a driver employed by E&P Travel was specifically cited for failing to satisfy the English language proficiency requirements of the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration.[4]

Eight months passed between that citation and the crash that killed five, during which the carrier kept operating, kept putting drivers on I-95 at two in the morning, kept hauling passengers between New York and the Carolinas. FMCSA's Safety Measurement System tracked every violation, logged every inspection, assessed E&P Travel across seven Behavioral Analysis and Safety Improvement Categories. All of that data existed, and the system worked exactly as designed, which is the most damning sentence in this article.

And the system's response to a motorcoach carrier with a 50% inspection failure rate and a driver fitness violation for the exact failure mode that would later kill five people was nothing that prevented those deaths from occurring.

63,715
Motor carriers currently exceeding FMCSA intervention thresholds in at least one safety category

Scale makes this worse, not better. FMCSA tracks 2,075,020 motor carriers with USDOT numbers, of which 1,256,718 show recent activity. Of those active carriers, 63,715 currently exceed intervention thresholds in at least one BASIC category: 26,018 for vehicle maintenance, 23,109 for hours-of-service compliance, 15,760 for unsafe driving, 7,303 for driver fitness.[5] Being above the threshold means FMCSA "may prioritize the motor carrier for further monitoring," language so tentative it could have been drafted by a liability attorney rather than a safety regulator.

Meanwhile, 5,340 people died in large-truck-and-bus crashes in 2024, according to the National Safety Council. Seventy percent of those fatalities were occupants of other vehicles, people like the Doncevs, whose only involvement was being in the wrong lane when a bus failed to stop.[6] The number of interstate passenger carriers has been declining steadily since 2017, from 12,771 to roughly 10,268 by 2021, which means the surviving carriers are carrying more passengers per bus mile driven, concentrating more human lives behind each FMCSA-monitored USDOT number.[7]

The strongest counterargument to this indictment is resource reality: FMCSA cannot shut down every carrier that accumulates violations, because most violations are minor, most carriers are small, and the agency monitors two million entities with finite staff and budget. A 50% inspection failure rate drawn from six total inspections is a sample size that would embarrass a freshman statistics course. Speed violations and inoperative lamps do not predict mass-casualty events with any precision, and pre-emptive carrier shutdowns based on incomplete data would decimate an industry that moves 124 million motorcoach passenger trips annually.

All of that is true, and none of it matters to the Doncev family, who are buried instead of at a wedding. An English proficiency violation is not a broken taillight, is not a speed-related ticket, is not statistical noise; it is a categorical statement that a driver employed by this carrier could not satisfy the basic cognitive prerequisite for operating a commercial vehicle on American roads. When that specific failure appears in a carrier's record, and eight months later the same carrier deploys a driver who allegedly cannot speak English into a work zone at 2:35 a.m., the enforcement gap is not a data gap but a decision gap, and the question nobody at FMCSA appears prepared to answer is what, precisely, all that data is for.

What You Can Do

Before booking any charter or intercity bus, look up the carrier at FMCSA's SaferBus tool. Check BASIC scores, inspection history, and out-of-service rates. If a carrier shows above-threshold alerts in Driver Fitness or Unsafe Driving categories, choose a different operator. Your ticket price does not include adequate odds of arriving alive.
