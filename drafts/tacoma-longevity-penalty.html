The Toyota Tacoma's Best Feature Is Killing People

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
March 30, 2026

A 2005 Toyota Tacoma will run for 300,000 miles, hold 78% of its value after five years, and show up in FARS data with 1,731 fatalities from model years that predate the electronic stability control mandate.[1] That is 76.2% of all Tacoma deaths in the decade-long dataset. Three out of four people who die in a Tacoma die in one old enough to vote.

Toyota built the Tacoma to last. Buyers love that. Actuaries should not. The average Tacoma involved in a fatal crash during 2014–2023 was a model year 2006 — meaning the typical death truck was 12.4 years old at impact.[1] Compare that to the Chevrolet Colorado, the segment's safety darling, where the average fatal-crash model year is 2011 and the average age is just 7.2 years.

This is not an abstract fleet-age problem. It is a Tacoma-specific paradox. The truck retains value better than anything else in its class — 78% after five years, per CarEdge data[2] — which means a 2005 Tacoma still commands enough money to stay registered, insured, and driven daily instead of being crushed into a cube at a salvage yard. A 2005 Ford Ranger, by contrast, was worth so little by 2015 that many exited the fleet through attrition. Ford's inferior reliability became, accidentally, a safety feature.

The ESC Divide

Electronic stability control became mandatory for all passenger vehicles in model year 2012, following NHTSA's 2007 final rule.[3] IIHS estimates ESC prevents 33% of all fatal crashes and 49% of fatal single-vehicle crashes, with an even larger 73% reduction in single-vehicle rollover fatalities.[4]

Rollovers are particularly relevant to midsize trucks. The Tacoma's pre-2012 models had a higher center of gravity, body-on-frame construction with a narrower track width, and no electronic intervention to catch a slide before it became a barrel roll. Some 2009–2011 Tacomas offered ESC as an option, but the data doesn't suggest it was popular enough to move the needle. The death curve peaks at model years 2003–2006, accounting for 608 fatalities from just four model years.[1]

The Longevity Penalty

Apply the IIHS's conservative 33% overall ESC effectiveness figure to the 1,731 pre-2012 Tacoma deaths, and you get an estimated 571 preventable fatalities over the decade — roughly 57 per year. That calculation carries real uncertainty (ESC effectiveness varies by crash type, and newer trucks also have better structures and more airbags), but the direction is unambiguous: old Tacomas without stability control are dying at rates that ESC-equipped trucks do not.

The midsize truck segment makes this painfully clear:

  • Ford Ranger: 2.91 deaths per 100M VMT — but 98.3% of its fatalities come from pre-2012 models. Ford discontinued the Ranger from 2012–2019, so almost every Ranger on the road was ancient. Average age at fatal crash: 18.2 years.
  • Toyota Tacoma: 0.80 deaths per 100M VMT. 76.2% pre-2012. Continuously produced, but buyers keep choosing old ones. Average age: 12.4 years.
  • Nissan Frontier: 1.45 deaths per 100M VMT. 67.4% pre-2012. The second-gen ran nearly unchanged from 2005–2021. Average age: 10.7 years.
  • Chevrolet Colorado: 0.28 deaths per 100M VMT. 58.2% pre-2012. The current generation launched 2015 with modern safety tech from day one. Average age: 7.2 years.

The ranking of "safest midsize truck" is, functionally, a ranking of which truck has the youngest fleet. Colorado wins because it barely existed before 2015. The Ranger loses because it was absent for seven years, ensuring only geriatric models remained.

The Uncomfortable Math

The Tacoma occupies a unique and uncomfortable position. It was continuously available throughout the FARS period, with a modern third-generation model on sale from 2016 onward. Buyers had access to a Tacoma with Toyota Safety Sense, pre-collision braking, lane departure alert. Many chose a 15-year-old one instead, because a 2006 Tacoma TRD Off-Road with 140,000 miles still fetches $15,000 and still starts every morning.

That is, in isolation, remarkable engineering. In aggregate, it is 2,274 deaths.

Strongest Counterargument

Toyota did not cause these deaths by building reliable trucks. Every old vehicle lacks newer safety features. The blame, if any, belongs to the absence of periodic safety inspections in most U.S. states, insurance pricing that does not penalize driving vehicles without ESC, and a federal regulatory framework that mandates safety tech for new vehicles but has no mechanism to retire or retrofit the old ones. If every 2005 Tacoma vanished tomorrow, many of those buyers would simply purchase another old truck without ESC. The demand for cheap trucks does not disappear because you make Toyota feel bad about quality.

Limitations

FARS captures only fatal crashes. A vehicle with low fatalities might still have high injury-crash rates. The VMT-based death rate uses estimated miles traveled, not odometer data, introducing ±15% uncertainty for individual models. Fleet size estimates come from registration data that may double-count or under-count vehicles near the end of their service life. The "longevity penalty" calculation assumes ESC effectiveness would apply uniformly to the Tacoma population, but the actual reduction depends on crash types, road surfaces, and driver demographics specific to Tacoma owners. Finally, some 2009–2011 model year Tacomas did have ESC as optional equipment, making the pre-2012 cutoff imprecise.
