# Research: IIHS Heavy Truck First Scorecard

## Slug: iihs-heavy-truck-first-scorecard
## Journalist: Axle McScatter (Data Visualization Editor)
## Kicker: By The Numbers
## Article Number: 344

## Angle
IIHS has released its FIRST-EVER safety evaluations for heavy-duty trucks and commercial vehicles — and more than half failed. They didn't even need to crash them; just checking whether basic safety features EXIST was enough to fail 5 out of 9 vehicles.

This is a direct follow-up to Crash Report #340 ("Nobody Crash-Tests America's Work Trucks. That's 6,535 Funerals a Year." by Rex Driverton).

## Kill Test
- **Genuinely newsworthy?** YES — first-ever IIHS ratings for commercial vehicles in the org's 30-year history
- **Novel angle?** YES — connecting IIHS results to our existing FARS data on work truck fatalities
- **Surprising?** YES — 5 of 9 failed a CHECKLIST (not even a crash test). The bar was "do you have airbags and working seatbelts?" and 5 vehicles couldn't clear it.

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: IIHS Announcement (March 2026, results June 2026)
- First-ever safety evaluations for Class 3 trucks (GVWR 10,001-14,000 lbs) and cargo vans
- IIHS strategic plan: accelerating commercial vehicle safety is one of three pillars
- Phase 1: engineering/documentation checklist, NOT crash testing
- Future: headlight performance, AEB evaluations; expand to Class 4-6 vehicles

### Source 2: IIHS First Results (June 17-18, 2026)
- 9 vehicles tested: 3 Class 3 pickups + 6 cargo vans
- 5 criteria checked:
  1. Front airbags
  2. Side airbags
  3. Seatbelt pretensioners
  4. Seatbelt force limiters
  5. Seatbelt reminders (IIHS standard: 90 seconds loud tone + visible light; federal standard: only 4-8 seconds)

**PASSED all 5:**
- 2026 Chevrolet Silverado 3500HD
- 2025 Ford F-350 SuperCrew
- Chevrolet BrightDrop 400 (cargo van)
- Ram ProMaster 2500 (cargo van)

**FAILED (seatbelt reminder):**
- Ford T250 Transit (cargo van) — no persistent seatbelt alert
- Mercedes-Benz Sprinter 2500 (cargo van) — no persistent seatbelt alert
- Rivian Delivery 500 (cargo van) — no persistent seatbelt alert
- Ram 3500 (pickup) — seatbelt reminder failed volume/persistence criteria

**WORST:**
- 2025 Chevrolet Express 2500 — FAILED TWO criteria: no seatbelt reminder AND no driver-side force limiters. The Express omitted force limiters entirely. This is a vehicle that has been in production since 1996 with minimal changes.

### Source 3: NHTSA/FARS Fatal Crash Data
- 6,535 people killed in crashes involving medium/heavy-duty trucks and light vans in 2023
- This represents 16% of all US roadway fatalities
- This figure has DOUBLED over the past 7 years
- 160,000+ injuries in similar crashes during same period
- Our FARS data: Ford TRANSIT has 178 deaths, rate 0.14 per 100M VMT

### Source 4: IIHS Seatbelt Reminder Research
- IIHS data: persistent (90+ second) seatbelt reminders increase overall seatbelt usage by 30%
- Federal standard only requires 4-8 second chime — effectively useless for delivery/trade workers who hop in and out frequently
- The 90-second IIHS standard matters because these are work vehicles with frequent entry/exit

## Novel Contribution
Cross-reference IIHS's new scorecard results against our FARS fatality data for the same vehicle models. The Ford Transit has 178 deaths in FARS — and it just failed the seatbelt reminder check. The Chevy Express has been essentially unchanged since 1996. What fraction of those 6,535 annual deaths are in vehicles that just FAILED the most basic safety checklist?

## Original Calculation
The Chevy Express is the worst offender: no force limiters, no persistent seatbelt reminders. It's been in production for 30 years. If IIHS data shows persistent reminders increase belt usage by 30%, and unbelted fatality rate in work vehicles is roughly 3x belted rate, we can estimate how many deaths could have been prevented by just adding a 90-second beep.

## Strongest Counterargument
Commercial vehicles serve different use cases than passenger cars. Delivery drivers making 200+ stops per day would find a 90-second seatbelt reminder infuriating. The federal standard of 4-8 seconds exists partly because extended chimes can be so annoying that drivers disconnect them entirely. Force limiters add cost per vehicle, and commercial fleets buy on price.

## Limitations
- IIHS evaluated only 9 vehicles in the first wave — a tiny sample of the commercial fleet
- These are equipment checks, NOT crash tests — a vehicle could pass all 5 criteria and still fold in a crash
- FARS data covers all crash types, not just ones where seatbelts/airbags would matter (e.g., rollovers, fires)
- The "doubled in 7 years" stat for truck fatalities partly reflects increased vehicle miles traveled, not just increased danger
