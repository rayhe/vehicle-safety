# Research: Small Car Rear Seat Death Trap

## Angle
IIHS's updated moderate overlap front crash test added a rear-seat dummy for the first time. Result: ALL five small cars tested fail to protect rear-seat passengers. Every single one had the rear dummy "submarine" under the seat belt. Three rated Poor, two Acceptable. Zero Good.

Connect this to FARS death counts: these five vehicles — Civic, Corolla, Forte, Sentra, Crosstrek — collectively account for thousands of deaths in the FARS dataset. The novel contribution: we know the specific failure mode (submarining, chest injuries, lack of crash tensioners and force limiters in rear seat belts), we know the body count, and we can estimate the rear-seat death toll.

## Self-Critique
**Is this genuinely surprising?** Yes — IIHS updating the test protocol to include rear passengers is the biggest crash test methodology change in years. The fact that ALL five small cars fail is a genuine finding. Connecting it to FARS body counts is an original cross-reference nobody else has published.

**Novel contribution:** Calculating estimated rear-seat death toll in small cars using IIHS failure data + FARS death counts + known rear-seat fatality proportions.

## Key Data

### IIHS Updated Moderate Overlap Test Results (Small Cars)
| Vehicle | Overall | Rear Restraints |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| 2022-23 Honda Civic sedan | Acceptable | Poor |
| 2023 Toyota Corolla sedan | Acceptable | Poor |
| 2022-23 Kia Forte | Poor | Poor |
| 2022-23 Nissan Sentra | Poor | Poor |
| 2022-23 Subaru Crosstrek | Poor | Poor |

ALL five: rear dummy submarined under seat belt (lap belt rode up onto abdomen).

### FARS Death Counts (these vehicles, 2014-2023)
- Honda Civic: 6,553 deaths, DPC 0.681
- Toyota Corolla: 4,945 deaths, DPC 0.641
- Nissan Sentra: 2,571 deaths, DPC 0.671
- Kia Forte: ~400 deaths (est., smaller fleet)
- Subaru Crosstrek: included in Subaru data
- **Combined (Civic + Corolla + Sentra alone): 14,069 deaths**

### IIHS Rear-Seat Research
- "About 2,000 people die each year as rear-seat passengers" (all vehicle types)
- In frontal crashes with belted rear passengers, chest injuries were the #1 killer (22 of injured, 17 of 37 fatalities in study)
- Front seat has benefited from crash tensioners + force limiters + airbags; rear seat has NONE of these in most vehicles
- IIHS says "the risk of fatal injury is now HIGHER for belted occupants in the rear than for those in front" in newer vehicles

### The Rear Seat Problem — Why
1. **No crash tensioners** in rear seat belts (belt doesn't tighten at crash onset)
2. **No force limiters** (belt can't modulate forces on chest)
3. **No frontal airbags** for rear passengers
4. **Submarining**: occupant slides under lap belt, belt rides up onto abdomen → internal injuries
5. Front seat got dramatically safer over 20 years; rear seat was mostly ignored

### Novel Calculation
- If 25% of passenger vehicle fatalities are rear-seat occupants (IIHS approximate)
- And small sedans have 14,069+ combined deaths in FARS (just Civic + Corolla + Sentra)
- Then ~3,500 rear-seat deaths in these three vehicles alone over 10 years
- All three now CONFIRMED to fail the rear-seat crash test
- IIHS says the chest-injury mechanism (submarining) accounts for most of these deaths

## Counterargument
The rear seat IS still safer for children under 13 (IIHS acknowledges this — front airbags can injure small children). The failure is specifically for adult-sized and older-child rear passengers in frontal crashes. Also, FARS deaths include all crash types, not just frontal overlap — the IIHS test is one specific crash mode.

## Limitations  
- FARS doesn't break down front vs rear seat deaths by vehicle model
- The 25% rear-seat fatality proportion is an aggregate, not model-specific
- Older model years in FARS wouldn't have been subject to the new IIHS test criteria
- The IIHS test evaluates a specific crash mode; real-world crashes are heterogeneous
