# Research: The Luxury SUV Arms Race

## Angle
Luxury SUVs function as death-exporting armor. A novel "weapon score" metric (other-person deaths / own-occupant deaths) reveals that the entire luxury SUV class kills more people in OTHER vehicles than in their own. Small sedans are the inverse: their occupants absorb the deaths.

## Kill Test
- **Is this genuinely newsworthy?** YES. After 187 articles, we have `aggressor-vehicles` (general concept) but never specifically quantified the luxury class or created the weapon score metric. The cross-tabulation of weapon scores by vehicle class is original work.
- **Novel contribution:** The weapon score metric applied systematically across luxury SUVs vs. economy sedans reveals the zero-sum nature of the vehicle safety arms race in quantitative terms.
- **Would a reader share this?** Extremely — it challenges the core purchase decision millions of families make.

## Journalist
**Vin Wreckage** — Existential Dread Columnist. The cosmic absurdity of buying safety for yourself at the direct cost of someone else's life.

## Key Data Points

### Weapon Score = (fatal crashes − own deaths) / own deaths
- Higher = more "other people" die per own-occupant death

### The Gap
- Audi Q7 weapon score: **2.17** (for every Q7 occupant who dies, 2.17 other people die)
- Chevy Cavalier weapon score: **0.17** (for every 6 Cavalier occupants who die, 1 other person dies)
- That's a **12.8x asymmetry** between the safest-for-you and safest-for-others vehicles

### Impairment Context
- Lexus RX: 17.7% impairment (BELOW average for all vehicles ~20%)
- So these aren't drunk drivers — these are sober drivers in heavy vehicles killing lighter road users through physics alone

## Strongest Counterargument
Physics is physics: heavier vehicles protect their occupants. You can't blame a parent for buying a safer car for their kids. The problem is infrastructure design and the absence of regulation limiting vehicle mass, not individual consumer choice. IIHS research shows the SAFEST outcome is when both vehicles are large and similar-sized — the problem is the MIX, not the SUVs themselves.

## Limitations
- FARS only tracks fatal crashes. Non-fatal injury crashes (where SUV mass still matters) aren't captured.
- "Other deaths" is derived: crashes − own_deaths. Some of those "other deaths" could be pedestrians, not other vehicle occupants.
- Fleet estimates introduce uncertainty for low-volume luxury models.
- Weapon score doesn't account for crash circumstances (speed, road type, multi-vehicle vs. single-vehicle).
