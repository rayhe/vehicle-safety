By The Numbers

The U.S. Just Hit 1.0 Deaths Per 100 Million Miles. Norway Is at 0.3.

Axle McScatter • Clautomotive Desk
June 1, 2026

I ran the numbers on February 2026 and they looked, for the first time in a century of American driving, almost hopeful. The National Safety Council estimates the U.S. traffic fatality rate dropped to 1.01 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled that month, eclipsing the previous all-time annual record of 1.08 set in 2014.[1] A full year of data from 2025 confirmed the second-lowest annual rate ever recorded: 1.10, with 36,640 deaths and the twelfth consecutive quarterly decline in fatalities.[2]

Celebration seemed warranted. I opened the OECD's International Traffic Safety Data for 2023 to see where America's triumph placed us globally, fully expecting to find ourselves closing in on the pack.[3]

Dead last. Not close-to-last. Last.

Norway's fatality rate is 0.2 deaths per 100 million vehicle kilometers traveled, which converts to roughly 0.32 per 100 million miles. Sweden sits at 0.48. Denmark at 0.48. The OECD median across all reporting nations is 0.64. America's historic, record-shattering, twelve-consecutive-quarters-of-improvement rate of 1.01 is still higher than every single developed country the OECD tracks, including the Czech Republic at 1.29, which recently lost its dubious position as the OECD's worst performer to us by a comfortable margin because we weren't comparing ourselves in the first place.[3]

The Math Nobody Wants to Do

Americans drove approximately 3.33 trillion miles in 2025.[2] At a rate of 1.01, that produces roughly 33,633 deaths per year. At Norway's rate of 0.32, that same mileage would produce 10,656. The difference is 22,977 people, which is not a rounding error or a methodological quibble but rather a body count roughly equivalent to every resident of Juneau, Alaska, vanishing annually because of how we build roads, set speed limits, and enforce laws.

Read that bottom row again. If Americans drove the exact same 3.33 trillion miles but on Norwegian-standard infrastructure with Norwegian enforcement and Norwegian speed policy, approximately 26,000 fewer people would die every year. Twenty-six thousand. That is not aspirational. That is arithmetic applied to a system that already exists and has operated at scale for decades in a country with harsh winters, rural stretches, and plenty of moose.

Why the Gap Exists

The CDC's Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report examined crash deaths across 28 high-income countries and found the U.S. population-based death rate was 2.3 times the average of the other 27 nations in 2019, with the gap widening rather than closing.[4] A 2022 follow-up confirmed the distance-based rate in the U.S. (1.11 per 100M VMT) exceeded the 20-country average (0.92) by 21%, even though the U.S. had once been the safest country in the world on a per-mile basis in the 1970s.[5]

The reasons are structural, not mysterious. Speed limits: the U.S. permits 70-85 mph on rural interstates where Norway caps at 110 km/h (68 mph) and Sweden at 120 km/h (75 mph). DUI thresholds: 0.08% BAC in most U.S. states versus 0.02% in Sweden. Seatbelt usage: 92% nationally in the U.S. versus 98% in Sweden. Speed enforcement: zero states have deployed Intelligent Speed Assistance; the EU mandated it for all new vehicles in 2022. Road geometry: American stroads funneling 45 mph traffic past strip-mall crosswalks are an infrastructure category that does not exist in Scandinavian road design vocabulary.

And the fleet itself. The average new vehicle sold in the U.S. weighs 4,329 pounds. In Europe, it is closer to 3,300. Heavier vehicles protect their occupants while transferring kinetic energy to everyone else, a dynamic the IIHS has documented extensively in its vehicle-size-and-weight research.[6]

The Strongest Case Against This Comparison

Americans drive more. Not a little more. Staggeringly more. Annual VMT per capita in the U.S. runs around 9,500 miles; in Norway it is closer to 7,200. Some portion of the rate gap reflects exposure differences that per-mile metrics do not fully capture, because the marginal mile driven by a fatigued commuter at 11 p.m. on a rural two-lane is not the same risk as the marginal mile on a divided highway at noon. The U.S. has more low-quality, high-risk miles in its mix.

The comparison is also blunted by population density. Norway has 5.5 million people. The U.S. has 340 million spread across a road network that includes Alaskan gravel roads and Mississippi Delta two-lanes that the Norwegian Public Roads Administration would classify as goat paths. Scaling Norwegian policy to American geography is genuinely harder than the arithmetic implies.

These objections are real. They might account for a quarter of the gap, perhaps a third. They do not account for a factor of three.

What 1.0 Actually Means

In 1925, the U.S. rate was 18 per 100 million VMT. It dropped to 5.5 by 1966. Seatbelt laws, airbags, crumple zones, ABS, electronic stability control, and a century of engineering brought it to 1.08 in 2014.[7] Each technology shaved a fraction off, and the compounding was extraordinary.

But the curve is flattening. The improvement from 5.5 to 1.1 took 50 years and a dozen regulatory revolutions. Getting from 1.1 to 1.0 appears to require a fourth straight year of declining deaths, a global pandemic's behavioral aftereffects fading, and a favorable February. Getting from 1.0 to 0.5 will require something the U.S. has never seriously attempted: redesigning the road system itself rather than just the vehicles on it.

Limitations

February is a low-VMT month. Annualizing a single month's rate overstates likely annual performance; the full-year 2026 rate will almost certainly come in above 1.01. International rate conversions from VKT to VMT introduce small rounding differences. The OECD comparison uses 2023 data while U.S. figures are 2025-2026; however, European rates have been relatively stable over this period, making the comparison directionally valid. Fleet composition, road geometry, and driving-pattern differences between countries mean that simply adopting another nation's rate is not a policy lever but rather the outcome of dozens of simultaneous policy decisions.

What to Do With This

If you are shopping for a car, this article changes nothing about your purchase decision. If you are a state legislator, it changes quite a lot. The U.S. does not have a vehicle engineering problem. Modern American cars are extraordinarily good at not killing their occupants. The U.S. has a road-system problem, a speed-policy problem, and an enforcement problem, and those are the domains where the 26,000-person gap between us and Norway lives.

Fifteen U.S. states still do not have primary seatbelt enforcement laws.[2] Zero states have adopted 0.05% BAC thresholds, let alone the 0.02% standard common in Scandinavia. Automated speed enforcement remains politically toxic in most of the country. We celebrated touching 1.0 for one month. Norway has operated below 0.4 for a decade. The rate is not a floor. It is a scoreboard, and we are losing.
