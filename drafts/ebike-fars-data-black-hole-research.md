# Research Notes: The E-Bike FARS Data Black Hole

## Angle
In 2022, NHTSA changed how FARS classifies e-bikes: they moved from "motor vehicles/motorcycles" to "pedalcyclists." Single-vehicle e-bike crashes dropped out of FARS entirely. This happened at the exact moment e-bike injuries and deaths were exploding nationwide. The 2024 FARS report shows 1,103 pedalcyclist deaths — but nobody can tell you how many were on e-bikes vs. traditional bicycles.

The result: the federal government's most important crash database is structurally blind to the fastest-growing vehicle safety crisis in the U.S.

## Key Data

### FARS 2024 Pedalcyclist Data (from Overview of Motor Vehicle Traffic Crashes in 2024, NHTSA Publication 813791)
- Pedalcyclist deaths: 1,103 (down 6.0% from 1,173 in 2023)
- Pedalcyclist injuries: 52,887 (UP 5.8% from 49,989 in 2023)
- Deaths down + injuries up = two populations moving in opposite directions being averaged together
- Note in report: "Starting in 2022, people on motorized bicycles are classified as pedalcyclists instead of motorcyclists."
- Single-vehicle motorized bicycle crashes NO LONGER CAPTURED in FARS at all

### CPSC Micromobility Data (via Health Affairs, June 2026)
- 2017–2024: 533 micromobility deaths total
- Annual micromobility deaths: 5 (2017) → 135 (2024) — 27x increase
- E-bike deaths specifically: 0 (2017) → 97 (2024)
- ED visits: 37,300 (2017) → 149,100 (2024) — 4x increase

### NEISS / JAMA Surgery / UCSF (2024 research letter)
- E-bicycle injuries: 751 (2017) → 23,493 (2022) — 30-fold increase
- 45,586 total e-bicycle injuries 2017–2022
- Head trauma from e-bikes: 49x higher in 2022 than 2017
- Hospitalizations: 43-fold increase
- Helmet use DECREASING: 5.6% lower odds of helmet use each year
- Less than half (44%) of injured e-bicyclists wore helmets

### NEISS Fracture Data (Journal of Orthopedic Trauma, 2024)
- 22,432 e-bicycle fractures in U.S. from 2019-2023
- Fractures increased 2,371% from 2019 to 2023
- Hospital admissions from e-bike fractures increased 3,146%

### California SWITRS Data (American College of Surgeons, 2025)
- E-bike incidents: 184 (2018) → 3,429 (2023) — 18.6-fold increase
- E-bike injuries more severe than bicycle injuries (severity score 1.94 vs 1.74)
- Most common e-bike violation: unsafe speed (18.6% vs 10.9% for bikes)

### NYC Data (NYC DOT / Vision Zero)
- 30 cyclists killed in NYC in 2023 — 23 were on e-bikes (77% of cyclist deaths)
- Traditional bike deaths DOWN 60% since 2017
- E-bike deaths "up very sharply" — from 1-2 per year to 7-18
- Motorized Two-Wheelers injuries up ~350% since 2017

### E-bike Injury Demographics (UCSF)
- E-bike riders injured: older (median age 39) vs traditional cyclists (median 30)
- Motor vehicle involvement in 35.4% of e-bike injuries — highest of all micromobility
- E-bike population-based injury rate: increased 293% (2019-2022)

### Regulatory Response
- Federal e-bike definition unchanged since 2002 — 24 years
- Safe SPEEDS Act introduced March 2026 (bipartisan, first federal e-bike safety bill)
- NTSB recommended NHTSA improve e-bike/e-scooter data collection (H-22-26, November 2022)
- Marin County, CA banned under-16 from Class 2 e-bikes
- American College of Surgeons called it "an emerging public safety problem" (June 2025)

## The Paradox
NHTSA made a reasonable methodological choice in 2022 — e-bikes aren't quite motor vehicles in the traditional sense. But the timing created a perfect data storm:

1. E-bike sales exploded (estimated 1M+ units/year in U.S.)
2. E-bike deaths went from 0 to 97/year
3. FARS stopped counting e-bikes as a distinct category
4. Single-vehicle e-bike crashes vanished from federal data entirely
5. The "pedalcyclist" category now mixes two populations with opposite trends:
   - Traditional bicycle deaths: likely declining (per NYC data)
   - E-bike deaths: likely surging
6. Net pedalcyclist deaths "down 6%" looks like progress — but could mask an e-bike epidemic

## Original Contribution
Cross-referencing CPSC e-bike death data (0→97) with FARS pedalcyclist data (deaths down 6%, injuries up 5.8%) and NYC's breakdown (77% of cyclist deaths = e-bikes) to show that FARS — the nation's most authoritative crash database — cannot answer the most basic question about the fastest-growing vehicle safety crisis.

## Limitations
- CPSC and FARS use different definitions and collection methods — direct comparison is approximate
- "Motorized bicycle" in FARS doesn't perfectly map to consumer "e-bike" category
- NYC may not be nationally representative
- The Safe SPEEDS Act may address some of these gaps if passed

## Strongest Counterargument
NHTSA's reclassification was defensible: e-bikes DON'T fit the motor vehicle framework (no registration, no license, no insurance). The CPSC, not NHTSA, has primary product-safety jurisdiction over e-bikes. So NHTSA stepping back from e-bike data collection could be seen as appropriate jurisdictional deference, not negligence. But: nobody stepped up to fill the gap.
