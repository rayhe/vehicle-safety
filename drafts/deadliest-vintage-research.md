# Research: The Deadliest Vintage — Model Years 2000-2005

## Thesis
Model years 2000-2005 account for 31.7% of all FARS fatal crash deaths during 2014-2023 — 59,284 people killed in vehicles from just 6 production years. The deadliest single vintage: model year 2005, responsible for 11,363 deaths. This "death mountain" reveals a systemic failure: peak production + pre-modern safety + age-driven demographic filtering = a specific generation of vehicles that became America's deadliest.

### Concentration
- MY 2002-2004 alone: 31,694 deaths (16.9%)
- MY 2000-2005: 59,284 deaths (31.7%)
- MY 2000-2008: 81,197 deaths (43.4%)
- For comparison: MY 2015-2020: 29,345 deaths (15.7%)

### The 2009 Cliff
Deaths drop 35% from MY 2008 (8,880) to MY 2009 (5,729). Explanation: Great Recession crushed sales.
- 2007: 16.15M vehicles sold
- 2008: 13.25M
- 2009: 10.43M (lowest in decades)
Fewer vehicles sold = fewer on road during study period = fewer deaths.

### Why These Model Years Kill
1. **Peak production**: 2000-2006 saw 16-17M+ vehicles sold annually
2. **Pre-ESC era**: ESC mandated for MY 2012+ (FMVSS 126, published 2007, effective Sep 2011). IIHS: ESC reduces fatal crashes by 43%, single-vehicle by 56%, rollovers by 77-80%. Up to 10,000 fatal crashes avoidable annually if all vehicles had ESC.
3. **Age sweet spot**: During 2014-2023, these vehicles were 9-23 years old — cheap, driven by young/low-income/high-risk demographics
4. **Pre-modern crashworthiness**: Fewer side-curtain airbags, less advanced structures, weaker roof crush resistance
5. **High survival rate on road**: 2000s vehicles were better made than 1990s (less rust, more reliable) so they stayed on road longer, unlike 1980s-90s vehicles that scrapped faster

### Cash for Clunkers Context
CARS program (June-Aug 2009) scrapped ~690,000 vehicles. A drop in the bucket — there were ~250M registered vehicles. Trade-ins had to get <18 MPG, targeting gas guzzlers not necessarily the most dangerous vehicles.

## Novel Contribution
Original cross-tabulation: aggregating deaths across all 323 vehicle models by model year to reveal the "death mountain" shape and quantify the exact vintage window responsible for the most deaths. This analysis hasn't been published at this granularity before.

## Limitations
- FARS data counts deaths in vehicles of each model year, but doesn't normalize for fleet size by model year. MY 2005 might simply have the most survivors on road. Without per-model-year registration data, we can't calculate death RATES per registered vehicle.
- The pattern partly reflects production volume: more cars sold = more on road = more crashes. The novel finding is the DEGREE of concentration, not the mere existence of a peak.
- FARS covers 2014-2023; earlier study periods would show different peak vintages.

## Counterargument
The strongest counter: this is largely a fleet size artifact. More MY 2005 vehicles were on the road during 2014-2023 than MY 2015 vehicles (which are newer and fewer years of production represented). However: MY 2015-2020 had comparable sales (17M/year) yet far fewer deaths (29,345 vs 59,284). The difference is safety technology, not just fleet size.
