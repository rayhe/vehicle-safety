Tesla's Phantom Braking Complaints Dropped 99%. Its Lawsuit Exposure Grew to $14.5 Billion.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
July 2, 2026

NHTSA has closed four separate Tesla investigations in 2026, citing declining complaint volumes and low-severity incidents. Phantom braking reports dropped from 300 when the probe opened to just three this year.[1] Steering loss was closed after a recall that pushed an over-the-air software update to 376,000 vehicles.[2] Smart Summon's hundred-odd parking lot fender benders were deemed not severe enough to warrant further action, and steering wheel detachment was closed without any manufacturer response at all.[3][4]

99%
Decline in phantom braking complaints since 2022, per NHTSA investigation records

Read only the regulatory channel and you'd think Tesla's autonomous driving systems are getting safer by the quarter, as software updates ship, complaint counts crater, and the agency closes its files with language like "low demonstrated hazard."

The litigation channel tells a starkly different story, and June 2026 alone illustrates the scale: Tesla quietly settled the Johna Story case, the first confirmed FSD pedestrian death, after a Model Y struck and killed the 71-year-old on an Arizona highway in November 2023.[5] A new class action, Waller v. Tesla, was filed June 4 covering every FSD purchase from 2017 through early 2023, built on Tesla's own earnings-call admission that Hardware 3 "simply does not have the capability to achieve unsupervised FSD."[6] And on June 19, a Model 3 allegedly running Autopilot crashed through a brick house in Katy, Texas, killing 76-year-old Martha Avila inside her own living room.[7] Combined lawsuit exposure: an estimated $14.5 billion.[6]

Then there's the criminal channel. Michael Butler, the 44-year-old driver in the Avila crash, was charged with manslaughter on July 2 after telling investigators the car was on Autopilot. Tesla's VP of AI software posted on X that Butler "manually overrode self-driving by pressing the accelerator all the way to 100%."[8] Both claims can be true simultaneously, and neither exonerates the software architecture that lets a two-ton sedan reach lethal speed on a residential street while the branding promises autonomy.

NHTSA hasn't abandoned FSD oversight entirely, because its engineering analysis covering 3.2 million vehicles remains open, one step from a recall, focused on FSD's inability to detect when its cameras are blinded by sun glare or fog.[9] But the agency already closed the investigations where complaint frequency was the primary yardstick, and courts do not grade on complaint frequency; courts grade on who survived.

A 99% complaint decline is genuinely meaningful for phantom braking survivors who no longer get rear-ended on highways. But complaint frequency and fatality prevention are not the same metric, and NHTSA's closure language reveals the gap: "low demonstrated hazard" works as a regulatory conclusion while a grandmother is being airlifted from the wreckage of her own living room.

What you should know: If you drive a Tesla with FSD or Autopilot, check your hardware version at tesla.com/support. If you bought FSD on Hardware 3, Tesla itself has admitted the chip cannot deliver what was promised, and a federal class action is now built around that admission.
