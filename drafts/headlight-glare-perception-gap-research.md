# Research: Headlight Glare Perception Gap

## Angle
The most-complained-about safety issue at NHTSA — headlight glare — barely registers in crash data. 60% of drivers say glare is a problem. It shows up in 0.1% of nighttime crash reports. The real nighttime killer is poor visibility, not blinding brightness. America is afraid of the wrong thing after dark.

## Novel Contribution
Cross-referencing the AAA Feb 2026 subjective survey (60% report glare as a problem, 73% say it's worse) with IIHS Oct 2025 objective crash data (1-2 per 1,000 nighttime crashes, no increase over decade). Quantifying the perception-reality gap: headlights' safety improvement (19% fewer crashes) dwarfs the danger they create (~0.1% crash contribution).

## Primary Sources

### AAA Headlight Glare Survey (February 2026)
- 1,092 respondents, ±4% margin of error, probability-based panel
- 60% of U.S. drivers report headlight glare as a nighttime problem
- 73% of those affected say it's worsened over the past decade
- 92% cite oncoming headlights as the primary cause
- **Pickup truck drivers: 41% report glare** (lowest)
- **Other vehicle drivers: 66% report glare** (sedan, car, SUV)
- Female drivers (70%) report more glare than male (57%)
- Prescription glasses wearers (70%) vs non-wearers (56%)
- Age alone NOT statistically significant for reporting glare
- Driver height NOT significant either

### IIHS Headlight Glare Study (October 15, 2025)
- Lead author: Matthew Brumbelow, principal research engineer
- 11 U.S. states where police can list glare as contributing factor
- Out of ~24 million total crashes, fewer than 150,000 had glare coded
- Nighttime glare crashes: 1-2 out of every 1,000 nighttime crashes
- Glare rate was HIGHEST in 2015 and LOWEST in 2020 — no increase
- Glare crash profile: more likely single-vehicle, wet/rainy roads, local undivided two-lane roads, low speed limits
- Drivers in glare crashes: older in age, driving older vehicles
- Drivers >70 most affected; drivers 55-60 no increased risk
- Quote (Harkey): "Although it can certainly be uncomfortable, headlight glare contributes to far fewer crashes than insufficient visibility"

### IIHS Headlight Rating Program Data
- 2016: only 1 out of 80+ headlight systems rated "good"
- 2025: 51% earn "good" rating
- 2016: 82% rated marginal or poor → 2025: only 16%
- Excessive glare: dropped from 21% (2017) to 3% (2025)
- Good headlights = 19% fewer nighttime single-vehicle crashes
- Good headlights = 23% fewer nighttime pedestrian crashes
- Only 18% of drivers use high beams when they safely could

### NHTSA / FMVSS 108 Regulatory Context
- NHTSA receives more consumer complaints about headlights than ANY other topic
- FMVSS 108 brightness standards (candela) haven't changed since 1997
- No glare limits for standard headlights — only for adaptive driving beams (luxury only)
- LEDs: 4x brighter than halogen, more blue-wavelength light
- 1° upward mis-aim = 8x increase in glare intensity
- Only 10 states require headlight alignment checks during inspection
- Adaptive driving beams: common in Europe for 10+ years, still NO vehicles in U.S. market equipped as of end of 2024 due to regulatory hurdles
- Rep. Gluesenkamp Perez (D-WA) proposed legislation July 2025 for study on headlight brightness impact

### The Regulatory Review (February 2026)
- NHTSA measures brightness in candela (lab); IIHS uses lux (real-world)
- Aftermarket LED headlights may exceed brightness limits but NHTSA "does not regulate modifications individuals make to their own vehicles"
- NHTSA acknowledged "unique" compliance challenges of high-mounted headlights in adaptive driving beam final rule
