## Critic 6: 🔬 Research Rigor

**Score: 8.5 / 10 — PASS** ✅

**Novel contribution:** The cross-tabulation — finding each vehicle's peak death model year, then counting how many vehicles peak at each vintage — is genuinely novel. This is not a standard FARS analysis. The finding that MY 2004 dominates with 23 vehicles (53% more than runner-up) is surprising and not previously published.

**Limitations transparency:** Excellent. The fleet size confound is explicitly named ("Without registration data we cannot calculate death rates, only counts") and the disclaimer calls it "the primary confound." The article avoids the phrase "most dangerous model year," using "peak death vintage" instead — correctly distinguishing counts from rates.

**Counterargument steel-manning:** The article grants fleet size its full explanatory weight, then explains why it can't account for the breadth of the spike ("across sedans, pickups, SUVs, and vans simultaneously"). This is honest argumentation.

**Methodology clarity:** "Peak death model year calculated as the model year with the highest absolute fatal crash count per vehicle model" — clear, reproducible definition using publicly available FARS data.

**Issues capping score:** The article doesn't discuss model-level granularity in FARS (how vehicle names are grouped affects counts). The research notes mention a 5th factor (pre-AEB, pre-side-curtain mandate) omitted from the article — appropriate trimming for word count but slightly reduces completeness. A careless reader could confuse "Model Year 2004" with "the year 2004" — the article clarifies this in the body but the headline alone could be misread.
