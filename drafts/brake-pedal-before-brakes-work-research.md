# Research: America Is Removing the Brake Pedal Before It Requires the Brakes to Work

## Angle
NHTSA proposed removing brake pedal requirements for autonomous vehicles on June 26, 2026 — eliminating the 2,500 vehicle/year exemption cap. Meanwhile, the AEB mandate (FMVSS 127) doesn't require automatic emergency braking in ALL new cars until September 2029. The regulatory sequencing is inverted: human controls are being stripped before automated replacements are mandated.

## Novel Contribution
A regulatory timeline cross-reference showing the deregulation of human safety controls is outpacing the mandating of automated ones. Nobody has mapped these FMVSS milestones against each other.

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: NHTSA Brake Pedal Proposal (June 26, 2026)
- NHTSA proposed updating FMVSS to eliminate manual brake pedal requirement for vehicles "designed to be driven exclusively by automated driving systems"
- Currently ALL vehicles require hand/foot-operated manual brake controls regardless of autonomy
- Current exemption: manufacturers can petition for up to 2,500 vehicles/year without human controls
- New rule: no production cap, but must meet same stopping distance performance criteria via alternative testing
- NHTSA Administrator Jonathan Morrison: "tearing down pointless barriers to innovative designs"
- Also withdrew Biden-era voluntary national framework for AV evaluation/oversight
- 30-day public comment period
- Primary beneficiaries: Tesla (Cybercab, no steering wheel/pedals, just entered production), Zoox (Amazon's robotaxi)
- Source: NHTSA press release via Gizmodo, TechCrunch, Reuters (June 26, 2026)

### Source 2: AEB Mandate - FMVSS No. 127 (finalized April 2024)
- ALL passenger cars and light trucks must have AEB + pedestrian AEB by September 2029
- NHTSA projects: save 360 lives/year, prevent 24,000 injuries/year
- Requirements: stop before lead vehicle at up to 62 mph; detect pedestrians day AND night up to 40 mph; auto-brake up to 90 mph for vehicles, 45 mph for pedestrians
- This is a BIPARTISAN Infrastructure Law mandate
- Source: NHTSA.gov, FMVSS 127 final rule

### Source 3: 2024-2025 Fatality Data (NHTSA)
- 2024 final FARS: 39,254 deaths, 1.19 per 100M VMT
- 2025 estimate: 36,640 deaths, 1.10 per 100M VMT — second-lowest rate in recorded history
- 11 consecutive quarterly declines (started Q2 2022)
- VMT increased 0.9% in 2025
- Fatalities decreased in 39 states + DC + PR
- Source: NHTSA press release, April 2026

### Source 4: Tesla Safety Record
- Reuters investigation (June 2026): Tesla misrepresented safety statistics to European regulators to secure FSD approval
- Tesla had not applied for NHTSA exemption for Cybercab as of announcement
- Under current rules, would be limited to 2,500 Cybercabs/year — new rules remove this cap
- Source: Reuters, Gizmodo

### Source 5: Prior AV Exemption History
- 2018: GM petitioned for 2,500 vehicles without steering wheels or brake pedals — withdrew 2020
- 2022: GM petitioned again — withdrew October 2024
- March 2026: NHTSA sought comment on Zoox deploying 2,500 steering-wheel-free robotaxis
- Biden administration: finalized rule allowing AVs without steering wheels (separate from brake pedal rule)
- Source: Reuters, TechCrunch

### Source 6: Rear Seat Belt Warning Rule (NHTSA, finalized 2026)
- Enhanced front seat belt warnings: September 1, 2026
- Rear seat belt warnings: September 1, 2027
- NHTSA estimates: 50 lives/year, 500+ injuries prevented
- Source: NHTSA.gov

### Source 7: Heavy Vehicle AEB Proposal
- NHTSA also proposed AEB for heavy vehicles (>10,000 lbs, class 3-8)
- Would prevent 19,118 crashes, save 155 lives, reduce 8,814 injuries annually
- Source: NHTSA.gov

## Regulatory Timeline (The Inversion)

| Date | What Happens | Direction |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| June 2026 | NHTSA proposes removing brake pedal requirement for AVs | ← Removes human control |
| June 2026 | Biden-era voluntary AV oversight framework withdrawn | ← Reduces oversight |
| Sept 2026 | Enhanced front seat belt warnings required | → Adds safety feature |
| Sept 2027 | Rear seat belt warnings required | → Adds safety feature |
| Sept 2029 | AEB mandatory in all new cars (FMVSS 127) | → Adds safety feature |

The inversion: removing the last-resort human override (brake pedal) is proposed NOW, while the automated replacement (AEB) isn't mandated for 3 more years. And the voluntary oversight framework for evaluating AV safety was simultaneously withdrawn.

## FARS Data Context
From our database:
- 20% of fatal crash drivers are impaired
- 80% are sober — meaning the majority of fatal crashes involve sober, alert humans who still couldn't stop in time
- This is actually the strongest argument FOR removing human controls — human braking is demonstrably inadequate
- BUT: AEB systems have their own failure modes (rain, darkness, unusual objects, sensor occlusion)
- 3/4 of pedestrian fatalities occur in non-daylight conditions — exactly where AEB pedestrian detection is weakest

## Counterargument (at full strength)
The strongest case for NHTSA's sequencing: the brake pedal requirement is only being removed for FULLY autonomous vehicles, not for any vehicle with a human driver. AVs by definition don't need a human to brake — that's the entire point. And these vehicles must still meet the SAME stopping distance requirements. The exemption cap removal simply acknowledges that if a vehicle is safe enough to not need a pedal, the number of such vehicles shouldn't be artificially limited.

The AEB mandate is for a DIFFERENT class of vehicles — human-driven ones. These are separate regulatory tracks for separate vehicle types. Comparing them is apples-to-oranges.

## Why this counterargument doesn't fully hold:
1. Tesla's "fully autonomous" claims are contested — FSD requires driver attention, and Tesla misrepresented safety data to European regulators
2. The voluntary oversight framework for evaluating AV safety was withdrawn simultaneously
3. GM withdrew its exemption petition TWICE — suggesting even manufacturers weren't confident in safety
4. The 2,500 cap existed as a scaling safeguard — allowing gradual deployment and learning. Eliminating it removes the safety ratchet.
