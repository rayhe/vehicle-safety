# Research: The Wrong List — Rate vs. Volume Divergence in Vehicle Safety

## Thesis
America's "most dangerous vehicles" and "deadliest vehicles" are two completely different lists. Only 5 of 15 vehicles appear on both. Safety journalism and consumer advice systematically conflate personal risk (death rate per 100M VMT) with population-level harm (annual body count). This matters because it leads to misallocated regulatory attention and confused consumer decisions.

## Original Finding
Cross-tabulating FARS 2014-2023 death rates with annual death counts reveals a rank divergence of up to 105 positions between the two lists. The median divergence among the top 15 deadliest vehicles is 31 ranks.

## Key Data Points

### Top 5 by ANNUAL DEATHS (most total lives lost)
1. Chevrolet Silverado — 959 deaths/year, rate 1.25 (rank #66 by rate)
2. Ford F-150 — 919 deaths/year, rate 1.04 (rank #79)

4. Honda Civic — 655 deaths/year, rate 2.25 (rank #30)

### Top 5 by DEATH RATE (most dangerous per mile)

## Epidemiological Framework
This is the classic population-attributable risk vs. relative risk problem from epidemiology:
- Relative risk (death rate) tells you: "How dangerous is this for the individual?"
- Population-attributable fraction (annual deaths × fleet penetration) tells you: "How much total harm does this cause?"
- A moderate-risk exposure that's ubiquitous kills more people than a high-risk exposure that's rare.

## Limitations
- Death rate calculations use estimated VMT based on fleet size and NHTS averages, not actual odometer data (±15% for low-volume models)
- Fleet size estimates from sales data may not reflect actual registered vehicles
- FARS only captures fatal crashes — a vehicle could be high-rate in fatal crashes but excellent at preventing fatalities in crashes generally
- Comparing discontinued vehicles (Tracker, Seville) with current-production vehicles mixes generational effects with engineering quality

## Strongest Counterargument
Per-vehicle rate is arguably the ONLY metric that matters for consumer choice. Nobody chooses a car based on how many total Americans it kills — they choose based on their own survival odds. The "volume" side of this analysis might be interesting for policy but is irrelevant at the car dealership. Counter-counter: the volume data DOES matter to consumers because those high-volume vehicles are also the ones you're most likely to be HIT BY. The Silverado's 959 deaths/year includes occupants of OTHER vehicles struck by Silverados.

## Actionable Insights
1. When car shopping: check the RATE, not the total deaths. A Silverado (rate 1.25) is safer per mile than a Civic (2.25).
2. When evaluating safety policy: weight by fleet penetration. The RAM needs different interventions (fleet policy, commercial driver standards) than the Veloster (design defects, driver demographics).
3. Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls regardless of where your vehicle ranks.
4. If buying used: avoid vehicles in the top 15 by rate — Veloster, Tracker, Land Cruiser (old MY), Cobalt, S-10.
