# Research: Drunk Trucking Surges 26% While Everything Else Gets Safer

## Primary Source
NHTSA, "Overview of Motor Vehicle Traffic Crashes in 2024," DOT HS 813 791, April 2026.
URL: https://crashstats.nhtsa.dot.gov/Api/Public/ViewPublication/813791

## Key Data Points (Table 6, p.8)

### Alcohol-Impaired Drivers in Fatal Crashes by Vehicle Type, 2023→2024:
- **Large Trucks: 178 → 224 (+46, +26%)** — ONLY category that INCREASED
- Passenger Cars: 4,462 → 4,239 (-223, -5.0%)
- Light Trucks: 4,928 → 4,839 (-89, -1.8%)
  - SUVs: 2,641 → 2,624 (-17, -0.6%)
  - Pickups: 2,048 → 1,994 (-54, -2.6%)
  - Vans: 238 → 221 (-17, -7.1%)
- Motorcycles: 1,656 → 1,606 (-50, -3.0%)
- Total: 11,724 → 11,367 (-357, -3.0%)

### As percentage of vehicle-type drivers in fatal crashes:
- Large trucks: 3% (2023) → 4% (2024)
- All others stayed flat or decreased

### Large Truck Crash Severity (Table 4):
- Total killed in large truck crashes: 5,478 → 5,340 (-138, -2.5%)
- Other vehicle occupants killed: 3,843 → 3,753 (-90, -2.3%)
- People INJURED in large truck crashes: 153,486 → 161,201 (+7,715, +5.0%)
- Other vehicle occupants INJURED: 107,666 → 116,475 (+8,809, +8.2%)
- Large truck single-vehicle injuries: +1,515 (+12%)

### Overall context:
- Total traffic fatalities: 41,025 → 39,254 (-4.3%)
- Alcohol-impaired driving fatalities overall: 12,382 → 11,904 (-478, -3.9%)
- Alcohol stays at 30% of all traffic fatalities

## The Irony
From the same NHTSA press release announcing these numbers (April 2026):
- Secretary Duffy: "cracking down on unqualified truckers driving big rigs"
- FMCSA Administrator Barrs participated in the event
- The DOT is celebrating the overall decline while burying the truck drunk driving surge

## FMCSA Drug Testing Context
- Random drug testing rate: 50% since 2020 (was 25%)
- Random alcohol testing rate: 10% since 2016
- Positive drug usage rate: 1.2% (2021, latest available)
- Drug & Alcohol Clearinghouse exists but doesn't prevent all violations

## Calculations
- 224 drunk truck drivers in fatal crashes out of ~5,600 total truck drivers in fatal crashes = 4%
- BUT: truck driver killed only 920 of 5,340 total deaths. Other vehicle occupants: 3,753 deaths.
- Ratio: for every drunk trucker who dies, ~4 people in OTHER vehicles die
- A drunk person driving an 80,000-lb vehicle vs a drunk person driving a 3,500-lb car — the lethality multiplication is structural

## Angle
The article argues:
1. NHTSA's own data shows drunk trucking surged 26% — the only vehicle category going the wrong way
2. This happened while the government claims to be cracking down on truckers
3. A drunk trucker is categorically more dangerous than a drunk car driver (weight disparity = guaranteed lethality for the other vehicle)
4. The testing regime catches drug use but may miss alcohol (only 10% random alcohol testing vs 50% for drugs)
5. Injuries in truck crashes surged 5% — more crashes, more hurt, and more of them are alcohol-involved

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES.
- 2024 FARS data was JUST released (April 2026)
- 26% surge in a single year is dramatic
- The contrast with DOT messaging is legitimate irony, not manufactured
- The lethality asymmetry (80,000 lbs vs 3,500 lbs) gives this stakes beyond the number
- Actionable: support higher random alcohol testing rates for CDL holders, check FMCSA Clearinghouse

## Strongest Counterargument
The absolute numbers are small: 46 additional drunk truck drivers in fatal crashes, out of 11,367 total impaired drivers. That's 0.4% of the impaired-driving problem. A 26% percentage increase on a small base can overstate the magnitude. The 2024 number (224) could be a statistical blip — year-to-year variation on small counts. Also, "alcohol-impaired" in FARS uses BAC ≥ 0.08 with imputed values when testing is unavailable, and large truck crashes may have different testing rates than other vehicle types, which could inflate the apparent change.

## Limitations
- FARS impairment data includes imputed BACs — not all truck drivers are actually tested
- We don't know the denominator: how many total truck-miles were driven by impaired drivers who DIDN'T crash
- The 2023→2024 change is one year; could be noise. But the direction, given overall declines, is alarming.
- 2024 data is ARF (Annual Report File), not Final File — numbers may be revised slightly
