The Gap

For Every Truck That Passes the Back-Seat Crash Test, America Buys 10 That Don't

Clara Rollover • Clautomotive Desk
March 27, 2026

Before you load the kids into that crew cab, consider what the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety found when it crashed the four best-selling half-ton trucks into a barrier at 40 mph: the dummy designed to simulate a 12-year-old slid under the seatbelt in every single one.[1]

10.6 : 1
Ratio of trucks sold that fail the rear-seat test to the one that passes it

The IIHS calls it "submarining." In a 40 mph moderate overlap crash, the rear seatbelt migrates upward toward the child dummy's neck while the lap belt slides from the pelvis onto the abdomen. Elevated risk of chest, abdominal, and head injuries. Ford's F-150 got a Poor rating. So did the Chevrolet Silverado. So did the Ram 1500.[2] Three trucks, three failures, zero excuses.

One truck passed. Toyota's Tundra scored Good in the updated 2025 test after earning Marginal in 2023.[3] It remains the only current half-ton with an IIHS Top Safety Pick+ rating. Toyota fixed the problem. Ford, GM, and Stellantis did not.

Run the sales numbers and the gap becomes absurd. In 2024, Americans bought 765,649 F-Series trucks, 549,945 Silverados, 373,120 Rams, and 324,734 Sierras.[4] That is 2,012,448 trucks from manufacturers whose rear-seat protection failed. Toyota sold 159,528 Tundras. Do the math: for every truck that passes the back-seat test, America purchases 10.6 that do not.

FARS data reinforces why this matters. Between 2014 and 2023, the Silverado recorded 9,591 fatalities, making it the single deadliest vehicle in the federal database. Ford's F-150 is second at 9,194. Add the Ram family (5,121) and the Sierra (3,337), and these three American truck lineages account for 26,529 deaths across a combined fleet of 18.9 million vehicles.[5] Pickups as a category represent 21.8% of all FARS deaths.

FARS does not isolate rear-seat fatalities by model, so we cannot say exactly how many of those 26,529 deaths were back-seat passengers. That is a real limitation. But we can say this: the dominant configuration for retail buyers is the crew cab, estimated at 75% or more of sales. That puts roughly 14 million trucks on American roads right now whose rear seats got a failing grade from the country's most rigorous independent crash test.

Ram actually tried. Its 2025 model year added pretensioners and load limiters to the rear outboard seatbelts, the same technology front passengers have had for years.[2] The IIHS crashed it anyway. Still Poor. Belt still migrated, the dummy still submarined, and the chest and abdominal injury risk remained elevated.

Physics is the strongest counterargument. Pickup trucks, by virtue of their mass, offer lower per-mile fatality rates than sedans in multi-vehicle crashes. An F-150 has a 1.04 death rate per 100 million VMT compared to 3.07 for the Honda Accord.[5] Being inside a 5,000-pound truck is still safer than being inside a 3,300-pound sedan, on average, in most crash types. That is true and worth acknowledging fully.

But "better than a Civic" is not the safety standard parents are buying. When you spend $55,000 on a 2025 Ram 1500 Crew Cab and buckle your kid into the back seat, you are buying a promise. The IIHS says that promise is broken at 40 mph.
