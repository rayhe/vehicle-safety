# Research: IIHS 2026 Zero Minivans

## Angle
IIHS raised the bar for 2026 safety awards. Zero minivans qualified for either TSP or TSP+. The reason: a mandatory new back-seat protection criterion that minivans — vehicles specifically marketed for families hauling kids in the back seat — can't pass. This is the most bitter irony in vehicle safety: the vehicle designed for your children can't protect the seat they're sitting in.

## Original Contribution
Cross-tabulation: IIHS 2026 winners vs FARS death rates reveals that minivans have LOWER overall fatality rates than many vehicles that earned awards. Sienna (0.49/100M VMT), Pacifica (0.19) beat plenty of TSP+ winners (Camry 2.03, Sonata 1.56, Pathfinder 0.93). The minivan failure isn't about being generally unsafe — it's about a SPECIFIC structural deficiency in rear-seat protection. Low overall rate, specific back-seat vulnerability. For families, the specific failure is the only one that matters.

## Key Data

### IIHS 2026 changes
- TSP+ now requires: good pedestrian crash prevention, acceptable/good vehicle-to-vehicle front crash prevention (new test at 31/37/43 mph), good moderate overlap front test (emphasizes rear-seat safety), all systems standard
- TSP now also requires good moderate overlap front test (previously only TSP+)
- 63 vehicles qualified (45 TSP+, 18 TSP) — up from 48 at same point in 2025
- Source: IIHS press release March 24, 2026; selfdrivenews.com

### The zero categories
- **ZERO minivans** earned any award (TSP or TSP+)
- **ZERO minicars** earned any award
- **ZERO small pickups** earned any award
- Only 2 large pickups: Tesla Cybertruck, Toyota Tundra crew cab
- David Harkey (IIHS President): "It's disappointing that minivans continue to struggle to provide the best-available protection for passengers in the back, considering that these are supposed to be family vehicles."

### The paradox
- Toyota Sienna (0.49 rate) is safer per-mile than Subaru Outback (0.45), Toyota Camry (2.03), Honda Civic (2.25), and Ford Explorer (1.54) — all of which earned awards
- Chrysler Pacifica (0.19 rate) is safer per-mile than EVERY TSP+ winner with enough FARS data
- But minivans fail the specific rear-seat moderate overlap test
- The test measures something FARS aggregate rates don't: where in the vehicle the protection fails

### Limitations
- FARS data covers 2014-2023 and includes mostly older model years. A 2026 Camry is structurally different from a 2005 Camry
- FARS rates reflect driver demographics, road types, and miles driven — not just vehicle safety
- IIHS tests a new vehicle in controlled conditions; FARS measures decade-old mixed fleet
- The minivan FARS rates include older models (Grand Caravan discontinued 2020) alongside current ones (Sienna, Pacifica)
- Per-mile rate doesn't distinguish front vs rear occupant injuries

### Counterargument
IIHS awards are about CURRENT production vehicles, not fleet averages of decade-old cars. The 2026 Sienna failing the rear-seat test means the CURRENT vehicle has a specific structural weakness — regardless of how safe older Siennas were overall. FARS rates are a different question entirely (who drives the car, where, how fast). The strongest counter to the "minivans are actually safe" argument is: safe for the driver, perhaps. Not for the kid in row two.

### Actionable takeaways
- If you're buying a minivan for family use, know that ZERO current minivans meet the highest rear-seat protection standard
- Check IIHS's moderate overlap front test rating for rear-seat dummy performance specifically
- Consider whether a midsize SUV (Hyundai Santa Fe, Subaru Ascent) gives better documented rear-seat protection
- The Kia K4 starts at $22,290 and earned TSP+ — safety doesn't require a $50K budget
