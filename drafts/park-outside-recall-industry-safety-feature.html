Since 2020, Automakers Have Told 11 Million Vehicle Owners to Park Outside. That's the Fix.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
July 2, 2026

Samsung discovered its Galaxy Note 7 could catch fire. It recalled every single unit, stopped production, and the FAA banned the phone from aircraft. Total deaths: zero.

Automakers discovered the same problem across eleven million vehicles. Their response: park in your driveway.

90+
"Park outside" recalls issued since 2020, covering 11 million+ vehicles

Since 2020, more than 90 recalls have included the instruction to park affected vehicles "outside and away from structures," according to a Wall Street Journal analysis of NHTSA filings.[1] The affected fleet spans Hyundai, Kia, Stellantis, Ford, and GM. One recall alone covered 3.3 million Hyundai and Kia vehicles whose ABS modules can leak brake fluid, short-circuit, and ignite the engine compartment while the car sits in your garage with the key on the counter.[2] Stellantis accounts for roughly 20% of all "park outside" vehicles. In June 2026, it added another 1.07 million Jeep Wranglers and Gladiators after 51 fires traced to power steering pump wiring that overheats with the ignition off.[3]

Eleven million is about 3.8% of the 290 million registered vehicles in the United States. One in 26.

What makes these recalls different from the standard "bring it to the dealer" notice is that many have no fix. Ford recalled 39,000 2021 Expeditions and Navigators after 16 confirmed fires, mostly in unattended rental vehicles, and told NHTSA it had identified "no known cause or remedy."[4] That is not a recall. That is a confession that the vehicle is on fire and the manufacturer does not know why. Chrysler issued a similar notice for 16,700 Pacifica plug-in hybrids: park outside, do not charge, no remedy identified.[5]

Jeep's plug-in hybrid fire saga is the clearest illustration that "park outside" is not a temporary measure. Stellantis first recalled the Wrangler 4xe for battery fires in 2024. That recall's software fix was deployed to thousands of owners. Then nine of the fixed vehicles caught fire anyway.[6] Stellantis admitted the remedy was "ineffective" and issued a new recall in late 2025 covering 320,000 vehicles, again with no available fix and the same instruction: park outside, do not charge. Samsung SDI, which manufactured the batteries, described the root cause as "separator damage combined with other complex interactions within the battery cells," which translates to: we are not entirely sure.

Fair point, pragmatically: vehicles are essential, and a family cannot throw away a $50,000 Jeep the way they toss a $900 phone. The per-unit fire probability is genuinely low: 51 fires across 1.07 million Wranglers is 0.005%. Stranding a million owners with a stop-drive order would create its own safety crisis, and the "park outside" instruction targets the worst-case scenario, a garage fire spreading to a home. Reasonable.

But 171,500 vehicle fires occur in the United States every year, killing roughly 345 people annually.[7] The auto industry has known for decades that its products occasionally combust. The "park outside" recall is not a new safety tool. It is an admission that the recall system cannot handle a defect the manufacturer does not understand yet, so it transfers the burden from the engineering department to your property arrangement. Your garage is now a crumple zone, and the recall letter is the airbag.

Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls. If your vehicle carries a "park outside" recall, follow it. If you had a Jeep 4xe repaired under the 2024 recall, check whether the 2025 recall supersedes it. Do not assume you are covered.
