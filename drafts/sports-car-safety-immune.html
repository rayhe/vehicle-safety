Existential Dread

Twenty Years of Safety Tech. Zero Improvement. Sports Cars Don’t Care.

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
March 28, 2026

I cross-tabulated FARS fatalities by vehicle class and model year era, expecting to find the same pattern everywhere: newer vehicles, fewer deaths. ESC, side curtain airbags, better crash structures, AEB. Twenty years of engineering. And for sedans, pickups, SUVs, and vans, it worked. Sports cars dropped just 13% from the 2000–04 era to 2015–19. Pickups dropped 70%. SUVs dropped 40%.[1]

-13%
Sports car fatality reduction across four model-year eras, vs. −70% for pickups

Look at it vehicle by vehicle and it gets worse. Ford Mustangs account for 2,739 deaths at a 6.02 rate per 100M VMT. Hyundai Velosters: 598 deaths at an 8.54 rate, the highest in the class despite being sold as an affordable hatchback, not a muscle car. Chevy Corvettes kill at an 88% lethality rate. Of 362 FARS-recorded crashes involving a Corvette, 320 produced a fatality.[1] For context, the Dodge Challenger, heaviest in the segment at roughly 4,200 lbs, has a 52% lethality rate. Mass saves occupants. Corvettes have neither the mass nor, apparently, the crash structures to compensate.

GM Resurrected a Body Count

Chevrolet discontinued the Camaro in 2003. From 2005 to 2009, model-year Camaro deaths in FARS: zero. Then GM relaunched the 5th generation in 2010, and 448 deaths followed by model year 2014. By 2015–19, the 6th gen added 319 more. GM literally manufactured fatalities by reintroducing a vehicle the market had absorbed the absence of.[1]

Impairment Is Higher, but Not by Enough

Sports car drivers in fatal crashes test positive for alcohol or drugs at 22.5%, versus 20.4% for sedans and 19.5% for SUVs.[1] That gap exists. It does not explain a 57-percentage-point divergence in safety improvement. If impairment were the primary driver, pickups (20.1%) should show similar resistance to safety gains. Instead, pickups saw the steepest decline of any class.

Sports cars also crash more often per fleet vehicle (3.15 per 1,000 fleet vs. 2.48 for sedans) and kill more efficiently when they do (682 deaths per 1,000 crashes vs. 524 for SUVs). Both numbers point to a class of vehicle that attracts risk-seeking behavior and then fails to protect against it.

Why Engineering Can’t Fix This

ESC reduces fatal single-vehicle crashes by roughly 50% for passenger cars and 56% for SUVs.[2] NHTSA mandated it on all light vehicles by model year 2012.[3] Frontal airbags cut driver deaths by 29%.[4] AEB is projected to save 360 lives per year once fully deployed.[5]

Every one of these technologies is present in modern sports cars. Every one of them works in every other vehicle class. In sports cars, the aggregate effect is a 13% reduction over two decades. Driver behavior is overwhelming the engineering at a rate no other segment experiences.

Limitations

FARS captures only fatal crashes; non-fatal injury rates may have improved for sports cars even as fatalities stayed flat. Model-year death counts are affected by how long vehicles remain on the road (survival bias), meaning older model years accumulate more exposure in a 2014–2023 dataset. “Sports Car” classification in FARS may not perfectly match market perception. Sales normalization by model year would strengthen or weaken this finding, but raw sales-by-model-year data is not available in our dataset.

Strongest Counter

Sports car sales volume shifted dramatically during this period. Camaro was discontinued 2003–2009. Challenger launched in 2008. Veloster debuted in 2012. New entrants could explain the flat death toll without implicating safety technology failure: if you normalize by vehicles sold per model year, the per-vehicle death rate probably did decline. This is a fair point. But it actually strengthens the system-level argument. New sports cars keep entering the market faster than safety tech can reduce deaths from existing ones. Whatever per-vehicle improvements exist, they are not translating into fewer people dying. And that is the number that matters.
