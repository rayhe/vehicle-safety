# Research: Whiplash — Most Common Injury, Never Properly Tested

## Angle
The most common auto insurance injury in America — neck sprains and strains (whiplash) — wasn't being properly tested by IIHS until January 2026. When they upgraded the protocol to include a 30 mph rear impact (up from 20 mph only), 78% of the first 18 SUVs tested failed to earn a Good rating. Three got Poor. The vehicles that scored Good happen to have some of the lowest FARS fatality rates.

### 3. IIHS Background on Whiplash
- Rear-end crashes account for ~29% of all US crashes (NHTSA data)
- Previous IIHS seat/head restraint evaluation used only a 20 mph simulated impact
- New evaluation uses BOTH 20 mph and 30 mph pulses
- Uses specialized dummy with articulated spine measuring T1 vertebra stresses

## Counterargument (strong)
The previous whiplash evaluation DID work — IIHS's earlier seat/head restraint ratings, introduced in 2003, dramatically reduced whiplash claims. Insurance data showed significant improvement. The new test is an evolution of an already-successful program, not an indictment. Progress was real and ongoing.

## Limitations
- FARS covers fatalities only, not the non-fatal neck injuries whiplash tests target. Different injury spectrum.
- Only 18 SUVs tested so far; results may improve as automakers respond
- Whiplash injuries are rarely fatal — the link between FARS fatality rates and whiplash protection is correlational, not causal
- Testing is for small SUVs only; sedans, trucks, and larger SUVs not yet tested
