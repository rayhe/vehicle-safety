16,880 People Didn't Die and Nobody Noticed

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
April 1, 2026

Forty vehicles on American roads are performing a vanishing act. Not from dealership lots. From the federal fatal crash database. Cross-referencing registered fleet size against FARS crash records for 2014–2023 produces a metric I'm calling the FARS Visibility Index: a vehicle's share of fatal crashes divided by its share of the national fleet.[1] Score 1.0 and your vehicle shows up in FARS exactly as often as expected. A Chevy Impala scores 4.35. A Tesla Model Y scores 0.033.

16,880
Fewer deaths than expected across 40 vehicles with sub-0.20 visibility scores

Read that Model Y number again: for every 100 fatal crashes you'd expect from a fleet of 1.75 million vehicles, it produced 3.3. Not 33. Three point three. Fifty-seven occupant deaths in the FARS window against an expected count of 1,951.[1] Kia's Seltos manages 4.4% visibility on 437,500 registered units. Its big sibling, the Telluride, hits 7.2% on 612,500. Hyundai's Palisade lands at 8.1% on 525,000.[7]

Collectively, these 40 vehicles represent 16.87 million registered cars and trucks. Nearly one in ten American vehicles. At fleet-average fatality rates, they should have contributed approximately 18,811 deaths over the decade. Actual count: 1,931. A gap of 16,880 is a mid-sized city's worth of people who kept breathing because of crumple zone geometry, automatic emergency braking, and structural steel that somebody at an engineering desk decided should be two millimeters thicker.[1][6]

What binds this ghost fleet isn't brand or price. It's form factor. Of 19 modern ghost vehicles (post-2015, 100K+ fleet), 17 are crossover SUVs. And the pattern holds across wildly different sticker prices: a $23,000 Hyundai Venue sits in the same invisibility bracket as a $58,000 Porsche Macan.[5] Subaru's Crosstrek, on sale since 2013 with nearly a million registered, still manages 11.4% visibility at a death rate of 0.08 per 100 million VMT. After a decade on the road, that's not an exposure artifact. It's evidence.[1]

Now compare the other end. Nissan's Maxima appears in fatal crashes at 4.53 times its fleet share. Chevy's Cobalt at 3.71 times. Ford's Mustang at 3.53.[1] Per registered vehicle, a Maxima driver is 137 times more likely to appear in FARS than a Model Y driver. Not a typo. A measurement of how wide the safety chasm in America's fleet has become.

Class-level data reveals the structural story. SUVs compose 39.6% of the registered fleet but only 24.3% of FARS deaths: a visibility of 0.66. Sedans account for 32.5% of the fleet and 46.6% of deaths: 1.27.[1][4] America's slow-motion migration from sedans to crossovers is, accidentally, the largest passive safety intervention since the seatbelt mandate. Nobody planned it. Nobody celebrated it. But the math says it's saving thousands of lives per year, one CX-5 purchase at a time.

Methodology

Visibility Index = (vehicle FARS crashes / total FARS crashes) / (vehicle fleet size / total fleet). Fleet estimates derive from cumulative industry sales data adjusted for vehicle age attrition.[7] FARS records cover 2014–2023. "Expected deaths" assumes each vehicle contributes deaths proportional to its fleet share. Only vehicles with 100,000+ estimated registered units qualify for inclusion.

Limitations

Fleet estimates are not DMV registration counts. Actual vehicles on the road may diverge from sales-based projections, particularly for high-attrition models. Newer vehicles have fewer calendar years of FARS exposure, which inflates the ghost effect: a Model Y sold in 2022 has had only one or two years to appear in the 2014–2023 window. Per-VMT death rates independently confirm the finding, but exposure time still matters for rare events.[1] FARS captures only fatal crashes. These vehicles could have high injury or property-damage rates invisible here. Driver demographics confound everything: Porsche Macan buyers are not the same population as Chevy Cobalt buyers, and controlling for income, age, and driving environment would narrow the gap.[4]

Strongest Counterargument

Maybe the ghost fleet is a demographic mirage. Buyers of $35,000+ crossovers tend to be older, wealthier, suburban. They drive fewer miles in lower-risk environments. They are less likely to be impaired. Engineering matters, but the person behind the wheel might matter more. IIHS has acknowledged that driver behavior explains a meaningful share of vehicle-level safety variation.[4] Hand every Cobalt driver a Model Y tomorrow and the fatality gap would narrow. How much? Honestly, this data can't answer that. Which is exactly why you should be skeptical of anyone who can.
