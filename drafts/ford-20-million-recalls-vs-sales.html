Ford Recalled 20 Million Vehicles Last Year. It Sold 2.2 Million.

Vin Wreckage • Clautomotive Desk
May 29, 2026

A company that manufactures 2.2 million things per year and then issues safety corrections on 20 million of them is not, in any meaningful sense, a manufacturing company. It is a recall company with a sideline in transportation.

~9:1
Vehicles recalled per vehicle sold in the 12 months ending March 2026

That is not hyperbole. An iSeeCars analysis of NHTSA data found Ford Motor Company recalled close to 20 million vehicles in the 12 months ending March 2026.[1] Second place was Toyota at roughly four million, with Stellantis taking bronze at 3.4 million. Ford alone recalled more than every other major automaker in America combined. Then it published a press release celebrating 2025 as its best sales year of the decade: 2,204,124 vehicles, up 6%.[2]

The math here is a kind of koan. How do you recall nine vehicles for every one you sell? Recalls reach backward: a single defect in a 2018 F-150 transmission generates a recall covering every truck with that part, even if you sold it seven years ago. Ford's 2015-to-2022 model years are still detonating like unexploded ordnance across the NHTSA database, and each one drags millions of previously-sold vehicles into the count.

Ford set the single-year recall record in 2025 with 152 campaigns, shattering GM's 2014 ignition-switch-crisis mark of 77.[3] By mid-2026, they had already filed 34 more.[4] Twenty-seven percent of the 2025 campaigns were re-recalls: vehicles previously recalled and supposedly fixed, except Ford's over-the-air update system had a bug that quietly reported failed software patches as successful.[3] So a quarter of the record was spent recalling the recall.

NHTSA fined Ford $165 million in November 2024 for dragging its feet on rearview camera defects.[5] That penalty represented 0.85% of Ford's 2024 automotive revenue. Cost of doing business. Cost of business that then generated 19 additional rearview camera recalls across 2025, because the underlying problem kept surfacing in new model years.

The most existentially alarming number belongs to the Lincoln Aviator. An iSeeCars statistical model projects the Aviator will accumulate 92 recalls over a 30-year lifespan.[1] The industry median is 3.9. Ninety-two divided by thirty is roughly three per year, or one every four months. If you buy a Lincoln Aviator and keep it for a decade, you should expect to receive approximately 30 recall notices, which is not a vehicle ownership experience so much as a subscription to bad news.

Four of the top five spots on the projected lifetime-recall list are Ford products: the Aviator, the Lincoln Corsair, the Ford Maverick, and the Ford Bronco.[1] CEO Jim Farley has made quality his stated priority since taking over in late 2020. Analyst Karl Brauer of iSeeCars pointed out that the Maverick launched in late 2021, sitting fully within Farley's tenure.[1] The numbers suggest the message has not yet reached the cars.

Meanwhile, FARS tells a parallel story that operates in an entirely separate dimension. Ford vehicles accumulated 34,954 deaths across NHTSA's fatal crash database from 2014 to 2023.[6] The F-150 alone: 9,194 deaths at a rate of 1.04 per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, sitting unremarkably in the middle of the full-size pickup class because sheer fleet size matters more than per-mile risk when 6.5 million of them exist. The Mustang runs at 6.02 per 100M VMT, nearly six times the fleet average, because sports cars and their drivers do what they do regardless of recall volume.

None of this is directly connected, because a rearview camera recall doesn't cause a highway fatality and a wiper arm recall doesn't prevent one. But together they describe a company operating in two parallel realities: one where it generates more safety corrections than products, and another where its products keep killing people at rates that have nothing to do with the corrections being made.

The strongest counterargument is that a high recall count may signal a company that is more proactive, not less safe. Ford would say it finds problems faster and discloses voluntarily. NHTSA's $165 million fine for delayed disclosure somewhat undermines that narrative, as does the 27% re-recall rate proving the fixes themselves were defective. A company that proactively discovers defects and then proactively fails to fix them is not proactive. It is busy.

The brands with the lowest projected lifetime recall rates, per iSeeCars: Subaru, Honda, Toyota, Mazda.[1] Three of the four are Japanese. All four sell millions of vehicles. They manage to do this without generating a recall volume that exceeds their sales by an order of magnitude.

What this means for you: If you own a Ford or Lincoln, check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls today. Statistically, there is a recall waiting for you. If you're shopping and reliability matters to you, the data shows Honda, Toyota, Subaru, and Mazda generating a fraction of the recall load. And if you own a Lincoln Aviator, maybe set up a Google Alert for your model name. You are going to need it.

Limitations: Recall volume does not directly measure safety risk. Many recalls address convenience features, software bugs, or labeling errors that pose no crash hazard. The 20-million figure counts every affected vehicle in every recall campaign, meaning a single vehicle recalled twice counts twice. Ford's per-mile fatality rates in FARS are driven by fleet demographics and driver behavior as much as vehicle design. The iSeeCars 30-year projection model extrapolates from current recall trends, which may not persist as older model years exit the fleet.
