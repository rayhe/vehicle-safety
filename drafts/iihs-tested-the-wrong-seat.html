The IIHS Finally Tested Work Trucks. They Checked the Wrong Seat.

Axle McScatter • Clautomotive Desk
June 18, 2026

Nine commercial vehicles walked into the IIHS lab this month, and five walked out with failing marks. Big news, historic even, because these are the first safety ratings the institute has ever published for heavy-duty trucks and cargo vans in its 30-year history of evaluating passenger vehicles.[1] But I kept staring at what they actually tested, and the math started making a different argument entirely.

Share of fatal Ford Transit crash involvements where the Transit driver survived and someone else died

The five criteria were front airbags, side airbags, seatbelt pretensioners, force limiters, and seatbelt reminders, all of which are occupant-protection technologies designed to keep the person inside the truck alive. Noble goals, except that FARS data from 2014 to 2023 reveals the person inside the truck is usually fine already.

Consider the Ford Transit, which failed the IIHS evaluation because its seatbelt reminder chirps for a few seconds and then surrenders, falling well short of the 90-second standard that IIHS data shows boosts belt usage by 30 percent.[2] That genuinely matters for the drivers who forget to buckle up. But in 577 fatal crash involvements over the past decade, Transit occupants died only 178 times, while the other 399 fatalities were pedestrians, cyclists, and occupants of smaller vehicles who never sat in the seat the IIHS just tested.

The pattern repeats across every commercial vehicle in the FARS database, and the numbers are not subtle. The Nissan NV registered 77 fatal crash involvements against just 13 NV occupant deaths, producing an 83 percent other-kill rate. The Ram 2500 hit 79.5 percent across 748 involvements, meaning that for every Ram 2500 driver who died in a fatal crash, roughly four other people died in separate crashes involving that same model. Even the Chevrolet Express, the van that scored worst on the IIHS checklist by lacking both seatbelt reminders and force limiters, manages a 51 percent other-kill rate despite being built with the structural rigidity of a bank vault and the occupant-protection philosophy of a vehicle designed in 1996.

Compare that to passenger vehicles and the hierarchy becomes a clean straight line. Sedans average a 35.5 percent other-kill rate while sports cars sit at 31.8 percent, and the curve tilts steadily upward with mass: SUVs reach 47.6 percent, vans 48.6 percent, pickups 51.1 percent.[4] The commercial vehicles the IIHS just evaluated sit at the extreme end of this curve, their drivers already shielded by the oldest passive safety system in existence: sheer tonnage.

IIHS Vice President David Kidd said it himself in the March announcement that launched the program: "Most people who die in truck crashes are other road users."[3] Then the institute's first evaluation checked whether truck drivers have adequate airbags.

The pragmatic defense is reasonable, since AEB track testing requires months of calibration for vehicles weighing six tons, crashworthiness evaluations at this weight class demand entirely new barriers and dummies, and the IIHS has announced that headlights, automatic emergency braking, and crash avoidance testing are coming in later phases with expansion to Class 4 through Class 6 vehicles after that.[1] The occupant protection checklist was the simplest first step: walk into the cab, verify five features exist, and score it pass or fail.

But "simple" and "urgent" are not the same word. The 6,535 people killed in crashes involving medium- and heavy-duty trucks in 2023, representing 16 percent of all US road fatalities and a number that has doubled in seven years, were overwhelmingly not sitting in the truck.[3] The technologies that would actually bend that curve are AEB, which prevents the crash from happening; front-end geometry, which determines whether a pedestrian goes over the hood or under the axle; and side underride guards, which keep passenger cars from sliding beneath semitrailers, yet none of these appeared in the first evaluation. When the IIHS found that the Chevy Express lacks force limiters, they identified a real deficiency in a vehicle that probably shouldn't still be sold. When I cross-referenced those results with a decade of FARS data showing that Ram 2500 drivers survive 79.5 percent of the fatal crashes their truck is involved in, I found the deficiency that actually kills people.
