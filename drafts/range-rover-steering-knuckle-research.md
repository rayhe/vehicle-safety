# Research: Range Rover Steering Knuckle Fracture

## Topic
NHTSA Engineering Analysis EA26003 into front steering knuckle fractures on 2014-2022 Range Rover and Range Rover Sport (331,559 vehicles). JLR's recall fix is a brace. Knuckle fractures = loss of steering control. 522 manufacturer-reported failures. NHTSA says JLR's information is "contradictory."

## Sources

### Source 1: NHTSA ODI Resume EA26003 (Primary)
- Investigation EA26003, opened April 24, 2026
- Upgraded from PE25006 (opened June 27, 2025)
- Vehicles: 2014-2022 Range Rover Sport (L494) and Range Rover (L405)
- Population: 331,559 vehicles
- 522 manufacturer-reported incidents, 21 VOQs from owners, 1 crash, 0 injuries, 0 fatalities
- Problem: "crack at upper clevis portion of the aluminum front steering knuckle assembly → detachment of upper control arm"
- JLR recall 25V514 (Aug 5, 2025): covers 2014-2017 only
  - Cracked knuckles: replaced with "new knuckle of a substantially similar design"
  - Uncracked knuckles: brace to "prevent the component from completely separating should a fracture develop"
- NHTSA: "Much of the information is contradictory and requires a deeper analysis"
- New failures on MY 2020 and MY 2021 Range Rover L405 — outside recall range
- Scope expanded to 2018-2022
- Investigator: George Jones. Reviewer: Peter Kivett. Approver: Tanya Topka
- Supplier: SuperAlloy Industrial (Taiwan) — 20 part numbers listed

### Source 4: FARS Data (2014-2023)
- Land Rover Range Rover Sport: 26 deaths, 95 crashes, rate 0.16 per 100M VMT
- Deaths/crashes ratio: 0.274 (in 73% of fatal crashes, RR Sport occupant survives)
- Among 104 SUVs in FARS data: median death rate 0.41, average 0.72
- Range Rover Sport rate is less than half the SUV median
- Safest SUVs: Tesla Model Y (0.03), Kia Telluride (0.04), Hyundai Palisade (0.06)
- Deadliest SUVs: Chevy Tracker (7.83), Toyota Land Cruiser (6.27)

## Key Angles
1. Engineering Analysis = NHTSA's highest investigation level (above Preliminary Evaluation)
2. JLR's fix for cracked knuckles: replace with "substantially similar design" — same flawed design
3. JLR's fix for uncracked knuckles: a BRACE to prevent separation — doesn't fix the cracking
4. 522 failures and counting — defect persists across 9 model years (2014-2022)
5. $95K-$185K vehicles with a cast aluminum component that fractures
6. NHTSA calls JLR's data "contradictory" — unusually pointed language
7. FARS paradox: one of America's safest SUVs by death rate has a fundamental structural flaw. Safety comes from mass (5,300+ lbs), not engineering excellence.
