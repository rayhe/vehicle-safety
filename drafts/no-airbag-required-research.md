# Research Notes: No Airbag Required

## Angle
The IIHS just conducted its FIRST EVER safety evaluation of heavy-duty trucks and cargo vans (June 9, 2026). These vehicles — the work pickups and delivery vans Americans share roads with every day — have never been crash-tested by IIHS or NHTSA. Federal regulations don't even require them to have airbags. The preliminary results are damning: 5 of 9 vehicles failed even basic safety checks.

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES.
- First-ever data point on a vehicle class responsible for 16% of all US traffic deaths
- Reveals a regulatory black hole: Class 3+ trucks exempt from passenger vehicle safety standards
- 5 of 9 failed — and these are the EASY tests (presence of equipment, not crash performance)
- The Chevy Express 2500 has been sold since 1996, still lacks force limiters in 2025
- Fresh data (IIHS published June 9, 2026)
- Cross-references with FARS data we already have

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: IIHS Press Release (June 9, 2026)
- URL: https://www.iihs.org/news/detail/iihs-launches-evaluations-of-commercial-vehicles-starting-with-driver-protection
- IIHS launched first commercial vehicle evaluations
- 6,535 deaths in 2023 from crashes involving medium/heavy trucks or light vans = 16% of all US roadway fatalities
- Part of IIHS "30x30" vision: reduce fatalities 30% by 2030
- Many government standards for passenger vehicles don't apply to delivery vans, box trucks, tractor-trailers, certain work pickups
- Features as basic as airbags NOT REQUIRED on these vehicles
- No federal requirement for AEB on commercial vehicles
- Current federal seatbelt reminder standard: 4-8 seconds
- IIHS standard: 90 seconds (loud audible tone + visible warning light)

### Source 2: IIHS Test Results
**Passed all 5 criteria:**
1. 2026 Chevrolet Silverado 3500HD
2. 2025 Ford F-350 SuperCrew
3. 2025 Chevrolet BrightDrop 400 (cargo van)
4. 2025 Ram ProMaster 2500 (cargo van)

**Failed:**
5. 2025 Ford Transit T250 — no effective seatbelt reminder
6. 2025 Mercedes Sprinter 2500 — no effective seatbelt reminder
7. 2025 Rivian Delivery 500 — no effective seatbelt reminder
8. 2025 Ram 3500 — no effective seatbelt reminder
9. 2025 Chevrolet Express 2500 — no effective seatbelt reminder AND no standard force limiters

**Key stats from IIHS:**
- Front airbags reduce driver fatalities in frontal crashes by 29%
- Side airbags reduce driver death risk in side crashes by 25-52%
- Persistent belt reminders increase belt use by 30%
- IIHS plans to expand to headlights, AEB, and Class 4-6 trucks

### Source 3: FARS Data Cross-Reference
From our FARS database (2014-2023):
- Chevrolet EXPRESS: 475 deaths, rate 0.92/100M VMT, fleet ~437,500
- Ford TRANSIT: 178 deaths, rate 0.14/100M VMT, fleet ~1,050,000  
- Ford F-350: 164 deaths, rate 0.15/100M VMT, fleet ~787,500
- Ford F-250: 909 deaths, rate 0.43/100M VMT, fleet ~1,575,000
- Total tracked pickup deaths: 41,593 (21.8% of all tracked)
- Total tracked van deaths: 7,897 (4.1% of all tracked)

### Source 4: Autoblog Coverage (June 17, 2026)
- Heavy-duty trucks "historically eluded federal crash-test safety and fuel-economy standards"
- Existing regulations did not classify them as passenger vehicles
- IIHS plans to expand to Class 4-6 commercial trucks

## Novel Analysis
The Chevy Express 2500 has a FARS fatality rate of 0.92/100M VMT with 475 deaths — and the IIHS just confirmed it STILL doesn't have seatbelt force limiters as standard in 2025. This vehicle has been in production since 1996. Nearly 30 years without force limiters.

The Ford Transit has a dramatically lower fatality rate (0.14) despite also failing the seatbelt reminder test — suggesting the Transit's modern architecture saves lives that the Express's 1990s platform doesn't.

The Express represents what happens when a vehicle platform is never forced to compete on safety: it sits at 0.92/100M VMT while the Transit (a modern competitor) sits at 0.14. Same job, 6.6x the death rate.
