# Research: AEB Saves Lives and Causes Crashes — The Most Conflicted Safety Technology Ever Mandated

## Angle
AEB (Automatic Emergency Braking) is the most effective crash-prevention tech ever deployed — cutting rear-end crashes by 52% across 98 million vehicles. But phantom braking complaints surged 1,280% in a single year. The federal government mandated AEB on all new vehicles by 2029. Then the auto industry sued to block the mandate. Then the agency that wrote the rule delayed it. A safety system that prevents half of all rear-end crashes is simultaneously creating a new category of crashes that didn't exist a decade ago.

## Primary Sources

### 1. MITRE Study (2015-2023 data)
- 98 million vehicles, 21.2 million crashes in the U.S.
- AEB cut rear-end crashes by 52% (up from 46% in prior study period)
- Front-to-rear crash reduction: 49%
- 9% reduction in single-vehicle frontal crashes with non-motorists (pedestrians, cyclists, scooters, wheelchairs)
- 168 models examined
- Source: Ford Authority reporting on MITRE study, January 2025

### 2. NHTSA Complaint Data Analysis (Cardog/NHTSA, 2015-2025)
- 2,154,334 NHTSA safety complaints analyzed
- Phantom braking complaint timeline:
  - 2015: 27, 2016: 13, 2017: 24, 2018: 48, 2019: 53, 2020: 61
  - 2021: 132, **2022: 1,820 (+1,279%)**, 2023: 855, 2024: 307, 2025: 261 (YTD)
- 2022 spike correlates with Tesla transitioning to camera-only (Tesla Vision, radar removed by May 2022)

### 3. Manufacturer Breakdown (Phantom Braking Rate)
- Tesla: 13,385 total complaints, 2,292 phantom braking, **17.12%** rate
- Mercedes-Benz: 3,256 total, 50 phantom, 1.54%
- Honda: 20,006 total, 166 phantom, 0.83%
- Toyota: 12,678 total, 14 phantom, 0.11%
- Ford: 28,310 total, 20 phantom, 0.07%
- Tesla's rate is 11x the next closest manufacturer

### 4. AEB Crash/Injury Stats from NHTSA Complaints
- Forward collision + AEB complaints since 2019: 1,742 crashes, 1,169 injuries
- AEB component: 8,957 complaints, 10.6% crash rate, 953 crashes, 643 injuries, 9 deaths
- Vehicle Speed Control: 7,899 complaints, 13.2% crash rate, 1,042 crashes, 592 injuries, 111 deaths
- 1,286 complaints mention BOTH phantom braking AND rear-end collision concerns

### 5. Hyundai FCA Recall (May 19, 2026)
- 421,078 vehicles: 2025-2026 Santa Cruz, Tucson, Tucson Hybrid, Tucson PHEV
- Forward Collision-Avoidance (FCA) system activates prematurely due to software bug in front cameras
- 376 reports between Oct 2024 and April 2026
- 4 crashes (all rear-ended by following vehicles), 4 injuries
- No fatalities, no fires
- Fix: free software update at dealership, notifications July 2026

### 6. Honda AEB Investigation
- NHTSA investigating Honda's AEB across ~295,000-3,000,000 vehicles (reports vary)
- 412 reports of crashes and fires linked to AEB issues
- Carscoops: NHTSA deems some activations "normal" but unsafe

### 7. NHTSA AEB Mandate (FMVSS)
- Finalized: AEB required on all passenger cars and light trucks ≤10,000 lbs by September 2029
- NHTSA estimated: would save 362 lives and prevent 24,321 injuries annually
- 2016 voluntary pledge: all automakers agreed to make AEB standard by 2025 — met
- Alliance for Automotive Innovation filed lawsuit Jan 17, 2025 in D.C. Circuit to block the rule
- Trump administration put the rule "on hold" (delayed implementation)
- NCL (National Consumers League) criticized the delay

### 8. NHTSA-IIHS Original Commitment
- 2016 pledge: 20 automakers committed to AEB standard by 2022 (later 2025)
- Expected to reduce crashes by 28,000 and injuries by 12,000 annually
- Only 19% of 2017 models had AEB; as of 2025, effectively 100% of new models

## Original Contribution
Cross-referencing MITRE effectiveness data with NHTSA complaint data produces a net benefit calculation no outlet has published:
- AEB prevents ~49% of rear-end crashes. In 2023, there were ~2.6M rear-end crashes in the US. 49% reduction = ~1.27M crashes prevented annually among AEB-equipped fleet.
- AEB/FCA complaints generated 953 crashes (from 8,957 AEB-specific complaints) since 2019 — roughly 160 per year.
- Net ratio: AEB prevents roughly 8,000 crashes for every 1 it causes through phantom braking (conservative estimate based on complaint data, which is massively under-reported on both sides).
- The paradox is real but the math is overwhelmingly in AEB's favor. The auto industry's lawsuit isn't about safety — it's about cost and compliance timelines.

## Limitations
- NHTSA complaint data is self-reported and represents a fraction of actual incidents
- The MITRE study examines crashes that occurred, not crashes prevented — the 49% reduction is estimated from comparison
- Phantom braking may be severely underreported (most incidents don't result in crashes, so drivers don't file complaints)
- Tesla's dominance in complaint data partly reflects Tesla owners' higher engagement with NHTSA complaint process
- The 2022 spike was Tesla-specific (camera-only transition); industry-wide phantom braking remains low-rate

## Strongest Counterargument
The auto industry's position isn't about preventing AEB — it's about the specific performance thresholds NHTSA mandated (stopping at 62 mph, automatic braking at 90 mph). The Alliance argues current technology cannot reliably meet those thresholds without increasing false positives (phantom braking). They're not wrong: more aggressive detection parameters inevitably increase false triggers. The mandate may force manufacturers to choose between failing compliance tests and deploying systems that brake too aggressively. This is a genuine engineering tradeoff, not corporate resistance to safety.
