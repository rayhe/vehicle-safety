# Research: The Sedan Extinction Safety Dividend

## Angle
America's abandonment of sedans for SUVs/crossovers is accidentally the single largest vehicle-level contributor to declining traffic deaths. FARS data shows sedans die at 2.5x the rate of SUVs per mile driven. As the fleet shifts, bodies drop. Nobody planned this.

## Self-Critique Gate
- **Genuinely surprising after 230 articles?** YES — we've covered individual model comparisons, class-level gaps, and fleet age effects, but never calculated the CLASS SHIFT DIVIDEND: how many deaths the sedan→SUV migration prevents annually. The 5,412 lives/year hypothetical is a novel calculation nobody has published.
- **Not just another data dump?** The paradox drives the story: the thing environmentalists hate most about American driving habits (SUV addiction) is coincidentally saving ~5,000 lives per year. That's a genuine tension worth exploring.
- **Verdict:** PROCEED

## Key FARS Data (2014-2023)

### Class-Level Death Rates (per 100M VMT)
| Class | Deaths/100M VMT | Crash Lethality | Total Deaths |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Sedan | 13.92 | 64.5% | 89,127 |
| Pickup | 8.86 | 48.9% | 41,593 |
| SUV | 5.47 | 52.4% | 46,442 |
| Van | 6.54 | 51.4% | 7,897 |
| Sports Car | 26.88 | 68.2% | 6,134 |

### The Hypothetical
If every sedan VMT mile were driven in a vehicle with the average SUV death rate:
- Current sedan deaths: 89,127 (10 years)
- Hypothetical at SUV rate: 35,005
- **Lives saved: 54,122 over 10 years = 5,412/year**
- **60.7% reduction in sedan-class deaths**

### Supporting External Data
1. **Sedan market share collapse:** Sedans now <20% of new-vehicle sales (S&P Global Mobility). SUVs/crossovers >50%.
2. **NHTSA/NSC 2025:** Traffic deaths fell to 36,640 (lowest since 2019), 15-quarter decline, 6.7% YoY drop.
3. **PMC study (2023):** In head-on crashes, SUV drivers had 4.5x lower fatality odds than passenger car drivers — vehicle TYPE outperformed crash safety ratings as a predictor.
4. **IIHS driver death rates:** Show consistent class-level gap — smaller/lighter vehicles have higher death rates per registered year.
5. **Policygenius (2013-2022):** SUV/pickup fatal crash involvement rose 62%, but that's fleet share growth, not increased per-mile danger.

### Novel Contribution
**Original calculation:** The "sedan extinction dividend" — quantifying how many deaths the sedan→SUV market shift prevents annually using FARS class-level VMT-adjusted rates. Nobody has published this specific decomposition.

### Limitations
1. Sedan fleet includes many OLD vehicles (pre-ESC Cavaliers, Cobalts, LeSabres) that inflate the class rate. Modern sedans (Camry, Accord) are much safer than the class average. The gap would be smaller for new sedans vs new SUVs.
2. FARS captures fatal crashes only — not all crashes. SUVs' higher mass protects their OWN occupants but may increase risk to OTHER road users (pedestrians, cyclists, smaller vehicles). The "aggressor" problem partially offsets the dividend.
3. VMT estimates use survey data (NHTS), not actual odometer readings. ±15% uncertainty for class-level estimates.
4. Correlation ≠ causation: people who choose SUVs may differ systematically from sedan buyers (age, wealth, driving patterns).
5. The environmental cost of the SUV transition is real and uncounted in this analysis.

### Strongest Counterargument
The SUV "safety dividend" is at least partially an artifact of **who** buys each vehicle type, not the vehicles themselves. Sedan fleets skew older, cheaper, and are disproportionately driven by younger/lower-income drivers with higher crash risk profiles. Replacing the sedan fleet with SUVs might not save 5,412 lives because the high-risk drivers would bring their risk profiles with them. The vehicle helps, but the DRIVER is the confound.

### Actionable Takeaways
1. If shopping for a first car for your kid: the data favors a compact SUV (CX-5, Forester, RAV4) over a sedan (especially an older one). The class-level gap is real.
2. If you drive a pre-2012 sedan: check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls. These vehicles predate both ESC mandate and backup camera requirements.
3. The cheapest sedan on a used lot is NOT the safest option — a similarly-priced compact SUV typically has a lower death rate.
