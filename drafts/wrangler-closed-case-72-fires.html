Stellantis Closed Its Own Wrangler Fire Investigation. Then 72 More Burned.

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
June 9, 2026

Sometime in early 2024, engineers at Stellantis looked at a stack of fire reports from Jeep Wrangler and Gladiator owners, ran the numbers against their installed base, and made a call: the fire rate was too low to act on. They closed the internal investigation. The wiring connector in the electric hydraulic power steering pump kept melting. Vehicles kept catching fire in driveways, parking lots, garages. By the time Stellantis reopened the case in August 2024, the connector had become the subject of a federal probe, a class-action lawsuit, and at least 72 documented fires.[1]

72
Fires tied to the power steering pump wiring defect across 1.3 million Jeeps

The timeline is worth reading slowly. Stellantis says it first investigated fires in certain Wranglers and Gladiators during 2023 and into early 2024, then closed the investigation because the incident rate appeared low. In August 2024, with fires still coming in, the company reopened the file. In September 2024, NHTSA launched its own probe covering roughly 781,000 vehicles from the 2021–2023 model years, flagging the hydraulic power steering pump wiring as a potential ignition source—one capable of starting fires even with the key out of the ignition.[2] By November 2024, owners from California, Kentucky, Oregon, and Tennessee had filed suit in Detroit federal court, describing Wranglers that burst into flames minutes after the engine began smoking.[3]

Stellantis, to their credit, did not phone this one in. The company bought back affected vehicles, pulled returned parts for lab analysis, ran CT scans and X-rays on wiring harnesses, tested materials, and dissected the vehicle design looking for the failure mode. That forensic campaign lasted more than a year. In late May 2026, Stellantis concluded what the lawsuit plaintiffs had argued 18 months earlier: the defect posed an unreasonable risk to motor vehicle safety. The recall covers every 2021 through 2025 Wrangler and Gladiator built—1.08 million in the United States alone, 1.3 million worldwide. Owners are told to park outside, away from structures and other vehicles, until the fix is done.[1]

The root cause is almost insultingly simple. A loose electrical connection at the power steering pump can generate enough resistance heat to melt surrounding materials, which then ignite nearby combustibles. No exotic battery chemistry, no software fault, no manufacturing contamination from a third-party cell supplier. A connector that wasn't tight enough. One injury reported across 72 fires is improbable luck, not engineering margin—several of these fires started with the vehicle parked and the ignition off, a scenario where nobody expects their Jeep to become a bonfire.

This recall doesn't land in isolation. In March 2026, Stellantis recalled 320,065 Wrangler 4xe and Grand Cherokee 4xe plug-in hybrids for an entirely separate fire risk: high-voltage battery cells with damaged separators that had already caused 19 fires.[4] In May, another 419,000 Stellantis vehicles came back for improper side airbag deployment. Today, alongside the 1.08 million power steering recalls, NHTSA disclosed a further 17,277 Stellantis vehicles recalled for yet another battery pack defect that can cause fire even when the vehicle is turned off.[2] Add it up and Stellantis has recalled roughly 1.8 million vehicles in the United States in 2026 alone. That likely exceeds its full-year 2025 US sales volume.

FARS paints a grimmer backdrop that no recall will touch. Jeep Wranglers generated 1,842 occupant deaths in the FARS database from 2014 through 2023, an average of 184 per year, at a fatality rate of 0.84 per 100 million vehicle miles traveled.[5] That rate sits well above the crossover SUVs that dominate modern showrooms—the Toyota RAV4 runs at 0.19, the Honda CR-V at 0.53, the Subaru Forester at 0.26. Fire risk is dramatic and terrifying in a way that generates recalls and headlines, but routine crash lethality grinds through 184 Wrangler occupants every single year without a press release. The 72 fires across 1.3 million vehicles represent an incidence rate of 0.006 percent. The crash death rate is structural, persistent, and orders of magnitude larger.

Impairment doesn't explain it either. Only 19.3 percent of Wrangler drivers in FARS fatal crashes tested positive for alcohol or drugs, below the national vehicle average near 21 percent.[5] These are largely sober drivers dying in a vehicle whose body-on-frame architecture, high center of gravity, and short wheelbase have been trademarks since the platform's military ancestor. Stellantis can fix the connector. It cannot recall the physics.

What Wrangler and Gladiator owners should do now

If you own a 2021–2025 Jeep Wrangler or Gladiator: park it outside, away from your house and other vehicles, immediately. Stellantis says the recall fix—an inspection with possible wiring harness or pump replacement—should be available by July 2026. Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls or call Stellantis customer care at 1-800-853-1403. If you also own a 2020–2025 Wrangler 4xe or 2022–2026 Grand Cherokee 4xe, you may be covered by the separate March 2026 battery fire recall—check that VIN too, and do not recharge until the battery fix is complete.
