# Research: The F-150's Asymmetric Lethality

## Angle
The Ford F-150 is involved in more fatal crashes than any other vehicle in America (20,066 over 2014-2023). Its occupants die less than half the time. In 10,872 of those fatal crashes, the F-150 driver walked away while someone else didn't. No single nameplate generates more asymmetric death. The vehicle that "keeps you safe" is also the vehicle most likely to kill the person you hit.

## Kill Test
- Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES. The F-150 is the second-deadliest vehicle in absolute terms (9,194 deaths) and #1 in fatal crash involvements (20,066). It has NEVER been profiled in 193 articles.
- Novel angle on data? YES. The "asymmetric lethality" calculation - crashes where occupants survived but someone else died - is an original cross-tabulation.
- Would someone share this? Absolutely. Everyone has an opinion about pickup trucks.

## FARS Data (2014-2023)

### F-150 Core Stats
- Fatal crash involvements: 20,066 (#1 in America)
- Occupant deaths: 9,194 (#2, behind Silverado's 9,591)
- Lethality ratio: 45.8% (below 48.8% pickup avg, well below 64.3% sedan avg)
- Asymmetric fatal crashes: 10,872 (54.2% - in these, F-150 occupant survived, someone else died)
- Fleet size: ~6.5M registered
- Death rate: 1.04 per 100M VMT (moderate - below Silverado's 1.25)
- Annual fatalities: ~919.4

### Toxicology
- Drivers in fatal crashes: 21,195
- Any impairment: 18.9% (4,011 of 21,195)
- Alcohol positive: 14.4%
- Drug positive: 8.0%
- Impairment is moderate for pickups - not a DUI truck, not a sober truck

### Comparison: Full-Size vs Midsize Trucks
| Vehicle | Crashes | Deaths | Lethality | Rate |
|---------|---------|--------|-----------|------|
| F-150 | 20,066 | 9,194 | 45.8% | 1.04 |
| Silverado | 19,732 | 9,591 | 48.6% | 1.25 |
| Dodge RAM | 10,110 | 4,407 | 43.6% | 0.78 |
| Ram 1500 | 2,095 | 714 | 34.1% | 0.13 |
| Ford Ranger | 4,476 | 3,089 | 69.0% | 2.91 |
| Toyota Tacoma | 2,274 | 52.3% | 0.80 |

Key insight: Ranger (midsize) has 69% lethality vs F-150's 45.8%. Same brand. Smaller truck = way deadlier for its occupants. But F-150 generates 4.5x more fatal crash involvements.

### Sedan Comparison
| Vehicle | Crashes | Deaths | Lethality |
|---------|---------|--------|-----------|
| Accord | 11,021 | 7,102 | 64.4% |
| Civic | 9,627 | 6,553 | 68.1% |
| Camry | 10,670 | 6,328 | 59.3% |

If you're in a Civic that's in a fatal crash: 68% chance you die. If you're in an F-150: 46%. Mass is armor.

### Novel Contribution
Original calculation: "asymmetric lethality index" = fatal crash involvements where occupants survived / total fatal crash involvements. F-150 scores 54.2% - highest absolute count of any single nameplate.

### Limitations
- FARS captures fatal crashes only; can't extrapolate to total crash outcomes
- "Asymmetric" crashes include single-vehicle fatals where a pedestrian died, not just multi-vehicle
- Fleet size estimates have ±15% uncertainty for high-volume models
- VMT-based rate uses estimated, not actual, odometer data
- Can't determine from aggregate data whether F-150 was at fault in multi-vehicle crashes

### Strongest Counterargument
The F-150's high involvement count is primarily a fleet-size artifact. With 6.5M on the road, it's the most common vehicle - of course it's in the most fatal crashes. Its per-mile death rate of 1.04 is unremarkable. Blaming the F-150 for generating the most fatal crashes is like blaming oxygen for being at the scene of every fire.

This counterargument is correct on rate. But it misses the asymmetry: the F-150's mass means that when it IS in a fatal crash, the other party disproportionately dies. The Ford Ranger generates fewer fatal crashes per vehicle but kills its OWN driver at 69% vs the F-150's 46%. The F-150 exports lethality.

### Actionable Takeaways
1. If you drive an F-150: your vehicle protects YOU but is disproportionately lethal to others. Drive accordingly.
2. If you drive a sedan: physics puts you at severe disadvantage in a collision with full-size pickups. No amount of crumple zone engineering overcomes a 2,000+ lb weight gap.
3. The safety arms race (buy bigger = safer for you) is a collective action problem. Individual rationality produces collective harm.
4. Check the F-150's IIHS ratings and your own vehicle's at iihs.org/ratings.
