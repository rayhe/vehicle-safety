# Research: Hood Height and Pedestrian Fatalities

## Thesis
America's trucks got 11% taller hoods since 2000 while pedestrian fatalities surged 80% since 2009 — and IIHS proved the geometry of the front end, not just the mass, determines whether a pedestrian walks away or dies.

## Angle / Novel Contribution
No existing Crash Report article isolates pedestrian deaths by striking vehicle body type from FARS, then cross-references with the IIHS front-end geometry study. The aggressor-vehicles piece looked at external kills broadly. This one asks: **what happens specifically when a pickup or SUV hits a person on foot, vs. when a sedan does?**

Key cross-tabulation idea:
- FARS pedestrian fatalities by striking vehicle body type (car vs. light truck vs. large truck), 2010-2023
- Track the shifting share over time as the fleet composition changed
- Layer in IIHS hood-height data showing the 40-inch threshold and the 45% increased fatality risk

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: IIHS Front-End Geometry Study (November 2023)
- **Finding:** Vehicles with hoods >40 inches high are 45% more likely to cause pedestrian fatalities than those ≤30 inches with sloped profiles
- **Vehicles with 30-40 inch hoods:** Blunt fronts = 26% more likely to kill pedestrians; sloped fronts = no increased risk
- **Flat hoods** (≤15 degree angle): 25% increase in fatality risk regardless of height
- **Mechanism:** Tall/blunt vehicles throw pedestrians forward (torso injuries); tall/sloped vehicles roll them onto hood (head injuries still, but slightly better outcomes)
- **Sample:** 17,897 single-vehicle-single-pedestrian crashes, 2,958 unique vehicle models
- URL: https://www.iihs.org/news/detail/vehicles-with-higher-more-vertical-front-ends-pose-greater-risk-to-pedestrians

### Source 2: GHSA Pedestrian Fatality Report (July 2025, 2024 data)
- **7,148 pedestrian deaths in 2024** — down 4.3% YoY but still nearly 20% above 2016
- **Light trucks (SUVs, pickups, vans) = 54.1% of pedestrian fatalities** in 2023 (where vehicle type known)
- Passenger cars = 37.3%, large trucks = 7.3%
- **80% increase** in pedestrian deaths since 2009 low
- **75%+ of pedestrian deaths occur after dark**
- **25% are hit-and-runs**
- URL: https://www.ghsa.org/resource-hub/pedestrian-traffic-fatalities-2024-data

### Source 3: Consumer Reports Hood Height Analysis (June 2021, updated Aug 2024)
- Hood height of passenger trucks increased **average 11%+ since 2000**
- New pickups grew **24% heavier** on average from 2000-2018
- Ford F-250 hood leading edge: **55+ inches** off ground — as tall as roof of some sedans
- Front blind spots on some trucks **11 feet longer** than sedans
- Ford admitted: shifted from softer lines because "customers prefer more purposeful looks"
- GM: bigger grilles needed for engine cooling when towing
- URL: https://www.consumerreports.org/cars/car-safety/the-hidden-danger-of-big-pickup-trucks-a9662450602/

### Source 4: AAA Pedestrian AEB Night Test (October 2025)
- 2019 test: AEB systems were **0% effective at night** for pedestrians
- 2025 retest: **60% effective** — huge improvement but still fails 40% of the time
- 2025 Toyota Camry: perfect score (100% avoidance at night)
- Honda Accord: only avoided 40% at night
- **75%+ of pedestrian fatalities happen after dark** — so nighttime AEB matters enormously
- NHTSA mandating pedestrian AEB standard by September 2029 (FMVSS No. 127)
- URL: https://www.carscoops.com/2025/10/how-automatic-emergency-braking-took-pedestrian-detection-from-0-to-60-in-six-years/

### Source 5: NHTSA FMVSS No. 127 — AEB Mandate
- All new passenger cars and light trucks must include AEB by September 2029
- Must detect pedestrians in **both daylight and nighttime**
- Must prevent collisions at speeds up to **62 mph**
- URL: https://thebrakereport.com/nighttime-pedestrian-detection-systems-improve/

## Strongest Counterargument
Fleet composition shift (more trucks/SUVs on the road) alone could explain the rising share of pedestrian deaths from light trucks — it's not necessarily the hood height. Counter-counter: the IIHS study controlled for other factors and isolated front-end geometry as an independent risk factor, with a sample of nearly 18,000 crashes.

Also: pedestrian behavior changes (smartphones, more walking, infrastructure gaps) account for some of the increase. But the 45% fatality increase from tall hoods is after controlling for speed, pedestrian age/sex, etc.
