# Research: Ford Explorer's Shadow Recall — The Fix That Didn't Fix It

## Angle
Ford identified a safety defect in the Explorer/Aviator second-row seats a YEAR ago. Instead of issuing a formal safety recall, they handled it through a "Field Service Action" (FSA) — a below-the-radar fix that doesn't get reported to NHTSA. The supplier modified their installation process. Problem solved, right?

Eight new reports of seat movement came in. From vehicles that had ALREADY been "fixed." The fix didn't work. Ford had to escalate to a full safety recall: 387,911 vehicles.

This is a story about the shadow recall system — Field Service Actions and Technical Service Bulletins — that manufacturers use to fix safety defects without triggering NHTSA oversight. And what happens when the quiet fix fails.

## Primary Sources

### 1. NHTSA Recall Notice (July 2026)
- 387,911 vehicles: 2020-2026 Ford Explorer, 2020-2027 Lincoln Aviator
- Defect: Easy-entry second-row seat switch can bind/stick, seat unlatches/tips/slides
- "A seat that moves unexpectedly may not properly restrain an occupant during a crash"
- Ford's CCRG identified 14 reports of unintended seat movement while in drive
- 6 of 14 involved vehicles already remedied under earlier recall/FSA
- 8 involved vehicles NOT covered by previous campaign
- No crashes or injuries reported
- Remedy: replace switch bezel and housing with revised design
- Source: NHTSA via Fox Business, CarBuzz (July 22, 2026)

### 2. CarBuzz Detail
- Problem traced to a particular supplier
- Ford identified issue ~1 year ago reviewing logistics practices
- Thought tier-one supplier remedied it by altering installation process
- Real-time customer reports revealed unrequested seat movement still happening
- Expanded previous FSA into full safety recall
- Vehicles assembled Oct 19, 2018 – June 24, 2026

### 3. FARS Data (2014-2023)
- Ford Explorer: 3,797 deaths, rate 1.54 (SUV avg 1.15 — 34% above segment)
- Impairment: 19.5% any, 14.8% alcohol, 8.1% drug
- Sober fatal crash rate: 80.5% of fatal crashes involved sober drivers
- Explorer is 4th deadliest SUV by rate (after Tracker, Trailblazer, Yukon)
- Lincoln Aviator: 189 deaths, rate 1.15 (exactly at segment average)

### 4. NHTSA Report to Congress on Recall Completion Rates
- Seat defects have the LOWEST recall completion rate of all component categories
- Overall industry completion rate varies; large recalls often have lower rates
- GM weighted completion rate was 56% in 2014

## Novel Contribution
This is the first Crash Report article examining the FSA-to-recall escalation pattern:
- How many safety fixes happen below the NHTSA reporting threshold?
- FSAs are invisible to consumers checking NHTSA recall database
- The "fixed" vehicle problem: owners who got the FSA repair think they're safe
- When a shadow fix fails, the manufacturer gets a second chance — but consumers lost a year

## Limitations
- No data on total FSA volume vs. recall volume (FSAs aren't reported to NHTSA)
- No crash/injury data from the seat defect specifically (Ford reports none)
- FARS data is 2014-2023; doesn't capture 2020+ Explorer models heavily
- Cannot determine if any FARS Explorer deaths involved seat failures

## Strongest Counterargument
Ford identified this proactively, not after injuries. The FSA system worked as designed: identify, attempt fix, escalate when fix fails. This is the manufacturer doing exactly what NHTSA wants — continuous monitoring. The alternative (waiting for injuries before acting) would be worse. The FSA system isn't a dodge; it's a triage tool for lower-risk issues that don't meet recall thresholds.
