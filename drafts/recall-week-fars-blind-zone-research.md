# Research: Recall Week — FARS Blind Zone

## Angle
1.3 million vehicles recalled in one week (July 7–14, 2026). Most defects kill through mechanisms FARS doesn't measure: fire, child entrapment, rollaway while parked, fender detachment. FARS only counts crash deaths. The recall system is increasingly addressing a threat universe that the primary federal fatality database can't see.

## Key FARS Data Points

- FARS total: 191,193 deaths across 336,202 fatal crash events (2014–2023)
- Kia Telluride in FARS: 31 deaths, 86 crashes, rate 0.04/100M VMT — one of the safest vehicles in the entire dataset
- Yet the Telluride just got the LARGEST recall of the week (463K units) for fire risk
- None of the other recalled vehicles (Mach-E, Vistiq, Grand Wagoneer) even appear in FARS — too new

## The Paradox
- FARS measures crash deaths. It's the gold standard for vehicle safety research.
- But the modern recall system is increasingly addressing NON-CRASH deaths: fire, entrapment, rollaway, thermal runaway
- A vehicle can be "the safest in FARS" while carrying a defect that kills through a mechanism FARS never counts

## Strongest Counterargument
"These recalls are precautionary — no injuries reported." Rebuttal: A 2-year-old named Lucia Ayala died in a Hyundai Palisade from the EXACT same power-seat defect now found in the Cadillac Vistiq. GM only investigated AFTER that death. The "no injuries" line is survivorship bias measured in children.

## Limitations
- FARS is specifically designed to capture motor vehicle traffic crash fatalities. It was never intended to capture fire deaths, entrapment, or manufacturing defects that kill through non-crash mechanisms.
- NHTSA's recall database and complaint system partially fills this gap, but there is no comprehensive federal database of non-crash vehicle deaths.
- The 2-of-7 vs 5-of-7 ratio from one week is illustrative, not a rigorous sampling methodology.
