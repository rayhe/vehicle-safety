# Research Notes: The Rear-Seat 46% Penalty

## Angle
IIHS updated its moderate overlap frontal crash test to include a rear-seat dummy for the first time. The result: most vehicles fail because rear restraints were never engineered to the same standard as the front. IIHS research shows belted rear-seat occupants in post-2007 vehicles face a 46% higher risk of fatal injury than those in front. Cross-referencing the failing vehicles with FARS data reveals 25,441 deaths in those models during 2014-2023.

## Journalist
Clara Rollover — consumer safety advocate, "before you sign that lease" energy

## Primary Sources

### 1. IIHS Updated Moderate Overlap Front Test
- Added a 5th-percentile female Hybrid III dummy in the rear seat behind the driver
- Evaluates submarining, head excursion, chest compression, shoulder belt position
- Launched in 2025 testing cycle; results published through 2026
- Source: iihs.org — multiple results pages

### 2. IIHS Research Finding
- "In vehicles from model year 2007 onward, the risk of a fatal injury is 46 percent higher for belted occupants in the rear seat than in the front."
- NOT because the rear got less safe — the front got MUCH safer (improved airbags, advanced belts) while the rear stayed the same
- Source: IIHS press releases on updated moderate overlap test

### 3. NHTSA FARS Data (2014-2023)
- 25,441 total deaths in vehicles that received marginal or poor ratings in the IIHS rear-seat evaluation
- Top failing vehicles by FARS deaths: Camry (6,328), Altima (4,787), Sentra (2,571), CR-V (2,072), Sonata (1,887)
- Honda Accord PASSED the test — it has 7,102 deaths but presumably with better rear-seat protection

### 4. NHTSA 2025 Early Estimates
- Passenger deaths dropped 12% in 2025 vs. driver deaths dropped only 5%
- Total fatalities: 36,640 (down 7% from 2024's 39,254)
- Passengers comprise ~14% of total fatalities

## Key Data Points

### Vehicles that FAILED rear-seat test (marginal or poor):
**Midsize sedans:** Toyota Camry, Nissan Altima, Hyundai Sonata, Hyundai K5, VW Jetta
**Small cars:** Nissan Sentra, Kia Forte, Subaru Crosstrek (gas), Subaru WRX, Kia K4 hatchback
**Small SUVs:** Honda CR-V, Mazda CX-5, Honda HR-V, Hyundai Tucson, Buick Encore, Jeep Renegade, Mitsubishi Eclipse Cross, Mitsubishi Outlander Sport, Audi Q3, Nissan Rogue
**Midsize SUVs:** Jeep Grand Cherokee, Jeep Wrangler 4-door, Honda Pilot, Hyundai Palisade (pre-Nov 2025), Nissan Murano, Mazda CX-9

### Vehicle that PASSED:
Honda Accord — good rating for rear-seat protection

### Common failure mode: SUBMARINING
- Lap belt slides off pelvis onto abdomen during crash
- Exposes soft abdominal tissue to belt forces
- Risk of internal organ injuries
- The fix: pretensioners and load limiters in rear seats (standard in front, rare in back)

## Original Contribution
Cross-tabulation of IIHS rear-seat test failure list with FARS model-level death data. The 25,441 combined deaths in failing vehicles represents occupants who rode in vehicles that never had their rear seats tested. The 46% rear-seat penalty means approximately 14-15% of those deaths were rear-seat passengers — roughly 3,500+ people who died behind a restraint system nobody had evaluated.

## Counterargument
The 46% penalty and test failures don't prove rear-seat design *caused* those deaths — many were drivers, many were front passengers, many involved factors (speed, impairment) unrelated to restraint performance. The FARS data doesn't break down by seating position. However, the data shows these vehicles are high-volume, high-death-count models, and the systematic rear-seat deficiency across nearly ALL of them suggests a broad engineering gap.

## Limitations
- FARS doesn't distinguish driver vs. passenger deaths at the model level
- The 25,441 figure includes all occupant deaths, not just rear-seat deaths
- Some vehicles have been redesigned since the FARS data window (2014-2023) 
- The 46% stat is for model year 2007+ vehicles specifically
