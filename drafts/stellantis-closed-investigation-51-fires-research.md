# Research Notes: Stellantis Closed the Investigation. 51 Fires Later.

## Angle
Stellantis/Chrysler investigated Wrangler power steering pump fires from May 2023 to April 2024, closed the investigation "due to low rate of occurrence," reopened it in August 2024 after more fires, and finally recalled 1,076,999 vehicles in June 2026. During that time, NHTSA documented 51 fires and 1 injury. The story is about what happens when a manufacturer gets to decide when its own defect rate is "low enough" to ignore.

## Primary Sources

### 1. NHTSA Recall 26V363 (Stellantis 26S43)
- Filed: June 9, 2026
- Vehicles: 1,076,999 Jeep Wrangler and Gladiator, MY 2021-2025
- Defect: Electrical connection issue in electric hydraulic power steering pump wiring
- Risk: Wiring may overheat and cause fire even when vehicle is parked with ignition off
- Remedy: Not yet finalized, expected by July 2026
- Advisory: Park outside, away from buildings and other vehicles
- Incidents: 51 fires, 1 injury (NHTSA aware)
- Additional: 106,258 in Canada, 23,704 in Mexico, 124,297 outside North America

### 2. Investigation Timeline (from Daily Caller / recall filing)
- May 2023: Chrysler begins internal investigation of fires originating near power steering pump
- April 2024: Chrysler CLOSES investigation "due to the low rate of occurrence"
- August 2024: Chrysler REOPENS investigation after receiving more fire reports
- 2024: NHTSA ODI opens own investigation after field reports of engine compartment fires
- June 9, 2026: Recall issued

### 3. WSJ Article (June 12, 2026) — Park Outside Epidemic
- "Since 2020, more than 90 recalls have been issued by automakers for more than 11 million vehicles with [park outside] warnings"
- Stellantis accounts for ~20% of these vehicles
- Followed only by Hyundai (Kia engine fires) and GM (Bolt EV/EUV)
- One complaint: 2022 Gladiator caught fire while parked inside a garage in California

### 4. FARS Data — Jeep Wrangler Fleet
- Deaths: 1,842 (2014-2023)
- Fatal crash involvements: 3,718
- Estimated fleet: 1,750,000
- Fatality rate: 0.84/100M VMT
- Impairment: 19.3% of drivers impaired (14.5% alcohol, 7.8% drugs)
- Death-per-crash ratio: 0.495 (meaning in half of fatal crashes involving a Wrangler, the Wrangler occupant dies; in the other half, someone else does)

### 5. Stellantis Fleet FARS Totals
- Total occupant deaths across all Stellantis brands: 23,245
- Total fatal crash involvements: 46,409
- Major contributors: Ram (4,407 deaths), Cherokee (2,276), Wrangler (1,842), Grand Caravan (1,782)

- 2022: Pacifica Hybrid fire recall #1

- June 2026: Pacifica Hybrid fire recall #3 (17,277 vehicles, NHTSA 26V362)

## Original Analysis / Novel Finding
1. **The "low rate" calculation:** Chrysler decided 51 fires across ~1.08 million vehicles was initially a "low rate." That's 1 fire per 21,137 vehicles. For comparison, NHTSA considers 1 complaint per 10,000 vehicles a potential threshold for investigation opening. Chrysler dismissed a rate that was half the typical NHTSA investigation threshold.
   
2. **The investigation gap cost:** From first reports (May 2023) to recall (June 2026) = 37 months. Every month of delay kept 1.08 million potentially defective vehicles on the road (and in garages). The "park outside" advisory didn't exist for the first 25 months of the defect's known existence.

3. **Cross-reference with FARS:** The Wrangler already has 1,842 occupant deaths in FARS over the 2014-2023 period. While the fire defect is separate from crash dynamics, it adds a NEW failure mode to a vehicle already involved in 3,718 fatal crashes. The Wrangler's death-per-crash ratio of 0.495 means it participates in roughly twice as many fatal events as its own occupant deaths suggest — it's frequently the other vehicle in someone else's fatal crash.

## Counterargument
Stellantis identified the issue through internal investigation and self-reported to NHTSA. No deaths have been linked to the fire defect. The company provided an interim safety measure (park outside advisory) while developing a permanent fix. The closure of the initial investigation could reflect legitimate engineering judgment that the data at the time was insufficient to confirm a systemic defect. Fire rates across the industry for similar wiring issues may be comparable.

## Limitations
- The 51 fire count is NHTSA's knowledge as of the recall filing; actual fires may be higher (underreporting is well-documented)
- The "low rate" characterization comes from the recall filing, not from Chrysler's internal documents — the full analysis behind the closure decision is not public
- FARS data captures fatal crashes, not fires; the fire defect and FARS fatalities are separate failure modes being compared for context, not causation
- The 1 fire per 21,137 rate is rough; not all 1.08M vehicles were on the road during the entire investigation period
