# Research: The Pickup Size Death Inversion

## Angle
As pickup trucks get bigger, WHO dies in fatal crashes inverts. Compact pickups kill their own drivers. Heavy-duty pickups kill everyone else. The crossover point is around full-size — that's where pickups become net exporters of fatality.

## Kill Test
- **Novel?** YES. We've covered individual pickups (S-10, Ranger, Dakota, Ram 1500, Silverado vs F-150) and the general pickup body count. But nobody has cross-tabulated the FOUR SIZE TIERS against lethality direction to show the inversion pattern.
- **Surprising?** Absolutely. Most people assume "bigger truck = safer" without realizing the safety transfers TO the truck FROM everyone else.
- **Data-backed?** 100%. All from FARS cross-tabulation.
- **Verdict:** PROCEED.

### THE INVERSION
- Compact: 68.7% of deaths are the pickup's own occupant → pickup is the VICTIM
- Midsize: 52.7% → roughly even
- Full-size: 46.3% → tipping point, more deaths are OTHER party
- Heavy-duty: 34.1% → pickup is the AGGRESSOR (66% of deaths are other party)

### Ram 2500 extreme: 79.5% of fatal crash deaths are the OTHER party

## External Sources
1. NHTSA FARS database — primary data source
2. IIHS vehicle size/weight study — "for each additional 500 lbs over fleet average, crash death rate of smaller vehicle drivers rose by 7, pickup occupant rate declined by 1" — 7:1 externality ratio
3. IIHS 2011-2016 vs 2017-2022 comparison — occupants of smaller vehicles 90% more likely to die in crash with large SUV (5,000+ lbs) in earlier period, down to 20% in later period due to compatibility improvements
4. "Pounds that Kill" study (2011) — in any given crash, chance of fatality is 0.19% (1 in 500), but 1,000 lbs heavier raises fatality risk by 47% (1 in 357)

## Novel Contribution
Cross-tabulation of four pickup size tiers against occupant-vs-other-party death ratio reveals a linear inversion: compact pickups are self-destructive, heavy-duty pickups are destructive to others. The crossover point sits at approximately 5,000-5,500 lbs (full-size threshold).
