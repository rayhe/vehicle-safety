## Self-Critique Gate
**Angle:** IIHS tightened 2026 standards (rear-seat protection, advanced crash avoidance). Result: some of America's safest-by-FARS vehicles lost their safety awards, while vehicles with worse real-world records earned them. The Chevy Traverse (0.20 death rate — top 10 safest) lost its award; the Hyundai Elantra (1.50 death rate — 7.5x worse) earned one. Zero minivans, zero minicars, zero small pickups qualified. Two large pickups: Cybertruck and Tundra.

**Challenge:** Is this just a rehash of existing IIHS or ratings articles? No — we've never directly compared IIHS award results against FARS fatality rates. Article #107 covered minivan rear-seat failure, but not the broader IIHS-vs-real-world-data disconnect. This has a current news hook (March 2026 IIHS announcements) and a data journalism backbone.

**Verdict:** PROCEED.

## Core Thesis
Crash test awards measure a vehicle's **structural performance in controlled scenarios**. FARS fatality rates measure **whether people actually die driving a vehicle**. These are fundamentally different questions, and consumers treat them as interchangeable. The 2026 IIHS overhaul makes this disconnect more visible than ever.

## IIHS 2026 Changes (Sources)
1. **Rear-seat protection** now requires "Good" rating in moderate overlap front test for BOTH award tiers (was only required for TSP+, "Acceptable" was enough for TSP)
2. **Crash avoidance** must include standard front crash prevention with pedestrian detection AND vehicle-to-vehicle at higher speeds, including motorcycle and semitrailer targets
3. **Result:** 63 vehicles qualified so far (vs 48 at same point 2025), but entire categories wiped out
4. **No minivans, no minicars, no small pickups** earned any award
5. **Only 2 large pickups:** Tesla Cybertruck and Toyota Tundra
6. IIHS President David Harkey: "This year, we're asking automakers to make excellent protection for back seat passengers the norm"

## Why the Disconnect Exists (Nuance)
- IIHS tests the CURRENT model year vehicle's structure. FARS data reflects the ENTIRE fleet (mostly older vehicles)
- The 2026 Traverse is on a new platform — could be genuinely worse structurally than the old one
- FARS rate is influenced by driver demographics, geography, road type — not just the vehicle
- IIHS tests specific crash modes (moderate overlap, side, roof). Real crashes are messy.
- BUT: a consumer reading "Top Safety Pick" thinks "this is one of the safest cars" — which the data doesn't support for many winners
