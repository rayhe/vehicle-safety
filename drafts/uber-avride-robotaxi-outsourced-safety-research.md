# Research Notes: Uber Outsourced Its AV Future. Sixteen Crashes Followed.

## Angle
Uber sold its own autonomous vehicle division (ATG) after the 2018 fatal Tempe crash, then turned around and invested $375M in Avride (ex-Yandex, Nebius subsidiary) for robotaxi service. Within 4 months of Dallas launch, Avride racked up 16 crashes. Safety monitors were present in all 16 vehicles. They intervened in exactly one crash. NHTSA opened an investigation May 2026.

The novel contribution: quantifying the "outsourced safety" model. Uber collects the ride revenue, Avride operates the vehicles, and when crashes happen, nobody clearly owns the liability. The safety monitor intervention rate (1/16 = 6.25%) is a measurable failure of the "human backup" assumption that underpins AV regulation.

## Kill test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES. NHTSA investigating a robotaxi startup after 16 crashes in 4 months is a concrete, data-rich story. The Uber-to-Aurora-to-Avride pipeline reveals a pattern: Uber repeatedly externalizes the hardest part of AV (building safe autonomy) while keeping the platform revenue. The 1-in-16 safety monitor intervention rate is an original quantification nobody else has framed.

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: NHTSA Office of Defects Investigation
- Investigation opened into Avride ADS (Automated Driving System)
- 16 crashes identified between Dec 2025 and March 2026
- Crash patterns: lane changes into adjacent vehicles, failing to stop for stopped/slow vehicles, failing to avoid stationary objects
- "Instances of the AVs changing lanes into the path of or directly into other vehicles traveling in an adjacent lane and in close proximity to an AV"
- All crashes occurred with safety monitor in driver's seat
- Only one intervention by a safety monitor across all 16 crashes

### Source 2: TechCrunch (Sean O'Kane, May 8, 2026)
- Avride is subsidiary of Nebius (formerly Yandex NV, Netherlands-based)
- Sold off Russian business in 2024
- Uber and Nebius committed $375M in investments to Avride
- Avride launched robotaxi in Dallas Dec 2025
- Hyundai Ioniq 5 vehicles used
- Dec 2025 injury crash: Avride vehicle clipped open door of parked pickup truck, minor injury
- Another Dec crash: Avride tried lane change to avoid parked pickup, hit van beside it
- One crash involved Avride vehicle hitting a dumpster
- Avride declined to explain why safety monitors didn't intervene

### Source 3: Reuters
- NHTSA citing "excessive assertiveness" and "insufficient capability" in Avride's system
- Potential traffic safety violations identified

### Source 4: Uber-Aurora ATG Sale (Dec 2020)
- Uber sold ATG to Aurora for ~$4B (stock deal)
- $400M cash investment from Uber into Aurora
- 1,200 ATG employees transferred
- Context: followed 2018 fatal Tempe crash (Elaine Herzberg, first pedestrian killed by AV)

### Source 5: FinanceBuzz AV Crash Statistics 2026
- AV crashes doubled in 2024: 544 vs 288 in 2023
- Self-driving cars 2x more likely to crash per mile than human-driven
- But significantly less injurious/fatal
- Waymo: 907 total ADS crashes (most of any operator)
- Cruise: 155 total ADS crashes (now defunct)
- Avride not in top 15 yet — fleet too new/small

## Original Analysis
- Safety monitor intervention rate: 1/16 = 6.25% — meaning in 93.75% of crashes, the human backup did nothing
- Uber's AV timeline: built ATG → fatal crash → sold ATG → partnered with Avride → crashes again
- "Outsourced safety" model: platform captures revenue, partner bears liability and operational risk
- Uber went from spending billions building AV internally to investing $375M in someone else doing it
- The regulatory question: if Uber dispatches customers to Avride robotaxis through its app, who is responsible when the car crashes?
