# Research: Pickup Rear Seat IIHS Reckoning

## Angle
Only 2 of 9 large pickup truck configurations earned an IIHS award in 2026: Tesla's Cybertruck (Top Safety Pick+) and Toyota's Tundra (Top Safety Pick). America's three best-selling vehicles — the Ford F-150, Chevrolet Silverado 1500, and Ram 1500 — all received POOR ratings in the moderate overlap front test, which evaluates rear-seat passenger protection. Cross-referencing with FARS 2014-2023 data: these three trucks plus the GMC Sierra (which also fails) collectively account for 26,529 fatalities.

The shared failure mode: rear passengers "submarine" under lap belts during frontal crashes. IIHS President David Harkey: "Submarining was a problem for all four pickups, and belt forces were too high in all but the Tundra."

## Original Contribution
Novel cross-tabulation: IIHS 2026 moderate overlap front test results for large pickups vs. FARS 2014-2023 fatality data for the same vehicles. Nobody has compared the trucks that CAN'T pass the new crash test with their cumulative body counts.

### Key IIHS Findings
- All four conventional pickups (F-150, Silverado, Ram, Tundra) were tested on the moderate overlap front test
- The test was updated in 2022 to emphasize rear-seat safety
- For 2026, a Good rating in this test is required for EITHER award (TSP or TSP+)
- Specific failure: rear passengers "submarine" under lap belts in frontal crashes
- "Chest injuries and head or neck injuries would be likely in the F-150 and Ram 1500"
- "The risk of those injuries was somewhat lower but still excessive in the Silverado"
- "For the Tundra, the risk of chest injuries was also too high due to poor belt positioning, but the risk of head or neck injuries was only slightly elevated"
- So even the Tundra barely passes — it still has elevated chest injury risk

## Limitations
- FARS data spans 2014-2023, covering multiple generations of each truck. The current (2026 model year) trucks have different structures than earlier ones.
- Death rates reflect all crash types, not just frontal moderate overlap scenarios.
- The moderate overlap test failure specifically concerns rear-seat protection, while FARS death counts include all seating positions.
- Impairment rates for these trucks range 18.9-20.6%, meaning about 1 in 5 fatal crashes involves impairment — a confounding factor.
- Cybertruck is too new for meaningful FARS data, so we can't compare its real-world safety record.

## Counterargument (strongest)
Volume explains part of the disparity. The F-150 fleet is 6.8x the Tundra's fleet. More vehicles = more deaths. But rate matters: the Silverado's death rate (1.25/100M VMT) is 33% higher than the Tundra's (0.94). Even adjusting for volume, the Silverado kills at a higher rate AND can't protect rear passengers in the lab. The rate gap isn't as dramatic for Ram (0.78, actually lower than Tundra) or F-150 (1.04, 11% higher than Tundra), so the IIHS failure adds a dimension that volume-adjusted rates alone don't capture: these trucks specifically fail to protect the people sitting behind the driver.

## Actionable Takeaways
1. If you're shopping for a large pickup truck and sometimes carry rear passengers: the 2026 Tundra is the only traditional option with an IIHS award. The Cybertruck also passes but at $80K+.
2. Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls — all three of the Big Three trucks have multiple open recalls.
3. If you own an F-150, Silverado, or Ram: your rear passengers face elevated injury risk in moderate overlap frontal crashes. This isn't theoretical — the IIHS tested it.
4. The Rivian R1T earned Acceptable (not Poor) on the moderate overlap test — better than the Big Three but still not Good enough for an award. EVs don't automatically equal safe.
