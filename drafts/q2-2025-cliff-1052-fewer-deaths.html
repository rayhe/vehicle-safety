1,052 Fewer People Died in Spring 2025. Nobody Can Explain Why.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
June 22, 2026

Buried inside NHTSA's annual CrashStats report for 2025 is a number that doesn't fit. Between April and June 2025, 9,055 people died in motor vehicle crashes, a 10.4% decline from the same quarter in 2024, making it the steepest quarterly drop of the year by a wide margin.[1] The quarterly declines surrounding it were modest by comparison: Q1 fell 5.3%, Q3 fell 4.3%, Q4 fell 6.5%. Q2 nearly doubled the pace. And it did so while Americans collectively drove 8.2 billion more miles than the previous spring.

The monthly pattern is stranger still. April dropped 8.5%. May dropped 11%. June dropped 12%.[1] An accelerating decline across an entire quarter, during peak driving season, with vehicle miles traveled ticking upward. That's 1,052 fewer bodies in morgues over 91 days. If the rest of 2025 had matched Q2's pace, the annual death toll would have been roughly 33,500, which would have shattered 2014's all-time fatality-rate record and saved an additional 3,100 lives beyond what actually occurred.

Nobody at NHTSA, the IIHS, or any state DOT has published an explanation for why this specific quarter diverged so sharply. The agency's press releases celebrate the annual trend without examining the quarterly variance. So what happened?

Three hypotheses compete, and none of them is fully satisfying on its own. The first is fleet turnover: the average American vehicle is 12.6 years old, which means the median car on the road in spring 2025 was a 2012 or 2013 model, right at the edge of the 2012 ESC mandate's coverage, and just ahead of the wave of vehicles with voluntary AEB that manufacturers began installing as standard equipment around 2016 and 2017. Spring is also when newer vehicles flood the roads after tax-refund purchases and spring lease turnover, potentially shifting the active fleet's safety profile by a few percentage points for exactly one quarter before the seasonal mix reverts. The second hypothesis is automated enforcement reaching a tipping point in several large cities simultaneously: New York alone seized over 100,000 ghost cars between 2022 and mid-2025 and ran speed cameras 24/7, producing a 37.5% decline in motor vehicle occupant deaths during the first half of 2025.[2] If a dozen other metro areas implemented half of NYC's playbook, the aggregate effect could plausibly explain a chunk of Q2's national drop. The third hypothesis is the simplest and the least interesting: Q2 2024 was a slightly elevated baseline, and Q2 2025 is regression to the mean dressed up in a percentage decline.

That last explanation deserves its full weight. Q2 is traditionally the second-deadliest quarter after Q3, and the 2024 Q2 figure of 10,107 was only 2.7% below 2023's Q2, meaning the baseline wasn't dramatically inflated. If regression to the mean explains the anomaly, then the "real" decline rate is something like Q3's 4.3%, and the entire narrative of an accelerating decline is a mirage. Possible. But then you have to explain June 2025's 12% monthly decline, which is steeper than any single month in the dataset except the pandemic-distorted March 2020 numbers. A month where 411 fewer people died than the same month a year earlier is not a statistical burp.[1]

The honest answer is that we don't know. NHTSA's projections are just that: projections. The final 2025 FARS data won't land until early 2027, and when it does, the quarterly and monthly numbers will shift slightly as late-reported crashes get coded. The Q2 anomaly might shrink. It might grow. What we know right now is that the rate of improvement in American traffic safety is not a straight line. It surges and stalls for reasons the data can describe but not explain, because FARS captures what happened but not why, and the "why" is an unmeasured composite of fleet age, enforcement intensity, road design, weather, and the collective decision-making of 230 million licensed drivers who wake up every morning and choose whether to check their phone at 75 miles per hour.

What to do with this: If you bought a vehicle in the last three years, your car almost certainly has AEB and ESC working silently in the background. Good. If you're driving something from 2011 or earlier, you're in the fleet segment that's doing most of the dying, and the gap between your car and a 2020 model isn't cosmetic, it's structural. Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls regardless. Also: 39 states got safer in 2025, but nine got worse, led by Hawaii (+26.5%), Wyoming (+15.9%), and New Mexico (+12.0%).[1] National averages mask local divergence. Your ZIP code matters more than the national trend.
