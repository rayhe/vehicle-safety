# Research: The Near-Zero Death Rate Club

## Angle
31 vehicles in the FARS database (fleet ≥ 200K) have achieved death rates below 0.15 per 100M VMT — essentially approaching zero occupant fatalities. This cluster represents a solved engineering problem: we already know how to build cars that almost never kill their occupants. The question is why these vehicles exist alongside models with rates 30-50x higher.

## Self-Critique Gate
**Proposition:** The near-zero death rate cluster is a genuine structural finding — not just "new cars are safer" (which is obvious and covered).
**Challenge:** Isn't this just fleet age? New cars with less exposure? Also covered partially in "record-low-fragile-miracle" and "safety-is-a-luxury-good."
**Verdict:** Proceed — but the angle must be sharper than "new cars safe, old cars not." The novel contribution is:
1. Quantifying the actual cluster size (31 vehicles, 21 SUVs)
2. The physics split: mass (Ram 3500 — 0 deaths) vs engineering (Tesla Model Y — 0.03)
3. The consumer action: specific vehicles to buy/avoid today

## Key Data Points

### The Near-Zero Club (rate ≤ 0.15, fleet ≥ 200K)
- **31 vehicles** have achieved rates below 0.15 per 100M VMT
- **21 of 31 (68%) are SUVs** — crossovers dominate the safety frontier
- **4 pickups** (Ram 3500: 0 deaths, Ram 1500: 0.13, Ram 2500: 0.13, Ford F-350: 0.15)
- **4 sedans** (Toyota Matrix: 0.02, Tesla Model 3: 0.05, Dodge Intrepid: 0.04, Acura TLX: 0.11)
- **2 vans** (Dodge Caravan: 0.04, Ford Transit: 0.14)
- Average fleet size: 697,177

### The Stars
- **Ram 3500:** 0 deaths. 437,500 fleet. Zero fatalities. Pure mass physics.
- **Toyota Matrix:** 6 deaths. 0.02 rate. Discontinued in 2014. A hatchback that achieved near-zero.
- **Tesla Model Y:** 57 deaths. 0.03 rate. 1.75M fleet. Largest fleet in the near-zero club.
- **Kia Telluride:** 31 deaths. 0.04 rate. Arrived 2020. Fastest to near-zero of any midsize SUV.
- **Kia Seltos:** 21 deaths. 0.04 rate. A subcompact crossover.
- **Hyundai Palisade:** 38 deaths. 0.06 rate. The Telluride's twin.

### The Class Divide
- **Sports Cars class rate:** 26.88 per 100M VMT (class aggregate)
- **Sedans class rate:** 13.92
- **Pickups class rate:** 8.86
- **Vans class rate:** 6.54
- **SUVs class rate:** 5.47
- **Near-zero club average:** ~0.08

The near-zero club is 69x safer than sports cars and 174x safer than the deadliest individual vehicles.

### The Deadliest (for contrast)
- Hyundai Veloster: 8.54 rate
- Ford Mustang: 6.02
- Chevrolet Cobalt: 5.10
- Nissan Maxima: 5.11
- Chevrolet S-10: 4.83

Veloster rate is **284x** the Tesla Model Y rate.

## Strongest Counterargument
Newer vehicles with fewer accumulated VMT will mechanically have lower observed death rates due to:
1. Less total exposure time
2. Survivors driving newer/better-maintained vehicles
3. Selection bias: people buying Tellurides and Palisades may be more cautious drivers

This must be stated at full strength. The Toyota Matrix (discontinued 2014) partially undermines this argument — it achieved near-zero despite being older.

## Kill Test
**Is this genuinely newsworthy?** Yes — nobody has published the specific cluster of 31 vehicles or quantified the gap. The Ram 3500 zero-death data point alone is headline-worthy. The SUV dominance (68% of near-zero vehicles) is a structural finding with consumer implications.

**Novel angle?** Yes — existing "safest vehicle" articles focus on individual models. This is the first systematic analysis of the near-zero cluster as a phenomenon.
