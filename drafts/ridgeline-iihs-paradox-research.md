# Research: Ridgeline IIHS Paradox — FARS Safest Pickup Fails Crash Test

## Angle
We published "The Truck That Isn't a Truck Has the Lowest Death Rate of Any Midsize Pickup" (stories/ridgeline-unibody-safest-truck.html) praising the Honda Ridgeline as the safest pickup in FARS data (0.24 per 100M VMT vs Ranger's 2.91). On May 19, 2026, the IIHS gave the 2026 Ridgeline a **Poor** rating in its updated moderate overlap front crash test — the worst possible score.

This is a genuine data paradox worth exploring: how can the statistically safest pickup also fail a modern crash test?

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? **YES.** This is a self-correction article. We made a specific claim based on FARS data, and new IIHS testing contradicts it. That's novel. No other automotive outlet is cross-referencing FARS death rates against IIHS 2026 results. This is the kind of original analysis the site exists for.

## Primary Sources

### 1. NHTSA FARS Database (2014-2023)
- Ridgeline: **0.24 deaths per 100M VMT**, 84 total deaths, 262,500 estimated fleet
- Lowest death rate of ANY pickup truck in the dataset
- Comparison: Ford Ranger 2.91, Chevy Colorado 1.15, Toyota Tacoma 1.06, Nissan Frontier 1.41
- Only 84 deaths over 10 years — very small sample size

### 2. IIHS 2026 Test Results (May 19, 2026)
- **Poor** overall in updated moderate overlap front crash test
- **Poor** in front crash prevention (vehicle-to-vehicle)
- **Poor** in front crash prevention (pedestrian)
- Driver scores remain Good in small overlap and side impact
- Rear passenger: **Poor** head score, **Marginal** chest score
- Driver dummy's head barely contacted the frontal airbag before **sliding off and hitting the dashboard**
- Rear headrest dislodged during rebound phase, piece of plastic flew near rear occupant's head
- Lap belt moved from pelvis to abdomen (injury risk)
- **Marginal** headlights (halogen high beams, inadequate curve visibility)
- Poor LATCH system for child seats (hard-to-find, too deep)
- Sources: autoevolution.com, autoblog.com, IIHS YouTube

### 3. NHTSA 5-Star Rating (conflicting)
- NHTSA gave the Ridgeline its maximum overall safety rating
- This is a DIFFERENT test methodology than IIHS
- Both are valid but measure different things

### 4. IIHS 2026 Pickup Landscape
- Only 2 pickups earned TSP+ in 2026: Tesla Cybertruck, Toyota Tundra Crew Cab
- Hyundai Santa Cruz earned TSP (not TSP+)
- Criteria updated: now includes rear seat safety, pedestrian AEB, stricter headlight standards

### 5. Platform Age

- Now 10 years old on the same platform
- Uses previous-gen J35Y6 V6 (shared with Odyssey)
- Priced at $40,795+ MSRP

## Original Contribution
Cross-referencing FARS historical death rates with IIHS 2026 test results reveals a **temporal mismatch** in safety metrics:

1. **FARS is a lagging indicator.** The 0.24 death rate reflects 2014-2023 crash outcomes — predominantly from the 2017-2022 model years of Ridgeline Gen 2. This data can't reflect how the vehicle performs against IIHS's 2026 protocol.

2. **Small sample bias.** Only 84 deaths over 10 years. The Ridgeline has a small fleet (262,500) driven modest miles. Low volume means low deaths, regardless of how the truck performs in a 40mph offset crash.

3. **The FARS rate measures who died, not who was protected.** A vehicle with few deaths could simply be one that's rarely driven fast, rarely driven drunk, and rarely driven by high-risk demographics — not one that's inherently crashworthy.

4. **Real paradox:** The unibody construction that gives the Ridgeline car-like ride quality probably DOES contribute to better crash geometry in most real-world scenarios. But the IIHS test exposed specific failure modes (airbag deployment, rear seat protection) that unibody construction can't fix.

## Strongest Counterargument
The IIHS tightened its criteria significantly for 2026. Many vehicles that earned TSP/TSP+ in prior years now fail. The Ridgeline isn't uniquely bad — the bar moved. Also, NHTSA's 5-star rating using a different methodology suggests the Ridgeline is still reasonably safe. The "Poor" ratings are specifically about the updated moderate overlap test with rear-seat evaluation and the new pedestrian AEB criteria.

## Limitations
- FARS data covers 2014-2023; cannot speak to 2026 model year changes
- Fleet size of 262,500 means the 0.24 death rate has WIDE confidence intervals (±50% wouldn't be surprising)
- IIHS moderate overlap test is ONE crash geometry — real crashes are infinitely variable
- Can't isolate cause: is the low FARS rate because of good engineering, or because of who buys Ridgelines (suburban families driving at moderate speeds)?
- The IIHS test vehicle may not represent the full production range (trim level, equipment)
