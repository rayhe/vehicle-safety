# Research: When the Recall Fix Doesn't Fix Anything

## Angle
NHTSA opened investigation RQ25003 (July 3, 2025) into 1,187,232 Ram trucks (MY 2013-2018) because the recall repair itself failed. These trucks were all fixed under recall 17V-821 or 18V-100 — and they still roll away. The safety net has a hole nobody tracks.

## Core Data Points

### Ram BTSI Case (Primary)
- **Investigation:** RQ25003, opened July 3, 2025
- **Vehicles affected:** 1,187,232 Ram trucks (1500, 2500, 3500, 4500, 5500) MY 2013-2018
- **Requirement:** Column-mounted shift lever
- **Complaints:** 14 VOQ (Vehicle Owner Questionnaire) reports + 6 EWR Death & Injury reports
- **Injuries:** 7 confirmed
- **Original recalls:**
  - 17V-821: 1,482,874 Ram trucks — BTSI solenoid plastic housing swells from heat, grease degrades, locking pin sticks in open position → shifter moves out of Park without brake/key
  - 18V-100: 180,462 additional trucks (2017-2018 MY extension)
- **The fix:** Install bracket, replace attached solenoid
- **The failure:** 14+ vehicles that had the recall repair done still experienced BTSI failure → rollaway
- **Status:** Investigation open, no new recall yet. ODI examining: (1) effectiveness of remedy, (2) root cause of additional rollaways, (3) other possible BTSI root causes

### Recall Completion Rate Context (NHTSA Report to Congress, 2018)
- **Overall recall completion rate:** 58.4% (2012-2016, 5th quarter)
- **By vehicle age:**
  - 0-3 years: 79.78%
  - 4-9 years: 59.52%
  - 10+ years: 42.25%
- **By component:** Power Train 68%, Takata Air Bags 46%, Suspension 52%
- **Key limitation from report:** "The Agency is unable to verify the numbers of remedied vehicles reported by manufacturers"
- **CRITICAL GAP:** NHTSA tracks completion rates (how many vehicles got fixed) but NOT remedy effectiveness (whether the fix actually worked)

### Broader Pattern
- NHTSA's own DOT OIG audit found "inadequate processes and oversight" in recall management
- Consumer Reports/federal audit found NHTSA poorly tracks recall repairs, especially Takata
- No systematic "remedy effectiveness rate" exists anywhere in the system
- Manufacturers self-report completion numbers — no independent verification

## Original Contribution
Cross-referencing the Ram BTSI failure with NHTSA's own completion rate data reveals a blind spot: the entire recall system is optimized around a single metric (completion rate) while ignoring whether the repair itself works. The 58.4% completion rate is already terrible — but for the 58.4% who did show up, there's no data on how many left the dealer with the same defect.

## Strongest Counterargument
14 complaints out of 1.2 million trucks is a 0.001% failure rate. Most recall remedies probably work fine. The BTSI case could be an outlier, not a systemic problem.

Counter to the counter: NHTSA itself acknowledges it can't verify remedy effectiveness. 14 complaints is what was reported — actual failures are certainly higher (reporting bias, many owners don't file VOQs). And 7 injuries from "fixed" trucks is 7 too many.

## Limitations
- We don't know the total number of remedy failures across all recalls — only cases where NHTSA opened investigations
- VOQ reporting is voluntary; actual failure count is unknown
- The BTSI fix may have worked for the vast majority and failed for a subset due to additional root causes
- Completion rate data is from 2018 report; current rates may differ

## Self-Critique
Q: Is this genuinely surprising after 289 articles?
A: Yes. We've covered unfixed recalls (people not getting them done) but never recall fixes that fail. The meta-angle — that NHTSA has no "remedy effectiveness rate" — is a genuine blind spot in how we talk about vehicle safety. This isn't another data dump.
