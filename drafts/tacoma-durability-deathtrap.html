Toyota’s Immortal Truck Has a Mortality Problem

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
April 3, 2026

A 2004 Toyota Tacoma that rolled off the line without electronic stability control, without side curtain airbags, without automatic emergency braking, and without a single forward-facing camera is still worth $14,000 on the used market. That resale figure is Toyota's proudest talking point. It is also, structurally speaking, a body count.[1]

of Tacoma fatal crash victims were in pre-2010 models

FARS data across 2014 to 2023 records 2,274 deaths in crashes involving Toyota Tacomas, yielding a rate of 0.80 per 100 million vehicle miles traveled. The Chevy Colorado posts 0.28. The Honda Ridgeline: 0.24.[1] Nearly three times the Colorado. More than three times the Ridgeline. For a truck whose owners will tell you, unprompted and at length, that it is the safest thing on four wheels.

Anatomy of an aging fleet

Pull the model year distribution and the mechanism becomes obvious. Average model year of a Tacoma involved in a fatal crash: 2006. Colorado: 2011. Almost half of all Tacoma fatalities (47.9%) involve pre-2005 models built before federal ESC requirements existed.[1][4] IIHS research shows ESC reduces fatal single-vehicle pickup crashes by 56%.[3] Nearly half the Tacoma death fleet never had the technology.

Newer Tacomas tell a completely different story. Post-2015 models average roughly 50 deaths per year. Pre-2005 models: 150+. A 2024 Tacoma crew cab earned IIHS Top Safety Pick.[2] A 2003 Tacoma earned nothing because the tests that matter did not yet exist.

It is not the driver

Standard rebuttal: old trucks attract reckless operators. FARS toxicology says otherwise. Tacoma drivers test positive for any impairment at 19.4%, below the midsize truck segment average of 20.0%. Alcohol positive: 14.6% versus 15.1% segment.[1] Tacoma drivers are marginally more sober than their peers. They are dying in larger numbers because the vehicle around them was designed when Bill Clinton was president.

Calculating the durability tax

If the Tacoma's fleet carried the same age distribution as the Colorado (average death model year 2011.3 instead of 2006.1), roughly 1,089 of those 2,274 deaths would not have occurred. That works out to 109 excess deaths per year attributable to fleet age alone.[1] This is the durability tax: the measurable human cost of a truck that refuses to die mechanically while its occupant protection degrades with every passing model year. NHTSA recalled 2005 to 2011 Tacoma frames for corrosion, offering replacements and buybacks.[5] Plenty of pre-2005 frames with no recall coverage remain on the road.

One more paradox

Tacoma occupant lethality runs 52.3%. In the larger, heavier Tundra: 41.5%.[1] You are more likely to die inside a Tacoma than inside its bigger sibling, because the Tundra's mass absorbs crash energy that the Tacoma's midsize frame transfers directly to the occupant compartment.[6] Midsize mass plus ancient structural design is a combination that crumple zone engineers have a technical term for: bad.

What to do about it

Shopping for a used Tacoma? Do not buy anything older than 2016. Specifically: 2016 was the first model year with standard Toyota Safety Sense (pre-collision, lane departure, adaptive cruise). If your budget only reaches 2012 to 2015, you at least get mandatory ESC. Anything older is a mechanical heirloom with the structural protection of a filing cabinet. If you already own a pre-2011 Tacoma, check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls for the frame corrosion campaign. And understand that what keeps your truck running is not what keeps you alive.

Limitations

FARS captures only fatal crashes. A vehicle with low fatality rates might still produce high injury rates, and we cannot measure that here. VMT estimates use average annual mileage assumptions; off-road-heavy Tacomas may log fewer highway miles, which would inflate the per-VMT rate. Deaths include all people killed in crashes involving the vehicle, not just Tacoma occupants. Fleet size estimates may actually undercount surviving old Tacomas, given their unusual mechanical longevity, which would push the true rate lower.

Strongest counterargument

The Colorado launched in 2004. The Tacoma has been in continuous production since 1995. Of course the Tacoma has more old vehicles on the road. A fair comparison would restrict both trucks to post-2015 models only, where the Tacoma's safety technology is competitive. This counterargument is valid and supported by the data: post-2015 Tacomas are dramatically safer. But it does not help the 1.4 million pre-2015 Tacomas still registered and driven daily.
