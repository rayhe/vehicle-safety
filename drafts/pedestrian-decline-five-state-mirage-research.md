# Research: Five States Saved 250 Pedestrians. Twenty-Four Made It Worse.

## Angle
The 11% decline in U.S. pedestrian fatalities in H1 2025 — "the largest drop in 15 years" — is being reported as a national safety triumph. It's not. Five states drove more than two-thirds of the improvement while 24 states saw pedestrian deaths *increase*. The improvement is geographically concentrated, not systemic. The national number is a weighted average that obscures a diverging reality.

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? Yes — every outlet is reporting the 11% decline as unqualified good news. Nobody is decomposing it geographically and showing that most of America is getting worse at keeping pedestrians alive. The Crash Report's angle: the headline is the mirage; the state-level data is the reality.

## Novel Contribution
Geographic concentration analysis of pedestrian fatality changes. The 371-death national decline is not uniformly distributed — it's dominated by 5 states. This means whatever caused the improvement (pedestrian AEB adoption? infrastructure changes? random variance?) is NOT operating nationally. If it were a technology effect, it would be more uniform. If it's infrastructure (New Mexico's crosswalk flashers), it's hyperlocal and not generalizable.

Original calculation: the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) applied to safety improvement concentration — a metric from antitrust economics applied to traffic safety. If improvement were evenly distributed across all 50 states, HHI would be ~200. If 5 states own 2/3, HHI is far higher, indicating a "monopoly" on safety improvement.

## Primary Sources

### 1. GHSA "Pedestrian Traffic Fatalities by State" — 2025 Preliminary Data (March 2026)
- H1 2025: 3,024 pedestrian fatalities (down 10.9% from H1 2024's 3,395)
- Largest year-over-year drop since GHSA began tracking in 2011
- Still 2.5% above pre-pandemic H1 2019 (2,951 deaths)
- 23 states + DC saw decreases; 24 states saw increases
- **Five states accounted for >2/3 of the decline:** Alabama, California, Maryland, New Mexico, New York
- New Mexico: 53 → 27 deaths (nearly halved, -49%) — crosswalk flashing light tech
- Hawaii: 16 → 25 deaths (+56%) — worst individual increase
- Highest rates (per 100K): Hawaii 3.5, Louisiana 3.4, FL/SC/AZ 3.0
- Lowest rates: ID & RI 0.5, MN & SD 0.6, WI 0.7
- Rate per 100K: 0.90 (lowest since 2020)
- Rate per billion VMT: 1.86 (lowest since 2019)
- URL: https://www.ghsa.org/resources/Pedestrians25

### 2. NHTSA Early Estimate of Motor Vehicle Traffic Fatalities 2025 (April 2026)
- Full-year 2025: 36,640 total traffic fatalities (-6.7% from 2024's 39,254)
- 15th consecutive quarterly decline since Q2 2022
- Rate: 1.10 per 100M VMT (second lowest ever, behind 2014's 1.08)
- VMT increased by ~29.8 billion miles (+0.9%)
- 39 states + DC + Puerto Rico saw overall fatality decreases
- URL: https://crashstats.nhtsa.dot.gov/Api/Public/ViewPublication/813737

### 3. IIHS Pedestrian AEB Research
- Pedestrian AEB cuts crashes ~27% overall, ~30% for injury crashes
- BUT: virtually no effect at night on unlit roads
- 3/4 of pedestrian fatalities occur at night
- Most AEB systems don't work in the dark — 8 of 12 vehicles with basic/no credit in nighttime test had superior/advanced daytime ratings
- Only 4 of first 23 vehicles tested earned "superior" in nighttime evaluation
- URL: https://www.iihs.org/topics/pedestrians

### 4. Kids and Cars Safety / NHTSA (June 2026)
- Frontover deaths of children growing as vehicle designs get larger
- "The bigger the vehicle, the bigger the blind zone"
- Calling for 360° camera systems in all new vehicles
- Current AEB systems often cannot detect small children at low speeds

## Counterargument (must state at full strength)
Small-number statistics at the state level are volatile. A state with 16 deaths jumping to 25 (Hawaii) or 53 dropping to 27 (New Mexico) could be random variance, not a meaningful trend. When you decompose a national number into 50 state-level numbers, you're making the sample sizes small enough that year-over-year swings may be noise. The 5-state concentration could mean those states have the largest pedestrian populations and VMT, so naturally dominate any aggregate change. The national trend IS real — 15 consecutive quarterly declines — and nitpicking geographic distribution may distract from genuine progress.

## Limitations
- GHSA data is preliminary and subject to revision
- H1 data may not represent full-year trends (seasonal variation in pedestrian activity)
- No demographic breakdowns in the state-level data (age, time of day, road type)
- Can't directly attribute New Mexico's decline to crosswalk technology without controlling for other factors
- No data on pedestrian AEB fleet penetration by state to test the technology hypothesis
