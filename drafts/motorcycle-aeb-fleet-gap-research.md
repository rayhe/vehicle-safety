# Research: Motorcycle AEB Fleet Gap

## Angle
IIHS just added motorcycle-target AEB testing for 2026 awards. 77% of new cars pass. But 93%+ of cars on the road have no motorcycle-detecting AEB at all. 6,335 motorcyclists died in 2023 — the highest ever — and 65% were in multi-vehicle crashes (i.e., a car was involved). The technology to prevent many of these deaths exists. It's just not in the fleet.

## Primary Sources

### 1. IIHS 2026 Award Criteria (March 2026)
- Source: https://www.iihs.org/news/detail/iihs-pushes-improvements-in-crash-avoidance-with-2026-awards
- 63 vehicles qualify for awards (up from 48)
- New vehicle-to-vehicle front crash prevention test includes motorcycle target at 31/37/43 mph
- 77% of 2026 models tested pass the motorcycle AEB standard
- IIHS President Harkey: "The safest crash is the one that never happens"
- No minivans, no minicars, no small pickups qualify

### 2. IIHS Motorcycle Fatality Statistics (July 2025, FARS 2023 data)
- Source: https://www.iihs.org/research-areas/fatality-statistics/detail/motorcycles-and-atvs
- 6,335 motorcyclists died in 2023 — HIGHEST EVER recorded
- 26% increase since 2019
- 15% of all motor vehicle crash deaths
- 65% in multi-vehicle crashes (4,118 deaths involved another vehicle)
- 35% of fatally injured motorcycle drivers had no valid license
- Motorcycle death share: 7% in 1975 → 15% in 2023 (doubled)
- Per mile traveled: 22x more deadly than cars (NHTSA 2024)

### 3. NHTSA AEB Final Rule (2024)
- Source: https://www.hoganlovells.com/en/publications/nhtsa-announces-final-rule-on-automatic-emergency-braking
- AEB mandatory for ALL new light vehicles by September 1, 2029
- Small volume manufacturers get until September 1, 2030
- Implements 2021 Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act mandate

### 4. NHTSA 2024 Fatality Estimates
- Source: https://aashtojournal.transportation.org/nhtsa-projects-decline-in-traffic-fatalities-for-2024/
- 39,345 projected deaths in 2024 (3.8% decline from 2023)
- Fatality rate: 1.20 per 100M VMT (lowest since 2019, still above pre-pandemic 1.13)
- First time below 40,000 since 2020

### 5. Fleet Age Data
- Average age of vehicles on US roads: 12.6 years (2024, S&P Global Mobility)
- ~283 million registered vehicles in US
- AEB was rare before 2017; became common ~2020
- Roughly 15-20% of fleet is new enough to potentially have basic AEB
- Motorcycle-specific AEB (detecting smaller targets) is even newer

## Original Calculation

### Deaths that involved another vehicle:
- 6,335 motorcycle deaths (2023) × 65% multi-vehicle = 4,118 deaths where a car/truck was involved

### How many cars could theoretically detect motorcycles?
- 77% of 2026 model year vehicles pass IIHS motorcycle AEB test
- But only ~5-7% of the US fleet is MY 2024-2026 (new enough for motorcycle AEB)
- Even generous estimate: ~7% of fleet × 77% pass rate = ~5.4% of cars on the road could detect motorcycles

### AEB effectiveness estimate:
- IIHS research: AEB reduces rear-end crashes by ~50% (Cicchino 2017)
- Motorcycle crashes differ (often turning conflicts, not rear-end)
- Conservative estimate: 25-30% crash avoidance rate for motorcycle-involved AEB

### Lives saveable calculation:
- IF all cars had motorcycle AEB: 4,118 × 30% = ~1,235 motorcycle lives/year
- ACTUAL fleet penetration: 4,118 × 5.4% × 30% = ~67 lives/year
- Gap: Saving ~5% of what's theoretically possible
- Even after 2029 mandate: full fleet turnover takes until ~2042-2045

### The dark math:
- From now to full fleet saturation (~2045): ~19 years
- At current rates: ~19 × 4,100 = ~77,900 motorcycle deaths in multi-vehicle crashes
- Growing gap: motorcycle deaths are INCREASING (26% since 2019) while car occupant deaths are falling
- Motorcyclists' share of road deaths: 7% → 15% in 48 years, and still climbing

## Limitations (must include)
- AEB effectiveness on motorcycle-specific scenarios (turning, lane-splitting) is unproven at scale
- IIHS test is controlled conditions; real-world performance varies
- Not all multi-vehicle motorcycle deaths are preventable by AEB (high-speed, intersection geometry)
- Fleet age estimates use industry averages, not VIN-level AEB data
- Motorcycle deaths include passenger deaths, not just riders
- Our 30% effectiveness assumption is extrapolated from rear-end AEB studies; motorcycle-specific data doesn't exist yet

## Strongest Counterargument
The real problem is motorcycle rider behavior, not car technology. 35% of fatally injured motorcycle drivers had no valid license. Alcohol involvement is high. Single-vehicle crashes (35%) can't be solved by car AEB at all. Even in multi-vehicle crashes, the motorcyclist is often at fault (speeding, lane-splitting). Investing in rider training, licensing enforcement, and helmet laws might save more lives per dollar than waiting for the entire car fleet to get motorcycle AEB.

This counterargument is partially supported by the data: the 35% unlicensed rate is alarming, and single-vehicle crashes are driver-caused by definition. But it doesn't negate the 4,118 multi-vehicle deaths where a car's AEB COULD have helped.
