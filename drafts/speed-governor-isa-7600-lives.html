NYC Put Speed Governors on 700 Cars. Speeding Dropped 64%. Nobody Sells Them.

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
May 12, 2026

New York City installed a device on 700 fleet vehicles that limits the accelerator pedal when the car exceeds the speed limit by more than 11 miles per hour. A US Department of Transportation evaluation found that speeding incidents dropped 64 percent.[1] Among the worst habitual speeders, the reduction was still 49 percent. It costs a few hundred dollars. It does not touch the brakes. It simply makes the gas pedal stop responding.

7,608
estimated annual US deaths preventable if ISA were standard on all vehicles

The European Union has required intelligent speed assistance on every new car sold since July 2024.[2] In America, zero automakers install it. The Ford Mustang ships with 486 horsepower and a 6.02 fatality rate per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, which is six times the national average and the second-highest death rate of any vehicle in the FARS database with more than 200 fatalities.[3] It does not ship with a device that prevents its driver from going 90 in a 55.

NHTSA attributes 29 percent of all traffic fatalities to speeding. In 2023, that figure was 11,887 dead people.[4] Run NYC's 64 percent through that number. That product is 7,608 funerals that did not need to happen, which works out to roughly 21 per day, which is more than the daily pedestrian death toll and more than the number of motorcyclists killed annually. One device. One intervention point. A throttle that quits when the speedometer reads illegal.

And here is the engineering detail that matters: ISA works because it targets the behavioral layer, not the crash layer. Seatbelts and airbags try to keep you alive after you have already decided to die at 85 miles per hour. AEB tries to stop the crash in the last 1.5 seconds before impact. ISA prevents the condition that makes the crash unsurvivable in the first place. It is upstream of every other safety system in the vehicle, and it is the only one that no American manufacturer will touch.

Keith Kerman, the DCAS deputy commissioner who runs New York's fleet program, told Streetsblog the quiet part out loud: "We now can design a vehicle that won't speed. We don't have to depend on the driver doing the right thing."[1] He said it like it was obvious, because it is. Meanwhile, one NYPD officer accumulated 547 speed-camera and red-light violations since 2022 and remains on the force.

FARS data reveals where the speed tax falls hardest. Sports cars carry a class-average death rate of 1.95 per 100 million VMT, roughly triple the SUV average of 0.63.[3] The Mustang alone has accumulated 2,739 deaths over the past decade. Of those, 78 percent involved sober drivers. When impairment is not the explanation and vehicle design is not markedly worse than peers, speed is what remains. The Camaro carries 1,204 deaths at a 3.44 rate. The Corvette adds 320 more. These vehicles are engineered to exceed 150 miles per hour and sold to people who will drive them on public roads governed by 65-mph speed limits. There is no engineering rationale for that gap that does not also explain the body count.

The counterargument is obvious and already answered by the data. "Drivers will override it." NYC tested this on the exact population most likely to override: municipal employees with documented histories of habitual speeding. Repeat offenders still reduced speeding by 49 percent. Not 100 percent. Not zero. Forty-nine. Seatbelts followed the same trajectory, which took decades of mandates and cultural shift before compliance stabilized above 90 percent but began saving lives immediately.

New York State just included the "Stop Super Speeders" law in its 2026 budget, requiring ISA installation for any vehicle caught on speed cameras 16 or more times in 12 months.[1] An estimated 15,000 New Yorkers qualify. Albany calibrated the law for the worst offenders, not the general fleet, and it is already more ambitious than anything NHTSA has proposed at the federal level.

Section 24220 of the 2021 Bipartisan Infrastructure Law mandated that NHTSA develop impaired-driving prevention technology standards for new vehicles. That process has moved at geological speed. ISA, a far simpler technology with a decade of European deployment data and now a US-government-evaluated pilot program showing 64 percent effectiveness, has no corresponding federal mandate, no rulemaking docket, and no timeline.

The arithmetic is not ambiguous. If the Ford Mustang's 6.02 death rate is partially a speed problem, and ISA addresses the speed layer, and NYC proved it reduces speeding by 64 percent among even the worst offenders, then we are choosing 7,600 deaths per year because we prefer to sell cars with speedometers that read 160.

What you should do

If you drive a vehicle with more than 300 horsepower, know that your fatality risk per mile is statistically higher than the fleet average, and the dominant contributing factor after impairment is speed. You cannot buy ISA from a US dealer. You can buy aftermarket GPS-based speed alert systems that beep. They are not the same thing, but they shift the behavioral default. Check NHTSA's speed-campaign page for enforcement calendars in your state. If you drive in New York, Albany just voted to make ISA mandatory for chronic speeders.
