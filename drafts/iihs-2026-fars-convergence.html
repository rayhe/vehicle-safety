The Gap

IIHS Gave Out 63 Awards. The Vehicles Killing You Didn't Qualify.

Clara Rollover • Clautomotive Desk
April 26, 2026

Before you sign that lease, you might want to see this. IIHS just published its toughest award criteria in 45 years of crash-testing vehicles, and the 2026 results read like a shopping list written by the grim reaper's accountant.[1] Sixty-three vehicles qualified, forty-seven of them SUVs or crossovers, which means 74.6% of all safety awards went to a single vehicle class while zero minicars, zero minivans, and zero small pickups made the cut.

74.6%
Share of 2026 IIHS safety awards captured by SUVs and crossovers

IIHS President David Harkey called the minivan shutout "disappointing."[1] That word is doing a staggering amount of heavy lifting considering that minivans are marketed almost exclusively to families with small children in the back seat, and the rear-seat protection failures that disqualified every single one of them are, by definition, failures to protect those children, the very passengers strapped into the seats those minivans were designed around.

A vehicle class that cannot pass rear-seat safety tests while advertising itself with footage of toddlers in car seats is something considerably worse than disappointing.

Now cross-reference those awards against FARS fatality data from 2014 through 2023.[2] SUVs average 0.63 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, the lowest of any class, while sedans sit at 1.02, pickups clock 1.09, and sports cars lead the morgue procession at 1.95. For a decade, IIHS test ratings and FARS real-world death data told different stories about which vehicles were safe. In 2026, those stories converged, and the overlap is nearly exact: the class with the lowest fatality rate received three quarters of the awards, while the classes with the highest rates received almost nothing.

We ran the numbers across all five FARS vehicle classes and mapped them against the IIHS 2026 qualifier list. Non-SUV classes account for roughly 75.7% of all FARS fatalities in the dataset but received only 25.4% of the awards.[2] Among large pickups, exactly two qualified: the Tesla Cybertruck (too new for FARS data) and the Toyota Tundra crew cab (FARS rate: 0.94). The F-150, Silverado, Sierra, and Ram did not make the cut. Those four nameplates collectively appear in over 26,500 fatal crash records.[2]

The honest counterargument deserves its full weight. Modern SUVs dominate the award list because they are new, heavy, and loaded with standard safety technology, not necessarily because the SUV form factor is inherently superior.[3] They score well in FARS for the same reasons, and their exposure time in the 2014 to 2023 dataset is limited because newer models have not accumulated enough fleet miles to build up death counts proportional to their actual risk. A 2005 Corolla failing IIHS 2026 criteria does not prove Corollas are dangerous; it proves that 2005 engineering cannot pass 2026 tests. The convergence may simply mean that new vehicles are safer than old vehicles, which nobody disputes.

But even granting that caveat, the practical implication for a car buyer walking onto a lot today is unchanged: if you want a vehicle that both passes the hardest crash tests IIHS has ever administered and belongs to the class with the lowest real-world fatality rate, you are buying a crossover or a midsize SUV. Many TSP+ winners start under $30,000, with the Kia K4 at $22,290 being the cheapest on the list.[1] If you are shopping for a minivan because you have three kids and need the space, you should know that not a single minivan on sale in America qualified for any IIHS award in 2026, and the specific failure mode is rear-seat protection, precisely the zone where your children sit.

What to do: Check the full IIHS 2026 qualifier list at iihs.org/ratings before buying anything. If you already own a minivan, verify your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls for outstanding safety recalls. And if a salesperson tells you their pickup truck is "one of the safest vehicles on the road," ask them which two pickups actually earned IIHS awards this year. Watch their face.

Limitations: FARS data covers 2014 through 2023 and aggregates all model years within a nameplate, making direct model-for-model comparison with current IIHS criteria impossible. The "van" class in FARS includes commercial vans, which may pull the average fatality rate lower than minivans alone would show. VMT-based rate estimates carry approximately ±15% uncertainty for low-volume models. IIHS criteria test current model-year vehicles; FARS deaths skew heavily toward the older fleet still on the road.
