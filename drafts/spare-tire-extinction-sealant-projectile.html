45% of New Cars Have No Spare Tire. The Replacement Just Sent Eight People to the Hospital.

Clara Rollover • Clautomotive Desk
June 14, 2026

Pop quiz. You get a flat on the shoulder of I-95 at 11 p.m. in the rain. You open your trunk, looking for the spare. You find a Styrofoam tray, a small plastic box labeled "Tire Repair Kit," and absolutely nothing resembling a tire. According to Consumer Reports, that scenario now applies to roughly 45% of new vehicles sold in the United States.[1]

1,049,883
Honda vehicles recalled because the sealant kit that replaced the spare tire can launch its bottle cap like a projectile

On June 11, NHTSA published recall campaign 26V306. Honda is pulling back over a million CR-V Hybrids, Accord Hybrids, and CR-V Fuel Cell EVs because the tire repair kit stowed in each one has a design flaw that can turn the sealant bottle into a small cannon. If the nozzle isn't seated perfectly on the tire valve, pressure builds inside the bottle instead of flowing into the tire. A relief valve is supposed to prevent exactly this. It doesn't. Some were improperly adjusted during manufacturing. Others include a one-way valve that traps pressure in but won't let it out. Eventually, the cap detaches. And the bottle fires it across the road shoulder at whoever is crouching next to the flat.[2]

Eight people have been injured so far. Honda has logged 54 warranty claims. Zero deaths, thankfully, but keep in mind: this is a kit you'd be using at the side of a highway, at night, cars screaming past at 70 mph, your hands shaking because you've never opened this box before in your life and the instructions are printed in 6-point font on a bag you threw away three years ago.

TEK Corporation, the supplier responsible for the kit, told Honda the problem was "customer misuse." Honda received its first report in April 2023 and accepted that explanation for nearly three years before finally inspecting the valve assembly themselves in March 2026.[3] Three years of bottle caps popping because a supplier said users were holding it wrong.

But the recall isn't really the story. It's a symptom. What matters is broader: automakers have been quietly eliminating spare tires for two decades, and the replacement they chose is a can of chemical goo that doesn't work for the flats most likely to strand you.

AAA tracked the trend. In 2006, only 5% of new cars shipped without a spare tire. Today that number is approximately 30%, putting more than 30 million vehicles on American roads right now with no spare in the trunk.[4] Consumer Reports puts the figure even higher: since 2020, only 9% of vehicles they tested came with a full-size spare. Another 46% included a space-saver donut. Of those, 34% got sealant kits, 7% got run-flat tires, and 4% got nothing at all.[1]

50 lbs
Weight saved by removing a spare tire, jack, and lug wrench. The battery in a CR-V Hybrid weighs roughly 200 lbs.

The rationale sounds responsible: CAFE fuel economy standards tightened, and every pound matters when the EPA is measuring your fleet average. A spare tire, jack, and lug wrench weigh 30 to 60 pounds. Removing them yields a marginal fuel economy gain that, multiplied across millions of vehicles, helps a manufacturer hit its regulatory target. It also saves the cost of the tire, the wheel, the jack, the tools, and the cargo well depth needed to house them. Per-vehicle savings are modest. Across a million units, they're enormous.

But notice which vehicles Honda recalled. All 1,049,883 of them are hybrids or hydrogen fuel-cell models. Not a single gasoline-only Honda is on this list. That's because hybrids and EVs face an additional constraint beyond CAFE: their battery packs physically occupy the space where a spare tire would go. In a CR-V Hybrid, the battery sits under the cargo floor, in the exact spot Toyota, Ford, and every other automaker used to put a donut spare. No room.

So the electrification push creates a peculiar irony. Automakers removed the spare tire from conventional cars to save 50 pounds. Then they built hybrids that add 200 or more pounds of lithium-ion cells in the same cavity. The sealant kit doesn't exist because the car got lighter. It exists because the car got heavier in a place the spare used to live.

And what does that sealant kit actually fix? Consumer Reports and AAA agree on the limitations: a sealant kit can temporarily patch a small tread puncture, the kind caused by a nail or a screw. It cannot fix a sidewall puncture. Cannot fix a blowout. Cannot fix any hole larger than about a quarter inch. Cannot fix a rim-damaging pothole strike. Cannot fix a separated tread. Those are the failure modes most likely to leave you stuck, and the kit is useless against all of them.[4]

You'd think NHTSA would mandate spare tires. It doesn't. In 2008, Maryland asked NHTSA whether sealant kits qualified as a "technological improvement" that could legally replace spare tires under state law. NHTSA politely declined to answer, noting it has no federal requirement that vehicles carry spare tires at all.[5] The absence of regulation is the regulation. Automakers can ship you a $45,000 SUV with no way to change a flat, and that is fully compliant with federal motor vehicle safety standards.

Meanwhile, the shoulder of the highway remains one of the most dangerous places an American driver can stand. The AAA Foundation for Traffic Safety identified 123 roadside assistance providers who were struck and killed while working between 2015 and 2021, nearly four times as many as national crash data alone captured. Eighty-nine percent of those deaths occurred at locations with speed limits of 55 mph or higher.[6] Those are professionals with flashing lights, reflective vests, and positioning experience. A Honda CR-V owner in a dark sweater wrestling with a sealant nozzle they've never seen before has none of those advantages.

If you own one of the recalled Hondas, the fix is free: dealers replace the nozzle and sealant bottle with updated parts. But here is what the recall doesn't fix. It doesn't put a spare tire in your trunk. After the dealer appointment, you will still own a vehicle that cannot change its own flat tire for any failure more serious than a punctured tread. You'll still be one sidewall gash away from a tow truck and a 45-minute wait on a highway shoulder where the speed differential between you and passing traffic is 70 mph.

What you should do: Open your trunk right now, before you need to. If you find a sealant kit instead of a spare, decide today whether you're comfortable with that. If you're not, aftermarket spare tire kits run $250 to $400 and fit most vehicles with cargo well space. For the recalled Hondas specifically, check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls. And if you're shopping for a new car, ask the salesperson one question before you sign: does this vehicle have a spare tire? The best time to discover you don't have one is in the showroom. The worst time is on the shoulder of I-95 at 11 p.m., reading 6-point instructions by phone flashlight, while a can of goo prepares to shoot its cap at your face.
