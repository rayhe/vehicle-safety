# Research Notes: FMVSS 127 Cyclist AEB Exclusion

## Angle
The 2029 federal AEB mandate (FMVSS 127) requires cars to detect and brake for pedestrians. It explicitly excludes cyclist detection, despite cyclist fatalities rising 86% since 2010. The EU mandates cyclist AEB by July 2026. Zero automakers told NHTSA cyclist AEB was infeasible. Congress is trying to fix it with HR 7353 (Magnus White Act).

## Journalist
Mia Crumplezone — Safety Engineering Editor. This is squarely her beat: safety tech, vehicle design, safety standards.

## Key Data Points

### Cyclist fatality crisis
- 1,166 cyclists killed in 2023 — up 86% since 2010 (NHTSA FARS)
- 45% of fatal cyclist crashes are parallel-path (same direction, not crossing) — pedestrian AEB doesn't address this
- Pedestrian fatalities also rose ~50% since 2013 in the US (IIHS/GHSA data)

### The regulatory gap
- FMVSS 127 (finalized April 2024): mandates AEB + pedestrian AEB by September 2029
- **Explicitly excludes cyclist detection** despite record cyclist fatalities
- EU General Safety Regulation 2: mandates cyclist AEB on ALL new vehicles by July 2026
- US falls at least 3 years behind European safety standards
- NHTSA published ANPRM on cyclist AEB, received comments, but has NOT issued a rule

### Technology readiness
- 20-29% crash reduction: Two Swedish real-world studies (2023, 2025) found ~20% reduction in cyclist crash risk for vehicles with cyclist AEB
- IIHS found 29% reduction in parallel-path crashes with cyclist AEB
- 28,600 police-reported crashes addressable annually (IIHS analysis of 159,474 crashes)
- 600 fatal bicycle crashes per year could be addressed
- Waymo: 83% fewer cyclist injury crashes vs human drivers (127M miles)
- No automakers told NHTSA cyclist AEB was infeasible when asked

### Cost analysis
- Best case: $23-30/vehicle (software upgrade where pedestrian AEB hardware already exists)
- Midrange: $82-200/vehicle (camera/sensor upgrades needed)
- NHTSA's own AEB cost estimate: $82.15 per vehicle
- Net lifetime benefits: $5.8-7.3 billion from base AEB rule
- Benefit-to-cost ratio: ~17:1
- Cost per life saved: well below DOT's $11.6M Value of Statistical Life

### Which automakers already have it
- GM: Cyclist AEB on ~20 models but NOT on highest-volume trucks or entry-level vehicles
- Tesla: Model Y first to pass all new NHTSA ADAS tests (including pedestrian AEB)
- Stellantis (Ram/Dodge/Chrysler): No confirmed standard cyclist detection
- Jeep (Grand Cherokee, Compass, Wagoneer): Offers it but not standard

### HR 7353 — Magnus White and Safe Streets for Everyone Act of 2026
- Named for Magnus White, 17-year-old cyclist training for Team USA, struck and killed by driver who never saw him
- Requires detection of vulnerable road users (bicyclists, motorcyclists, wheelchair users)
- Compliance deadline: September 1, 2029 (same as FMVSS 127)
- Passed House Energy & Commerce Subcommittee by voice vote Feb 10, 2026

## Novel Contribution
Cross-referencing the FMVSS 127 regulatory text with cyclist fatality trends, EU regulatory timelines, and automaker cost data to show: the technology exists, the cost is trivial, the deaths are mounting, and the US rule deliberately excluded cyclists. The cost of the exclusion can be calculated: ~600 preventable cyclist deaths per year × 3-year delay behind EU = ~1,800 deaths during the regulatory gap.

## Strongest Counterargument
Current cyclist AEB systems show limited effectiveness in darkness, which is when a disproportionate share of fatal cyclist crashes occur (>70% of pedestrian deaths at night; similar for cyclists). The mandate might create false confidence in daytime-only capability.

## Limitations
- The 20-29% crash reduction figures come from limited real-world data (Sweden)
- US cycling infrastructure and behavior patterns differ from European contexts
- "Addressable" crashes don't equal prevented crashes — AEB has false negative rates
- Cost estimates vary widely ($23-200/vehicle) depending on existing hardware

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES. Active legislation (HR 7353), named for a dead teenager, with concrete data showing the technology works, costs almost nothing, and NHTSA chose not to require it. The EU is doing it now. The regulatory exclusion is a policy choice with a body count.
