# Research: The Trillion-Dollar Crash Tax

## News Hook
- **"The $1.77 Trillion Wreck"** — Avian Law Group data report released June 5, 2026
- Motor vehicle crashes cost the U.S. economy $1.77 trillion in total societal harm
- $1,000+ per American per year in crash costs
- 39,254 killed and 2.42 million injured in 2024
- Post-pandemic speeding violations up 55.5% despite only 1.2% more miles driven
- DUI rising across ALL age groups — 66-90 year olds up 44.8% since 2019
- Key finding: "A decades-long failure to translate vehicle safety advances into reduced injuries"

- NHTSA DOT Value of Statistical Life: $12.5M per fatality (2024 guidance)
- NHTSA 2015 comprehensive crash cost study: $836B (2010 dollars)

### Key Findings
- **Top 5 models = $48.5B/year** (20% of total from just 5 models)
- **Two pickups (Silverado + F-150) = $23.5B/year** — more than all 17 sports cars combined ($7.7B)
- Total across all 337 models: $239B/year in fatality costs alone
- That's $724 per American per year — just from deaths, not injuries
- **Sedans cost $337.60/American/yr** (most expensive class despite lower per-VMT rates — volume)
- **Pickups cost $157.55/American/yr** (only 26 models, but massive fleet sizes)

### Novel Contribution
Nobody has calculated the per-model "crash tax" — what each vehicle model costs every American per year in fatality costs. The framing shifts the conversation from "which cars are dangerous" to "which cars cost YOU money."

The headline paradox: the Honda Accord and Toyota Camry — perennial "safe, reliable" family sedans — cost Americans more than all sports cars, all vans, and most SUVs. Safety reputation ≠ societal cost.

### Limitations
- FARS only captures fatalities, not injuries — real costs are much higher
- VSL method assigns equal value to each life regardless of age/productivity
- Fleet size matters enormously — high-volume models dominate even with moderate rates
- Does not capture other-party costs (pedestrians killed by, not in, these vehicles)
- 10-year window smooths over model year improvements

### Strongest Counterargument
Volume is the real driver here, not danger. The Silverado and F-150 top the list because they're America's best-selling vehicles, not because they're disproportionately deadly. Per-VMT, an Accord (rate 3.07) is more dangerous than a Silverado (rate 1.25). The "crash tax" framing conflates popularity with peril. A fair analysis would normalize by fleet size — and when you do, the Chevy Tracker (rate 7.83) is 6x more expensive per mile than the F-150.

### Actionable Insight
If you want to lower YOUR crash tax, buy a newer vehicle in a lower-rate class. SUVs built after 2015 have half the fatality rate of sedans. The Tesla Model Y (rate 0.03), Kia Telluride (0.04), and Mazda CX-30 (0.12) have fatality costs under $1 per American per year each. The cheapest crash tax comes from newer, heavier vehicles with modern safety tech — which creates its own perverse incentive toward larger vehicles.
