# Research Notes: Distraction Ban Two-Year Decay

## Angle
America has banned texting while driving in 49 states. Hands-free laws cover 31 states + DC. And distracted driving deaths haven't budged. A 2025 Michigan State University study (Gupta et al.) provides the missing piece: cellphone ban effectiveness decays after two years. Worse, texting-only bans — the kind 19 states rely on — are POSITIVELY correlated with fatal crashes. The ban didn't just stop working. In some states, it's actively making things worse.

## Kill Test
- Genuinely surprising? YES. Most people assume phone bans help. The finding that texting-only bans are positively correlated with crashes (likely through concealment behavior — hiding the phone below dash) is counterintuitive and alarming.
- Novel angle after 353 articles? YES. Existing coverage: `distracted-driving-undercount-3x.html` (about the 3x undercount), `speed-phone-compound-risk.html` (about speed+phone compounding), `speeding-phone-use-iihs-correlation.html` (IIHS correlation). None cover the ban-effectiveness-decay phenomenon.
- 3+ primary sources? YES (see below).

## Primary Sources

1. **NHTSA 2024 Distracted Driving Data** — 3,208 killed, 315,000+ injured, 8% of fatal crashes. 32,717 deaths 2015-2024. Source: nhtsa.gov/risky-driving/distracted-driving

2. **Gupta, Megat-Johari & Megat-Johari (2025)** — "Evaluating the impacts of cellphone use policies on cellphone-distracted fatal crashes." Journal of Safety Research, 94:329-339. PMID: 40930647. Key findings:
   - Handheld bans are negatively correlated with distracted fatal crashes (they work)
   - Texting bans alone are POSITIVELY correlated with crashes (they make things worse)
   - Combined handheld + texting bans produce the greatest safety benefit
   - Effectiveness of ALL ban types tapers off after 2 years
   - Two-way random effects negative binomial model, state-level monthly data, 2011-2021

3. **NHTSA 2024 Full-Year Estimate** — 39,345 total traffic deaths, fatality rate 1.20/100M VMT. 2023 final: 40,901 deaths. Source: nhtsa.gov press release.

4. **NHTSA Understanding the Problem (Dingus et al., 2016 / SHRP2 NDS data)** — Handheld phone use increases crash risk 3.6x. Texting: 6.1x. Dialing: 12.2x. Reading/writing: 9.9x.

5. **Colorado DOT (2025)** — Michigan's 2023 handheld ban led to 12.8% decrease in distracted driving crashes within first year. Colorado joined as 30th state on Jan 1, 2025.

6. **Autoblog / Mercury Insurance (2025)** — 31 states + DC have full hands-free laws. 19 states without one: Alaska, Arkansas, Florida (partial — school/work zones only), Kansas, Kentucky, Mississippi, Montana, Nebraska, New Mexico, North Carolina, North Dakota, Oklahoma, South Carolina, South Dakota, Texas (partial — school zones), Utah, Wisconsin, Wyoming.

## Novel Contribution
1. **The Two-Year Decay Curve:** Most states enacted their texting bans 2010-2017. If effectiveness decays after 2 years (Gupta et al.), the majority of bans in America have already "expired" in terms of behavioral impact. Only the most recent hands-free adopters (Colorado 2025, Ohio 2023, Pennsylvania 2022) are still in their effective window.
2. **The Texting Ban Paradox:** 19 states have texting-only bans with no handheld restriction. The Gupta study shows these bans are positively correlated with fatal crashes — possibly because drivers hide phones below the dashboard to avoid detection, creating MORE dangerous distraction (eyes further from road for longer).
3. **The Flat Line:** 2015: ~3,477 distraction deaths. 2024: 3,208. A decade of legislative action, enforcement campaigns, and 32,717 deaths later, the annual number is essentially unchanged.

## Strongest Counterargument
The flat trendline might mask real progress: phone usage while driving has INCREASED dramatically (smartphones, social media, video apps), so holding deaths flat could represent the bans preventing an even worse spike. Without them, the number might be 5,000+/year. The study covers 2011-2021 — a period when smartphone adoption went from ~35% to ~85%. The counterfactual is unknowable but plausible.

## Limitations
- Gupta et al. covers 2011-2021; post-2021 policy changes (several new handheld bans) aren't captured
- NHTSA's 3,208 figure is a known undercount (~3x per our earlier analysis and NHTSA's own admission of classification difficulty)
- The "positive correlation" finding for texting bans doesn't prove causation — it could reflect states with worse baseline crash rates being more likely to adopt texting bans
- State-level analysis doesn't capture local enforcement variation (NYC vs rural upstate NY)

## Methodology Transparency
- 32,717 total = NHTSA reported cumulative 2015-2024
- Annual trend: NHTSA publishes distraction-affected fatal crash counts annually in FARS
- Ban enactment dates from GHSA, cross-referenced with state legislation records
- "Expired" bans = those enacted 2+ years before 2024 in texting-only states
