The Gap

Tomorrow, Every New Car in Europe Must See Pedestrians Coming. America’s Brakes Are Frozen.

Mia Crumplezone • Safety Engineering Editor
July 6, 2026

On July 7, 2026, the European Union’s General Safety Regulation enters its final phase. Every existing vehicle model sold in Europe must now carry pedestrian and cyclist automatic emergency braking, camera-based driver distraction monitoring, and intelligent speed assist that reactivates every time you turn the key. Not optional. Not a trim-level upgrade. Standard equipment, on every car, effective tomorrow.

360
Lives per year NHTSA says its own AEB mandate would save. That mandate is currently frozen under regulatory review

The American equivalent, FMVSS No. 127, was finalized in May 2024 with a September 2029 compliance deadline.[1] Five months later, the Alliance for Automotive Innovation—representing GM, Toyota, Volkswagen, and most of the industry—sued to block it, calling the requirement to stop for vehicles at 62 mph “practically impossible with available technology.”[2] Three days after that lawsuit was filed, the incoming administration imposed a regulatory freeze. The D.C. Circuit appeal has sat in abeyance since February 2025. And in March 2026, the Department of Transportation filed a status report announcing it was preparing a proposed rule to extend the compliance timeline by two additional years, pushing real-world deployment to 2031 at the earliest.[3]

Meanwhile, in Brussels, the regulation went live on schedule. No freeze, no abeyance, no proposed extension: Phase One hit in July 2022, Phase Two in July 2024, and Phase Three arrives tomorrow, on time, covering every model still on sale.[4]

The arithmetic is not ambiguous. NHTSA projected FMVSS 127 would save 360 lives and prevent 24,000 injuries annually.[1] That projection assumed a 2029 start date. If the proposed two-year extension holds, the US mandate won’t bite until five full years after Europe’s, and the cumulative toll of the delay, by NHTSA’s own model, reaches 1,800 lives and 120,000 injuries. These are not advocacy estimates pulled from a press release. They come directly from the agency tasked with preventing exactly this outcome, published in the Federal Register alongside the rule it has since frozen.

FARS data from 2014 through 2023 sharpens the picture. Pickups and SUVs were involved in 51.7 percent of all fatal crashes tracked across 337 vehicle models. In those 173,696 crashes, 85,661 deaths occurred outside the truck or SUV: pedestrians, cyclists, occupants of smaller vehicles who were on the wrong end of a mass differential that no crumple zone can equalize.[5] The Ford F-150 alone appeared in 20,066 fatal crash events over the decade; in 10,872 of them, the person who died was not inside the truck. The Chevrolet Silverado: 19,732 fatal crashes, 10,141 deaths outside the cab. The vehicles with the tallest front ends generate the most outside-vehicle fatalities, and those front ends have gotten taller every model year for two decades.

IIHS research quantifies the mechanism precisely: at 27 mph, a median-height pickup has an 83 percent chance of causing moderate pedestrian injuries and a 62 percent chance of serious ones, compared to 60 percent and 30 percent for a median-height car.[6] Separately, vehicles with large driver-side blind zones (the kind generated by thick A-pillars and tall hoods) are 70 percent more likely to strike pedestrians while turning left.[7] Pedestrian AEB exists to catch exactly these scenarios: the 45 mph detection threshold in FMVSS 127 covers the speed range where most urban pedestrian fatalities occur, and the nighttime detection requirement addresses the fact that 77 percent of pedestrian deaths happen after dark.

US pedestrian fatalities hit roughly 7,500 in 2023, the highest level since the early 1980s and a 77 percent increase since 2010, a period during which vehicle occupant deaths remained roughly flat.[5] The rise tracks almost perfectly with the shift from sedans to trucks and SUVs. Europe experienced no equivalent spike, and the regulatory divergence now widening between the two markets will only accelerate the gap.

The counterargument, at full strength

The auto industry’s objection is not trivial. The 62 mph vehicle-detection threshold in FMVSS 127 does push the edge of current sensor fusion, particularly in degraded conditions: rain, glare, occluded radar returns from highway clutter. Pedestrian AEB at 45 mph in darkness requires cameras and radar to distinguish a person from a mailbox, a shopping cart, a road sign, at closing speeds that leave under two seconds of reaction time. False positives (phantom braking events on highways) are not hypothetical; they have caused real crashes.[8] And the EU’s requirement applies in a market where average vehicle speed, average vehicle size, and average road width are all smaller than their American counterparts, which means the engineering challenge is genuinely harder in the US context.

None of that changes the arithmetic. Europe decided the engineering difficulty was worth 30 percent fewer pedestrian fatalities now, with performance requirements that tighten over time. America decided the engineering difficulty justified waiting. The bodies that accumulate during the wait are real, NHTSA counted them in advance, and we are now watching the count begin.

What you can actually do

If you are buying a car today: check whether it already has pedestrian AEB. Most 2024+ models from Toyota, Hyundai, Honda, Subaru, and Volvo include it as standard equipment—not because the US requires it, but because Euro NCAP penalizes vehicles that lack it and manufacturers build to the stricter standard. Look for “pedestrian detection” or “PAEB” in the spec sheet. If it isn’t listed, the car cannot see the person in the crosswalk. If you drive a pre-2020 truck or SUV with a hood height above 40 inches, you are operating a vehicle that IIHS data says is 70 percent more likely to hit someone you cannot see. That is not an insult; it is a measurement, and it means your margin for inattention at intersections is narrower than you think.

Limitations

FARS tracks fatal crashes only. AEB’s primary benefit is crash avoidance and speed reduction in near-misses that never appear in fatality statistics, so NHTSA’s 360-lives-per-year figure likely underestimates total harm reduction. Our FARS-derived “outside vehicle” death counts combine pedestrians, cyclists, and occupants of other vehicles; we cannot isolate pedestrian-only fatalities per vehicle model from the aggregate data. Fleet composition projections assume current SUV/pickup adoption trends continue; a rapid EV-driven shift to lower-hood platforms would narrow the pedestrian risk gap independently of regulation. The EU and US test protocols are not identical, so direct performance comparisons between GSR2-compliant and FMVSS 127-compliant systems will require separate evaluation once both standards are enforced.
