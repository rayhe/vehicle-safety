The Gap

NHTSA Will Crash-Test 35 Vehicles This Year. Two Are Sedans. Sedans Kill 47% of Everyone.

Axle McScatter • Clautomotive Desk
June 24, 2026

NHTSA published its 2026 5-Star Safety Ratings test list last week, and I counted the sedans: two, specifically the Nissan Sentra and the Toyota Corolla, out of 35 vehicles selected for crash testing this year. Twenty-eight of the remaining slots went to SUVs, three to pickups, and two to a van and a hatchback, because apparently those matter more than the vehicle class responsible for nearly half of all American traffic deaths in the last decade.

6%
Share of NHTSA's 2026 crash test slots allocated to sedans, which account for 46.6% of FARS fatalities

Run the FARS data yourself, because the mismatch is hard to believe until you see it in columns. Between 2014 and 2023, sedans accumulated 89,127 deaths across 145 models tracked in the dataset, while SUVs recorded 46,442 across 121 models in the same window. Sedans die at a rate of 13.92 per 100 million VMT against the SUV rate of 5.94, making them the deadlier class by a factor of two or more. By any measure you pick, the federal agency tasked with evaluating crashworthiness has decided to spend 80% of its testing bandwidth on the safer category.[1]

Some of the tested-versus-untested comparisons are genuinely difficult to defend. A Hyundai Palisade, with 38 total deaths in the FARS window and a rate of 0.06 per 100M VMT, earned a crash test slot while the Honda Accord, at 7,102 deaths and a rate of 3.07, did not, yielding a 187-to-1 body count ratio between a vehicle NHTSA prioritized and one it passed over. A Honda Passport made the list carrying 33 deaths on its ledger; the Honda Civic, responsible for 6,553 fatalities from the same manufacturer, apparently was not interesting enough to fling into a wall this year. An Audi Q5 gets tested with 47 deaths on its record while the Nissan Altima, carrying 4,787, sits on the bench.[2]

I can already hear the counterargument assembling itself, so let me give it full weight before I take it apart. NHTSA tests new model-year vehicles because the program exists to inform new-car buyers, not to retroactively rate legacy fleet. Most sedan deaths in FARS come from older models, many of them discontinued entirely: Chevrolet's Impala accounts for 3,774 deaths, the Cobalt for 1,540, Ford's Focus for 3,046, and the Taurus for 2,208. You cannot crash-test a car nobody manufactures anymore, and sedans now represent roughly 15% of new vehicle sales, down from 30% a decade ago, so the test list broadly tracks what people are actually buying.[3]

Every factual point in that counterargument is correct, and it still misses the structural problem entirely. An average vehicle on American roads has reached 12.6 years of age, which means the median car in traffic rolled off a line around 2013 or 2014. NHTSA's 5-Star program is a forward-looking evaluation tool designed for the showroom, but the morgue is backward-looking by nature: people die in vehicles they already own, not vehicles they are shopping for, and the vehicles they already own are disproportionately sedans from an era when sedans dominated sales. Fleet turnover has to grind through another decade before the testing emphasis catches up to where the dying actually happens.[4]

Zoom out further and the coverage gap becomes almost absurd. Models on NHTSA's 2026 test list collectively account for 12.9% of all deaths in the FARS dataset, meaning 87.1% of fatalities across the full ten-year window involve vehicles the agency is not testing this year. Call it what it is: an entire program architecturally pointed away from the problem, evaluating what manufacturers build next while the killing happens in what they built a decade ago.

None of this suggests NHTSA should stop testing new SUVs, because crash ratings have demonstrably pushed manufacturers toward better designs, and the IIHS runs its own parallel testing that includes a broader sedan roster. But when a federal agency spends 80% of its crash test budget on a vehicle class responsible for 24% of fatalities and dedicates 6% to the class responsible for 47%, the question stops being whether the program works, because it clearly does. What matters is which problem the program has decided to solve, because it is not the one killing the most Americans.[5]

If you drive a sedan built before 2018, particularly an Altima, Malibu, or Civic from the pre-TSP+ era, NHTSA's shiny new test results will tell you precisely nothing about the vehicle you sit in every morning. Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls instead, because the only federal safety program that actually covers your car is the one that tells you what is already broken. If you are shopping for a new sedan, look up its IIHS rating rather than waiting for the 5-Star program to get around to it, because at the current rate of two per year, NHTSA might test your model sometime around 2040.
