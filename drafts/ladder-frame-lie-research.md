# Research: The Ladder Frame Lie — Body-on-Frame SUVs vs Unibody Crossovers

## Angle
Body-on-frame (BOF) SUVs are marketed as tough, rugged, safe. The steel ladder frame underneath supposedly protects you. But FARS data shows BOF SUVs are 3-12x deadlier per 100M VMT than unibody crossovers from the SAME manufacturer. The rugged image is literally killing people.

## Kill Test
- Is this genuinely surprising? YES. Most consumers believe bigger/heavier/truck-based = safer. The data says the opposite for occupant fatality rates.
- Novel after 142 articles? YES. We haven't done a construction-type analysis. We've covered classes (sedan vs SUV), brands, individual models, but never the engineering question of body construction.
- Original contribution: FARS cross-tabulation of BOF vs unibody within same-brand pairs eliminates the brand/price confounders.

## Primary FARS Data — Same-Brand BOF vs Unibody Pairs

| Comparison | BOF Vehicle | Rate | Unibody Vehicle | Rate | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Chevy full-size SUV | Tahoe | 2.49 | Traverse | 0.20 | 12.5x |
| GMC large SUV | Yukon | 2.55 | Acadia | 0.30 | 8.5x |
| Ford mid-size | Explorer (mostly BOF era) | 1.54 | Edge | 0.46 | 3.3x |
| Nissan mid-size | Xterra | 1.39 | Murano | 0.43 | 3.2x |
| Nissan family | Pathfinder (mixed) | 0.93 | Rogue | 0.35 | 2.7x |
| Ford large | Expedition | 2.31 | Explorer (newer) | 1.54 | 1.5x |
| **COUNTEREXAMPLE** | Jeep Grand Cherokee (BOF) | 0.51 | Jeep Cherokee (unibody) | 1.73 | 0.3x (GC safer!) |

Key insight: The Tahoe and Traverse are sold by the same company, often at the same dealership. The Tahoe costs more and weighs more. Yet it's 12.5x deadlier per mile driven.

## Counterexample: Jeep Grand Cherokee
The Grand Cherokee (body-on-frame) has a LOWER death rate (0.51) than the unibody Cherokee (1.73). This is the honest strongest counterargument. Possible explanations:
- Grand Cherokee fleet is newer on average (current gen is very modern)
- Higher price = wealthier/older/safer demographics
- Cherokee was a budget model attracting different driver profiles
- Newer Grand Cherokee has extensive active safety tech

## External Sources

### Source 1: Ossiander et al. (2014) — Peer-reviewed study
"Crash fatality risk and unibody versus body-on-frame structure in SUVs"
Accident Analysis & Prevention, 2014;70:267-72. PMID: 24821629
- Finding: Unibody SUVs pose LESS danger to other vehicle occupants while providing comparable self-protection
- Methodology: Case-control study, 1995-2008 US crashes, MY 1996-2006

### Source 2: IIHS vehicle size and weight research
https://www.iihs.org/topics/vehicle-size-and-weight
- IIHS consistently finds that vehicle size/weight matters but STRUCTURE matters more
- Crumple zones in unibody construction absorb energy; rigid ladder frames transfer it to occupants

## Limitations
- Fleet age confound: BOF SUVs (Tahoe, Yukon) have much older fleet averages than newer crossovers (Traverse, Acadia)
- Demographic confound: BOF SUVs may attract different driver profiles (rural, higher speed roads)
- The FARS data doesn't separate BOF-era Explorer from post-2020 unibody Explorer
- VMT estimates introduce ±15% uncertainty for lower-volume models
- This is observational data, not a controlled experiment

## Actionable Takeaway
If you're shopping for a family SUV: the unibody crossover is almost always safer per mile driven. The Traverse (0.20), Acadia (0.30), Edge (0.46), Murano (0.43) all dramatically outperform their body-on-frame stablemates. Check specific model ratings at iihs.org/ratings. The "rugged = safe" assumption kills people.
