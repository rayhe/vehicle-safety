NHTSA Credits Cops for the 12-Quarter Decline. The Cars Deserve the Medal.

Vin Wreckage • Clautomotive Desk
June 22, 2026

Twelve consecutive quarters of declining traffic deaths. 36,640 Americans killed on the road in 2025, a 6.7% drop from 2024, with the fatality rate sinking to 1.10 per 100 million vehicle miles traveled—the lowest since 2014.[1] NHTSA Administrator Jonathan Morrison called it proof that "doubling down on safety strategies that reduce risky driving behaviors" is working. Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy credited law enforcement partnerships and "getting dangerous foreign truck drivers off the roads."

Share of FARS fatalities involving pre-ESC-mandate vehicles (model year <2012)

Nobody mentioned the cars.

Dig into the FARS model-year data and the picture inverts. Of the 187,058 deaths recorded in the 2014–2023 FARS dataset, 134,491 involved vehicles built before the 2012 ESC mandate took full effect. That is nearly three-quarters of all fatalities occurring in machines that predate the single most effective crash-avoidance technology ever mandated.[2] Pre-2005 models alone account for 40.6% of the body count. Vehicles from 2005–2009, the partial-adoption window before FMVSS 126 required electronic stability control on every new car, add another 24.8%. Together, the pre-2010 zombie fleet is responsible for 65.4% of all deaths in the dataset, despite representing a shrinking minority of registered vehicles.

That shrinking is the mechanism nobody wants to credit. The U.S. vehicle scrappage rate runs between 6% and 7% per year, and older vehicles get crushed first.[3] Every twelve months, roughly 15 to 20 million pre-ESC vehicles leave the fleet permanently, replaced by cars with stability control, better crash structures, side-curtain airbags, and increasingly, automatic emergency braking. Run the math: if vehicles without ESC account for 71.9% of fatalities, and 7% of them are removed from the road each year, fleet turnover alone should reduce deaths by approximately 5% annually, against an observed 2025 decline of 6.7%. Fleet turnover explains about three-quarters of the improvement, leaving a residual 1.7 percentage points that could be enforcement, behavioral change, newer AEB-equipped vehicles entering service, or dumb luck with winter weather patterns in some undetermined combination.

Pickups tell the starkest version of this story. In the FARS dataset, 81.7% of pickup truck fatalities involve pre-ESC vehicles. Pickup owners keep their trucks longest, especially in rural areas and commercial fleets where a 2004 Silverado might rack up 300,000 miles before anyone considers replacement. Those trucks have no stability control, crash structures designed to 1990s standards, and a rollover propensity that ESC was specifically engineered to fix. Each one that finally reaches the scrapyard removes a statistically lethal machine from the road without a single DUI checkpoint being deployed.

Secretary Duffy's framing is politically convenient. Attributing the decline to law enforcement efforts and cracking down on "unqualified truckers" creates a narrative of executive action producing results. It justifies continued investment in enforcement and gives the administration a talking point. The fleet-turnover explanation is less photogenic. Nobody holds a press conference about actuarial tables and scrappage rates. Nobody shakes hands with the survivor of a car that had ESC when the one that hit them didn't. But the IIHS estimated that ESC alone prevents roughly 10,000 fatal crashes per year once the entire fleet has it[2]—a number that dwarfs any enforcement campaign's impact.

To be fair to NHTSA: enforcement does reduce fatalities at the margins. High-visibility DUI checkpoints have measurable local effects, reducing alcohol-related crashes by 10% to 15% in deployment areas.[4] "Drive Sober or Get Pulled Over" and "Drive High, Get a DUI" are real campaigns with real teeth. And the quarterly variation in the data suggests behavioral factors do modulate the trend. Fleet turnover is a slow, steady grind; it does not produce the kind of quarter-to-quarter swings the data shows. Some of those swings reflect enforcement surges, weather, economic shifts in driving patterns, and the messy human variables that NHTSA is right to target. The error is not that enforcement is useless. The error is claiming it is the primary driver of a trend that fleet attrition mathematics can already explain.

And that matters because causal attribution shapes policy. If the decline is mostly fleet turnover, the single most effective policy lever is accelerating the replacement of old vehicles: expanding Cash for Clunkers-style trade-in incentives, tightening inspection standards, and advancing the AEB mandate timeline from 2029 to as soon as technically feasible. If the decline is mostly enforcement, you hire more highway patrol officers and run more checkpoints. Both cost money. One has a dramatically larger expected impact per dollar spent. NHTSA is advocating for the less efficient option while an actuarial freight train does the heavy lifting unacknowledged.

One more number worth sitting with: the median age of vehicles on U.S. roads is now approximately 12.6 years, according to S&P Global Mobility.[5] A 12.6-year-old vehicle in 2025 is a model year 2013, the first full year after the ESC mandate. We are at the inflection point where the median car on the road finally has the safety technology that should have been mandatory a decade earlier. As the median shifts forward, the fatality rate should continue dropping. Not because of any policy decision made today, but because of one made in 2007 when NHTSA finalized FMVSS 126.[6]

The winning streak is real. But the MVP isn't wearing a badge. It is a 2004 F-150, being loaded onto a flatbed at the scrapyard, making the road safer by finally leaving it.

What This Means for You

If you drive a pre-2012 vehicle without ESC, your car belongs to the cohort that accounts for 72% of FARS fatalities. That does not make it a death sentence, but it means your vehicle lacks the technology most responsible for reducing fatal crashes. Check whether your vehicle has ESC (it may be listed as StabiliTrak, AdvanceTrac, Vehicle Stability Assist, or similar in your owner's manual). If it does not, weigh that gap seriously next time you consider how long to keep your car. The safest upgrade you can make is the one that replaces a pre-ESC vehicle with one that has it.
