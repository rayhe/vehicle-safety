# Research: The Hyundai Elantra — Affordable Killer

## Angle
The Hyundai Elantra has 2,407 FARS deaths (2014-2023) — more than the Jeep Cherokee, Dodge Charger, or any Chrysler product. Its death rate of 1.5 per 100M VMT is well above the sedan average. But its impairment rate is BELOW average (18.6% vs 20.4% sedan class). This isn't a drunk driver problem. It's a car problem.

## Novel Contribution
Cross-tabulation: The Elantra's below-average impairment + above-average death rate = the "sober killer" signal. When you remove impairment as an explanation, what's left is the vehicle itself. Additionally, the 2013 model year has 338 deaths (14% of all Elantra deaths from a single MY) — the largest single-year spike relative to the model's baseline. This correlates with Hyundai's post-MPG-scandal sales push.

## The Thesis
The Elantra is the empirical counterpoint to "cheap cars are just as safe as expensive ones." At $16K-$20K MSRP (2013 pricing), it attracted exactly the demographic that FARS over-represents: younger, first-time buyers who drive more miles in less-forgiving conditions (commuting, late night, distracted). But even controlling for impairment — the strongest behavioral variable — the Elantra still kills at above-average rates.

## Strongest Counterargument
Sales volume. Hyundai sold ~240K+ Elantras in 2013 alone, making it one of the best-selling compacts. High fleet numbers naturally inflate raw death counts. And the 1.5 rate, while above average for sedans, isn't in the "extreme danger" zone (compare to Cobalt at 5.1 or Cavalier at 2.43). The Elantra may be "slightly worse than average" rather than genuinely dangerous. The 2013 MY spike could also partly reflect the data window (2013 MYs had maximum exposure during 2014-2023).

## Limitations
- FARS captures fatal crashes only — the Elantra's injury crash rate is unknown
- Fleet estimates use sales proxies, not registration data
- VMT estimates assume average driving patterns; Elantra demographics may drive more miles than average, deflating the true rate
- The MPG scandal correlation is suggestive, not causal — we can't prove discount pricing led to riskier buyer demographics
