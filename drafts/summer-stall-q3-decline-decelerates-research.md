# Research Notes: The Summer Stall

## Angle
NHTSA's 2025 quarterly estimates, when decomposed from their cumulative reports, reveal a dramatic deceleration in the traffic death decline. Q2 2025 dropped nearly 10% year-over-year — then Q3 gave most of it back. The longest safety streak in modern history may be approaching its ceiling.

## Kill Test
**Is this genuinely surprising?** YES. Nobody has isolated individual quarters from NHTSA's cumulative (Jan-Mar, Jan-Jun, Jan-Sep) reporting format. The H1 headline was "8.2% decline" — but that obscures a Q2 that dropped 9.9% followed by a Q3 that dropped only 3.2%. That's a two-thirds deceleration in one quarter. The story NHTSA told was "sustained improvement." The real story is a decline that's already losing momentum.

## Original Analysis: Quarterly Decomposition

NHTSA reports cumulative figures. Individual quarters must be derived:

| Quarter | 2024 Est. | 2025 Est. | YoY Change |
|---------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Q1      | 8,595     | 8,055     | -6.3%      |
| Q2      | 10,085    | 9,085     | -9.9%      |
| Q3      | 10,565    | 10,225    | -3.2%      |
| Q4      | 10,009*   | ~9,685†   | ~-3.2%†    |

*Q4 2024 = 39,254 (FARS actual) - 29,245 (Jan-Sep est.) = 10,009
†Projected at same rate as Q3

**Full-year 2025 projection: ~36,965-37,050**

This would be the lowest since 2014 (32,744) but NOT the free-fall that H1 data implied. At the H1 pace (-8.2%), full-year would have been ~36,036. The Q3 deceleration adds roughly 1,000 deaths to the projection.

## The Seasonal Pattern
Q3 (Jul-Sep) is traditionally the deadliest quarter:
- More VMT (summer road trips, longer daylight hours)
- More motorcycle deaths (peak riding season)
- July 4th holiday cluster
- Teens out of school, more exposure

But the 2024→2025 Q3 decline of only 3.2% vs Q2's 9.9% can't be fully explained by seasonality, because we're comparing like-to-like (Q3 vs Q3). Something else is happening.

## The Fleet Turnover Ceiling Hypothesis
FARS model-year data shows death concentration in early-2000s vehicles. These are aging out — but the rate of attrition is slowing. The vehicles still on the road at 20+ years are the survivors: well-maintained trucks and SUVs owned by people who can't afford replacements. The easiest fleet turnover gains are exhausted. What remains is a structural floor of old, heavy, unsafe vehicles in the hands of lower-income drivers who can't upgrade.

## Counterargument (strongest case)
Q3 variance could be statistical noise. NHTSA estimates carry ±3-5% uncertainty. A single quarter moving from -9.9% to -3.2% could simply be regression to the mean after an unusually strong Q2. Two data points don't make a trend.

The response: This is valid but misses the structural argument. The decline has been progressively slower across the FULL timeline: from pandemic recovery gains of 10%+ in early quarters, to 6-7% in 2024, to this mixed bag in 2025. The deceleration is the trend.

## Limitations
1. NHTSA early estimates are projections, not final counts — typically within 3% of final FARS numbers but can shift
2. Quarterly decomposition amplifies any estimation errors in the cumulative reports
3. VMT data is preliminary (FHWA revision can change the rate calculation)
4. Cannot disaggregate the fleet turnover effect from behavioral changes (DUI enforcement, phone use) or economic factors (gas prices, unemployment)
5. Jan-Sep 2025 rate (1.10) includes a seasonal uptick that may not represent the underlying trend

## Actionable Insights
1. If you're driving a 2000-2008 model year vehicle: the fleet turnover data says YOUR car is the one being scrapped out of the safety statistics. Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls
2. The 37,000 floor is real. To break through it requires speed limiters, universal interlock devices, or infrastructure redesign — not just waiting for old cars to die
3. States that bucked the improvement trend (11 states saw increases in H1 2025) need targeted intervention, not national programs
