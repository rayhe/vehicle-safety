# Research: IIHS Whiplash Test Speed Gap

## Angle
The IIHS discontinued its original whiplash test in 2022 because virtually all vehicles earned "good" ratings. It was a solved problem. Except it wasn't — insurance claim data showed occupants of good-rated vehicles were STILL getting whiplash injuries. The new test (launched Feb 2026) adds a 30 mph component to the existing 20 mph test. Result: only 4 of 18 small SUVs earn "good." Three earn "poor." The 10 mph difference exposed a decade of false confidence.

## Kill Test
**Is this genuinely newsworthy?** YES. The IIHS essentially admitted its own test was inadequate for years, and the replacement results show most of America's best-selling small SUVs provide mediocre-to-terrible whiplash protection at realistic speeds. This is a consumer safety story with real purchase implications. Fresh news (Feb 2026 IIHS announcement). Has NOT been covered as an article on this site.

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: IIHS Official Announcement (Feb 2026)
- URL: https://www.iihs.org/news/detail/iihs-launches-new-whiplash-prevention-test
- Key quote: "Neck sprains and strains are the most frequently reported injuries in U.S. auto insurance claims" — IIHS President David Harkey
- Old test discontinued 2022; new test adds 30 mph
- 18 small SUVs tested:
  - GOOD (4): Audi Q3, Hyundai Ioniq 5, Subaru Forester, Toyota RAV4
  - ACCEPTABLE (9): Buick Encore GX, Chevy Equinox, Honda CR-V, Jeep Compass, Kia Sportage, Mercedes GLB, Mitsubishi Outlander, VW Taos, Volvo XC40
  - MARGINAL (2): BMW X1, Nissan Rogue
  - POOR (3): Ford Bronco Sport, Hyundai Tucson, Mazda CX-50
- Marcy Edwards, IIHS senior research engineer, developed the new evaluation
- In poor vehicles: dummy head moved dramatically relative to spine; cervical vertebrae stretching/straightening visible on video
- Tucson: head restraint pushed chin down toward chest
- CX-50: allowed head to slide backward and upward
- Bronco Sport: "particularly poor support for head and spine" — delayed head restraint contact, high velocity difference between pelvis and head

### Source 2: Jalopnik Coverage (Feb 24, 2026)
- URL: https://www.jalopnik.com/2108282/compact-suvs-failed-iihs-whiplash-prevention-test/
- Confirmed IIHS findings, detailed each poor-rated vehicle's specific failure mode
- Notes: rear impacts most common cause of whiplash; whiplash = most common U.S. auto insurance claim

### Source 3: FARS Data (vehicle-safety.org internal, 2014-2023)
- Hyundai Tucson: 669 deaths, 1,472 crashes, 0.454 lethality ratio, 0.34 rate per 100M VMT
- Nissan Rogue (marginal): 968 deaths, 1,777 crashes, 0.545 lethality
- Toyota RAV4 (good): 914 deaths, 1,834 crashes, 0.498 lethality, fleet 3.76M
- Honda CR-V (acceptable): 2,072 deaths, 3,886 crashes, 0.533 lethality
- Subaru Forester (good): 396 deaths, 711 crashes, 0.557 lethality
- Ford Escape (same segment as Bronco Sport): 2,284 deaths, 4,099 crashes, 0.557 lethality
- Mazda CX-5 (related to CX-50): 162 deaths, 375 crashes, 0.432 lethality (best in class)

### IIHS Research Context
- Rear-end crashes account for approximately 30% of all crashes
- IIHS tested seats from 36 vehicles at 3 impact speeds
- Correlated dummy metrics with HLDI (Highway Loss Data Institute) insurance claim rates
- Key metrics: head contact time, T1 vertebra acceleration, pelvis displacement, head moment relative to neck
