# Research: The Honda CR-V's Safety Reputation vs. Its Body Count

## Angle
The Honda CR-V is universally considered one of America's safest compact SUVs. It consistently earns IIHS Top Safety Pick+. Honda's brand cachet reinforces the perception. And yet FARS data reveals a different picture: **2,072 deaths** across the observation period — the second-highest body count among compact SUVs, trailing only the Ford Escape (2,284). Its death rate of 0.53 per 100M VMT is 3rd worst in class. The CR-V's "safe" reputation may be costing lives by discouraging buyers from considering genuinely safer alternatives.

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES. The CR-V is the #2 best-selling SUV in America. Millions of families buy it specifically because they believe it's safe. Quantifying the gap between reputation and FARS reality — and calculating "excess deaths" relative to the actual safest compact SUV — is a novel contribution that could change purchasing decisions.

## Novel Contributions

### 1. "Excess Deaths" Calculation
If every CR-V on the road had the RAV4's death rate (0.19):
- Expected deaths: 0.19 × 39,375 / 100 = 748
- Actual deaths: 2,072
- **Excess deaths: 1,324** — more than 1,300 people who might be alive if they'd bought a RAV4 instead

If the CX-5's rate (0.12):
- Expected: 0.12 × 39,375 / 100 = 473
- **Excess: 1,599**

### 2. Impairment Paradox
CR-V has the LOWEST impairment rate in the compact SUV class (17.6%). This means sober drivers are dying at disproportionate rates. The conventional excuse — "it's the drivers, not the vehicle" — doesn't apply here. CR-V drivers are more sober than CX-5, Forester, Terrain, Sportage, Rogue, and Equinox drivers. Yet they die at higher rates.

### 3. Legacy Fleet Drag
Pre-2005 CR-V model years: 54+59+86+68+92+98+76+102 = 635 deaths (30.6% of total)
Post-2017 model years: 93+66+30+35+16 = 240 deaths (11.6%)
The CR-V's old model years are dragging up its overall numbers. BUT even stripped of legacy data, the CR-V's recent-MY death counts (93 for MY2018, 66 for MY2019) are still higher than the RAV4's equivalent years.

## Strongest Counterargument
The CR-V's higher body count is substantially driven by fleet age composition. With model years dating back to 1997, many CR-Vs in FARS data are 20+ year old vehicles without modern safety features. The RAV4, by comparison, may have a younger fleet average. If you only count post-2015 model years, the gap narrows. The CR-V is not inherently less safe — it's paying for Honda's success in building vehicles that last forever.

## Limitations
- FARS only captures fatal crashes; the CR-V might have excellent injury-crash outcomes we can't see
- Fleet age estimates introduce ±15% uncertainty in VMT and death rate calculations
- We cannot control for driving patterns (urban vs rural, highway vs local)
- "Excess deaths" assumes drivers are interchangeable across vehicles — they're not
