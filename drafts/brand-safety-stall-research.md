# Research: Brand Safety Stall — Which Brands Got Left Behind?

## Angle
Cross-tabulating FARS model-year deaths by manufacturer brand to calculate improvement rates between 2002-2006 and 2017-2021 model year cohorts. Reveals that **Hyundai's model-year-cohort deaths barely declined (-6%)** while most brands dropped 70%+.

## Kill Test
✅ **Genuinely newsworthy.** Hyundai leads IIHS in TOP SAFETY PICK awards (their press releases brag about it constantly), yet their FARS fatality improvement is dead last among major brands. That disconnect between crash-test awards and actual road deaths is a novel finding.

## Self-Critique
"Is this genuinely surprising after 159 articles?" → YES. We've done brand-level pieces (honda-brand-paradox, subaru-safest-brand, nissan-subprime-death-pipeline) but never ranked ALL brands by improvement rate. The finding that the "winners" of IIHS awards are the worst improvers in FARS deaths is counterintuitive and novel.

## Original Contribution
A FARS cross-tabulation nobody has published: brand-level model-year cohort death improvement rates. The calculation takes total deaths across all of a brand's models for MY 2002-2006 vs MY 2017-2021.

## Hypothesis for the Gap
1. **Sales volume surge** — Hyundai US sales tripled from ~400K (early 2000s) to ~700K+ (2020s). More cars → more deaths even if per-mile rate improved.
2. **Demographic shift** — Budget Korean brands attract younger, less experienced drivers (similar to Nissan subprime pipeline)
3. **IIHS vs FARS disconnect** — IIHS tests specific crash types under controlled conditions; FARS captures all fatal crashes including high-speed, rural, impaired driving scenarios where vehicle engineering matters less
4. **Portfolio composition** — Hyundai added more models in the high-death segments (compact sedans, subcompact SUVs)

## Counterargument
The strongest counter: sales volume increased ~75% for Hyundai, which alone could explain flat deaths. If you sell 75% more cars and deaths stay flat, the per-vehicle death rate actually IMPROVED by ~43%. However, competitors also grew sales and still achieved massive death reductions. Honda grew similarly but dropped 66%. The volume argument is necessary but insufficient.

## Limitations
- FARS model-year data tracks cumulative deaths — newer model years have had less time to accumulate deaths, creating inherent bias toward showing improvement
- Within that bias, the RELATIVE ranking of brands is valid since all face the same time exposure
- No per-mile normalization possible at brand level
- FARS only captures fatal crashes (subset of all crashes)
