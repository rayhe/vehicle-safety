The Other Silverado: How Badge Engineering Hides America's Deadliest Vehicle Platform

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
March 29, 2026

Every vehicle safety ranking in America has a blind spot the size of a full-size pickup truck. FARS, the federal fatality database, counts deaths by nameplate. Silverado: number one, 9,591 deaths over the 2014–2023 period. GMC Sierra: number seven, 3,337 deaths. Two separate line items. Two separate conversations. One problem: they are the same truck.[1]

12,928
Combined Silverado + Sierra deaths, 2014–2023. America's deadliest vehicle platform.

Same frame. Same powertrain. Same safety cage. GM's full-size truck platform has worn both badges since the GMT400 launched in 1988. On the current T1XX generation, introduced for 2019, you get identical high-strength steel construction, matching crash structure geometry, and the same airbag calibration. What separates them is a grille, some interior trim, and roughly $5,000.[2]

Aggregate them the way an engineer would, and rankings shift. GM's full-size truck platform killed 12,928 people in a decade. Ford's F-Series (F-150 plus F-250) killed 10,103. Ram's combined platform: 5,121.[1] GM leads by 2,825 deaths, or 28%. That gap never appears in standard FARS rankings because the database was built to track VINs, not platforms.

Same Truck, Different Odds

Stranger still: these twins don't kill at the same rate. Silverado's fatality rate runs 1.25 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled. Sierra's rate: 1.01. A 24% gap between trucks that rolled off adjacent assembly lines.[1]

GM accidentally built a natural experiment in buyer demographics. Sierra, marketed under GMC's "Professional Grade" branding, skews toward higher-income buyers who spec Denali and AT4 trims with full safety suites: forward collision alert, automatic emergency braking, lane-keep assist. Silverado's fleet includes an enormous population of WT (Work Truck) and Custom trims bought by fleets, contractors, and budget buyers, many stripped of advanced driver-assistance tech.[2]

Impairment profiles are functionally identical: 21.0% of Sierra drivers in fatal crashes tested positive for alcohol or drugs versus 20.6% for Silverado. Same truck, same vices, same 15.7% alcohol rate.[1] A 24% death rate gap, with impairment held constant, points directly at trim-level safety equipment as the variable.

Killing Others

Combined, GM's truck platform has a self-fatality ratio of 0.482. In fatal crashes involving a Silverado or Sierra, fewer than half the deaths are the truck's own occupant. Over 51.8% are pedestrians, sedan drivers, motorcyclists, and passengers in smaller vehicles absorbing 5,500 pounds of body-on-frame steel.[1]

An IIHS study published in November 2023 found that vehicles with hood heights above 40 inches are 45% more likely to kill pedestrians. Both Sierra and Silverado clear that threshold easily.[3] At platform scale, that risk multiplier applies to 8.1 million registered trucks on American roads.

A Methodology Problem

Platform-level aggregation is a calculation FARS was never designed to perform. Deaths link to individual VINs, which decode to specific makes and models. Nobody at NHTSA publishes a report titled "deaths by shared platform." So GM's truck safety record stays permanently split: one ledger for Silverado (which draws scrutiny as the single deadliest nameplate) and another for Sierra (sitting comfortably at seventh, far enough down to avoid headlines).[4]

Other manufacturers benefit from nameplate splitting too. Ford separates F-150 and F-250. Stellantis split Dodge Ram from Ram 1500 when it spun the brand off. But no company gains as much from fragmented mortality data as GM, because no other company sells two versions of the same full-size truck in the same market through overlapping dealer networks.

Strongest Counterargument

Silverado's higher rate could reflect commercial use patterns rather than equipment differences. Fleet Silverados may log 20,000+ miles a year versus the 13,500-mile household average our VMT estimates assume. If actual Silverado mileage is 48% higher than estimated, the rate gap shrinks to near zero. Fleet use also means more loaded-bed driving, more rural highways, more towing, all factors that elevate crash risk independent of safety features.

And the platform comparison with Ford isn't perfectly clean. Ford's F-250 is a heavier-duty vehicle than Sierra or Silverado 1500. A strict apples-to-apples comparison would pit only the F-150's 9,194 deaths against GM's combined 12,928, a gap that narrows from 28% to 41% in GM's disfavor. Depending on how you draw the platform boundary, the story changes.

What We Can't See

FARS captures fatal crashes only, not the roughly 6.7 million annual crashes total. A vehicle platform could perform better in survivable collisions while performing worse in fatal ones. VMT estimates carry ±15% uncertainty, particularly for commercial trucks. Trim levels, option packages, and buyer income are invisible in FARS data. "Platform aggregation" assumes shared engineering produces shared safety outcomes, which holds approximately but not perfectly across cab configurations, bed lengths, and model year generations.
