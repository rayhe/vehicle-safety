### 1. 🔍 General Editor — 9.0/10
**Structure:** Excellent. Opens with the specific Katy crash, zooms out to the regulatory gap, provides history (NTSB 2017), current state (SGO, investigations), counterargument, solutions, limitations, actionable takeaway, and closes by returning to Martha Avila. Textbook investigative structure.

**Engagement:** The headline is outstanding — personal, direct, provocative without being clickbait. Pull stat (15 vs. 1,000+) immediately communicates the gap. The bookend structure (opening and closing with Martha Avila) provides emotional grounding.

**Prose quality:** Dense but readable. The "Translation:" device after the SGO quote is effective. Paragraphs are appropriately sized for the complexity of the material.

**Weaknesses:** The middle paragraphs on NTSB recommendations and SAE standards are information-heavy; a subheading or two could help readers navigate. The article runs ~1,400 words which is on the long side for web but appropriate for an investigation piece.

**Fixed:** Removed unsourced "4.7 seconds" detail that was not in the research file.

### 6. 🔬 Research Rigor — 8.7/10
**Novel contribution:** The framing of ADAS crash data as an evidentiary asymmetry problem (not a Tesla problem) is genuinely useful. The comparison to aviation's FDR/CVR chain-of-custody framework is apt and underutilized in existing coverage. The CISS benchmarking study comparison (Tesla 9, VW 9, Mercedes 21) is a concrete data point most readers won't have seen.

**Limitations:** Three specific, honest limitations stated:
1. Elluswamy's claims unverified (could be accurate, inaccurate, or selectively true)
2. SGO reporting gaps make the denominator unknown
3. Tesla investigation count reflects telematics advantage, not necessarily higher crash rates

This is excellent — the third point in particular (that the asymmetry cuts both ways) shows genuine intellectual honesty.

**Counterargument:** Stated at full strength and engaged with substantively.

**Verifiability:** All major claims traceable to cited sources. The "roughly 15 data elements" and "hundreds to thousands of continuous data channels" are order-of-magnitude correct (Part 563 specifies 15 minimum elements; ADAS systems do record vastly more). The "nearly 50 special crash investigations" aligns with NHTSA records.

**Removed concern:** The unsourced "4.7 seconds" was identified and removed during review.

---

### 7. 📊 Data Presentation — 8.5/10
**Pull stat:** "15 vs. 1,000+" is clear, memorable, and communicates the central gap immediately.

**Numeric integration:** Numbers are used precisely (73 mph, 76 years old, 3.2 million vehicles, 44 additional elements, 9 vs 21 voluntary elements) and always in context.

**Benchmarking data:** The SAE J1698/1 comparison (Tesla/VW at 9, Mercedes at 21 out of 44) is effective at showing the inconsistency.

**Weakness:** The article is text-heavy without tables, charts, or comparative visuals. A simple comparison table (EDR vs ADAS data elements) would strengthen the "15 vs 1,000+" claim visually. This is a stylistic choice that works for long-form but limits data accessibility.
