The Gap

Every Car Passed the Whiplash Test. So IIHS Made a Harder One. 78% of SUVs Failed.

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
May 15, 2026

By 2022, the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety had a problem with its own whiplash test. Nearly every vehicle on the market aced it. The test simulated a 20-mph rear-end impact, and automakers had tuned their seats to pass with room to spare. IIHS retired the evaluation entirely.

Meanwhile, in actual insurance offices across the country, whiplash claims kept piling up. People in those supposedly excellent seats were still getting their necks wrenched in parking lots and on highway on-ramps. The test said the seats worked. The emergency rooms disagreed.

Compact SUVs that earned a Good rating in the redesigned IIHS whiplash test

So IIHS rebuilt the test from scratch. Engineers now pull the actual seat out of the vehicle and bolt it to a sled rig. A crash dummy with a fully articulated spine gets strapped in. The sled fires at both 20 and 30 mph while high-speed cameras and sensors track how the head moves relative to the cervical vertebrae, how quickly it contacts the restraint, and whether the spine maintains its natural curvature or gets violently straightened. That 30-mph addition is what separates the new evaluation from the old one, and it is what broke the curve.

The results from 18 compact SUVs landed in February 2026. Four earned Good ratings: the Audi Q3, Hyundai Ioniq 5, Subaru Forester, and Toyota RAV4. Nine scraped by at Acceptable, including the Honda CR-V and Volvo XC40. Two more, the Nissan Rogue and BMW X1, rated Marginal. And three popular models scored Poor: the Ford Bronco Sport, the Hyundai Tucson, and the Mazda CX-50.

Each failed differently, which makes the engineering negligence harder to excuse. The Tucson's head restraint shoved the dummy's chin downward into its chest during impact. The CX-50's seat let the dummy slide backward and upward, rendering the head restraint decorative. The Bronco Sport provided what IIHS called "particularly poor support for the head and spine," with a long, ugly delay before the dummy's head even touched the restraint, and a dangerous velocity gap between the pelvis and skull that practically begs for a cervical injury.

Cross-reference these results against NHTSA FARS data and a different gap opens. The Tucson has a fatality rate of 0.34 per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, among the lowest in its segment. In a frontal crash, this vehicle does its job. But FARS only records deaths. It says nothing about the 2.5 million people injured in US crashes annually, and whiplash dominates that population. You can build a car that rarely kills its occupants and still sends them to physical therapy for six months after a 30-mph tap.[1]

Whiplash injuries cost American drivers and insurers more than $8 billion a year, making them the single most common injury type in US auto insurance claims.[2] The old test gave automakers a free pass to ignore this for decades. They optimized seat geometry for a 20-mph simulation while the real world moved at 30, and nobody flagged the discrepancy until IIHS finally decided to measure what actually happens to a human spine in a parking lot rear-ender.

If you own a Tucson, CX-50, or Bronco Sport, none of this means your car will spontaneously injure you. It means that in the most common crash scenario in America, your seat is demonstrably worse at protecting your neck than a Forester or RAV4. The fix is seat foam density, restraint geometry, seatback energy absorption. Not exotic engineering. Stuff that costs single-digit dollars per unit at production scale and that four manufacturers already got right. The other fourteen chose not to.

Limitations: IIHS tested 18 models. The full compact SUV market is larger. The new evaluation has not been correlated with real-world injury reduction data yet, as Marcy Edwards, the IIHS senior research engineer who developed it, acknowledged: "We don't fully understand the mechanisms that cause whiplash injuries."[3] FARS data covers 2014 to 2023 and captures only fatal crashes, not injuries. The $8 billion annual cost figure reflects insurance claim frequency and severity, not medical outcomes alone.

Strongest counterargument: Automakers passed the test they were given. The old IIHS whiplash evaluation was the benchmark, and manufacturers met it. Blaming Ford, Hyundai, and Mazda for not anticipating a test that didn't exist until February 2026 is retrospective judgment. Their seats complied with every applicable federal standard and third-party rating. The question is whether they will fix the seats now that the bar has moved. IIHS plans to expand the evaluation to more vehicle categories, and history shows that manufacturers do redesign when their ratings suffer. Whether they'll do it before the 2027 model year refresh cycle is the bet.
