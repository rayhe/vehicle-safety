# Research: The Unlicensed Motorcycle Death Epidemic

## Thesis
One in three motorcyclists killed on American roads in 2023 had no valid license to ride — a rate nearly double that of car drivers, and a number that jumped 40% during COVID and never came back down. The licensing system that's supposed to keep unprepared riders off the road is a screen door on a submarine.

## The Data

### Core FARS/IIHS stats (2023)
- 6,335 motorcyclists died in 2023 — **highest number ever recorded** (FARS via IIHS, July 2025)
- 28x deadlier per mile than cars (NHTSA 2025, pub 813732)
- **35% of fatally injured motorcycle drivers had no valid license** (2,094 of 6,025)
- Compare: only **18% of car drivers** killed had no valid license (3,303 of 18,315)
- The unlicensed rate jumped from 30-31% (2014-2019) to **38% in 2020-2021**, settling at 35% in 2023
- That COVID-era jump: from ~1,500 unlicensed deaths/year to 2,000+ — and it stayed there

### The Aging Rider Paradox
- Median motorcycle owner age: **50 years old** in 2018 (was **27 in 1980**) — Motorcycle Industry Council
- In 1982: 4% of dead motorcyclists were 50+. In 2023: **~33% were 50+**
- Among 70+ population: motorcycle deaths went from **5 in 1975** to **295 in 2023** — nearly 6,000% increase
- 70+ motorcycle deaths as % of 70+ motor vehicle deaths: <1% (1975) → **5% (2023)**

### Helmet Laws Are a Patchwork
- Only **17 states + DC** require all riders to wear helmets
- 20,000+ riders would have survived since mid-1970s with universal helmet laws (IIHS, Oct 2024)
- Helmets reduce death risk by 37-42%

### Gender Gap
- Women: 19% of motorcycle owners but only **4% of drivers killed**
- Women were **91% of motorcycle passengers killed** in 2023
- This means the kill-demographic is overwhelmingly male

## FARS Query Methodology
- Source: FARS 1975-2023 via IIHS Fatality Facts, posted July 2025
- Cross-tabs: motorcycle driver deaths × license status × year (2014-2023 series)
- Age distribution: 70+ motorcycle deaths from "Older People" fatality facts
- Validation: numbers consistent across IIHS overview, trends, and motorcycle-specific pages

## Novel Contribution
The existing motorcycle-safety-scissors article covers the macro trend (cars got safer, motorcycles didn't). This story is about a **specific policy failure**: the licensing system. Nobody has cross-tabulated the COVID-era unlicensed spike with the aging demographic. The story isn't just "motorcycles are dangerous" — it's "we have a regulatory mechanism (licensing) that's supposed to prevent unqualified riders from dying, and it's failing at double the rate of car licensing, and the failure got permanently worse after COVID."

The 10-star version: When 35% of the people dying in an activity were unlicensed to do it, the licensing system isn't a safety mechanism — it's a fee.

## Strongest Counterargument
"Unlicensed" doesn't mean "unskilled." Many experienced riders let licenses lapse, ride with learner permits, or have endorsement issues. The licensing status might not correlate with riding competence. However, NHTSA research shows that unlicensed motorcycle operators are significantly more likely to be in single-vehicle crashes and to be alcohol-impaired — both indicators of risk-taking behavior, not just paperwork issues.
