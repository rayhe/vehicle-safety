# Research: The F-150 — America's Favorite Killer

## Angle
The Ford F-150 is the best-selling vehicle in America for 40+ consecutive years. It is also the #2 deadliest vehicle in the FARS database with 9,194 deaths over 2014-2023. The paradox: it's not especially dangerous per mile (1.04 per 100M VMT), but its colossal fleet of 6.5 million vehicles generates a massive body count through sheer volume.

## Kill Test
- Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES — nobody has written about the F-150 specifically on this site despite it being #2 in total deaths
- Novel angle? YES — the "volume toll" concept: how a relatively safe-per-mile vehicle becomes a mass killer through market dominance
- Timely? YES — IIHS just rated the F-150 (gas + Lightning) "Marginal" in new rear-seat crash test (Sep 2025)

## FARS Data (2014-2023)
- **Deaths:** 9,194 (#2 behind Silverado's 9,591)
- **Annual deaths:** 919.4 per year
- **Fleet:** 6,562,500 estimated
- **VMT:** 88,594 million
- **Rate:** 1.04 per 100M VMT (moderate — 27th overall among vehicles with 500+ deaths)
- **Crashes:** 20,066 in FARS
- **Lethality:** 45.8% (deaths/crashes)
- **Impairment:** 18.9% (below the ~20% average)

## Comparisons
### Within Ford lineup:
- Mustang: 6.02 rate (6x F-150) but only 2,739 deaths
- Explorer: 1.54 rate, 3,797 deaths
- Ranger: 2.91 rate, 3,089 deaths
- Transit: 0.14 rate (safest Ford) — F-150 is 7.4x more dangerous per mile
- F-250: 0.43 rate, 909 deaths — half-ton is 2.4x deadlier per VMT than Super Duty
- F-350: 0.15 rate, 164 deaths

### Among pickups:
- Silverado: 1.25 rate, 9,591 deaths — F-150 is safer per mile but almost matches on body count
- Sierra: 1.01 rate, 3,337 deaths
- Ram: 0.78 rate, 4,407 deaths — Ram is 25% safer per VMT
- Tacoma: 0.8 rate, 2,274 deaths
- F-150 lethality (45.8%) vs Silverado (48.6%) vs Ram (estimated from 4,407/10,110 = 43.6%)

### Volume toll calculation:
- If every F-150 owner drove a Ram 1500 instead (rate 0.13): estimated deaths drop from 9,194 to ~1,151. **~8,043 excess deaths** attributable to choosing an F-150 over a Ram 1500.
- If every F-150 owner drove a Tacoma (rate 0.80): estimated deaths ~7,088. About 2,106 excess.
- However: this ignores that the F-150's rate partly reflects its older fleet age, usage patterns, and demographics. These aren't pure design comparisons.

## IIHS Rear-Seat Test Failure (Sep 2025)
- F-150 crew cab rated "Marginal" in updated moderate-overlap test
- Rear-seat dummy showed "high risk of chest injuries and injuries to the head or neck"
- Lap belt moved from pelvis onto abdomen (internal injury risk)
- F-150 Lightning also rated "Marginal" for same issue
- Gas F-150 "aced" front/side tests in 2021 — the problem is specific to rear passengers

## Novel Contribution
Original cross-tabulation: fleet-weighted excess death calculation comparing the F-150's actual body count to what it would be if all those miles were driven in competitor trucks. This quantifies the "popularity penalty" — how market dominance converts moderate risk into mass casualties.

## Limitations
- Fleet estimates use industry sales data and estimated VMT, not actual registrations or odometer data. ±15% uncertainty on per-model rates.
- The "excess deaths" calculation assumes identical driving patterns between F-150 and Ram 1500 owners. In reality, demographics, geography, and usage differ significantly.
- FARS captures only fatal crashes. The F-150 may have different injury-crash rates.
- The F-150 spans many model years with different safety equipment. Older F-150s (pre-ESC mandate) skew the aggregate data.

## Strongest Counterargument
The F-150's body count is purely a function of volume, not design deficiency. At 1.04 per 100M VMT, it's safer per mile than the Honda Accord (3.07), Toyota Camry (2.03), or even the Honda Civic (2.25). Comparing it to the Ram 1500's 0.13 rate is misleading because the Ram 1500 data captures a different fleet vintage and demographic. If anything, the F-150's moderate rate despite being driven by an enormous, diverse population (including very old trucks) is a testament to Ford's engineering over the decades.
