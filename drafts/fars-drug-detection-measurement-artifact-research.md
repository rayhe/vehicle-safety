# Research: FARS Drug Detection Enhancement — The Measurement Artifact

## Headline Angle
"FARS Changed How It Counts Drugs in 2018. 'Drugged Driving' Didn't Spike. The Spreadsheet Did."

## Journalist: Dale Impactor III (Toxicology Desk Chief)
Sardonic, statistical. Perfect for a meta-data story about how measurement artifacts create false narratives.

## Kill Test
✅ PASSES. This is a genuine counterintuitive finding backed by peer-reviewed Columbia University research. The "drugged driving epidemic" narrative is partially an artifact of a database change. Direct policy implications: every post-2018 drugged driving policy relies on inflated baselines. Two primary research papers, FARS data, and an actionable consumer angle.

## Primary Sources (3+)

### Source 1: Chihuri & Li, 2026 — Columbia University
- **Paper:** "Impact of the enhanced Fatality Analysis Reporting System on drug detection in fatally injured drivers"
- **Journal:** Accident Analysis & Prevention, Vol 226, March 2026
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.aap.2025.108354
- **PMID:** 41401642
- **Sample:** 138,505 fatally injured drivers (68,741 pre-enhancement 2013-2017; 69,764 post-enhancement 2018-2022)
- **Key finding:** In 2018, FARS expanded non-alcohol drug reporting beyond a 3-drug maximum
- **Results:**
  - Alcohol stayed flat: ~37% (37.1% → 37.9%)
  - Any nonalcohol drug: 39.9% → 55.0% (p<0.001)
  - Marijuana: 14.4% → 26.4% (nearly doubled, p<0.001)
  - Stimulants: 9.3% → 25.1% (nearly tripled, p<0.001)
  - Depressants: 9.1% → 13.7% (p<0.001)
  - ≥2 nonalcohol drugs: 13.6% → 19.2% (p<0.001)
  - ≥3 drugs: 2.0% → 5.8% (nearly tripled, p<0.001)
  - Mean drugs per driver: 1.36 → 1.50 (p<0.001)
  - Alcohol + marijuana combo: 8.3% → 11.8% (p<0.001)
  - **Adjusted odds of detecting any nonalcohol drug: 42% increase (aOR 1.42, 95% CI 1.37-1.47)**
  - **Adjusted odds of detecting ≥3 drugs: 201% increase (aOR 3.01, 95% CI 2.80-3.24)**
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41401642/

### Source 2: Gerona et al., 2025 — UC San Francisco/Davis + Harbor-UCLA
- **Paper:** "New Psychoactive Substances in Roadway Crash Victims in California"
- **Journal:** Frontiers in Toxicology, Vol 7, 2025
- **DOI:** 10.3389/ftox.2025.1572324
- **Sample:** 1,000 serum samples from CA roadway crash victims (2024)
- **Key findings:**
  - 290/1,000 (29%) positive for at least one drug
  - 8 unique NPS detected (novel psychoactive substances not on standard panels)
  - Only CNS depressants found: designer benzodiazepines, synthetic opioids, kratom alkaloids
  - Most common NPS: bromazolam (7), para-fluorofentanyl (4), mitragynine/kratom (3)
  - Other NPS: acetyl fentanyl, N-methyl norfentanyl, protonitazene, etizolam, xylazine
  - Almost all NPS-positive cases also had traditional drugs (fentanyl, meth, THC)
  - 16/17 NPS cases tested NEGATIVE for ethanol
  - No roadside test exists for any of these substances

### Source 3: AAA Foundation for Traffic Safety
- **Study:** Cannabis Use Among Drivers in Fatal Crashes in Washington State
- **Finding:** THC-positive fatal crash drivers doubled from 8.8% pre-legalization to 18% five years post-legalization
- **URL:** https://aaafoundation.org (specific study page)

### Source 4: NHTSA FARS Database
- Our own FARS toxicology data (2014-2023): 490,736 drivers, 8.7% drug-positive, 15.1% alcohol-positive, 20.0% any impairment
- Note: our data spans both pre- and post-enhancement periods (blended)
- **URL:** https://www.nhtsa.gov/research-data/fatality-analysis-reporting-system-fars

## The Story Structure

**Lede:** The stat everyone cites — drugged driving "spiked" after 2018. Pull stat: 42% increase.

**The twist:** FARS changed its spreadsheet in 2018. Before that, it could only record 3 non-alcohol drugs per driver. If a driver had meth, fentanyl, THC, and a benzo, FARS recorded 3 and threw the rest away. After 2018, it counted everything.

**The data:** Walk through Chihuri & Li's findings. Marijuana "doubled." Stimulants "tripled." Polysubstance use "tripled." Alcohol — the one substance FARS always counted correctly — stayed flat at 37%.

**The deeper problem:** Even the enhanced system is blind. UC researchers found 8 novel drugs in California crash victims that no standard tox panel detects. Bromazolam. Protonitazene. Xylazine. If you die on a California highway with para-fluorofentanyl in your blood, the standard FARS coding might record "fentanyl" — or might not distinguish it at all.

**The policy failure:** Every state drugged-driving policy written after 2018 is working from inflated baselines. Legalization studies that compare pre-2018 to post-2018 THC rates are comparing two different measurement systems.

**Actionable:** Check if your state's drugged driving policy cites post-2018 FARS data. If it does, the baseline is inflated by at least 42%. The real trend is unknowable from the public data alone. If you're a juror evaluating toxicology evidence, ask: was this drug on the standard panel, or was it detected by advanced mass spectrometry?
