# Research: GM Orphan Sedan Body Count

## Angle
GM discontinued seven sedan/hatchback models between 2005 and 2011. Between 2014 and 2023, those seven dead models killed 7,370 people — every single death in a vehicle GM had already stopped manufacturing. The total across all manufacturers (12 orphan models) is 11,907. GM owns 62% of orphan car deaths.

## Original Contribution
Cross-tabulation of manufacturer discontinuation dates × FARS death counts × NHTSA recall completion rates. Nobody has quantified the total body count from discontinued-model fleets as a distinct category.

### 2. NHTSA Recall Completion Rates (Report to Congress)
- Vehicles 1-3 years old at recall: ~80% completion
- Vehicles 1-5 years old: ~76%
- Vehicles 6-10 years old: **56%**
- Vehicles 11+ years old: **37%** (2003 Camry example)
- A 2003 Camry recalled in 2013 (11 years old): 37% completion
- A 2013 Camry recalled in 2013 (brand new): 88% completion
- Source: NHTSA Report to Congress on Vehicle Safety Recall Completion Rates

**Cobalt ignition switch recall (2014, NHTSA 14V047000):**
The Cobalt was at the center of the GM ignition switch scandal — 2.6M vehicles, linked to at least 124 deaths. When the recall was issued in 2014, the youngest Cobalt was already 4 years old. Recall completion for vehicles that age: ~47-69%. Many Cobalts were driving with unfixed ignition switches.

### 3. IIHS/NHTSA Safety Feature Timeline
- ESC mandate: 2012 model year (none of the orphan sedans have ESC standard across all years)
- Automatic emergency braking: 2018+ (voluntary agreement, 2022 mandate announced)
- Forward collision warning: 2015+ on most new models
- Every orphan sedan predates mandatory ESC. The newest (HHR, 2011) just barely missed it.

### 4. TheStreet/WSJ (Dec 2025)
- Between 2015-2024: ~12 million vehicles recalled for airbag deployment defects
- 22% (2.6 million) remain unfixed
- 69% overall recall completion in 2024 — 31% of recalls ignored
- 12 people confirmed dead in unfixed-recall vehicles

## Comparison Point — Still-Produced Sedans
- Toyota Corolla: 4,945 deaths, rate 1.85 — still in production, getting safer each year
- Honda Civic: 6,553 deaths, rate 2.25 — still in production
- These models get new safety tech every generation. Orphan cars are frozen.

## Strongest Counterargument
Fleet age effects: older cars always have higher death rates because they lack modern safety features, and their owners tend to be lower-income (less maintenance, worse roads, longer commutes). This isn't specific to "orphaned" models — ANY car gets more dangerous as it ages. The counter-counter: orphan cars are UNIQUELY dangerous because (1) no new units enter the fleet to lower the average age, (2) parts availability declines, (3) recall completion drops to 37%, and (4) the manufacturer has zero incentive to improve safety.

## Limitations
- FARS only captures fatal crashes — can't measure injury rates
- Fleet size estimates have ±15% uncertainty for low-volume models
- The "deaths" count includes multi-vehicle crashes where fault/cause varies
- Can't isolate how many orphan car deaths were caused BY the car's age vs driver behavior
- The 12 orphan models are not exhaustive — Saturn, Oldsmobile, etc. would add more
