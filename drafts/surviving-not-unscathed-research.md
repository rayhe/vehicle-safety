# Research: Surviving, Not Unscathed

## Angle
NHTSA's 2024 data shows a paradox: traffic fatalities fell 4.3% (41,025→39,254) while injuries in truck-involved crashes ROSE 5%. The 2025 early estimate shows an even sharper death decline (36,640, -6.7%). Everyone celebrates the death decline. Nobody talks about the injury surge.

FARS data provides the mechanism: crash survivability varies dramatically by vehicle class. Sports cars produce 1.47 crashes per death. Pickups produce 2.05. As the fleet shifts toward SUVs and pickups (higher crashes-per-death), more people survive crashes they would have died in — but they survive injured. The death count drops. The injury count rises. The crash count doesn't change.

## Novel Contribution
First article to connect NHTSA's injury-death divergence to vehicle-class-level crash survivability ratios derived from FARS data. Quantifies how much of the death decline is "survival shift" vs. "crash reduction."

## Key Data Points

### NHTSA 2024 Report (released April 2, 2026)
- 2024 fatalities: 39,254 (down 4.3% from 41,025 in 2023)
- 2025 estimated fatalities: 36,640 (down 6.7% from 2024)
- 2025 fatality rate: 1.10 per 100M VMT (second lowest in FARS history)
- VMT increased 0.9% in 2025 (29.8 billion more miles)
- Truck crash injuries UP: single-vehicle +12%, multi-vehicle +8.2%
- Source: crashstats.nhtsa.dot.gov publications 813791 and 813800

### FARS Cross-Tabulation: Crash Survivability by Class
- Sports Car: 8,990 crashes → 6,134 deaths = 1.47 crashes/death (68% lethal)
- Sedan: 138,154 crashes → 89,127 deaths = 1.55 crashes/death (65% lethal)
- SUV: 88,568 crashes → 46,442 deaths = 1.91 crashes/death (52% lethal)
- Van: 15,362 crashes → 7,897 deaths = 1.95 crashes/death (51% lethal)
- Pickup: 85,128 crashes → 41,593 deaths = 2.05 crashes/death (49% lethal)

Key finding: An SUV crash is 25% more survivable than a sedan crash. A pickup crash is 33% more survivable than a sedan crash. As the fleet shifts from sedans (32.5% fleet, 46.6% deaths) to SUVs (39.6% fleet, 24.3% deaths), deaths decline mechanically while crashes (and injuries) persist.

### Fleet Composition Shift
- Autoblog (2025): "For every sedan sold, nearly four SUVs rolled off the lot"
- New registrations shifted: sedans declining, SUV/crossover dominant
- Sedans: 32.5% of fleet but 46.6% of deaths (1.44x overrepresented)
- SUVs: 39.6% of fleet but 24.3% of deaths (0.61x underrepresented)

## Thesis
"Traffic deaths are declining because Americans are surviving crashes they once would have died in — not because they're having fewer crashes. The injury numbers prove it."

## Strongest Counterargument
The injury increase was specific to truck-involved crashes and may reflect increased truck VMT, not a general survivability shift. Additionally, NHTSA's data shows fatalities declined across ALL vehicle types in 2024, not just sedans. The fleet composition argument explains the direction of the trend but may overstate its magnitude. Road safety improvements (AEB adoption, road design changes, speed management) likely contribute too.

## Limitations
- FARS captures fatal crashes only — no injury data per model
- Our crash survivability ratio uses FARS crashes (which by definition involve at least one fatality), so the "crashes" here are all fatal-crash events, not total crashes
- VMT estimates introduce ±15% uncertainty for fleet-level calculations
- Cannot separate cause vs. correlation: are SUV drivers safer, or are SUVs safer?
- Truck injury data may reflect reporting changes, not actual injury increases
