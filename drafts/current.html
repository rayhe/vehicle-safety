Range Rover’s Steering Can Snap Off. Land Rover’s Fix Is a Brace.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
July 15, 2026

Cast aluminum front steering knuckles on the Range Rover and Range Rover Sport can crack at the upper clevis joint. When the crack propagates, the upper suspension arm detaches entirely, and the driver of a 5,300-pound, $95,000-to-$185,000 SUV loses directional control. NHTSA opened an investigation in June 2025.[1]

522
steering knuckle fractures reported by JLR to NHTSA

On April 24, 2026, NHTSA upgraded that investigation to an Engineering Analysis, its highest tier, because it found “much of the information” JLR provided “contradictory.”[1] JLR’s recall 25V514, filed August 2025, covers only certain 2014 and all 2015 to 2017 vehicles. Its fix is revealing: cracked knuckles get replaced with “a new knuckle of a substantially similar design,” and uncracked knuckles get a metal brace designed to “prevent the component from completely separating should a fracture develop.” Note what the brace concedes. It does not prevent the fracture; it only prevents the arm from fully detaching once the fracture has already formed.[2]

Recall coverage spans four model years, but the defect spans nine. Recent owner complaints describe knuckle fractures on 2020 and 2021 Range Rover L405 vehicles, built years after the recall cutoff.[1] NHTSA expanded the investigation to cover 2018 through 2022, bringing total affected population to 331,559 vehicles. FARS data from 2014 to 2023 shows just 26 occupant deaths in 95 fatal crashes involving Range Rover Sports, a rate of 0.16 per 100 million VMT, less than half the SUV class median of 0.41.[3] In 73 percent of those fatal involvements, it was someone outside the Range Rover who died. Safety at that price point comes from mass, not from the quality of the castings connecting your front wheels to the chassis.

Counterargument and limits: Zero fatalities or injuries are attributed to knuckle fractures so far, and JLR acted before anyone died. FARS captures fatal crashes only; 26 deaths over a decade is below the threshold for statistically robust per-model conclusions, and estimated VMT introduces roughly ±15 percent uncertainty. None of that explains why 2020 models share the failure mode with 2014s, why replacement knuckles use “substantially similar” geometry, or why NHTSA found the data contradictory enough to invoke its heaviest investigative tool.

What to do: Own a 2014 to 2022 Range Rover or Range Rover Sport? Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls for recall 25V514. Model years 2018 to 2022 are not yet covered, but NHTSA’s expanded investigation may change that. Any clunking or looseness in the front suspension warrants an immediate inspection, because your knuckle may already be cracked.
