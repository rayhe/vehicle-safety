# Research: October Is the Deadliest Driving Month

## Angle
October — not July, not December — is the deadliest month for drivers in America. The John Foy & Associates analysis of 2023 NHTSA data shows October had 3,505 fatal crashes, 12% above the monthly average of 3,138. A state-by-state analysis shows October is the #1 deadliest month in 22 states. Southern states are hit hardest: 10 of 16 southern states peak in October. The paradox: summer gets all the safety campaigns, but the month after summer ends is when people actually die.

## Sources
1. **John Foy & Associates analysis** (2026): October had 3,505 fatal crashes in 2023 — the most of any month. 37,654 total fatal crashes, 40,901 deaths, 2.6M injuries in 2023. Summer and Fall both recorded ~10,000 crashes each, but October pushed fall to near-parity with summer.
2. **Travel and Tour World** (July 16, 2026): State-by-state study. October leads in 22 states total. Wyoming has highest per-capita October rate: 2.04 per 100K. Mississippi, Montana, South Carolina, Alabama close behind. DC, Massachusetts, Rhode Island, NY, Utah have lowest October rates. Southern states dominate October (10 of 16). Midwest peaks in September. Northeast split between July and October.
3. **University of Michigan Transportation Research Institute** (Michael Sivak): October has highest fatality rate per distance driven (10.2 deaths per billion km) from 1994-2006 data. Risk in October is 16% greater than in March.
4. **Contributing factors**: End of Daylight Saving Time → sudden dark commutes. Deer activity peaks in October/November. Fall weather variability. Holiday travel buildup.
5. **NHTSA FARS 2014-2023**: Total 2023 deaths were 40,901. Rate of 1.25 per 100M VMT.
6. **2024 FARS**: 39,254 deaths, 1.19 per 100M VMT. 11th consecutive quarterly decline.

## Cross-reference with FARS dataset
From site's FARS data: The vehicles with highest crash involvement rates (Maxima 888/100K, Impala 852/100K, Cobalt 727/100K) are predominantly older sedans with poor crashworthiness. These vehicles are driven disproportionately during evening commutes — exactly when October's darkness penalty hits hardest.

## Original contribution
Nobody has combined the October deadliest month finding with the VEHICLE-LEVEL crash data. The insight: October's darkness penalty falls hardest on drivers in the oldest, cheapest, most dangerous vehicles — the ones without adaptive headlights, lane-keep assist, or AEB. The fleet composition amplifies the seasonal risk.

## Counterargument
October's high crash count could partly reflect higher VMT (people driving more after summer). Weather and road conditions vary massively by state. The "Daylight Saving Time" explanation is convenient but only accounts for the last week of October — most of the month still has evening daylight.
