### Critic 1: 🔍 General Editor — 9.0/10

**Structure:** Strong template adherence. Kicker → Headline → Lede → Pull stat → 5 sections → References → Disclaimer. Logical flow: incident → corporate response → systemic problem → data → regulatory gap → credibility → consumer action.

**Headline:** "Every Car in America Has the Same Backdoor That Killed Martha Avila" — provocative, specific, accurate. Ties individual tragedy to systemic vulnerability without clickbait distortion. The word "backdoor" is technically metaphorical (AEB override is an intentional design choice, not a hidden flaw), but it earns the metaphor by explaining the engineering rationale honestly.

**Lede:** Opens with the person, not the vehicle. The staccato rhythm ("She was 76 years old. She died at the hospital.") is deliberate and effective. Grounds the piece in human cost before pivoting to systems analysis.

**Length:** ~900 words body, exceeding the 300-500 guide target. Justified for a flagship investigation with scholarly rigor requirements (limitations, counterargument, methodology), but could be tightened in the FARS section.

**Engagement:** The "safety net with a hole shaped like the people who need the net most" metaphor is the strongest single line. The closing paragraph's pivot from courtroom to living room lands hard.

**Weakness:** The EU ISA section, while important for regulatory context, slows momentum between the FARS data punch and the Tesla credibility section.

### Critic 6: 🔬 Research Rigor — 8.7/10

**Original contribution:** Two novel analyses:
1. The universal override thesis — framing AEB override as an industry-wide architectural vulnerability rather than a Tesla-specific problem. No other outlet has made this argument systematically.
2. The impairment-override paradox — cross-tabulating FARS impairment rates with override design to show the safety net fails against the population most likely to trigger it.

**Limitations acknowledged:** ✅ Four explicit limitations in the disclaimer:
- FARS doesn't track ADAS features active in fatal crashes
- Tesla FARS sample is small vs. established brands
- Investigation ongoing, final determination pending
- Implicit: no data on override frequency in pre-crash scenarios (acknowledged in research notes)

**Strongest counterargument at full strength:** ✅ "If the system falsely detects an obstacle and brakes in heavy traffic, the driver needs to be able to override that false positive before a semi-truck rear-ends them. You cannot make a system that takes away the driver's ability to accelerate without creating scenarios where that restriction itself causes crashes." Not strawmanned, not dismissed.

**Verifiability:** ✅ All factual claims traced to 7 cited sources with working URLs.

**Methodology transparency:** ✅ FARS data periods stated (2014-2023), sample sizes given (765, 204), percentages with denominators, confidence caveats.

**Gap:** The "100 million vehicles" AEB fleet estimate is not cited. This number comes from industry estimates and could be sourced more precisely.
