18,336 Drivers Died With Alcohol AND Drugs in Their Blood. America Has 10,000 Cops Trained to Notice.

Dale Impactor III • Clautomotive Desk
March 26, 2026
18.6%
Of impaired drivers killed in fatal crashes tested positive for both alcohol AND drugs. The breathalyzer on the officer's belt detects exactly one of those substances.

A peer-reviewed study published in Accident Analysis & Prevention in 2026 tracked 60,741 drivers who died within one hour of a crash and had toxicology screening.[1] The polysubstance impairment rate rose from 14.9% in 2018 to 18.8% in 2022. That is a 38% increase in four years. Cannabis was the most common non-alcohol drug detected in 25% of polysubstance cases, followed by stimulants at 19.2% and narcotics at 8.3%.

Drivers who died in nighttime crashes were nearly three times more likely to be polysubstance-impaired than those who died during the day.[1] The growth trend held after controlling for demographics. Every year from 2018 to 2022, the odds of dying polysubstance-impaired went up. Steadily. Without pausing.

The FARS Breakdown

We ran our own cross-tabulation on the FARS toxicology database: 490,736 drivers involved in fatal crashes with toxicology data available, covering 2014 through 2023.[2] Of the 98,348 who tested positive for any impairment, here is how the chemistry breaks down:

Alcohol only: 55,852 drivers (56.8% of impaired). The classic. What the breathalyzer was built for.

Drugs only: 24,160 drivers (24.6% of impaired). Invisible to the breathalyzer. Requires either a Drug Recognition Expert or lab confirmation to catch.

Alcohol AND drugs: 18,336 drivers (18.6% of impaired). The cocktail. The breathalyzer will flag the alcohol, but the officer has no field tool to identify the stacked impairment that makes these crashes deadlier.

Add the drug-only and polysubstance categories together: 43.2% of impaired fatal-crash drivers had drugs in their system. The breathalyzer is blind to the entire drug component.

Which Vehicles

The polydrug cocktail rate is not evenly distributed. Among vehicles with 150 or more drivers and 20 or more impaired in the FARS data, the polysubstance share of impaired drivers ranges from 5% to 40.5%. An eightfold spread.[2]

The Pontiac Bonneville leads at 40.5%: of its 37 impaired fatal-crash drivers, 15 had both alcohol and drugs. The Acura TLX runs 31.5%. The Mercedes-Benz GLK-Class, 30.8%. The Cadillac SRX, at 27.1% with a sample of 155 impaired drivers, is one of the most statistically robust entries on the list. Five Cadillac and Mercedes models appear in the top fifteen.

At the bottom: the Mercury Sable at 5.0%, the Tesla Model S at 6.1%, and the Fiat 500 at 7.1%. The Toyota Land Cruiser and Subaru Ascent barely register polysubstance cases at all.

10,000 Officers

The United States has approximately 10,000 certified Drug Recognition Experts spread across all 50 states.[3] Washington, D.C. has six. The DRE curriculum is demanding, the career incentive is minimal, and most officers who consider the training decide against it. A DRE evaluation takes 30 to 90 minutes and must be conducted in a controlled environment, not on the roadside.

Meanwhile, the breathalyzer takes seconds. It is portable. Every patrol car has one. And it cannot detect cannabis, methamphetamine, fentanyl, benzodiazepines, or any of the other substances that showed up in 42,496 drug-positive drivers in our FARS dataset.

Oral fluid testing devices exist. A handful of states have authorized roadside saliva tests that can screen for THC, cocaine, opioids, and amphetamines in minutes.[4] But adoption is glacial. Most states have no statutory framework for roadside drug screening. The legal admissibility of oral fluid tests remains contested in courts across the country.

The BAC Debate Misses the Point

The National Transportation Safety Board has recommended lowering the legal blood alcohol limit from 0.08 to 0.05 g/dL.[5] Utah adopted the 0.05 standard in 2018. No other state has followed.

Lowering the BAC limit addresses the 56.8% of impaired drivers who are alcohol-only. It does nothing for the 43.2% who have drugs in their system, with or without alcohol. The fastest-growing segment of impaired fatal-crash drivers is not drinking more. They are mixing.

Strongest counterargument: FARS toxicology data measures the presence of substances, not active impairment at the time of the crash. Cannabis metabolites can linger in blood for days or weeks after consumption. A driver who tested positive for both alcohol and THC may have been drunk but not meaningfully impaired by cannabis consumed days earlier. The per-vehicle model variation likely reflects driver demographics (age, income, geography) more than any property of the vehicles themselves. And the DRE shortage, while real, may matter less than it appears: most polysubstance drivers who die in crashes were never stopped by police in the first place.

These are legitimate caveats. The published study addressed the metabolite concern by analyzing drivers who died within one hour of the crash and noting that average THC blood levels of 30.7 ng/mL far exceeded thresholds considered indicative of recent use.[6] The demographic confound for per-vehicle variation is real and acknowledged below. But the core finding stands: polysubstance impairment is growing, enforcement technology cannot detect it, and 18,336 people in our database died while their blood told a more complicated story than any breathalyzer can read.

Limitations: Our FARS data spans 2014 to 2023 and does not break down by individual year, so we cannot independently verify the year-over-year growth trend from the published study. The polysubstance count is calculated by inclusion-exclusion (alcohol-positive + drug-positive − any-impaired), which assumes these are the only impairment categories. Toxicology testing rates vary significantly by jurisdiction; not all deceased drivers receive full drug panels, which means the true polysubstance prevalence may be higher than reported. Per-vehicle model analysis reflects driver demographics and should not be interpreted as causal. Small samples on some models (e.g., Pontiac Bonneville with 37 impaired drivers) carry wide confidence intervals.
