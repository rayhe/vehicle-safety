# Research: NHTSA Killed the Speed Limiter Rule, Then Took a Victory Lap

## Angle
NHTSA and FMCSA withdrew the heavy truck speed limiter mandate in January 2026 — a rule their own 2016 cost-benefit analysis estimated would prevent 1,115 fatal crashes per year involving vehicles over 26,000 lbs on roads with 55+ mph speed limits. The agencies cited "advanced crash avoidance technologies" (AEB/FCW) as a better solution. But AEB for heavy trucks isn't even mandated yet — it was proposed in July 2023 (88 FR 43174) and hasn't been finalized. Three months after withdrawing the speed limiter rule, NHTSA released data showing 36,640 deaths in 2025 — a 6.7% decline from 2024 — and took credit for the improvement, citing their "Freedom Means Affordable Cars" initiative and law enforcement partnerships.

The contradiction: NHTSA withdrew a rule that would save ~1,115 lives per year citing technology that doesn't exist as a mandate, then celebrated declining deaths they didn't cause.

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES. The speed limiter withdrawal was barely covered in mainstream auto journalism (mostly trucking press). The juxtaposition with the victory-lap press release is new. The body count math — 1,115 deaths/year × however many years this has been in regulatory limbo (since 2016 = 10 years = ~11,150 preventable deaths) — is an original calculation nobody has run publicly.

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: FMCSA/NHTSA Speed Limiter Withdrawal (Jan 2026)
- Federal Register citation: withdrawal of 2016 NPRM + 2022 ANSPRM
- Key quote: "regulation in this area should not proceed until a more certain assessment of the effect of advanced crash avoidance technologies can be made"
- Cited Executive Orders: E.O. 14219 (DOGE deregulatory initiative), E.O. 14192 (deregulation)
- Original NPRM: September 7, 2016 (81 FR 61942)
- Cost estimates: $209M-$1.5B annually depending on set speed
- Estimated benefits: prevent 1,115 fatal crashes/year on roads 55+ mph

### Source 2: NHTSA 2025 Fatality Data (released ~April 2026)
- 36,640 traffic fatalities in 2025 (preliminary)
- 6.7% decline from 2024 (39,254 deaths final)
- Fatality rate: 1.10 per 100M VMT (second-lowest ever)
- VMT increased 29.8 billion miles (+0.9%)
- 12th consecutive quarterly decline
- NHTSA Administrator Jonathan Morrison claimed credit

### Source 3: AEB Heavy Truck NPRM (July 2023)
- Proposed requiring AEB on heavy vehicles
- 88 FR 43174
- NOT finalized as of June 2026
- This is the technology NHTSA cited as making speed limiters unnecessary
- But it doesn't exist as a mandate

### Source 4: FARS Data (2014-2023)
- Large truck fatality context from FARS aggregate stats
- ~5,000+ fatalities/year involve large trucks
- Trucks already equipped with ECUs capable of speed limiting
- Virtually zero additional cost to mandate (NHTSA's own finding)

## Novel Contribution
1. **Cumulative body count calculation:** 10 years of regulatory delay since 2016 NPRM × 1,115 fatal crashes/year = ~11,150 preventable fatal crashes. Not all of these are deaths (some crashes involve multiple fatalities, some are survivable), but NHTSA's own analysis says these are crashes that would not have happened.
2. **The "phantom technology" argument:** NHTSA withdrew a real rule citing a technology mandate that doesn't exist yet. AEB for trucks was proposed in 2023 and hasn't been finalized. The trucks already have speed-limiting hardware installed — every heavy truck since the early 2000s has an ECU capable of governor settings.
3. **The victory lap contradiction:** Same agency, 3 months apart — "we can't mandate speed limiters because it's too uncertain" then "traffic deaths are at record lows because of our policies."

## Strongest Counterargument
The strongest case FOR withdrawal: speed limiters create speed differentials on highways. If trucks are limited to 65 mph and cars are doing 80, the speed differential itself causes crashes — lane changes, rear-end collisions, road rage. Several owner-operator groups (OOIDA) argued this forcefully. FMCSA cited their inability to model these "secondary effects." This argument has merit — speed differentials ARE dangerous. The counterargument to the counterargument: NHTSA's own analysis already accounted for speed differential effects and still found net safety benefits of 1,115 fewer fatal crashes. Also, most large fleets (Werner, JB Hunt, Schneider) already voluntarily limit truck speeds to 62-68 mph.

## Limitations
- The 1,115 fatal crashes estimate is from the 2016 NPRM analysis; it may be outdated as fleet composition, speed patterns, and crash avoidance tech have changed
- "Fatal crashes prevented" ≠ "deaths prevented" — some crashes involve multiple fatalities, some don't result in death of the truck driver
- The AEB mandate may eventually be finalized, at which point the "phantom technology" argument weakens
- Speed limiter effectiveness depends heavily on the set speed, which was never determined
