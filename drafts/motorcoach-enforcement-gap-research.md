# Research Notes: Motorcoach Enforcement Gap — Virginia I-95 Bus Crash

## The Incident
- **Date:** May 30, 2026, ~2:35 AM, I-95 southbound, Stafford County, VA (near Quantico)
- **Carrier:** E&P Travel Inc, Kings Mountain, NC
- **Route:** NYC → Charlotte, NC
- **Driver:** Jing S. Dong, 48, Staten Island, NY. Naturalized US citizen from China. CDL obtained in New York in 2024. Charged with 2 counts involuntary manslaughter (additional charges pending).
- **What happened:** Bus failed to slow for work zone traffic. Struck a Chevrolet Suburban, which was forced into an Acura that caught fire. 6 vehicles total involved.
- **Killed (5):**
  - Dmitri Doncev, 45, Greenfield, MA
  - Ecaterina Doncev, 44, Greenfield, MA
  - Emily Doncev, 13 (their daughter)
  - Mark Doncev, 7 (their son)
  - Priscilla R. Mafalda, 25, Worcester, MA
- **Injured:** 44+ hospitalized (3 critical), ~34 passengers on bus
- **NTSB:** Launched parallel investigation; preliminary report expected in 30 days
- **Key quote (NTSB Tom Chapman):** "If there was any braking, there wasn't much"

## E&P Travel Inc — FMCSA Record (from DOT records, per NYPost/CNN reporting)

## Federal Regulatory Framework
- **49 CFR §391.11(b)(2):** CMV drivers must be able to read and speak English sufficiently to converse with the general public, understand highway traffic signs and signals, respond to official inquiries, and make entries on reports and records
- **Executive Order (2025):** Trump signed EO strengthening English proficiency requirements for commercial drivers; regulations call for drivers who cannot speak English to be removed from service
- **FMCSA BASIC categories:** Unsafe Driving, Crash Indicator, HOS Compliance, Vehicle Maintenance, Controlled Substances/Alcohol, HM Compliance, Driver Fitness
- **Intervention threshold:** Carriers above percentile thresholds may be "prioritized for further monitoring" — not automatically shut down

## The Enforcement Gap (Original Analysis)
The E&P Travel case illustrates a structural gap:
1. FMCSA's Safety Measurement System **collected the data**: violations in 50% of inspections, an English proficiency violation 8 months before the fatal crash
2. The system **assessed the carrier**: BASICs were tracked
3. But the system's response to a carrier with these red flags was... a notation in a database
4. The carrier continued operating. Eight months after one driver was cited for English proficiency failure, another driver allegedly couldn't speak English crashed into stopped traffic, killing five.

**The counter-argument:** FMCSA monitors 2 million carriers with limited resources. You can't shut down every carrier with a violation. The 50% inspection fail rate might be an artifact of small sample size (6 inspections). Many carriers have similar or worse profiles. The system is designed for statistical prioritization, not pre-crime prediction.

**The rebuttal to the counter-argument:** The English proficiency violation is categorically different from a broken lamp. It's a driver fitness violation that directly relates to the ability to read road signs — exactly the kind of sign that says "WORK ZONE AHEAD." When a carrier gets cited for exactly the failure mode that later kills five people, "we don't have the resources" isn't an answer. It's an admission.
