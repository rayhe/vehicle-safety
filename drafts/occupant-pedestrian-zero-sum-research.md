# Research: The Zero-Sum Safety Trade-Off

## Angle
The vehicles with the best OCCUPANT crash survivability are the same ones killing the most pedestrians. FARS data + the new NYT/IIHS 2026 hood height study reveal a quantifiable zero-sum game: every design choice that protects the driver externalizes risk to everyone outside the vehicle.

## Novel Contribution
Cross-tabulation of FARS occupant death/crash ratios by vehicle class with the NYT/IIHS 2.8%-per-inch pedestrian fatality finding. Computing the "safety externality" — the pedestrian cost of occupant protection.

## Primary Sources

### 1. FARS Data (2014-2023) — Occupant Death/Crash Ratios
Death-per-crash ratio by vehicle class (higher = deadlier for occupants):
- **Pickups: 0.489** (BEST for occupants, 85,128 fatal crashes)
- **Vans: 0.514**
- **SUVs: 0.524**
- **Sedans: 0.645** (WORST for occupants, 138,154 fatal crashes)
- **Sports cars: 0.682**

Best individual pickups (1,000+ crashes):
- Ram 1500: 0.341 (safest vehicle in a crash)
- Ford F-250: 0.364
- Toyota Tundra: 0.415
- Dodge RAM: 0.436
- Ford F-150: 0.458

Worst individual sedans:
- Chevrolet Cavalier: 0.857
- Buick LeSabre: 0.823
- Chevrolet Cobalt: 0.808

In a sedan crash, you are 1.3x more likely to die than in a pickup crash.

### 2. NYT/IIHS/Forensic Rock Study (June 2026)
- Every 1-inch increase in hood height → **2.8% increase** in pedestrian fatality odds
- ~3,000 pedestrian deaths from 2016-2024 attributable to taller hoods
- If hoods were 2002 heights: ~3,077 lives saved (10,000-simulation model)
- 200-400 extra pedestrian deaths per year from hood height growth
- Estimates are CONSERVATIVE (excludes parking lots, driveways, private roads)

Blind zone growth (3D scans):
- Chevrolet Silverado: blind zones nearly DOUBLED
- GMC Sierra: ~60% growth
- Toyota Tacoma: ~60% growth
- Ford F-150: ~25% growth

### 3. IIHS 2023 Study (earlier, but still relevant)
- Hoods >40 inches: 45% more likely to kill pedestrians
- Blunt front ends (>65°): 44% more likely to kill pedestrians
- "No functional benefit to these massive, blocky fronts" — Wen Hu, IIHS

### 4. NHTSA 2024 Full-Year Data
- 39,345 total traffic deaths (down 3.8% from 2023)
- Pedestrian deaths declined only 3% (vs 6-12% declines in other categories)
- Pedestrian deaths up 75% since 2009 nadir

## The Zero-Sum Calculation

Pickup hood ~47 inches vs sedan hood ~30 inches = 17 inches difference
17 inches × 2.8%/inch = **47.6% higher pedestrian fatality odds** for pickups

Meanwhile pickup d/c = 0.489 vs sedan d/c = 0.645
Occupant is **32% safer** in a pickup crash

**The exchange rate: every 1% improvement in occupant crash survivability costs approximately 1.5% in pedestrian fatality risk.**

Fleet-wide implications:
- 41,593 pickup occupant deaths over decade = low relative to crash volume (good for drivers)
- ~3,000 pedestrian deaths from hood height growth = cost exported to pedestrians
- NHTSA regulates occupant safety. Nobody regulates pedestrian impact geometry. Rep. Scanlon's hood height bill stalled.

## Counterargument (required)
Occupant safety and pedestrian safety are not PERFECTLY zero-sum. AEB with pedestrian detection can partially offset hood height. IIHS study controlled for AEB. But: "Safety advocates remain cautious about relying too heavily on technology. Automatic braking systems can struggle in darkness, bad weather, uneven road conditions, and situations involving children."

Also: fleet composition shift to pickups/SUVs is demand-driven, not manufacturer conspiracy. Consumers choose safety for themselves. The externality is structural, not intentional.
