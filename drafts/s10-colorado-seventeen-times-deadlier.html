The Gap

GM Built the Deadliest Compact Pickup in America. Then It Built the Safest.

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
July 16, 2026

Let's talk about what happens in the first 150 milliseconds. In a 1998 Chevrolet S-10 hitting the IIHS moderate overlap barrier, the footwell crushes inward 29 centimeters, the A-pillar retreats 16 centimeters toward the driver's skull, and the steering column rams 14 centimeters rearward while the dummy rebounds off the airbag, slams toward a bowed-out driver door, and nearly strikes the windowsill with its head.[2] IIHS rated restraint performance "Poor."

17.3×
Death-rate gap between the S-10 and its replacement, the Chevrolet Colorado

In a 2025 Colorado taking the same test at 40 miles per hour, footwell intrusion measures six centimeters, A-pillar rearward movement is zero, and the steering column actually shifts one centimeter forward, retreating from the driver instead of punching toward them.[3] Twenty years of structural engineering, applied to the same segment by the same company, turned a collapsing cabin into a rigid survival cell.

FARS data from 2014 through 2023 records 1,427 deaths in the Chevrolet S-10 at a rate of 4.83 per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, while the Colorado posted 348 deaths at 0.28.[1] Same Chevrolet badge, same compact-pickup purpose, and one kills at 17.3 times the rate of the other.

Fleet size makes the gap worse, not better: 918,750 Colorados share the road against only 218,750 S-10s, meaning the replacement has 4.2 times more registered vehicles yet accumulates 75 percent fewer deaths. Crashworthiness ratios explain why: for every fatal S-10 crash in the FARS database only 1.32 total crashes appear, meaning 76 percent of recorded crashes killed someone, while the Colorado's ratio of 2.04 means roughly half let someone walk away.

Impairment plays no meaningful role: S-10 drivers test positive for alcohol or drugs at 20.5 percent, nearly identical to the Colorado's 19.0 percent and the national fleet average. What separates them is crumple zones, high-strength steel, and a willingness to engineer survival space instead of treating the cabin as a suggestion.

Among compact and midsize pickups with at least 200 deaths in the dataset, the S-10 sits dead last: Ford Ranger at 2.91, Dodge Dakota at 2.62, even GM's own platform twin the GMC Sonoma at 2.13.[1] At the safe end, Toyota's Tacoma manages 0.80 and the Colorado anchors the bottom at 0.28. No other segment shows a 17-fold safety improvement within a single manufacturer's lineup.

GM discontinued the S-10 in 2004, but twenty-two years later 218,750 remain on the road, with model year 2000 alone accounting for 178 deaths. These trucks predate electronic stability control, advanced airbag staging, and high-strength door beams. By the standards of 2026, they are rolling monuments to what "acceptable risk" used to mean.
