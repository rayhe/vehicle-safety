# Research: The Nissan Kicks — The SUV Badge That Doesn't Protect You

## Angle
The Nissan Kicks has a 73.9% per-crash lethality rate — worse than the average sedan and 41% above the SUV class average. It's the deadliest "SUV" per crash in the FARS dataset. Consumers buy it thinking "SUV = safer." The data says otherwise.

## Novel Contribution
Per-crash lethality (deaths ÷ crashes in FARS) as a metric to measure whether an "SUV upgrade" from a sedan actually improves crash survivability. For the Kicks, switching from its platform sibling (Nissan Versa, 72.3% lethality) to the "SUV" version buys essentially nothing — just 2.2% less lethality. Meanwhile, actual SUVs like the Rogue (54.5%), Pathfinder (49.9%), or Armada (48.3%) offer dramatically better protection.

## FARS Data (Primary Source)
- **Nissan Kicks:** 339 deaths, 459 crashes, 0.739 lethality, 0.69 rate/100M VMT
- **SUV class average lethality:** 0.524 (from 121 SUV models)
- **Sedan class average lethality:** 0.645 (from 145 sedan models)
- **Kicks vs SUV avg:** 41% deadlier per crash
- **Kicks vs sedan avg:** 15% deadlier per crash

## Methodology
Lethality ratio = FARS occupant deaths ÷ FARS total crashes involving that vehicle (2014-2023). When a fatal crash occurs, this measures the probability that the vehicle's OWN occupant dies rather than surviving while someone else (other vehicle, pedestrian) dies. Lower = better occupant protection. A Ram 2500 at 0.205 means in 80% of its fatal crashes, the Ram occupant walks away — someone else died. A Kicks at 0.739 means the Kicks occupant dies nearly 3 out of 4 times.

## Limitations
1. FARS only captures fatal crashes (~36K/year), not the ~6.7M total crashes
2. Kicks entered US in 2018, so FARS window is ~6 years (2018-2023 model years in 2018-2023 calendar)
3. Demographic confounders: Kicks buyers may skew younger, less experienced, urban (higher-risk conditions)
4. Lethality ratio conflates single-vehicle and multi-vehicle crashes
5. The second-generation Kicks (2025+) is a full redesign on CMF-B platform — these findings may not apply

## Strongest Counterargument
The Kicks' low price (~$21K) attracts first-time and budget buyers who may drive in riskier environments (urban, congested, higher-speed stroads). The lethality metric doesn't control for crash severity or type. A heavier vehicle in the same crash scenario would have more kinetic energy to transfer. The Kicks' high lethality may partly reflect crash type distribution (more high-speed impacts relative to fleet) rather than pure structural failure. Also, the 2025 redesign addresses many of these issues with improved structure and IIHS Top Safety Pick rating.
