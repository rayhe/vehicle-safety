# Research: southern-slow-down-wrong-variable

## Angle
Operation Southern Slow Down — a five-state anti-speeding enforcement campaign (FL, AL, GA, SC, TN) — wraps up July 18, 2026. Its anchor state, Florida, has the single lowest speeding-related fatality percentage in the entire United States: 11% per NSC/FARS, or 6% per Florida's own 2024 Crash Facts report. Meanwhile, FARS data shows vehicle choice alone creates a 94x death-rate gap within the SUV class. The campaign is targeting a variable that barely matters in the state leading the charge.

- **Kentucky: 13%**
- **Tennessee: 19%**

## Kill Test
**Why it shouldn't run:** Speed enforcement has value beyond direct fatality prevention — it deters dangerous driving, establishes norms, and catches other violations (DUI, warrants) via traffic stops. Vehicle choice is a systemic, long-term factor; a one-week campaign can't address engineering. Comparing them is somewhat apples-to-oranges. Also, "6%" vs "11%" discrepancy between FL's own report and NSC suggests definitional differences in what counts as "speeding-related."

**Why it should run:** The data genuinely reveals a mismatch between enforcement emphasis and fatality causes. Florida having the nation's lowest speeding-related fatality percentage while anchoring an anti-speeding campaign is ironic and under-reported. The 94x vehicle-choice gap dwarfs any plausible effect of speed enforcement. Nobody has juxtaposed these two datasets.

## Novel Contribution
First known comparison of Operation Southern Slow Down's target states' speeding-related fatality percentages against FARS vehicle-choice death-rate gaps. The finding that Florida, the campaign's anchor, has the lowest speeding percentage nationally reframes the campaign's value proposition.

## Strongest Counterargument
Speed enforcement catches more than speeders. Traffic stops initiated during speed campaigns yield DUI arrests, warrant pickups, and other interventions. The 7,000 tickets and 8 arrests from 2025's campaign represent a broader deterrence infrastructure. Speed campaigns are cheap; vehicle fleet turnover takes decades.

## Limitations
- FARS death rates are cumulative across all model years, not just current production. Old vehicles dominate the high-rate list.
- "Speeding-related" is defined differently by Florida (6%) and NSC/FARS (11%). Florida may use a narrower definition. Both are low relative to the national 29%.
- Vehicle choice and speed are not independent variables. Higher-rate vehicles may attract more speed-prone drivers.
- Campaign effectiveness is measured in behavioral change, not solely fatality reduction — this analysis doesn't capture deterrence effects.
