For One Month, America's Roads Were Safer Than Before COVID. Don't Get Used to It.

Clara Rollover • Clautomotive Desk
July 6, 2026

March 2026 killed 3,030 people on American roads. That number dropped the mileage death rate to 1.07 per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, according to the National Safety Council's preliminary estimates. In the National Safety Council's monthly tracking, a single month's estimated fatality rate dipped below the 2014–2019 pre-COVID average of approximately 1.13 per 100 million VMT for the first time since the pandemic began.[1]

1.07
Deaths per 100M VMT in March 2026, the lowest NSC monthly estimate since before the pandemic

Read that again, because the milestone has an asterisk the size of a highway billboard. March is a low-season month: shorter recreational driving, fewer motorcycles on the road, no holiday-weekend corridor massacres. Comparing March 2026 to March 2025 controls for some of that seasonality, and the year-over-year improvement is genuine: deaths down 6%, miles driven up 1.6%, yielding a 7% reduction in the mileage death rate, a downward glide path visible across three consecutive Marches: 1.21 in 2023, 1.20 in 2024, and now 1.07.[1]

If 1.07 held for a full calendar year at 2025's roughly 3.36 trillion vehicle miles traveled, the annualized death toll would land around 35,950. That would be the lowest count in well over a decade. It will not hold. Summer driving historically pushes monthly rates 15 to 25 percent above March levels, and the National Safety Council's own estimates show that July and August are consistently the deadliest months on the calendar. A realistic full-year projection for 2026 sits closer to 1.10–1.15, which would yield somewhere between 37,000 and 39,000 deaths.

Seasonal noise aside, the improvement reflects something structural. NHTSA's 2024 final FARS count landed at 39,254 deaths, a 4% decline from 2023's 40,901 and the first annual count below 40,000 since 2020.[2] That marked the eleventh consecutive quarter of year-over-year decline, with a fatality rate of 1.19, the lowest since 2019 but still 5.3% above the pre-pandemic average, a gap that March 2026 closed entirely.

At the annual level, the crossing had already happened. NHTSA's preliminary full-year 2025 estimate put the rate at 1.10 per 100 million VMT across an estimated 36,640 deaths, technically below the pre-COVID floor for the first time on a yearly basis.[2] But annual averages compress summer spikes and winter dips into a single number. The NSC's month-by-month estimates reveal the texture inside that average, and March 2026's 1.07 is the lowest individual monthly reading in the post-pandemic series.

Twenty-One Thousand Bodies

Between 2020 and 2024, the elevated post-COVID fatality rate generated roughly 21,500 excess traffic deaths compared to what the pre-pandemic baseline would have predicted. Blunt arithmetic: take each year's actual rate, subtract the pre-COVID average of 1.13 (the mean of six annual FARS rates, 2014 through 2019: 1.08, 1.13, 1.17, 1.16, 1.13, 1.10), multiply by that year's VMT. Peak carnage came in 2021, when the rate hit 1.33 on 3.21 trillion miles, producing an estimated 6,400 excess deaths in a single year. By 2024, the excess had shrunk to roughly 2,000 per year, and with 2025's full-year rate landing at 1.10, the "COVID debt" appears finally paid in full.

That is a deeply strange thing to write about human lives. But the numbers don't carry the weight of individual loss; they carry a policy signal. Something about the way Americans drove during and after the pandemic killed an additional 21,500 people over five years. Higher speeds, more aggressive driving, lower seatbelt use in some demographics, and a documented increase in impaired driving all contributed to a behavioral shift that outlasted the lockdowns by years.[3]

Eleven States Got 20% Safer. Seven Got 20% Worse.

Pull the national average apart and a geographic fracture emerges that barely qualifies as the same country. Through the first quarter of 2026, the NSC identified eleven states where traffic deaths dropped 20% or more from the same period in 2025. New York led with a staggering 72% decline (89 fewer deaths), Alabama fell 43% (100 fewer), and Washington, Wisconsin, Illinois, Louisiana, and Nevada all posted double-digit percentage drops.[1]

Running the other direction, seven states and the District of Columbia saw increases exceeding 20%. DC's 300% spike sounds catastrophic until you see the base: three deaths became twelve. Small-number math makes rural states particularly volatile: North Dakota's 38% spike amounts to five additional deaths, South Dakota's 33% to six, all noise masquerading as trend. Mississippi is harder to dismiss: thirty-six additional deaths in a single quarter, a 33% jump, in a state that already had one of the nation's highest per-mile fatality rates.

A hypothesis hides in the geographic pattern, even if the data can't prove it yet: states with newer average fleet composition, more urban driving, and higher adoption of vehicles equipped with automatic emergency braking and electronic stability control are showing the steepest improvements, while states with older fleets, higher speed limits, and longer rural stretches are not. Safety technology works, but it works far better when people actually own vehicles that have it.

The Fleet Is the Mechanism

FARS data from 2014 through 2023 tells a clear story about which vehicles kill: the Chevrolet Cobalt at 5.10 deaths per 100 million VMT, the S-10 pickup at 4.83, the Ford Ranger at 2.91. These are aging platforms, most built before the 2012 ESC mandate, and they are slowly, steadily leaving the active fleet.[4] Average vehicle age in the United States stands at 12.6 years, which means the typical car on the road was built around 2013 and predates many modern safety mandates. But the oldest, deadliest vehicles, the ones manufactured in the early 2000s with fatality rates two to five times the fleet average, are finally hitting the point where their owners scrap them rather than repair them.

Meanwhile, the safest vehicle classes in the FARS data belong to models that entered production after 2015: Kia Seltos at 0.04 deaths per 100M VMT, Ford Bronco at 0.05, Hyundai Kona and Mazda CX-5 at 0.12 — all benefiting from standard AEB, better structural design, improved roof crush resistance, and in many cases, standard lane-keeping systems.[4] Between the worst vehicles still on the road and the best entering it, the gap spans a factor of forty or more.

What This Means for You

If you are shopping for a used car in 2026, this is the actionable takeaway: model year matters more than almost any other factor. A 2019 Honda CR-V and a 2005 Honda CR-V share a nameplate and almost nothing else in terms of crash protection. A 2019 CR-V has standard AEB, ESC, better side-curtain airbags, and a body structure redesigned to modern IIHS standards. The older one was built to pass tests that no longer exist.

Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls regardless. A Honda recall expansion announced this week alone covers 98,892 vehicles across nearly every Honda and Acura model from 2016 through 2026, for a seat weight sensor that can cause unintended airbag deployment during a crash.[5] Safety mandates and technology are pushing the rate down. Recalls are the reminder that no vehicle is immune to defects.

Don't Celebrate Yet

One month below the pre-COVID baseline is not a trend. The counterargument is strong and it deserves its full weight: March is seasonally mild, monthly data is noisy (preliminary NSC estimates are typically revised 5–10%), and the summer driving season historically erases the gains winter buys. July 2025 could have posted a rate above 1.30 and we wouldn't know for months.

Structural improvement is real, the 21,500-death COVID overshoot is likely ending, and the fleet is turning over in the right direction. But sustaining a rate below 1.13 through summer road trips, holiday weekends, and the annual motorcycle-season spike is what "safer than before COVID" on an annualized basis actually demands. America still kills roughly 107 people on its roads every single day, a number that would qualify as a public health catastrophe in any other context. March 2026 showed what is possible, and whether the country notices long enough to make it permanent is the only question left.
