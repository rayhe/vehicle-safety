# Research Notes: $665 Million for the Wrong 20%

## News Peg
- **NHTSA April 2026:** Released 2024 FARS annual (39,254 deaths, rate 1.19/100M VMT) + 2025 preliminary (36,640 deaths, rate 1.10, -6.7% YoY)
- **$665 million in traffic safety grants** announced for state highway safety offices
- Grants target: distracted driving, unbelted driving, impaired driving, excessive speed
- Source: AASHTO Journal, NSC, NHTSA press release (April 2026)
- NHTSA also running "Put the Phone Away or Pay" campaign (April 6-13)

## Thesis
NHTSA's $665M is almost entirely enforcement-focused (DUI checkpoints, seatbelt campaigns, speed enforcement, distracted driving campaigns). But FARS data shows:
1. Impairment is a factor in only ~20% of fatal crashes
2. The remaining 80% involve sober drivers in vehicles that fail to protect them
3. Model year improvements of 80-93% prove that ENGINEERING saves more lives than ENFORCEMENT
4. Zero of the $665M targets fleet turnover — the intervention with the highest demonstrated impact

## Original Contribution / Novel Analysis
Cross-referencing NHTSA grant categories (enforcement) against FARS data (vehicle engineering) to calculate the "addressable share" of each intervention:
- If you eliminate ALL impairment: you address ~20% of fatal crashes → ~7,250 lives/year (at 2025 rate)
- If you retire all vehicles with avg-age-at-death >15 years: you address the oldest, deadliest fleet
- Model year improvements prove 80-93% reduction per vehicle when old is replaced with new

The math: $665M / 7,250 impairment-addressable deaths = $91,724 per addressable death for enforcement
But enforcement doesn't eliminate 100% of impairment — best estimates suggest DUI enforcement reduces fatalities by 7-9% (RAND/CDC)
So realistic impact: $665M → ~520-650 lives saved → $1.02M-$1.28M per life saved

Cash-for-clunkers targeting the deadliest 10 vehicles in FARS could retire ~100,000 vehicles at $5,000 each = $500M
With 80% safety improvement per vehicle, that's potentially more lives saved per dollar.

## Counterargument (strongest case)
Enforcement has immediate behavioral spillover: a DUI checkpoint changes behavior of ALL drivers who see it, not just the one arrested. Economic multipliers of enforcement are harder to quantify but real. Cash-for-clunkers 2009 cost $3B and had mixed safety results because it wasn't targeting the deadliest vehicles.

## Limitations
- FARS doesn't distinguish between primary and contributing causes
- Impairment rate may undercount (toxicology not done on all fatalities)
- Model year improvements conflate engineering with fleet age effects
- Grant funding addresses multiple factors simultaneously
- State-level allocation decisions may differ from federal priorities
