# Research: Driver vs. Passenger Fatality Gap — 2025 NHTSA Sub-Category Report

## Primary Source
NHTSA DOT HS 813 829 — "Early Estimates of Motor Vehicle Traffic Fatalities and Fatality Rate by Sub-Categories in 2025" (July 2026)
URL: https://crashstats.nhtsa.dot.gov/Api/Public/ViewPublication/813829

## Key Data Points (2025 vs 2024)

### The Gap
- **Driver fatalities: down 5%**
- **Passenger fatalities: down 12%**
- Passenger improvement = 2.4x the driver improvement

### Supporting Context
- Total fatalities: 36,640 (down 6.7% from 39,254 in 2024)
- Fatality rate: 1.10 per 100M VMT (down from 1.19)
- PV occupant fatalities overall: down 10%

### What Improved (tech-addressable)
- Urban arterials: down 12% (AEB territory)
- Night crashes: down 8%
- Rollovers: down 11% (ESC)
- Ejections: down 13% (seatbelts + structural)
- Unrestrained occupants: down 13%
- Newer vehicles (<10 years): down 12%
- Speeding-related: down 11%

### What Didn't Improve (driver behavior)
- Rural interstates: UP 1% (high-speed, single-vehicle crash territory)
- 65+ age group: down 0% (no improvement at all)
- Pedalcyclists: UP 4%

### FARS Cross-Reference
- Overall impairment in fatal crashes: 20.0% (any substance)
  - Alcohol: 15.1%
  - Drugs: 8.7%
- Total fatal crash drivers in FARS sample: 490,736
- Impairment is overwhelmingly a DRIVER problem, not a passenger problem

### The Thesis
The 2.4x gap between driver and passenger fatality improvement is the fingerprint of technology reaching its limit against human behavior. Modern safety systems (AEB, ESC, curtain airbags, improved structures) excel at protecting occupants from EXTERNAL threats — the other car, the rollover, the side-impact. They are measurably less effective when the threat is the driver themselves: impairment, distraction, speeding.

Passengers benefit from both: they're protected from external crashes AND they're not the ones making the fatal decisions. Drivers only get half the benefit.

### Novel Contribution
FARS data shows 20% of drivers in fatal crashes were impaired. Those deaths are overwhelmingly single-vehicle, single-occupant scenarios where no amount of AEB or lane-keeping helps. The 5% driver improvement likely reflects ESC (roadway departure crashes down 9%) and structural improvements. The 12% passenger improvement reflects all of the above PLUS the absence of driver-choice mortality.

This is the first NHTSA sub-category report to show such a stark driver/passenger divergence. It suggests the next wave of safety gains will require targeting driver behavior (interlock, phone-blocking, ISA) rather than crash protection.

### Counterargument (Required)
The 12% passenger improvement could partly reflect demographics: fewer passengers per vehicle means fewer passenger-miles exposed. If passenger VMT dropped proportionally, the rate improvement is smaller than the count suggests. NHTSA does not publish passenger-specific VMT.

### Limitations (Required)
- This is preliminary NHTSA data based on ratio-adjusted FARS coding, not final counts
- Alcohol impairment data has significant reporting lag and is excluded from this report
- The driver/passenger split doesn't control for crash type, vehicle age, or belt use
- Passenger fatalities are a smaller absolute number, so the percentage change is more volatile
