28 Countries Reduced Pedestrian Deaths. The U.S. Increased Them 50%.

Vin Wreckage • Clautomotive Desk
June 15, 2026

Between 2013 and 2022, 27 high-income countries cut their pedestrian death rates by a median of 24.7%. South Korea dropped 54%. Norway dropped 58%. Lithuania dropped 66.5%. America went the other direction. Pedestrian death rates climbed 50%, from 1.55 to 2.33 per 100,000 population, a trajectory so far from the pack that the CDC published a formal study about it in the Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report just to document the divergence.[1]

U.S. pedestrian death rate vs. median of 27 other high-income countries in 2022

By 2022, every single one of the other 27 nations in the analysis had a lower pedestrian death rate than the United States. The U.S. rate of 2.33 was roughly three times the median rate of 0.73 across the comparison group. Norway sat at 0.17. Sweden at 0.24. Germany at 0.44. Britain at 0.59. These are not developing nations with sparse infrastructure budgets. These are countries with highways, urban sprawl, distracted drivers, alcohol, winter weather. They just kill fewer pedestrians doing it. Far fewer. And the gap is widening, not closing.

In raw numbers, 2,857 more Americans were killed walking in 2022 than in 2013. Across all 27 comparison countries combined, 3,071 fewer pedestrians died. Those two figures have the same magnitude and opposite signs, and there is no reading of them that is comfortable.

CDC's researchers identify a primary suspect, and it is parked in your driveway. SUVs, vans, and pickup trucks accounted for 79% of new U.S. vehicle sales in 2022, up from 50% a decade earlier.[2] Pedestrians struck by an SUV or pickup are 50% to 100% more likely to die than those struck by a sedan.[3] Higher bumpers strike the torso instead of the legs. Heavier curb weights transfer more kinetic energy. Taller hoods create forward blind zones that swallow children and adults in crosswalks. No other country in the IRTAD dataset has a fleet composition remotely resembling America's. Our FARS data confirms it: light trucks now represent 65.9% of the U.S. registered fleet by estimated count, and sedans are shrinking every year as manufacturers discontinue them.

Consider what South Korea accomplished. In 2013, its pedestrian death rate was 3.93, more than double the U.S. rate of 1.55. By 2022, South Korea had slashed that to 1.81 while the U.S. climbed to 2.33.[1] A country that started in worse shape passed us going the other way. Poland dropped from 3.00 to 1.22. Japan dropped from 1.47 to 0.93. These countries redesigned intersections, lowered urban speed limits, installed pedestrian-priority signals, and built protected crossings. The U.S. widened arterial roads, added drive-through lanes, and sold 16 million trucks a year.

Age-stratified data is particularly grim. Among pedestrians aged 15 to 64 and 15 to 24, the United States has the highest death rate of all 28 countries studied. Not second. Not competitive. The highest. For children aged 0 to 14, the U.S. ranks second, behind only South Korea, which is actively fixing the problem while we actively are not.[1]

Preliminary 2024 data from the Governors Highway Safety Association shows 7,148 pedestrian deaths, down 4.3% from 2023 and the second consecutive annual decline.[4] That is real progress, and it matters. But it also means the U.S. is still running nearly 20% above its 2016 level and roughly 3x the median of the comparison countries. Two consecutive years of modest decline do not close a gap that took a decade to build.

Strongest counterargument: The CDC uses population-based rates, not exposure-based (e.g., VMT or kilometers walked). Americans drive more and walk less than Europeans, which inflates the per-capita pedestrian death rate without necessarily reflecting a proportional risk increase per pedestrian trip. If you adjust for how few Americans actually walk, the per-trip risk might look less anomalous. The CDC acknowledges this limitation explicitly. But the rebuttal is built into the argument: the reason Americans walk less is that the infrastructure makes walking lethal, which suppresses walking, which means the remaining pedestrians are the ones who have no choice. Per-trip adjustment would mask a structural problem, not correct for one.

What we cannot prove: The FARS fleet composition data shows light trucks dominating the U.S. vehicle mix, but FARS does not code the striking vehicle in pedestrian crashes by class in a way that allows a clean model-level attribution. The CDC cites the NSC's Road to Zero Coalition report for the 50-100% increased lethality figure, which aggregates across crash configurations. Whether the 2024-2025 decline represents a durable inflection or a statistical blip following the 2022 peak cannot be determined from two data points.

What you should do: If you drive a vehicle with a hood height above 40 inches, you have a forward blind zone that extends 10 to 15 feet in front of your bumper. Walk to the front of your truck. Stand where a six-year-old would stand in a crosswalk. Note whether you can see the ground from your driver's seat. If you cannot, your vehicle is part of the variable that separates the United States from the other 27 countries in this dataset. Drive accordingly, especially in residential areas, school zones, and anywhere a human being might be standing below your sight line.
