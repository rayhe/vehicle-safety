The Gap

60% of Drivers Fear Headlight Glare. It Shows Up in 0.1% of Crash Reports.

Axle McScatter • Clautomotive Desk
May 19, 2026

I cross-referenced the AAA's February 2026 glare survey against the IIHS's October 2025 crash analysis of 24 million police reports, and the mismatch is so violent it made me check my math three times. Sixty percent of American drivers call headlight glare a nighttime hazard, but glare shows up as a contributing factor in roughly one or two out of every thousand nighttime crashes. The most-complained-about safety issue at NHTSA is statistically a rounding error in the body count.

Nighttime crashes where police cited headlight glare as a contributing factor, across 11 states from 2015 to 2023 (IIHS)

Put those numbers side by side and the gap is absurd. AAA surveyed 1,092 adults in a nationally representative panel and found that 73% of drivers who experience glare believe it has gotten worse over the past decade.[1] NHTSA reportedly receives more consumer complaints about headlights than about any other topic.[2] Meanwhile, IIHS principal research engineer Matthew Brumbelow combed through crash reports across 11 states where officers can code glare as a factor, spanning roughly 24 million total crashes from 2015 to 2023, and found fewer than 150,000 with a glare flag at all, and a far smaller fraction of those occurred at night. And that fraction has not increased, even as LEDs replaced halogens across the fleet and headlight output rose substantially.[3] Glare's peak year in the data was 2015, its trough was 2020. Glare's perception epidemic has no mortality signature.

What IS killing people at night is the opposite problem: not enough light. Vehicles with good IIHS headlight ratings are involved in 19% fewer nighttime single-vehicle crashes and 23% fewer nighttime pedestrian crashes than those with poor-rated headlights.[4] When the Institute released its first headlight ratings in 2016, exactly one system out of more than 80 earned a good score, and 82% rated marginal or poor. By model year 2025, 51% of tested systems earn good ratings, and only 16% remain marginal or poor. That shift has translated directly into fewer nighttime deaths. Headlights got brighter, and the crash rate went down.

So who is afraid, and who isn't? AAA breaks it down by vehicle type, and the answer is uncomfortably on-the-nose: pickup truck drivers report experiencing glare at a rate of 41%, while drivers of every other vehicle type report it at 66%.[1] The people sitting above the beam line barely notice a problem; the people sitting in the beam line complain loudly; and neither group is crashing because of it. Disparity: real. Discomfort: real. Danger: negligible. It is a textbook case of a perceived risk dwarfing an actual one, because the actual one is invisible to subjective experience: you don't feel the crash you avoid because your headlights illuminated a pedestrian 140 feet out instead of 90.

Regulation deepens the perception gap rather than closing it. FMVSS 108, the federal headlight standard, sets brightness in candela under lab conditions, and those limits have not changed since 1997.[2] NHTSA sets glare limits for adaptive driving beams, the fancy matrix headlights that selectively dim portions of the high beam around oncoming vehicles, but those are effectively limited to luxury makes like Audi and Mercedes, and as of the end of 2024, no vehicle in the U.S. market was equipped with them despite a decade of European deployment.[3] Standard headlights have no federal glare limit at all. Aftermarket LED bulbs consumers buy online to replace their halogens may exceed permissible brightness, but NHTSA has stated it "does not regulate the modifications individuals make to their own vehicles."[2] Translation: the agency punts to states, and only ten states require headlight alignment checks during inspections. One degree of upward misalignment increases glare intensity eightfold, and that misalignment accumulates through normal wear and tear on every car that ever hits a pothole.[2]

There is a constituency for whom glare presents a genuine elevated risk, and the IIHS data identifies them clearly: drivers over 70 and those with age-related macular degeneration.[3] Drivers between 55 and 60 showed no increased crash risk from glare. Prescription glasses wearers reported more discomfort (70% vs. 56% for non-wearers in the AAA data), though the crash data does not isolate that variable. For the broader population, though, the priority inversion stands: 82% of Americans drive vehicles whose own headlights are merely adequate or worse according to the IIHS scale, and only 18% of drivers use their high beams when traffic conditions are sparse enough to do so safely.[4] The average driver is functionally under-lighting their own path every single night while complaining that other people's lights are too bright.

If you drive at night, here is what the data says to do. First: look up your vehicle's IIHS headlight rating. If it is marginal or poor, you are statistically more likely to die in a nighttime crash than someone in the same car with a good headlight package; some vehicles offer dramatically different headlight systems across trim levels. Second: use your high beams when traffic conditions allow. The University of Michigan found that only 18% of drivers who were isolated enough on the road to safely use high beams actually did; that is thousands of feet of visibility you are leaving on the table. Third: get your headlight alignment checked, especially if you drive a vehicle with more than 50,000 miles on it. These three steps will do more for your nighttime survival odds than any amount of squinting at oncoming traffic.
