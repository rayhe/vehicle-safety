## Original Contribution: Kinetic Energy Math
- Tesla Model 3 mass ≈ 1,769 kg (3,900 lbs)
- At 142 mph (63.5 m/s): KE = ½ × 1,769 × 63.5² = 3,564,000 J ≈ 3.56 MJ
- .44 Magnum bullet: ~1,945 J
- Ratio: 3,564,000 / 1,945 ≈ **1,832× the energy of a magnum bullet**
- At 102 mph (impact speed, 45.6 m/s): KE ≈ 1,839,000 J ≈ 946× a .44 Mag
- At 70 mph (Katy crash): KE ≈ 860,000 J ≈ 442× a .44 Mag
- At 30 mph (speed limit): KE ≈ 159,000 J ≈ 82× a .44 Mag
- Energy scales with v². 142 mph has 22.4× the crash energy of 30 mph.

## Limitations
- FARS does not record impact speed — speeding classification is officer judgment
- Cannot determine from FARS how many crashes involve speeds >100 mph
- Murder charges are extremely rare for driving — most DAs prosecute vehicular manslaughter even for extreme cases
- Two cases do not establish a "trend" — this is an emerging prosecutorial posture, not settled law

## Strongest Counterargument
Murder requires "implied malice" — proof the defendant knew their actions created a substantial risk of death and consciously disregarded that risk. This is a MUCH higher bar than manslaughter. Defense attorneys argue everyone speeds, and the line between "I was going too fast" and "I accepted someone might die" is nearly impossible to draw without evidence of specific intent. The Rancho Mirage case took years to prosecute and was contested on exactly these grounds. Charging driving as murder could set a precedent that criminalizes behavior millions engage in daily — just at a different degree.
