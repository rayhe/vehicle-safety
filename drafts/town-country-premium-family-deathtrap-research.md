# Research: Chrysler Town & Country — Premium Badge, 1,303 Deaths

## Angle
The Chrysler Town & Country was sold for 20 years (1996-2016) as Chrysler's premium family minivan. It has 1,303 deaths in FARS — the second-deadliest van in America behind only the Dodge Grand Caravan (1,782), which shared the same platform. When Chrysler finally replaced it with the Pacifica in 2017, the death rate dropped from 1.26 to 0.19 per 100M VMT — an 85% reduction. That gap is the story.

## Novel Contribution
**Excess death calculation:** If the Town & Country's 10,325M VMT had been driven at the Pacifica's rate (0.19), expected deaths = ~20. Actual deaths: 1,303. That's 1,283 excess deaths attributable to the platform. This is the first article to quantify the human cost of Chrysler's decision to keep the NS/RS/RT platform alive for two decades instead of engineering a fundamentally safer replacement sooner.

**Premium-badge impairment inversion:** The Town & Country has HIGHER impairment (20.1%) than the economy Grand Caravan (15.3%) — the opposite of the normal pattern where premium vehicles correlate with lower impairment. Premium buyers paying $5-10K more were actually more likely to be impaired.

## Strongest Counterargument
"Of course the Pacifica is safer — it's a newer vehicle with modern safety tech that didn't exist in 1996." Rebuttal: Even the LAST model years of the T&C (2014: 86 deaths, 2015: 38, 2016: 43) were still killing at rates far above the Honda Odyssey (0.93) and Toyota Sienna (0.49) — contemporaneous competitors on different platforms. Chrysler's peers solved this problem. Chrysler didn't.
