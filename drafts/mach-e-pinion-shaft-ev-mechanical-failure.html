EVs Have Fewer Moving Parts. One of Ford’s Just Broke.

Mia Crumplezone • Safety Engineering Desk
July 9, 2026

Twenty moving parts. That is the number the EV industry quotes when it wants you to believe electric drivetrains are simpler than internal combustion. No timing chain, no clutch packs, no exhaust valves. Just a motor, a gearbox, and a shaft connecting them to the wheels. On July 7, Ford recalled 42,784 Mustang Mach-Es because that shaft (the rear differential pinion, supplied by BorgWarner) can fracture while you drive.[1]

Consequences: loss of drive power on the highway, or unintended movement while parked without the parking brake applied. Your crossover can strand you in traffic or roll away in your driveway. Ford traced the problem to a Euro-market 2023 model that failed in March, then found 62 warranty claims and 14 quality reports stacked behind it.[2] Sixty-two owners learned that fewer moving parts does not mean zero failure modes.

No software patch fixes this. Ford must physically replace the rear differential assembly with a “more robust” pinion shaft, engineering shorthand for admitting the original was not strong enough. Dealers will do it free, but the remedy will not be available until December 2026.[1] Five months on a shaft that may or may not hold, through summer road trips and the holiday crush, in a crossover Ford markets to families at $37,795.

For context: the gas Mustang whose badge Ford bolted onto this crossover has a FARS fatality rate of 6.02 deaths per 100 million miles, nearly triple the sports car average.[3] It kills 274 people a year. One in five fatal crashes involves impairment. Ford sold the Mach-E as the Mustang families could survive. A pinion shaft that snaps in half complicates that pitch.

Fairness demands the counterpoint: zero reported accidents or injuries. Sixty-two failures out of 42,784 vehicles is 0.14 percent. BorgWarner supplies EV drive units across the industry, and one component failure does not indict the architecture. Simpler systems genuinely fail less often, on average. That average was not much comfort to the 62 owners whose differential gave out.

If you own a 2021 to 2023 Mach-E, check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls and use your parking brake every time until December. Recall number: 26V417000. Next time a salesperson says an EV is simpler, nod politely and ask which supplier made the differential.
