# Research: Below 40,000 — The Victory Lap

## Angle
NHTSA released 2024 full-year estimates: 39,345 traffic deaths, "below 40,000 for the first time since 2020." DOT and NHTSA framed this as a major achievement — 11 consecutive quarters of decline, lowest fatality rate since 2019. But the 39,345 figure is still 8.2% above 2019 (36,355) and 20.2% above 2014 (32,744). The "decline" is a COVID reversion, not a safety breakthrough. Nobody has computed the cumulative excess deaths above pre-COVID baseline.

## Novel Contribution
Calculating cumulative excess traffic deaths 2020-2024 against pre-COVID (2019) baseline:
- 2020: 39,007 − 36,355 = 2,652 excess
- 2021: 43,230 − 36,355 = 6,875 excess  
- 2022: 42,721 − 36,355 = 6,366 excess
- 2023: 40,901 − 36,355 = 4,546 excess
- 2024: 39,345 − 36,355 = 2,990 excess
- **Total: 23,429 excess traffic deaths above 2019 baseline in 5 years**

Also: the fatality rate of 1.20/100M VMT is STILL above the pre-COVID 7-year average of 1.13 (per NHTSA's own statement). NHTSA essentially admitted the rate "remains significantly higher than a decade ago" and "high relative to peer nations."

## Strongest Counterargument
The decline IS real — 11 quarters in a row is not statistical noise. The post-COVID behavior shift (more aggressive driving, higher speeds, lower seatbelt compliance) was a genuine safety crisis that COULD have become permanent. Getting back toward pre-COVID levels required active policy work. The question is whether the framing as "achievement" is justified, not whether the decline happened.

## Limitations
- 2024 figure is an estimate, not final FARS count (usually close but can shift ±500)
- Pre-COVID "normal" included a 2016-2017 spike (distracted driving, smartphones) so using 2019 as baseline is generous
- Cumulative excess calculation assumes 2019 would have been maintained, not declined further — safety tech improvements could have pushed it lower
- International comparisons need VMT normalization, different reporting standards
