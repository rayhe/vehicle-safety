# Research: Joyson Safety Systems — Takata's Successor and the Persistence of Defect DNA

## Angle
The company responsible for the largest automotive safety recall in history (Takata — 100M+ airbag inflators, 27+ deaths, criminal guilty plea) went bankrupt in 2017. Its assets were bought by Key Safety Systems, which rebranded as Joyson Safety Systems. Now Joyson is building its own rap sheet: 7 NHTSA recalls covering ~747,310 vehicles, active lawsuits over airbag inflator defects, 20 years of falsified seatbelt test data inherited from Takata's Hikone plant, and a brand-new July 2026 Hyundai Kona seatbelt buckle recall over "inadequate inspection controls."

The question this story asks: When the company behind the worst safety scandal in automotive history goes bankrupt and gets reborn under a new name, does the defect culture actually die?

## Primary Sources

### 1. Takata Background
- Founded 1933, Shiga Prefecture, Japan
- Faulty PSAN airbag inflators could explode, sending metal shrapnel into vehicle compartments
- Largest automotive recall in US history: 42M+ cars initially, grew to 100M+ inflators worldwide by 2024
- Linked to 27+ deaths (primarily in US), hundreds of injuries
- Filed Chapter 11 bankruptcy June 25, 2017
- Pled guilty to criminal wrongdoing — fabricated inflator test results
- Assets acquired by Key Safety Systems → renamed Joyson Safety Systems
- Source: Wikipedia (Takata Corporation), NHTSA

### 2. Joyson Safety Systems — Current Defect Record
- **NHTSA recall history:** 7 recalls, ~747,310 vehicles affected
  - 6 airbag recalls, 1 seatbelt recall
  - Source: vehiclesafetyrecalls.com / NHTSA data

- **Head Side Airbag Inflator Recall (25E-030, April 2025):** HPH-A inflators may have end cap separation or sidewall split. "Root cause has not been identified." 8,127 units. This is eerily similar to Takata's pattern — inflator housing failures propelling material into vehicle interior.
  - Source: NHTSA Part 573 Safety Recall Report 25E-030

- **Curtain Airbag Inflator Recall (21E-095)** 
- **2024 Cadillac Driver Front Airbag Cushion Recall (24V-311)**
  - Source: NHTSA / Motley Rice lawsuit documentation

- **Active lawsuits:** Allegations of defective 5AT propellant seals allowing moisture absorption → unstable propellant → inflator rupture. At least one known inflator rupture during a minor event.
  - Source: Motley Rice (motleyrice.com)

### 3. Seatbelt Test Data Falsification
- **October 2020:** Joyson found "inaccuracies" in belt webbing test data at Hikone plant (former Takata) — 20-year period
- **June 2021:** Investigation found 1,000 cases of data falsification at Hikone AND Philippines plants. Test results altered to meet legal and client standards.
- Also falsified data for child safety seat belts — tens of thousands affected
- Joyson holds ~40% of Japanese seatbelt market, ~30% globally
- "The inaccuracies arose before its acquisition" — but the culture and systems remained
- Source: Reuters (Oct 2020, Jun 2021), Nikkei

### 4. Hyundai Kona Seatbelt Recall (July 2026)
- 47,749 vehicles (2025 Kona Electric + 2026 Kona)
- Rear center seat belt buckle may fail to properly restrain occupant in crash
- Supplier: Joyson Safety Systems
- Root cause: "inadequate inspection controls" → stamping dies used beyond lifespan → worn tooling
- Hyundai advises: DO NOT USE rear center seating position until repair
- NHTSA recall 26V452
- Source: NHTSA, USA Today, Fox Business

## The Pattern
1. **Takata (pre-2017):** Criminal data falsification on airbag inflator tests
2. **Joyson inherits Takata (2018):** Claims inaccuracies predated acquisition
3. **Joyson at Hikone (2020-2021):** 1,000 cases of falsified seatbelt test data found at same plant
4. **Joyson airbag recalls (2021-2025):** New inflator defects — end cap separation, "root cause not identified"
5. **Joyson seatbelt recall (2026):** "Inadequate inspection controls" — stamping dies used past lifespan

The language shifts but the pattern doesn't: falsified data → inadequate controls → "root cause not identified" → recall → repeat.

3. When shopping for vehicles, consider that safety components come from tier-1 suppliers — the brand on the badge isn't the company that made the airbag or seatbelt
