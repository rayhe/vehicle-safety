Robotaxis Can Navigate City Streets at 35 MPH. They Cannot See Fire.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
July 19, 2026

On June 20, an unoccupied Zoox robotaxi in California encountered an active fire scene. Heavy smoke obscured the road ahead, and the vehicle did what any competent human driver would not: it drove straight in.[1] The car braked hard, tried to steer away, stopped, and sat there until a remote operator reversed it out. First responders had to place traffic cones behind it because the autonomous vehicle that Amazon spent billions developing could not identify the universal visual signal that something is on fire.

105
Zoox vehicles recalled on July 17 for inability to detect heavy smoke. That is 100% of its operational fleet.

Nine days before that recall went public, NHTSA Administrator Jonathan Morrison sent a letter to every autonomous vehicle developer in the country, and the language was not diplomatic. He described a "clear pattern of driverless AVs interfering with law enforcement and other first responders," citing vehicles that "drove directly into active emergency scenes, blocked the paths of ambulances and firefighters, or failed to recognize and respond to basic safety conditions like flashing lights, flares, smoke, fire, and traffic cones."[2]

Read that inventory again. Flashing lights. Flares. Smoke. Fire. Traffic cones. These are not subtle environmental cues requiring advanced scene understanding. They are deliberately designed to be obvious to a distracted human doing 45 in the rain while arguing with a teenager about screen time, and the billion-dollar perception stacks powering autonomous vehicles cannot reliably parse them.

Morrison killed the industry's favorite excuse in the same letter: "Emergency scenes are not rare or extreme 'edge cases.'"[2] That sentence matters more than everything else in the document combined, because "edge case" has been the autonomous vehicle industry's all-purpose absolution for years. Vehicle drove into a construction zone? Edge case. Couldn't handle a manual traffic signal from a police officer? Edge case. Froze in the middle of an intersection while an ambulance blared its siren behind it? Complicated edge case, very sorry, software update incoming.

NHTSA is now saying: no. A fire truck with its lights on is not an edge case but the baseline, and if your vehicle cannot handle it, your vehicle does not work.

The incident catalog Morrison referenced without naming names is grim enough to reconstruct from public reporting. On March 1, during a mass shooting at Buford's bar on Austin's West 6th Street that killed three people and injured fifteen, a Waymo robotaxi froze perpendicular to traffic and blocked an ambulance trying to reach the scene.[3] A police officer had to physically open the vehicle's door and move it while the ambulance driver reversed to find another route. That took roughly two minutes during a mass casualty event. In late May, a Waymo vehicle in Dallas partially blocked the route fire trucks needed to reach a burning apartment building.[1] In a separate Atlanta incident, a Waymo drove straight through an active police scene with guns drawn, and the company's public response was to note that a police officer successfully "disengaged the vehicle" afterward, as if the act of manually removing a robot car from a felony arrest scene is an acceptable operational protocol.[4]

Meanwhile, a San Francisco fire chief told reporters that Waymo vehicles were frequently blocking fire stations in the city.[5] An Austin police official was more blunt: the tech "was rolled out too fast, in too many cars, before it was actually ready."[5]

Morrison's letter drew the comparison the industry would rather you not think about: "Every second matters when law enforcement officers, firefighters, or paramedics are answering a call because lives are on the line. That is why human drivers who impede these operations are subject to fines and even jail time."[2] The consequences for autonomous vehicles that do the same thing: a letter, a meeting scheduled for the end of July, and Zoox's remedy of choice, a software update pushed to 105 vehicles that will presumably teach them what smoke looks like.

This is Zoox's second fleet-wide recall in sixteen months. In March 2025, the Amazon unit recalled 258 vehicles after its cars braked so hard they caused two rear-end collisions that injured motorcyclists.[6] NHTSA closed that probe after a software update. The pattern is consistent across companies: Waymo has recalled vehicles for hitting a utility pole, for failing to predict road geometry, and most recently for passing stopped school buses with their lights activated in Texas, a violation of state law now under investigation by both NHTSA and the National Transportation Safety Board.[1]

The strongest counterargument is the familiar one: human drivers kill roughly 37,000 people per year on American roads, and autonomous vehicles have a much lower per-mile fatality rate. That is true and important and entirely beside the point Morrison is making. The question is not whether AVs are safer on average across all driving scenarios. The question is whether a vehicle that cannot detect fire, yield to an ambulance, or recognize a police officer waving it to stop should be on public roads at all, regardless of how well it handles highway lane changes.

The counterargument also has a structural weakness: we hold human drivers to legal standards precisely because we know they fail. A drunk driver who blocks an ambulance faces criminal charges not because we expected perfection, but because we designed a system of consequences to discourage that specific failure mode. The AV industry has operated under an implicit exemption from that consequence structure, and Morrison's letter is the first clear signal that the exemption is expiring.

0
Fines or operational suspensions levied against AV companies for blocking emergency responders. NHTSA has scheduled meetings instead.

If you see a robotaxi frozen in an intersection near an emergency scene, you can approach the vehicle and speak through its external speaker system, which is apparently how first responders are being trained to handle the problem. You can also call 911 and report the obstruction. If you are shopping for a new car with advanced driver-assistance features, understand that "autonomous" does not mean "situationally aware," and that systems marketed as capable of full self-driving still rely on geofenced operational design domains that specifically exclude many emergency scenarios. Check whether your vehicle's ADAS can detect and respond to emergency vehicles by reviewing NHTSA's standing general order on AV crash reporting at nhtsa.gov.

NHTSA's meetings with AV developers are scheduled for the end of this month. Morrison closed his letter with five words that should be printed on every robotaxi's bumper: "Public trust on our roads is earned, not given."[2]
