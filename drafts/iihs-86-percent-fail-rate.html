By The Numbers

IIHS Tested 16 Cars. Two Passed. Here's the Body Count Behind the Other Fourteen.

Axle McScatter • Clautomotive Desk
May 21, 2026

I ran the numbers on the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety's latest batch of test results. Then I pulled the Fatality Analysis Reporting System database. Then I put them next to each other. My spreadsheet didn't flinch. I did.

87.5%
Failure rate: 14 of 16 vehicles earned zero IIHS safety designation in the May 2026 batch

On May 14, IIHS published ratings for 16 vehicles.[1] The update that sank most of them wasn't exotic. IIHS didn't invent a new crash mode or simulate a meteor strike. They measured what happens to the person sitting behind the driver in a 40 mph moderate overlap front collision. That's it. One additional measurement in a test that already existed. Fourteen vehicles couldn't pass.

Two survived. Only the Hyundai Palisade and Toyota Prius earned Top Safety Pick+, the sole tier anyone reached.[2] Not a single vehicle in the batch earned the lower-tier Top Safety Pick. The industry didn't just miss the top shelf. It missed every shelf.

Among the failures: the Toyota Corolla, Ford Ranger, Chevrolet Equinox, BMW 3 Series, Toyota 4Runner, Nissan Frontier, Honda Ridgeline, Jeep Compass, Toyota Land Cruiser, Toyota Sequoia, Ford Maverick, Kia K4, Audi A3, and BMW i4. Honda's Ridgeline collected three Poor ratings, the worst in the batch.[3] The i4 managed two. BMW's 3 Series, a car that starts above $45,000, earned two Marginal ratings for a test that simulates something as mundane as a front-offset crash at city highway speeds.

Here is where FARS enters. Eleven of the 14 failures have enough fleet history for FARS tracking from 2014 to 2023. Three of them (the i4, K4, and Maverick) are too new for FARS tracking. Those 11 models account for 13,825 fatalities across an estimated fleet of nine million vehicles.[4] The two models that passed? A combined 533 deaths across 1.3 million vehicles.

26:1
Ratio of FARS deaths: failed vehicles (13,825) vs. passed vehicles (533)

The death rate gap is even wider than the raw count suggests. Palisade's FARS fatality rate is 0.06 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled. Prius sits at 0.55. Meanwhile, the Ford Ranger clocks 2.91, the BMW 3 Series 2.73, and the Toyota Land Cruiser, a vehicle that costs more than $58,000 and weighs 5,300 pounds, posts a rate of 6.27. That is 104 times the Palisade's rate. One hundred and four times. It failed the same test.

A counterargument writes itself: IIHS raised the bar, and most of the industry hasn't caught up yet. True. Prior to 2022, the moderate overlap test didn't evaluate rear-seat passengers at all. Moving the goalposts is how progress happens. That 87.5% failure rate measures how much margin automakers had been operating on when nobody was watching the back seat.

But here is the thing the counterargument misses. FARS was watching the back seat. Every year, roughly 1,600 rear-seat occupants die in frontal crashes across the U.S. fleet.[5] The IIHS test didn't invent that hazard. It confirmed what the fatality data had been saying for a decade: automakers over-engineered the driver's cocoon and forgot about the seat behind it. Corolla's moderate overlap test showed the rear dummy's head approaching the front seatback while the lap belt rode up from the pelvis to the abdomen.[6] That is a specific, mechanical failure with a specific body count. IIHS merely quantified it in a controlled environment.

For consumers, the useful insight is blunt. If you are shopping among these 16 vehicles, exactly two have full IIHS endorsement: the Palisade (built after November 2025 only, due to a rear seat belt buckle change) and the Prius. For the other 14, you are buying a vehicle whose rear-seat crash protection failed a 40 mph test. If you regularly carry passengers in the back—kids, carpool riders, elderly parents—that is worth factoring into the math. Check your specific model at iihs.org/ratings. If you already own one of the 14, it does not mean your car is a deathtrap. It means it is performing about as well as the industry average, which is another way of saying: not as well as the two vehicles that actually passed.
