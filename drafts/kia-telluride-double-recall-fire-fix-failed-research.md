# Research: Kia Telluride Double Recall — The Fix That Caught Fire

## Angle
Kia recalled 462,869 Tellurides in June 2024 for a fire risk from overheating front power seat motors. Dealers installed brackets and replacement knobs. Then fires kept happening — including in vehicles that had already received the recall repair. Now Kia is recalling the same vehicles again (July 2026), this time adding an electronic fuse as a failsafe. The engineering fix itself was inadequate.

## Key Data Points

### First Recall (June 2024, NHTSA 24V407000)
- 462,869 Kia Tellurides, model years 2020-2024
- Defect: Front power seat slide knob, if struck, causes internal switch misalignment → motor runs continuously → overheating → fire
- Fix: Install bracket on seat switch back covers + replace seat slide knobs
- Symptoms: inability to adjust seat, burning/melting smell, smoke from under seat
- "Park outside and away from structures" advisory
- At time of recall: 1 underseat fire + 6 motor melting reports

### Second Recall (July 2026)
- Same 462,869 vehicles
- Reason: First fix didn't solve the problem
- Kia investigation found:
  - "Sporadic dealer workmanship issues" — dealers may have reinforced the switch cover but NOT replaced the switch
  - Design flaw persists: "if occupant strikes the seat slide knob when entering vehicle, twisting forces may also result in a dislodged or misaligned seat switch"
- New fix: Install electronic fuse assembly to prevent continuous seat motor operation
- Updated incident count: 7 underseat fires + 11 motor melting incidents
- Owner notification: August 13, 2026
- VINs searchable starting July 17, 2026
- No injuries or deaths reported

### The Fuse Solution
The fuse is a failsafe — if the switch gets stuck and the motor runs continuously, increased current draw pops the fuse, preventing overheating and fire. This is a hardware circuit breaker, not a redesign of the flawed switch mechanism. Vehicles built after May 30, 2024 already have a reinforced switch.

## Original Contribution
- The "fix completion" problem compounds: NHTSA data shows ~39% of recalls go unfixed. But for Kia Telluride, even the 61% that got the first fix aren't actually fixed. Real exposure is somewhere between 39-100% of all 462,869 vehicles.
- Calculation: If 61% completed the first recall, that's ~282,350 vehicles. Of those, an unknown fraction are still vulnerable because the fix was inadequate. Plus the ~180,519 that were never repaired at all. Result: essentially ALL 462,869 are at risk until the second recall is completed.
- The fuse-as-failsafe approach reveals something: Kia didn't fix the root cause (the physical switch design). They added a circuit breaker to limit the consequence. This is a "Band-Aid on a Band-Aid" engineering approach.
