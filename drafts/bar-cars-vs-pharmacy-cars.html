The Corvette Is America's Bar Car. The Verano Is Its Pharmacy.

Dale Impactor III • Clautomotive Desk
May 7, 2026

Nobody asks what kind of impaired you are. FARS tracks whether a fatally injured driver tested positive for alcohol, drugs, or both, and then collapses the whole mess into one "any impairment" bucket. So I pulled the data apart, separated alcohol-only positives from drug-only positives from polysubstance overlap across every vehicle model with 300 or more fatally injured drivers in the FARS database from 2014 to 2023. What came back was a map of American intoxication, and it splits cleanly along your driveway.[1]

15.8%
Alcohol-only impairment rate among fatally injured Corvette drivers, highest of any model

Start with the booze. The Chevrolet Corvette sits at the top of the bar car rankings with a 15.8% alcohol-only impairment rate and just 4.9% drug-only, a 3.2-to-1 ratio that screams last call at the country club. Cadillac's CTS is right behind at 15.7% alcohol-only, and Audi's Q5 pushes the ratio to an absurd 4.8-to-1, with 15.5% bar and just 3.2% pharmacy. Infiniti G37, Subaru WRX, Mercedes E-Class: all bar cars, all north of 13% alcohol-only impairment.[1]

Now flip the table. Buick's Verano leads the pharmacy cars with an 8.3% drug-only rate against 11.6% alcohol-only. Hyundai's Veloster is the most symmetrical vehicle in the dataset: 7.2% drug-only versus 8.0% alcohol-only gives it a 0.90 drug-to-alcohol ratio, the closest any model comes to parity between the two kinds of impairment. Dodge Avenger (7.1% drug-only), Jeep Patriot (6.9%), and Pontiac Vibe (6.9%) round out the pharmacy fleet.[1] These are not exotic vehicles. They cost nothing. They are the cheapest things on the dealer lot, and their fatally injured drivers are more likely to test positive for non-alcohol substances than the drivers of anything else in the FARS database.

The math behind the split is basic inclusion-exclusion: alcohol-positive count plus drug-positive count minus any-impaired count gives you the polysubstance overlap. Subtract that overlap from each single-substance count and you get the pure bar and pure pharmacy numbers, which is why the arithmetic closes: FARS tracks each category independently per driver.[2] The limitation is that "drug-positive" in FARS covers everything from THC to fentanyl to Ambien without distinguishing between them, so a Veloster driver on benzodiazepines and a Veloster driver on methamphetamine both land in the same column. FARS does not break drug types at the vehicle level.

Class-level aggregation confirms what the model-level data implies, and it's not subtle. Sports cars lead overall impairment at 22.4%, with a 17.0% alcohol component that dominates. Vans sit at the bottom with 18.1% any impairment, but their 8.1% drug rate is remarkably close to the sports car drug rate of 9.5%. Vans close the drug gap while widening the alcohol gap, because van drivers drink less but take roughly the same amount of non-alcohol substances as Corvette drivers. That pattern holds across sedans, SUVs, and pickups too: the alcohol rate swings by class, the drug rate barely moves.[1]

Polysubstance impairment, where a driver tests positive for both alcohol and at least one drug, increased 38% from 2018 to 2022 nationally.[3] Two vehicles illustrate the extremity. At the model level, the BMW M5 leads with a 10.1% polysubstance overlap rate from a sample of 109 fatally injured drivers. Buick's Park Avenue comes in at 9.2% from 259 drivers. These are cars owned by people who, statistically, do not choose one substance when two are available. Research has consistently shown that alcohol-positive drivers face 13 times the fatal crash risk of sober drivers, while drug-positive drivers face roughly double the risk, and combining the two multiplies both effects in ways the existing crash risk models still struggle to quantify.[4]

What does this mean if you own one of these vehicles? Nothing about the car itself causes impairment: a Corvette doesn't make you drink, and a Verano doesn't hand you a prescription. But the buyer demographics, price points, and usage patterns of these vehicles correlate strongly enough with specific substance profiles that the data resolves into two distinct populations of fatally impaired drivers sharing the same roads. Alcohol skews toward performance and luxury vehicles, drugs cluster in affordable daily drivers, and the polysubstance population, growing 38% in four years, doesn't discriminate by driveway at all.

What to do with this

If you are shopping for a vehicle and care about the driving population you will share the road with: every car has sober and impaired drivers. But check FARS toxicology rates for any model you are considering at cdan.dot.gov/query. If a vehicle's any-impairment rate tops 25%, you are buying into a fleet where one in four fatal crashes involved a chemically altered driver. The national average across all models in our dataset is about 20%.

Limitations

FARS toxicology testing is not universal. Testing rates vary by state from 9% (Mississippi) to 81% (South Dakota), which means the true impairment rates are almost certainly higher than what FARS reports.[5] The inclusion-exclusion method assumes that FARS captures alcohol and drug positives independently per driver, which it does, but does not distinguish between drug classes. A "drug-only" result could be cannabis, opioids, benzodiazepines, or methamphetamine. We cannot determine whether pharmacy cars have more prescription drugs and bar cars have more recreational drugs; that inference is suggestive, not proven. Sample sizes for individual models range from 300 to over 13,000 fatally injured drivers, and models with smaller samples carry wider confidence intervals. Finally, this analysis cannot distinguish cause from correlation: vehicle choice and substance use are both downstream of demographics, income, geography, and age.

Strongest counterargument

The entire bar-car-versus-pharmacy-car framing may be an artifact of demographics, not vehicles. Corvette buyers skew older, male, and higher-income, demographics that drink more, while Verano buyers skew younger and lower-income, demographics with higher rates of drug use. The vehicle isn't the variable; the person is. This counterargument is strong, largely correct, and worth sitting with. The value of the vehicle-level framing is not causal but epidemiological: it tells you which driving populations overlap with which substance patterns, which matters for road-sharing risk assessment even if the car itself is irrelevant.
