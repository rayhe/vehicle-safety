# Research: Out-of-State Travel Death Decline

## Angle
NHTSA's 2024 subcategory data shows out-of-state travel deaths dropped 12% — the steepest decline of any category in the entire FARS dataset. Why? And what does the gap between this decline and the overall 3.8% decline tell us about where safety technology actually works?

## Self-Critique Gate
**Proposed angle:** The 12% decline in out-of-state travel deaths is 3x the overall decline. This isn't about road trips getting safer — it's about ADAS systems disproportionately saving lives on unfamiliar roads, where driver attention matters most.

**Challenge:** Is this genuinely surprising after 480 articles? YES — we have zero stories about out-of-state/travel deaths, and this is a novel cross-subcategory analysis connecting technology effectiveness to road familiarity.

**Verdict:** Proceed.

### NHTSA 2024 Final FARS
Source: https://www.nhtsa.gov/press-releases/traffic-deaths-2025-early-estimates-2024-annual
- 39,254 deaths in 2024 (final)
- Rate: 1.19 per 100M VMT
- Down from 40,901 in 2023 (3.8% decline)
- VMT increased 1%

### NHTSA 2025 Preliminary Estimates
- 36,640 estimated deaths in 2025
- Rate: 1.10 per 100M VMT (second-lowest in recorded history)
- VMT increased ~0.9% (29.8B additional miles)

## Original Contribution
Novel cross-subcategory analysis: The 12% out-of-state decline is 3.16x the overall decline rate (3.8%). The categories that declined fastest all correlate with unfamiliar-road driving behavior:
- Out-of-state (12%) = different roads entirely
- Ejections (9%) = high-speed loss of control
- Urban interstates (8%) = navigation-distracted driving
- Rollovers (6%) = lane departure on unfamiliar curves
- Roadway departures (5%) = drifting out of lane

These are exactly the failure modes that lane-keeping assist, forward collision warning, and AEB are designed to catch. The technology isn't saving lives evenly — it's saving them where drivers pay least attention.

## Strongest Counterargument
"Out-of-state travel" as a FARS variable captures anyone whose license state doesn't match the crash state. This includes daily border commuters (NJ→NY, MD→DC), not just vacationers. Post-COVID remote work may have reduced state-border commuting, deflating the number independently of any safety improvement. The 12% figure is also from H1 estimates only and could revise.

## Limitations
- NHTSA subcategory data is percentage changes from early estimates, not final counts. Actual death counts per subcategory not publicly released in this report.
- Cannot cross-reference with our FARS model-level data (FARS_BY_MODEL doesn't include in-state/out-of-state dimension).
- Causation unproven: ADAS adoption is one hypothesis; COVID-era travel pattern normalization, gas prices, and seasonal weather all confound.
- The decline could partially be denominator-driven (fewer long road trips, more flights).

## Actionable Takeaway
If you're driving out of state, activate every ADAS feature your vehicle has — lane keeping, adaptive cruise, forward collision warning. These systems are disproportionately effective on roads you don't know. And if your car is old enough that it doesn't have these features: that's the most important safety upgrade you can buy.
