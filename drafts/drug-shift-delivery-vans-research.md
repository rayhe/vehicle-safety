# Research: The Drug Shift — Commercial Vehicles Lead America's Impairment Pivot

## Angle
FARS toxicology data reveals a striking pattern: the drug-to-alcohol ratio among fatally-crashed drivers varies wildly by vehicle type. Commercial vans and delivery vehicles are the most "drug-shifted" — their drivers test positive for drugs at nearly the same rate as alcohol, while the national average skews heavily toward alcohol (0.57 drug-to-alcohol ratio). Meanwhile, European luxury cars and sports cars remain stubbornly alcohol-dominated.

## Kill Test
- **Genuinely surprising?** YES. Nobody expects a Nissan NV200 delivery van to have the highest drug/alcohol ratio in the entire FARS dataset. The commercial vehicle fleet's drug problem is invisible because these aren't flashy vehicles.
- **Novel cross-tabulation?** YES. No existing Crash Report article maps the drug/alcohol RATIO by vehicle type. cannabis-impairment-blind-spot.html covers the measurement problem; this covers the WHERE of the problem.
- **Actionable?** YES. Fleet managers, gig economy companies, and commercial vehicle operators need this data.

## Primary Sources

## Key Data Points

### National baseline (FARS 2014-2023):
- Alcohol impairment: 15.1% of drivers in fatal crashes
- Drug impairment: 8.7% of drivers in fatal crashes  
- Any impairment: 20.0%
- Drug-to-alcohol ratio: 0.57 (for every drug-positive driver, there are 1.75 alcohol-positive drivers)

### Most drug-shifted vehicles (drug/alc ratio > 0.75):
1. Nissan NV200 (Van) — drug/alc 1.08, drugs 12.7%, alcohol 11.8%, n=212
2. Mercedes-Benz M-CLASS (SUV) — drug/alc 1.00, drugs 10.8%, alcohol 10.8%, n=250
3. Hyundai VELOSTER (Sports Car) — drug/alc 0.92, drugs 9.4%, alcohol 10.2%, n=489
4. Acura RDX (SUV) — drug/alc 0.84, drugs 11.2%, alcohol 13.3%, n=383
5. Mini COOPER (Sedan) — drug/alc 0.83, drugs 9%, alcohol 11%, n=210
6. GMC SAVANA (Van) — drug/alc 0.82, drugs 11.2%, alcohol 13.6%, n=573
7. Buick VERANO (Sedan) — drug/alc 0.80, drugs 13.1%, alcohol 16.4%, n=397
8. Mercedes-Benz GL-CLASS (SUV) — drug/alc 0.79
9. Chevrolet UPLANDER (Van) — drug/alc 0.78
10. Ford TRANSIT CONNECT (Van) — drug/alc 0.77

Note: 4 of top 10 are commercial/cargo vans. The NV200 is Amazon/FedEx's workhorse delivery van.

### Most alcohol-dominated (drug/alc ratio < 0.40):
1. Ford WINDSTAR (Van) — drug/alc 0.30, alcohol 19.9%
2. Mazda MAZDA5 (Van) — drug/alc 0.31
3. Audi Q7 (SUV) — drug/alc 0.33
4. Tesla MODEL S (Sedan) — drug/alc 0.33, alcohol 19.1%
5. Nissan JUKE (SUV) — drug/alc 0.33
6. Audi A3 (Sedan) — drug/alc 0.35, alcohol 22.7% (!!)

8. Nissan 370Z (Sports Car) — drug/alc 0.40

### Novel finding: Van class split
Commercial vans (NV200, Savana, Transit Connect) are drug-shifted.
Family vans (Windstar, Mazda5, Sienna) are alcohol-shifted.
Same vehicle class, completely different impairment profiles.

### Cross-reference: Audi A3 alcohol outlier
Audi A3 has the highest alcohol percentage of any vehicle with 200+ drivers at 22.7%. That's 50% higher than the national average of 15.1%. European luxury entry-level cars attract a distinctly alcohol-heavy demographic.

## Strongest Counterargument
FARS toxicology testing is not uniform — testing rates and substances tested vary by jurisdiction and year. Drug testing was expanded significantly during this period, which could inflate drug positivity rates for newer vehicles (which are more likely to be involved in crashes in more recent years, when drug testing was more comprehensive). The NV200's drug/alc ratio might partly reflect WHEN those crashes occurred (more recent = more drug testing) rather than WHO was driving.

## Limitations
- FARS drug testing is not universal; some states test for drugs in <50% of fatal crashes
- "Drug-positive" includes prescription medications, not just illicit drugs — the NV200 driver on blood pressure meds isn't the same as the NV200 driver on methamphetamine
- Small sample sizes for some vehicles (NV200 n=212; minimum threshold used is 200)
- Can't distinguish between drug-only and poly-substance (drug + alcohol together)
- Fleet composition changes over time affect which model years dominate each vehicle's sample
