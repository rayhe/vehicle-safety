Tesla Says FSD Is 7x Safer. The Chevy Impala Got 8x Safer Without Any AI at All.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
June 22, 2026

Tesla told European regulators that Full Self-Driving makes its cars seven times safer than the average American vehicle. Reuters obtained the slide deck this month.[1] It is a remarkable claim. It is also a claim that a Chevrolet Impala can beat without a single line of autonomous driving code.

8.1×
Safety improvement of a 2017–2022 Chevy Impala over a 2005–2010 Impala, measured by FARS fatal crash counts. No AI involved.

We ran every model in the FARS database through a generational comparison: average annual fatalities for 2005–2010 model year vehicles versus 2017–2022 model year vehicles, using a decade of NHTSA fatal crash data.[2] The Impala dropped from 303 fatal crashes per year to 37. That is an 8.1x improvement, and the only software involved was a Bluetooth radio and maybe OnStar trying to sell you a subscription.

But the Impala is not alone in embarrassing Tesla's headline number: Chrysler's Sebring managed 7.9x, Ford's Taurus hit 7.7x, and a Dodge RAM pickup cleared 7.3x. Four vehicles, zero autonomy, all exceeding Tesla's claimed FSD advantage. Nine models total cracked 5x or better, including the BMW 3 Series (6.3x), Ford Ranger (6.2x), and the Honda Odyssey (6.1x), which achieved its safety gains while ferrying children to soccer practice in a vehicle shaped like a loaf of bread.

Fleet-wide, the numbers are just as damning for Tesla's claim. Across 93 models with sufficient data, 2017–2022 vintage vehicles produce 2.7 times fewer fatalities than 2005–2010 vehicles. Median improvement: 1.7x, with the average at 2.4x. These gains came from electronic stability control (mandated in 2012[3]), side curtain airbags, higher-strength steel, improved crumple zones, and automatic emergency braking becoming standard across manufacturers. Boring engineering, the kind that doesn't get keynote presentations.

Tesla's comparison methodology, as Reuters documented, stacks the deck three ways. First, it compares FSD crash rates using only airbag-deployment incidents against a national average that counts everything down to parking lot scrapes. Second, it compares brand-new Teslas against the average U.S. vehicle, which S&P Global Mobility pegs at 12.6 years old.[4] Third, it assumes every vehicle on the road could be replaced by a Tesla, including 18-wheelers, motorcycles, and the 1997 Corolla that has outlived three transmissions and two marriages. European regulators in Norway called the figures "self-produced" and "difficult to find correlation with the authorities' accident statistics."[1]

Our FARS data makes the trick visible. When Tesla says "7x safer than the average driver," a substantial fraction of that multiplier is vehicle age. A new anything is safer than a fleet average weighted toward older vehicles. The question is how much of the claimed improvement would survive if you compared FSD-equipped Teslas only to other 2022–2024 vehicles, controlling for the generational safety gains that every manufacturer delivers. Tesla has never published that comparison, and given that a minivan and a pickup truck already beat their number, we can guess why.

This matters beyond corporate marketing because Tesla used these statistics to secure FSD approval in the Netherlands, and Sweden is considering the same data for its own decision.[1] Greece cited unspecified data "from the other side of the Atlantic" in its move toward approval. Meanwhile, NHTSA is running three concurrent investigations into FSD, covering 58 incidents including traffic violations, reduced-visibility crashes, and questions about Tesla's crash reporting practices.[5]

There is a legitimate counterargument here, and it deserves full weight: our model year death counts do not perfectly control for exposure time. A 2022 vehicle spent fewer years on the road during our 2014–2023 observation window than a 2005 vehicle did, which inflates the apparent improvement. But this same bias applies to Tesla's comparison, and it applies with interest, because the average fleet vehicle Tesla benchmarks against has accumulated more exposure time than any individual new Tesla. Every deck is stacked the same way in both analyses, and ours just makes the stacking visible.

What you should do: If someone cites Tesla's "7x safer" statistic in a policy discussion, ask one question: seven times safer than what? If the baseline is the average U.S. fleet vehicle, the claim is measuring calendar years and steel thickness, not artificial intelligence. Any safety comparison worth trusting controls for vehicle age, compares like-for-like crash severity definitions, and comes from someone other than the company selling the product. As the European Transport Safety Council put it: "Give the data to a university, have it independently verified by a qualified researcher, and then let's talk."
