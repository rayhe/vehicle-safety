# Research: F-150 — America's Favorite Body Count

## Angle
The Ford F-150 is the #2 deadliest vehicle in FARS by total deaths (9,194), behind only the Chevy Silverado (9,591). It has never gotten a dedicated article. The paradox: it earns NHTSA 5-stars, IIHS Top Safety Pick, and ANCAP Platinum safety grading — while appearing in 20,066 fatal crashes. The "safest" truck in the segment is also one of the deadliest vehicles on American roads.

## FARS Data (2014-2023)
- **Deaths:** 9,194 (annual avg: 919.4 → ~2.5 per day)
- **Fatal crashes involving F-150:** 20,066
- **Death-to-crash ratio:** 0.458 (meaning F-150 occupants die in only 45.8% of fatal crashes; in 54.2%, someone in the OTHER vehicle dies)
- **Rate per 100M VMT:** 1.04 (moderate for trucks — Silverado is 1.25)
- **Fleet estimate:** 6,562,500
- **Impairment rate:** 18.9% (below truck average ~20%)
- **Share of ALL pickup deaths:** 22.1% (1 in 5 pickup fatalities involve an F-150)
- **Share of ALL vehicle deaths:** 4.8% (one single truck model accounts for nearly 5% of every vehicle death)
- **Rank:** #2 of 337 models by total deaths

## Model Year Breakdown
- Peak: 2001 model year = 672 deaths
- 10th gen (1997-2004) = 391+248+411+437+672+581+576+586 = 3,902 deaths (42.4% of all F-150 deaths)
- Post-2015 model years = 224+211+139+186+122+75+39+21+9 = 1,026 deaths (11.2%)
- The death curve maps nearly perfectly to the fleet age distribution, not to defects

## Safety Ratings (Current Model)
- NHTSA: 5-star overall (2022-2025 crew cab)
- IIHS: Top Safety Pick (2024+ crew cab, post-Sept 2024 production)
- IIHS: "Poor" on updated moderate overlap front test (REAR OCCUPANT protection)

- Industry-wide problem: ~42% of 139 vehicles tested score below "Good" on IIHS updated test

## Original Calculation
The F-150 appeared in 20,066 fatal crashes. Its occupants died 9,194 times. That means in ~10,872 fatal crashes involving an F-150, the F-150 driver walked away while someone else didn't. For comparison: the Toyota Camry appeared in 10,670 crashes with 6,328 deaths (ratio 0.593). The Camry's occupants die at a HIGHER rate than the F-150's — the truck protects its own at the expense of everyone else.

## Counterargument
The F-150's per-mile death rate (1.04) is actually LOWER than the Silverado (1.25), Sierra (1.01 ≈ tied), and far lower than the Ranger (2.91). The vehicle isn't defective. The body count is a function of ubiquity, not danger. But that's the policy problem: when the most popular vehicle in America is a 5,000+ lb truck with a 4-inch hood height advantage, "normal" truck lethality gets multiplied by enormous fleet size.

## Limitations
- FARS counts only fatal crashes; non-fatal injuries not captured
- VMT estimates use industry averages, introducing ±15% uncertainty
- D/C ratio doesn't distinguish occupant vs other-party deaths within a single crash
- Fleet age distribution confounds model year analysis (older vehicles have more exposure time)
