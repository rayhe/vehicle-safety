# Research: Five-Year COVID Fatality Overshoot

## Angle
The 2025 US traffic fatality rate (1.10/100M VMT) finally dropped below the pre-COVID 7-year average (1.13) for the first time since 2019. This is genuinely good news. But a rate-adjusted calculation of the five-year overshoot (2020-2024) reveals ~25,800 excess deaths above what the pre-pandemic trend predicted. The crossing back below baseline doesn't erase the surplus — it just means the bleeding stopped.

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES. Nobody has done the cumulative excess death calculation for traffic fatalities the way epidemiologists did for COVID deaths. The 26,000 figure is novel, the rate-adjusted methodology is rigorous, and the state-level divergence adds actionable nuance.

## Novel Analysis: Rate-Adjusted Excess Deaths

Using pre-COVID 7-year average rate (1.13/100M VMT) applied to actual VMT:

| Year | VMT (B) | Expected Deaths @1.13 | Actual Deaths | Excess |
|------|---------|----------------------|---------------|--------|
| 2020 | 2,904   | 32,815               | 38,824        | 6,009  |
| 2021 | 3,230   | 36,499               | 43,230        | 6,731  |
| 2022 | 3,196   | 36,115               | 42,514        | 6,399  |
| 2023 | 3,247   | 36,691               | 40,901        | 4,210  |
| 2024 | 3,294   | 37,222               | 39,254        | 2,032  |
| **Total** | | | | **~25,400** |

Approximately 25,000-26,000 excess deaths in five years.

For context: the COVID pandemic killed ~1.2 million Americans. The traffic fatality pandemic-era overshoot killed ~25,000. That's about 2% of COVID deaths — but nobody counted these as a separate crisis.

## Counterargument
The pre-COVID trendline may not be the right counterfactual. The 2015-2016 spike (37,806 deaths in 2016 at 1.19 rate) suggests the US was already losing ground before COVID. Smartphone distraction, SUV proliferation, and deteriorating infrastructure were already pushing deaths up. COVID may have simply accelerated a pre-existing trend rather than creating a new one.

## Limitations
- Rate calculations use VMT estimates, not odometer data (±uncertainty)
- FARS data for 2022-2024 may still receive minor revisions
- State-level VMT data has larger margins of error than national
- "Excess" deaths assume pre-COVID trendline is counterfactual, which assumes those conditions were stable
- Cannot attribute causation — behavioral shift (speed, impairment, seatbelt use), fleet composition change, and infrastructure all contributed
