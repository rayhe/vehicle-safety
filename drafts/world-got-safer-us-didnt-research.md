# Research: The World Got 21% Safer on Roads. The US Got Worse.

## Peg
WHO released new Global Status Report on Road Safety data on July 21, 2026.
Key finding: global road traffic deaths declined 21% between 2011 and 2025, despite adding 1+ billion motor vehicles to roads.

## Core Data

### WHO Global Data (Source: WHO, July 21 2026)
- 2011: ~1.47 million road traffic deaths (estimated baseline from 21% decline)
- 2025: 1.16 million road traffic deaths
- Decline: 21%
- Context: 1 billion MORE vehicles added to global roads in same period
- Road traffic injuries remain #1 cause of death for people aged 5-29
- UN General Assembly adopted new progress declaration on road safety same day

### US FARS / NHTSA Data
- 2011: 32,479 deaths
- 2014: 32,675 deaths
- 2016: 37,806 deaths (spike)
- 2018: 36,835 deaths (rate: 1.13 per 100M VMT)
- 2019: 36,355 deaths (rate: 1.10)
- 2020: 38,824 deaths (rate: 1.34) — COVID spike
- 2021: 43,230 deaths (rate: 1.38) — peak
- 2022: 42,514 deaths (rate: 1.35)
- 2023: 40,990 deaths (rate: 1.27)
- 2024: ~39,254 deaths (rate: ~1.19) — early estimate
- 2025: 36,640 deaths (rate: 1.10)

### The Divergence
- US 2011→2025: 32,479 → 36,640 = **12.8% INCREASE**
- Global 2011→2025: 21% DECREASE
- US is getting back to its pre-pandemic baseline while celebrating
- NHTSA's own statement: "America's traffic fatality rate remains high relative to many peer nations"
- 2025 rate of 1.10 per 100M VMT = same as 2019 (1.10) and barely below 2018 (1.13)
- US H1 2025: 17,140 deaths, rate 1.06 — "lowest mid-year fatality rate since 2014"
- 11th consecutive quarterly decrease in traffic fatalities as of Q4 2024

### Original Contribution
Nobody has compared the WHO's just-released global 21% decline figure against the US FARS trajectory over the same period. The contrast is damning: the world achieved a 21% reduction while the US experienced a 12.8% increase.

The US story is one of regression to mean: a pre-pandemic baseline that was already bad by peer-nation standards, a catastrophic COVID-era surge, and now a recovery that's being treated as progress when it's actually just getting back to where we started.

### Strongest Counterargument
The US has more VMT per capita than any peer nation, by a wide margin. Americans drive more, drive faster, on wider roads designed for speed. The comparison isn't entirely apples-to-apples. Also, the global decline is partly driven by massive improvements in low-and-middle-income countries that were starting from much worse infrastructure — their gains are cheaper to achieve. The US already had good roads, so incremental improvement is harder.

### Limitations
- FARS data is final through 2023; 2024-2025 are estimates/preliminary
- WHO global figure includes all road users (pedestrians, cyclists, motorcyclists); comparing to US total fatalities is valid but demographics differ
- VMT-adjusted rates vs population-adjusted rates tell different stories
- Per-capita comparison more favorable to US than per-VMT
- The 21% global decline figure needs the exact baseline year confirmed

### Actionable Insight
This isn't about individual vehicle choice. It's about infrastructure, enforcement, and policy. The 88% of road users surveyed who support tougher safety measures aren't getting them. Speed limit enforcement, road design that prioritizes pedestrians, mandatory AEB (coming 2029 in US) — these are the tools that made the global decline happen.
