### 1. 🔍 General Editor — 8.8/10

**Strengths:**
- Headline is specific, provocative, and widely relatable — the "every automaker" universality is the hook
- Opening paragraph efficiently establishes all four recalls with specific vehicle counts in one sweep
- Clean 4-section structure: setup → FARS context → what it means → actionable
- Pull-stat "30,000" is perfectly placed and instantly graspable
- Municipal fleet comparison gives tangible scale; Costco parking lot metaphor is memorable
- Tight at ~1,400 words with no padding
- Limitations and counterargument sections are substantive, not perfunctory

**Weaknesses:**
- Headline says "Three Million" but body total is 2,794,019 (~2.8M) — acceptable rounding for headline impact but notable
- Hyundai's 96K recall gets the least analytical depth compared to Ford and Honda
- The article's implicit thesis ("recalls are theater relative to baseline crash risk") could be stated more explicitly for readers who skim

### 3. ⚖️ Ethics Reviewer — 8.7/10

**Strengths:**
- Article doesn't moralize — lets the numbers carry the argument
- Counterargument is genuinely strong (1% reflects real QC architecture) and not a straw man
- Limitations section is honest about methodology gaps
- No manufacturer is singled out for moral condemnation
- Actionable section provides practical safety guidance without preachiness

**Weaknesses:**
- "left the subframe unprotected from the road salt" — still implies a conscious omission, though less editorializing than earlier "forgot to" phrasing
- Comparing recall risk vs baseline crash risk could be read as minimizing recall importance (addressed in limitations but framing leans toward skepticism)
- The 0.007% Stellantis stat compares *confirmed* fires to *total* recalled vehicles — the recall may have prevented fires. Partially addressed in limitations.

### 5. ⚖️ Legal Accuracy — 8.6/10

**Strengths:**
- Five references, all to primary or authoritative sources (NHTSA filings, FARS, Reuters)
- Specific vehicle counts traceable to filings
- "self-certified" language about 1% estimates is legally precise
- No claims of fraud or intentional misconduct
- Good use of "alleged" for unconfirmed injury counts
- Fatality rates given with units (per 100 million VMT)

**Weaknesses:**
- Reference URLs point to generic pages (nhtsa.gov/recalls, reuters.com) rather than specific filing pages
- "72 confirmed fires" for Stellantis should cite the specific filing section
- "orders of magnitude" for F-150 routine crash vs recall risk — should be verified (1 death in recall-related vs ~1,451/year routine is ~3 orders of magnitude, so the claim holds)

### 6. 🔬 Research Rigor — 8.7/10

**Strengths:**
- Novel contribution is clear: cross-tabulating a single week's recall filings against FARS data for the same vehicles
- The "every manufacturer says 1%" finding is genuinely original and verifiable
- FARS methodology is established and well-documented
- Counterargument engages the strongest available defense (QC architecture, not corporate spin)
- Limitations honestly flag the time-window conflation and self-certification issues
- Specific fatality rates with units (1.04, 0.29 per 100M VMT)

**Weaknesses:**
- 23,231 deaths combines all model variants across all years — should note this includes non-recalled model years
- Hyundai Tucson gets no FARS analysis despite being included in the 2.8M total
- "Orders of magnitude" comparison could be quantified: (recall deaths ÷ VMT) vs (routine crash deaths ÷ VMT) = specific ratio
- Recall completion rates cited (60-70%) lack a source
