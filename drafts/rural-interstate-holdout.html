America's Road Deaths Dropped 6.7%. One Category Got Worse.

Vin Wreckage • Clautomotive Desk
July 17, 2026

NHTSA released its 2025 subcategory fatality breakdown this month. It reads like a victory lap: urban interstates down 10%, urban arterials down 12%, nighttime crashes down 8%, rollovers down 11%, ejections down 13%, newer vehicles down 12%. The national toll fell from 39,254 to 36,640. The fatality rate dropped to 1.10 per 100 million VMT.[1]

+1%
Rural interstate fatalities in 2025 — the only subcategory that increased

One line item went the other direction. Rural interstates rose 1%, the only subcategory that increased, meaning every other category had to decline by more than 6.7% to compensate.

Speed is the first structural factor, and it's well quantified. An IIHS analysis of every speed limit change from 1993 to 2017 found that each 5 mph increase produced an 8% jump in the interstate fatality rate. Over 25 years, that totaled an estimated 36,760 additional deaths.[2] On rural interstates the increase was 9.1%, the highest of any road type, and forty-one states now post limits of 70 mph or above.

Vehicle mix compounds the problem, because IIHS 2024 data shows 66% of pickup occupant deaths occur in rural areas, compared with 44% for cars.[3] In our FARS dataset, the Silverado, F-150, Ram, and Sierra alone account for over 26,500 deaths during 2014–2023.[4] Apply that 66% rural share, and roughly 17,500 happened on the road type that just got worse.

Modern safety tech was engineered for a different theater — AEB systems are tested at 25 mph, with sharp performance drops above 40. On rural interstates where travel speeds cluster around 75, the crash-avoidance engineering that saved 12% more lives in newer urban vehicles during 2025 doesn't transfer.[1] After the crash, odds worsen further. Rural crash fatality risk is 62% higher than urban for identical severity, largely because EMS response averages 28 minutes longer and trauma centers follow population, not highway mileage.[5]

None of the levers America is pulling address this combination. Speed limits are set by state legislatures, not NHTSA. Pickup rollover geometry is constrained by consumer demand for towing height, and hospital funding follows population rather than pavement. Together these form a floor the national rate cannot breach without targeting rural interstates as their own problem.

Q1 2026 shows the broader decline accelerating, with the rate hitting 0.99 per 100 million VMT even as Americans drove 11 billion more miles than Q1 2025.[6] But until someone addresses the speed, the vehicles, and the distance to care, that +1% will keep appearing like a smudge that no amount of urban engineering can wipe clean.

What you can do: Prioritize a vehicle with high-speed AEB tested above 40 mph, check whether your state raised speed limits between 2019 and 2024, and know the nearest trauma center along your route. The golden hour matters more when the clock starts 45 minutes from the nearest surgeon.
