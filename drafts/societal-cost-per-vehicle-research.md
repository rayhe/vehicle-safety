# Research: Societal Cost Per Vehicle — The $2.4 Trillion Fleet Invoice

## Angle
Nobody has calculated which vehicle model imposes the highest cost on society per vehicle in fleet. Using FARS fatality data × DOT's Value of Statistical Life ($12.5M per death), we can compute the societal cost burden each model externalizes. The spread is staggering: 57x gap between the most and least costly vehicles per unit.

## Timely Hook
Avian Law Group released "$1.77 Trillion Wreck" report on June 5, 2026, calculating total societal harm from motor vehicle crashes. Our FARS-based fatality cost analysis provides a per-model breakdown that no one else has done.

## Primary Sources
1. **FARS 2014-2023** — per-model fatality counts and fleet estimates (NHTSA)
2. **DOT VSL** — $12.5 million Value of Statistical Life (DOT 2023 guidance, BCA memo)
3. **Avian Law Group report** — "$1.77 Trillion Wreck" (June 5, 2026) — 39,254 killed, 2.42M injured in 2024
4. **NSC preliminary estimates** — 37,810 deaths in 2025 (12% decrease from 2024)

## Novel Contribution
First per-model societal cost calculation using FARS deaths and DOT VSL. This metric captures both rate AND volume.

## Key Findings

### Highest Societal Cost Per Vehicle in Fleet (10-year total)
1. Chevrolet Tracker — $122,286/vehicle ($10.7B total, 856 deaths, 87,500 fleet)
2. Toyota Land Cruiser — $98,000/vehicle ($4.3B total, 343 deaths, 43,750 fleet) [NOTE: small fleet inflates per-unit; acknowledge as limitation]
3. Hyundai Veloster — $85,429/vehicle ($7.5B total, 598 deaths, 87,500 fleet)
4. Chevrolet S-10 Pickup — $81,543/vehicle ($17.8B total, 1,427 deaths, 218,750 fleet)
5. Nissan Maxima — $73,524/vehicle ($19.3B total, 1,544 deaths, 262,500 fleet)
6. Chevrolet Cobalt — $73,333/vehicle ($19.3B total, 1,540 deaths, 262,500 fleet)
7. Chevrolet Impala — $71,886/vehicle ($47.2B total, 3,774 deaths, 656,250 fleet)
8. Ford Mustang — $60,198/vehicle ($34.2B total, 2,739 deaths, 568,750 fleet)
9. Honda Accord — $44,112/vehicle ($88.8B total, 7,102 deaths, 2,012,500 fleet)

### Lowest Societal Cost Per Vehicle (min 200 deaths for statistical confidence)
1. Ram 1500 — $2,125/vehicle ($8.9B total, 714 deaths, 4,200,000 fleet)
2. Toyota RAV4 — $3,037/vehicle ($11.4B total, 914 deaths, 3,762,500 fleet)
3. Chevrolet Traverse — $3,155/vehicle
4. Subaru Forester — $4,041/vehicle
5. Honda Pilot — $4,589/vehicle

### By Class
- Sports Car: $26,880/vehicle
- Sedan: $20,016/vehicle
- Pickup: $14,948/vehicle
- Van: $9,642/vehicle
- SUV: $8,545/vehicle

### Total FARS-tracked societal cost
$2.39 trillion over 10 years (~$239B/year in fatality costs alone)

## Paradoxes to Highlight
1. SUVs (often demonized for pedestrian deaths) have the LOWEST societal cost per vehicle at $8,545 — less than half the sedan cost
2. The Honda Accord alone costs society $88.8B — more than the entire GDP of several nations
3. A single Chevy Tracker imposes more societal cost ($122K) than many of these cars cost to buy
4. Sedans are the most expensive vehicle class by societal cost despite being the "normal" choice

## Limitations
- FARS only captures fatalities, not injuries (which the $1.77T report includes)
- Fleet estimates are approximations based on sales + depreciation curves
- VMT varies by vehicle type; fleet denominator doesn't account for usage intensity
- Small-fleet vehicles (Tracker: 87,500) have inflated per-unit costs — statistical noise possible
- Cost doesn't account for WHO is killed (occupant vs other road user)
- DOT VSL is a statistical abstraction, not a literal price

## Strongest Counterargument
This calculation attributes all death cost to the vehicle model, but most fatal crashes involve human error — the vehicle is a vessel, not a cause. A Chevy Tracker doesn't kill people; the driver does. The counterargument: if you replaced every Tracker with a RAV4, fleet fatalities would plummet. The vehicle IS a variable.
