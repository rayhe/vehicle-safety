# Research: Volvo's Safety Floor — The Brand With No Bad Car

## Angle
Volvo's real safety advantage isn't having the single safest car in FARS data — it's having NO dangerous car. Every model falls between 0.17-0.44 deaths per 100M VMT. Compare to BMW: their safest (X1, 0.09) beats any Volvo, but their deadliest (3 Series, 2.73) is 30x worse. Mercedes ranges from 0.10 to 0.64. Lexus from 0.05 to 1.44. Volvo's floor is higher, but their ceiling never caves in. You can't buy a dangerous Volvo.

## Self-Critique
- **Is this surprising?** Yes. Most people assume Volvo = safest car. The data shows Volvo ≠ safest individual car, but = narrowest danger band. That's a genuinely novel reframe.
- **Is this different from the Subaru piece?** Yes. Subaru piece was "every Subaru beats its class." This is "Volvo's variance is uniquely narrow among luxury brands" — a consistency argument, not a dominance argument.
- **Strongest counterargument:** Sample sizes are small (Volvo sells relatively few cars in the US — 171 total deaths, 5 models with 19-67 deaths each). Small numbers can produce artificially narrow ranges. A brand that sells 5 models at 50K-100K units each will naturally have less variance than one selling 2M sedans. Counter-counter: BMW 3 Series alone (1,237 deaths, rate 2.73) has a sample size of 2,497 toxicology records. That's not noise. That's signal.
- **Kill test: genuinely newsworthy?** Yes. Reframes a tired "Volvo is safe" narrative with actual data + comparison to peers. Shareable among luxury car shoppers.
