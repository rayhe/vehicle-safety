# Research: The Automaker Lawsuit Against the AEB Mandate

## Angle
The Alliance for Automotive Innovation (representing nearly every major automaker) is suing to repeal NHTSA's automatic emergency braking (AEB) mandate (FMVSS 127). NHTSA estimates the rule would save ~360 lives and prevent ~24,000 injuries per year once fully implemented. The automakers say the "no contact" requirement is "practically impossible with available technology." Each year of delay = ~360 preventable deaths.

## Novel Contribution
Cross-tab FARS crash volume data with IIHS AEB effectiveness research to estimate which vehicles' owners would benefit most from mandatory AEB, and calculate the cumulative body count of regulatory delay. No existing article does this policy-data intersection.

## Kill Test
- Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES — active litigation against a life-saving regulation
- Novel data angle? YES — quantifying the human cost of specific legal/regulatory delay using FARS
- Different from existing articles? YES — we mention AEB in ~25 articles but never focused on the mandate fight, the automakers' legal strategy, or the cost-of-delay calculation

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: NHTSA FMVSS 127 Final Rule (April 29, 2024)
- Requires AEB standard on all new cars/light trucks by September 2029
- Must stop and avoid contact with lead vehicle up to 62 mph
- Must detect pedestrians day and night
- Must apply brakes up to 90 mph (vehicle) and 45 mph (pedestrian)
- NHTSA estimates: prevents ~360 deaths and ~24,000 injuries annually
- URL: https://www.nhtsa.gov/press-releases/nhtsa-fmvss-127-automatic-emergency-braking-reduce-crashes

### Source 2: IIHS AEB Effectiveness Study (2016, updated)
- AEB reduces rear-end crashes by 50%
- AEB reduces rear-end injury crashes by 56%
- Forward collision warning alone reduces crashes by 27%
- If all vehicles had AEB in 2013: 700,000+ fewer police-reported rear-end crashes (13% of all crashes)
- URL: https://www.iihs.org/news/detail/front-crash-prevention-slashes-police-reported-rear-end-crashes

### Source 3: Alliance for Automotive Innovation Lawsuit
- Filed in U.S. Court of Appeals for D.C. Circuit
- Seeks repeal of FMVSS 127
- Claims "no contact" requirement is "practically impossible with available technology"
- NHTSA's own data shows only ONE tested vehicle met the stopping distance requirements
- Industry invested >$1 billion developing AEB
- Automakers met 2016 voluntary commitment to make AEB standard by 2025
- Post-election letter to Trump urged reopening the rule
- URL: https://www.autosinnovate.org/posts/press-release/automakers-seek-repeal-of-biden-dot-flawed-aeb-rule

### Source 4: FARS Data (2014-2023, our dataset)
- Total deaths in dataset: ~189,503 across all classes
- Pickups: 85,127 crashes, 41,593 deaths
- Sedans: 137,137 crashes, 88,478 deaths
- SUVs: 86,564 crashes, 45,630 deaths
- Rear-end/frontal crashes are AEB's target — estimated ~30-40% of all FARS crashes
- At 50% reduction rate, AEB across the full fleet could have prevented ~28,000-38,000 deaths over this decade

### Source 5: Trump Admin Review (January 2025)
- Trump administration reviewing AEB rule
- Reuters reported review underway Jan 24, 2025
- URL: https://www.reuters.com/business/autos-transportation/trump-administration-reviewing-us-automatic-emergency-braking-rule-2025-01-24/

## Key Data Points from FARS
- Total fatal crashes in dataset: ~332,000
- IIHS estimates AEB-addressable crashes (front-to-rear): ~13% of all crashes
- 13% of 332,000 = ~43,000 crashes over the decade → ~4,300/year
- At 50% reduction: ~2,150 fewer fatal crash events per year
- But NHTSA's more conservative estimate: 360 deaths/year (accounts for partial fleet penetration, crash types, etc.)

## Cost Calculation
- AEB mandate effective September 2029
- If delayed 1 year: ~360 additional deaths
- If delayed 3 years: ~1,080 additional deaths
- If repealed entirely: indefinite ongoing toll as fleet penetration stalls
- Average fleet age: 12.6 years (S&P Global Mobility 2024)
- Full fleet turnover to 100% AEB: ~2042 under current mandate timeline
- Each year of delay pushes full fleet AEB back by a year

## Strongest Counterargument
The automakers' core claim has technical merit: the "no contact" requirement (car must STOP before hitting lead vehicle at up to 62 mph) may genuinely be infeasible with current brake system technology. NHTSA's own test data showed only one vehicle met the standard. An unachievable standard doesn't save lives — it just creates legal liability. The European standard (mitigate severity, not necessarily prevent all contact) might save nearly as many lives with achievable technology.

## Limitations
- FARS doesn't categorize crashes by type (rear-end vs. side-impact vs. head-on), so our AEB-preventable crash estimates use IIHS's 13% figure, not a direct FARS calculation
- The 360 deaths/year estimate comes from NHTSA's regulatory impact analysis, not our independent calculation
- Fleet penetration math assumes linear adoption, which oversimplifies
- AEB effectiveness varies by speed, weather, and system calibration — 50% is an average
