BMW’s Safest Car Beats Every Volvo. BMW’s Deadliest Is 30 Times Worse.

Axle McScatter • Clautomotive Desk
April 21, 2026

I sorted five luxury brands by death-rate spread across all models using FARS data from 2014 to 2023.[1] Volvo didn’t finish first. But the spreadsheet surfaced a story about variance that reframes how “safest brand” should be measured.

2.6×
Volvo’s death rate spread, safest to deadliest model. BMW’s spread: 30×.

Everyone assumes Volvo builds the safest car. By the narrowest metric, they don’t. Lexus NX posts 0.05 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled. BMW X1 hits 0.09. Volvo’s best performer, the XC70, manages 0.17.[1] If your only criterion is the single lowest fatality rate, you’re shopping Japanese or German, not Swedish.

But “safest car” is a bad question. A better one: what’s the worst car you can accidentally buy from a given brand?

Volvo’s worst is the S60, at 0.44 deaths per 100M VMT. BMW’s is the 3 Series, at 2.73, with 1,237 deaths and 2,497 toxicology records backing that number over a decade.[1] Not a sample size problem. A car problem.

Audi’s band is actually tighter than Volvo’s. Both are narrow enough that model choice barely matters for safety. Shopping BMW or Lexus is closer to roulette: an X1 on one side, a 3 Series on the other.

Limitations

One honest caveat: Volvo sells modest volume in the US. Across a decade their entire FARS body count is 171 deaths over five models.[1] Small samples naturally produce narrow ranges. But impairment rates across all five luxury brands sit in a consistent 17–22% band, so sober drivers don’t explain Volvo’s advantage.[1] And BMW’s worst model alone accounts for 7.2 times more deaths than Volvo’s entire lineup. Volume doesn’t hand-wave that away.

FARS also captures only fatal crashes. A vehicle with a low death rate could still have elevated injury rates we cannot see here. Our estimated rates use VMT approximations, not actual odometer data, introducing roughly ±15% uncertainty for low-volume models.[2]

Why Volvo’s Floor Holds

Volvo’s “Vision 2020” program aimed for zero deaths in new Volvos by 2020.[3] They missed. But speed caps at 180 km/h, driver monitoring systems, and energy-absorbing seat structures produced something more useful than a single standout model: a lineup where the floor never caves in. You cannot buy a dangerous Volvo.

Strongest Counterargument

Volvo’s narrow band might simply reflect a narrow lineup. Five models sold at moderate volume will naturally cluster tighter than BMW’s ten-plus models spanning coupes, sedans, and SUVs at wildly different price points and demographics. A brand selling niche, expensive vehicles to safety-conscious buyers is being compared against one selling mass-market sport sedans to 25-year-olds. Demographics, not engineering, could account for most of the gap. Fair point. Counter: BMW’s X1, X3, and X5 are the same kind of suburban family haulers as Volvo’s XC lineup, sold to similar buyers, and even their SUV range spans 0.09 to 0.40. Narrow-lineup effects don’t explain a 30-fold spread within a single brand.

What You Should Do

Stop comparing the best model from each luxury brand. Compare the worst. Volvo’s ceiling is 0.44. BMW’s is 2.73. Audi’s is 0.32, and equally worth your attention. Before you sign a lease, run the specific model through IIHS ratings at iihs.org/ratings.[4] Your brand loyalty has a variance problem.
