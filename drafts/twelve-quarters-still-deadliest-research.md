# Research: Twelve Straight Quarters Down. Still the Deadliest Rich Country on Earth.

## Angle
America is celebrating 12 consecutive quarterly declines in traffic fatalities — the longest sustained improvement since pre-pandemic. But the international data tells a different story: the US remains the deadliest rich country for drivers and pedestrians by a massive margin, and our "progress" barely dents the gap.

## Original Calculations

### Excess Deaths vs. Peer Median (2022)
- US population 2022: ~333M
- Peer median rate: 4.12 per 100K
- Expected deaths at peer rate: 333M × 4.12/100,000 = 13,720
- Actual US deaths (2022): 42,514
- **Excess: 28,794 deaths/year** — nearly 29,000 Americans die per year beyond what peer-country norms would predict

### Excess Deaths at 2025 Pace
- 2025 annualized (from H1 data): ~34,280
- US population ~340M (est.)
- Expected at peer median: 340M × 4.12/100,000 = 14,008
- **Excess at 2025 pace: ~20,272** — still 20K+ excess deaths even after the improvement

### Excess Pedestrian Deaths (2022)
- US pedestrian deaths: 7,768
- At peer median rate (0.73 per 100K): 333M × 0.73/100,000 = 2,431
- **Excess pedestrian deaths: 5,337/year**

### The "Norway Benchmark"
- At Norway's rate (2.14 per 100K): 333M × 2.14/100,000 = 7,127
- Excess vs Norway: 42,514 - 7,127 = 35,387
- Even at 2025's pace: 34,280 - 7,264 = 27,016

### Context Comparisons
- 28,794 excess deaths ≈ a 9/11 every 38 days
- ≈ entire US death toll in Afghanistan and Iraq wars (~7,000) every 89 days
- ≈ 79 people per day who would be alive under average rich-country norms

## Limitations
- CDC MMWR uses population-based rates, not VMT-adjusted. Americans drive more than most peers. But even VMT-adjusted, the US rate is substantially higher because America's built environment forces driving.
- FARS only captures fatal crashes — injury rates may diverge
- Country-specific factors (road design, distances, speed limits, enforcement) make direct comparison imperfect
- The "excess death" calculation assumes peer median is achievable — but the US has structural barriers (sprawl, car-dependency) that peer countries don't

## Strongest Counterargument
The VMT-adjusted rate comparison would narrow the gap because Americans drive significantly more than Europeans or Japanese. If you compare deaths-per-mile rather than deaths-per-population, the US gap shrinks. But that argument is itself a damning admission: America's infrastructure forces people to drive more, and that forced driving exposure IS the problem. Countries that reduced deaths did it partly by reducing the need to drive.

## Actionable Takeaways
1. Check your vehicle's recall status at nhtsa.gov/recalls
2. The safest vehicles in FARS data are modern mid-size SUVs with ESC (RAV4 at 0.19 rate, Forester at 0.26)
3. Avoid buying pre-ESC-mandate vehicles (pre-2012) — they account for the majority of FARS deaths
4. If shopping for safety, check IIHS ratings AND FARS data — a Top Safety Pick means nothing if the model year is old enough to lack critical features
5. Support local speed reduction and pedestrian infrastructure — the CDC study names arterial roadways and speed as primary factors
