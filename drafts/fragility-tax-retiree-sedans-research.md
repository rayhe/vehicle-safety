# Research: The Fragility Tax — Retiree Sedans

## Angle
Large sedans favored by older drivers have the highest lethality ratios in FARS despite being among the heaviest passenger vehicles. The "bigger is safer" assumption fails because occupant fragility overwhelms vehicle mass.

## Kill Test
- **Novel?** YES. No existing article covers the retiree-sedan lethality paradox as a class. Individual vehicles (Grand Marquis, Crown Vic) have articles but not the systemic pattern.
- **Surprising?** YES. Counterintuitive that the biggest sedans are the deadliest per crash.
- **Shareable?** YES. "Your grandma's car is statistically deadlier than a Civic" is a strong hook.

## Original Cross-Tabulation

### Group Lethality (deaths/crashes in FARS)
- **Retiree sedans** (Avalon, Town Car, LeSabre, Park Avenue, DeVille, Lucerne, LaCrosse, Crown Vic, Grand Marquis): **74.2%** lethality, 4,772 deaths from 6,428 crashes
- **Entry-level sedans** (Civic, Corolla, Altima, Jetta, Sentra, Focus): **65.3%** lethality, 23,277 deaths from 35,641 crashes
- **Modern crossovers** (RAV4, CR-V, CX-5, Rogue, Escape, Tucson, Forester, Crosstrek, HR-V, Equinox): **53.0%** lethality, 8,749 deaths from 16,507 crashes

### Impairment Rates
- Retiree sedans: **21.3%** (low - these are sober crashes)
- Dataset average: ~35% (typical)

### Standout Vehicles
- Buick Park Avenue: **88.2% lethality** (97 deaths from 110 crashes)
- Buick LeSabre: **82.3% lethality** (1,344 deaths from 1,633 crashes)
- Lincoln Town Car: **80.5% lethality** (260 deaths from 323 crashes)
- Mercury Grand Marquis: **76.1% lethality** (1,153 deaths from 1,516 crashes)

## Primary Sources (3+)

1. **FARS 2014-2023 data** — our own cross-tabulation of deaths/crashes by model
   - Source: https://www.nhtsa.gov/research-data/fatality-analysis-reporting-system-fars
   
2. **IIHS Older Drivers research** — Cicchino (2015) study showing fragility (not over-involvement) drives older drivers' elevated fatal crash rates
   - Source: https://www.iihs.org/topics/older-drivers
   - Key finding: "the main factor was not seniors' over-involvement in crashes but their fragility, or risk of death in a crash"
   - Fragility increases starting around middle age and continues to rise with age
   - 5,502 people age 70+ died in crashes in 2023
   
3. **NHTSA 2024 fatality estimates** — 39,345 total deaths, 3.8% decline from 2023
   - Source: https://www.nhtsa.gov/press-releases/nhtsa-estimates-39345-traffic-fatalities-2024
   
4. **IIHS vehicle size/weight research** — establishes the conventional wisdom that bigger = safer
   - Source: https://www.iihs.org/topics/vehicle-size-and-weight

## Methodology
- Lethality ratio = deaths / FARS-recorded crashes (only fatal crashes are in FARS, so "lethality" here means: of all parties involved in a fatal crash in this vehicle, what fraction died?)
- Grouped 9 "retiree sedans" by demographic proxy: large/luxury sedans with median buyer age 60+
- Compared against 6 entry-level sedans and 10 modern crossovers
- Impairment rates from FARS toxicology (BAC>0 or drug-positive)

## Limitations
- FARS only records fatal crashes, so "lethality" is the death rate within fatal crashes, not all crashes
- Demographic proxy (retiree sedans) is based on market data/conventional wisdom, not FARS driver age data
- Older model years dominate some vehicles (LeSabre, DeVille discontinued), confounding age-of-vehicle with age-of-driver
- Vehicle weight alone doesn't determine crash outcome — striking vehicle type, crash type, belt use all factor in

## Strongest Counterargument
These vehicles are OLD. The LeSabre, Park Avenue, and DeVille are discontinued. Their high lethality could reflect the age of the vehicles (pre-modern safety tech) rather than the age of the drivers. A 2003 LeSabre lacks stability control, side curtain airbags, and modern structural design. Separating "old car" from "old driver" is the central confound.
