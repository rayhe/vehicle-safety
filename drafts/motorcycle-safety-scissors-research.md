# Research: The Motorcycle Safety Scissors

## Thesis
Every major car safety technology of the past 50 years — airbags, electronic stability control, crumple zones, AEB — saved car occupants but did nothing for motorcyclists riding next to them. The result: a "scissors" pattern where passenger vehicle deaths fell from 77% to 59% of all traffic fatalities while motorcycle deaths tripled from 5% to 15%. In 2023, motorcyclists died at the highest rate ever recorded.

## The Scissors Chart (FARS 1975-2023 via IIHS)
| Year | Passenger Vehicle % | Motorcycle % | MC Deaths |
|------|-------------------|-------------|-----------|
| 1975 | 69% | 7% | 3,180 |
| 1985 | 68% | 10% | 4,562 |
| 1995 | 76% | 5% | 2,226 |
| 1997 | 77% | 5% | 2,116 (trough) |
| 2005 | 72% | 11% | 4,575 |
| 2015 | 64% | 14% | 5,026 |
| 2020 | 62% | 14% | 5,619 |
| 2023 | 59% | 15% | 6,335 (record) |

From 1997 trough to 2023: motorcycle deaths TRIPLED (2,116 → 6,335).
Same period: passenger vehicle deaths dropped 25% (32,343 → 24,238).

## Novel Contribution
The "safety scissors" cross-tabulation: plotting passenger vehicle occupant share vs motorcycle share of all traffic deaths from 1975-2023. The lines cross. Nobody has framed this as a technology exclusion story — most motorcycle safety coverage focuses on helmets, impairment, or rider behavior. The systemic angle is: car safety technology is a 50-year success story that explicitly excludes the most vulnerable motorized road users.

Additionally: AEB (the latest mandatory car tech, required by 2029) is 12 percentage points LESS effective at detecting motorcycles than other cars (41% vs 53% reduction, IIHS). So even the newest car tech continues the pattern.

## Strongest Counterargument
The demographic shift, not the technology gap, explains the divergence. Motorcycle riders got older (median 27 → 50), heavier (buying bigger cruisers and touring bikes), and more numerous. VMT may have increased. The technology gap is real but the death count is driven by exposure — more riders, riding more miles, on more powerful bikes. The scissors chart conflates a denominator shift (fewer car deaths from tech improvements) with a numerator shift (more motorcycle riders from demographic/cultural factors).

**Counter to the counterargument:** Even controlling for exposure (per-VMT), motorcycles are 28x deadlier than cars. That ratio has likely WIDENED as cars got safer. The EU mandated ABS in 2016 and studies show 22% reduction in fatal crashes — the US simply hasn't acted. Additionally, the helmet law patchwork accounts for 22,000+ preventable deaths. These are policy failures, not inevitable demographic outcomes. The technology to save motorcycle lives EXISTS (ABS, helmets) — it's just not required.
