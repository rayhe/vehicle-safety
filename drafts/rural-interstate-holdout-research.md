# Research: Rural Interstates — The One Category That Got Worse

## Angle
NHTSA's brand-new July 2026 sub-category report (DOT HS 813 829) breaks down 2025 fatality trends. Every subcategory declined — urban interstates (down 10%), urban arterials (down 12%), nighttime (down 8%), newer vehicles (down 12%), ejections (down 13%), rollovers (down 11%), single-vehicle (down 7%), weekends (down 7%), out-of-state travel (down 11%). ONE category went the wrong way: **rural interstates (up 1%)**. America won everywhere except the one place it keeps losing.

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: NHTSA DOT HS 813 829 (July 2026)
- "Early Estimates of Motor Vehicle Traffic Fatalities and Fatality Rate by Sub-Categories in 2025"
- 36,640 people died in 2025, down 6.7% from 39,254 in 2024
- Fatality rate: 1.10 per 100M VMT, down from 1.19 in 2024
- Rural interstates: UP 1% — only subcategory with an increase
- URL: crashstats.nhtsa.dot.gov

### Source 2: IIHS Urban/Rural Comparison (2024 data)
- 66% of pickup occupant deaths occur in rural areas
- 71% of large truck occupant deaths in rural areas
- Only 44% of car/minivan deaths in rural areas
- 50% of SUV deaths in rural areas
- Pickups and large trucks are overwhelmingly rural death machines
- URL: iihs.org/topics/fatality-statistics

### Source 3: IIHS Speed Limit Study (Farmer, 2019)
- Each 5 mph increase in maximum speed limit = 8% increase in fatality rate on interstates/freeways
- 36,760 additional deaths over 25 years (1993-2017) attributable to speed limit increases
- Speed limit increases on rural interstates = 9.1% increase in fatalities (highest of any road type)
- URL: iihs.org

### Source 4: NHTSA Q1 2026 Early Estimate
- Q1 2026 fatality rate dropped to 0.99 per 100M VMT — second-lowest Q1 in history
- 7,770 deaths, down 4.3% from Q1 2025
- Speeding-related fatalities in 2025: estimated 10,035 (down 11% from 2024's 11,288)
- 29% of all traffic fatalities in 2024 were speeding-related

### Source 5: GHSA Report "America's Rural Roads: Beautiful and Deadly"
- 19% of US population lives in rural areas, but 41% of traffic deaths happen there
- Risk of dying in rural crash = 62% higher than urban for same trip length
- 85,002 people died on rural roads between 2016-2020

### Source 6: FARS Data (Our Dataset)
- Full-size pickups in FARS: Silverado (9,591 deaths, rate 1.25), F-150 (9,194 deaths, rate 1.04), Ram (4,407 deaths, rate 0.78), Sierra (3,337 deaths, rate 1.01), Tundra (1,223, rate 0.94), Tacoma (2,274, rate 0.80), Ranger (3,089, rate 2.91)
- Combined pickup deaths in dataset: ~33,000+
- Pickup fatality rates are NOT the worst per-VMT, but pickups dominate rural interstate traffic
- Since 66% of pickup deaths are rural, that's ~21,780 rural pickup deaths in our dataset period

## Original Contribution
Cross-referencing the new NHTSA subcategory data showing rural interstates as the ONLY category getting worse with (a) IIHS data showing pickups account for 66% of their deaths in rural areas, (b) IIHS research showing speed limit increases caused 9.1% more deaths on rural interstates, and (c) FARS data showing the raw pickup body count. The thesis: rural interstates are structurally immune to the safety improvements working everywhere else because the vehicle mix (pickups), speeds (70-85 mph), enforcement density, and EMS response times create a floor that modern safety tech hasn't cracked.

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES.
- The NHTSA sub-category report was published July 2026 — brand new
- One outlier among a dozen declining categories is a clean, counterintuitive finding
- The "why" connects to structural factors (speed, vehicle mix, enforcement) not just driver behavior
- Actionable: avoid rural interstates when possible, choose vehicles with high-speed AEB, check speed limit laws in your state
