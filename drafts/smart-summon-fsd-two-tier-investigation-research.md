# Research: Smart Summon vs FSD — NHTSA's Two-Tier Standard for Tesla's Robots

## Core Angle
NHTSA closed the Smart Summon investigation (159 incidents, 0 injuries, 0 deaths) while simultaneously upgrading the FSD investigation (9 crashes, 1 death, 1 injury) to Engineering Analysis (pre-recall). Both features run on the same company's vehicles. The regulatory response reveals something about what "safe enough" means at different speeds.

## Kill Test
- **Newsworthy?** Yes — major regulatory action (closure + escalation) on the same company within weeks
- **Novel?** Yes — cross-referencing FARS death rates for Tesla models with the regulatory posture on automation features. Nobody's put NHTSA's two decisions side by side and asked "what death rate does FSD need to beat?"
- **3+ primary sources?** Yes — NHTSA closing document, NHTSA FSD Engineering Analysis, FARS data, Electrek reporting, Consumer Reports

## Primary Sources

### 1. NHTSA PE24033 Closure (Smart Summon)
- Opened Jan 2025, closed April 3, 2026
- 159 total incidents, 97 crashes
- 0 injuries, 0 fatalities, 0 airbag deployments
- 2,585,000 affected vehicles (2016-2025 Model S/X, 2017-2025 Model 3, 2020-2025 Model Y)
- "Millions of Summon sessions, fraction of 1% resulted in incident"
- Typical incidents: parking gates, adjacent parked vehicles, short bollards
- Camera blockage (snow) caused some crashes
- Tesla pushed 6 OTA updates during investigation
- NHTSA: "low incident occurrence and low incident severity"
- Source: NHTSA ODI, Electrek reporting (April 6, 2026)

### 2. NHTSA FSD Visibility Investigation (Upgraded to Engineering Analysis)
- Originally opened as preliminary evaluation
- Upgraded March 2026 to Engineering Analysis (pre-recall stage)
- 3.2 million vehicles covered
- 9 crashes, 1 fatality, 1 injury
- Focus: FSD's failure to warn when cameras blinded by sun glare, fog, other conditions
- Source: NHTSA, Electrek (March 19, 2026)

### 3. FARS Data on Tesla (2014-2023)
- **Model S:** 100 deaths, 134 crashes, est. fleet 175K, rate 0.5/100M VMT
- **Model 3:** 92 deaths, 212 crashes, est. fleet 1.575M, rate 0.05/100M VMT  
- **Model Y:** 57 deaths, 112 crashes, est. fleet 1.75M, rate 0.03/100M VMT
- **Model X:** 29 deaths, 45 crashes, est. fleet 157.5K, rate 0.15/100M VMT
- **Total Tesla FARS deaths: 278** (over 10-year period)
- Model 3 and Y are among the safest vehicles per VMT in the entire FARS database
- Model Y at 0.03 is essentially the floor

### 4. Human Parking Lot Baseline
- NSC estimates ~50,000 crashes/year in parking lots and garages
- ~500 deaths annually in parking-related incidents (NHTSA/NSC)
- Smart Summon's 97 crashes out of millions of sessions = dramatically lower rate

## Key Data Points for Article
- Smart Summon: 97 crashes / millions of sessions = fraction of 1%
- FSD: 9 crashes in ~3.2M vehicles (but measuring over much more driving)
- FARS total Tesla deaths: 278 (human driving, no automation distinction)
- Model Y death rate 0.03/100M VMT vs average passenger vehicle ~1.1
- Model 3 death rate 0.05 vs sedan class average ~0.8-1.0

## Limitations
- FARS doesn't distinguish Autopilot/FSD crashes from manual Tesla driving
- Smart Summon sessions are short (<5 min, low speed) — not directly comparable to highway driving
- "Millions of sessions" is Tesla's claim, not independently verified
- FSD investigation is ongoing; Engineering Analysis doesn't guarantee recall

## Strongest Counterargument
Comparing parking lot autonomy to highway autonomy is apples to oranges. Smart Summon operates at 6 mph in controlled environments. FSD operates at highway speeds among unpredictable traffic. The physics of a 6 mph parking lot bump vs a 65 mph highway collision are so different that treating NHTSA's responses as a "double standard" is misleading. Of course they're more concerned about the highway feature.
