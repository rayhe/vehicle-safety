The Gap

The Crossover Swap That Would Save 3,925 Lives a Year

Clara Rollover • Clautomotive Desk
March 29, 2026

Every sedan on a dealer lot sits next to its crossover sibling. Same badge. Same warranty. Sometimes even the same engine. Pick one, drive it home. Over the past decade, that choice killed 39,253 people.

39,253
Additional deaths from choosing sedans over crossovers, 2014–2023

We ran a simple counterfactual: take America's 11 best-selling sedans, match each to its same-brand crossover equivalent, and recalculate deaths using the crossover's fatality rate applied to the sedan's actual miles driven. Honda Civic buyers could have bought HR-Vs. Camry families could have walked across the showroom to the RAV4. Altima commuters could have picked the Rogue.

They didn't. And 3,925 of them, every year, paid with their lives.

The Swap Table

Each row holds manufacturer constant. Same company designed both vehicles, same engineering department, same crash lab. The only variable is body style.[1]

Honda Civic (2.25 deaths per 100M VMT) to the HR-V (0.13): 17.3 times safer, 6,174 lives. That single swap erases more fatalities than every motorcycle AEB system in development. Honda Accord to CR-V: 5.8 times safer, 5,875 lives. Toyota Camry to RAV4: 10.7 times safer, 5,735 lives. Nissan Altima to Rogue: 8.2 times safer, 4,206 lives.

Even the narrowest gap matters. Ford Focus to Escape is 2.7 times safer. That still translates to 1,899 people who didn't need to die.

Chevrolet's numbers are grotesque. Impala to Traverse: 25 times safer. The Impala kills at 5.0 per 100M VMT. The Traverse manages 0.20. Same dealership, probably the same salesperson, and one vehicle is running a fatality rate that belongs in a demolition derby.[2]

Why This Happens

Crossovers sit higher. In a side impact with a pickup or SUV, a sedan occupant absorbs crash energy at chest and head height. A crossover occupant absorbs it at door-panel height. IIHS research on vehicle size and weight confirms that ride height and mass are the two strongest predictors of occupant survival in multi-vehicle crashes, independent of safety ratings.[3]

Crossovers are heavier. A CR-V outweighs a Civic by roughly 500 pounds. When two vehicles collide, the lighter one decelerates faster and absorbs more force. That's 500 pounds of survival advantage you could have bought for roughly the same monthly payment.

Crossovers attract different drivers. The person buying a RAV4 for her family may be systematically more cautious than the Camry commuter running late at 7 AM. She's probably older, more suburban, less likely to be impaired. FARS cannot separate the vehicle effect from the driver effect.

The Counterargument, at Full Strength

Heavier crossovers transfer more kinetic energy to whatever they hit. IIHS has documented the "mass incompatibility" problem: when a 4,200-pound crossover strikes a 2,800-pound sedan, the sedan's occupants absorb a disproportionate share of crash energy.[4] If everyone swapped to crossovers, you'd eliminate the mass advantage. Crash outcomes might regress toward the mean.

Crossover hood heights also kill pedestrians. Vehicles with hoods above 40 inches are approximately 45% more likely to cause a pedestrian fatality than those with lower profiles, per IIHS data. A fleet-wide crossover swap could save 3,925 occupants annually while increasing pedestrian deaths by an unknown amount.[3]

These are legitimate objections. They reduce the net lives saved. They do not eliminate it. Even halving the estimate for aggressor effects and selection bias still leaves nearly 2,000 unnecessary deaths per year from vehicle choice alone.

What FARS Cannot Tell Us

FARS records only fatal crashes. Of roughly 6.7 million annual U.S. crashes, about 36,000 kill someone. Everything below that threshold is invisible to this analysis. A sedan might survive FARS scrutiny while racking up injuries that never make it into these numbers.[1]

VMT estimates carry approximately ±15% uncertainty, derived from NHTS survey data rather than actual odometer readings.[5] For high-volume models like the Civic (fleet: 2.5M, annual VMT: 29.2 billion miles), the estimate is robust. For the Kicks (fleet: 394K), less so.

Fleet age confounds the comparison. The Impala's 5.0 death rate reflects a platform sold from 2006 through 2020, spanning pre-ESC models. The Traverse debuted in 2009 with standard ESC. Newer sedans with full ADAS suites may close some of this gap.

The Math

For each pair: take the sedan's total VMT over 2014–2023, multiply by the crossover's death rate, subtract from the sedan's actual deaths. Civic example: 291,810 million VMT × 0.13 per 100M = 379 hypothetical deaths. Actual Civic deaths: 6,553. Difference: 6,174.[1]

Sum the 11 pairs: 39,253 lives. Divide by 10 years: 3,925 per year. NHTSA estimated 3,522 distracted driving deaths in 2021.[1] Vehicle choice is quietly killing more Americans than texting behind the wheel.

One uncomfortable footnote: crossovers typically cost $2,000 to $5,000 more than their sedan equivalents. For buyers stretching to afford any new car, the sedan is the only option. Some of these 39,253 deaths are a poverty tax dressed up as a consumer preference.

Americans are already making this swap anyway. Sedan market share cratered from 36% in 2014 to under 20% by 2024. The crossover migration is the largest spontaneous safety improvement in modern automotive history. Almost nobody talks about it in those terms.
