# Research Notes: Fentanyl's DUI Trajectory

## Angle
Fentanyl went from showing up in 26% of forensic labs' DUI cases (2016) to 89% (2024). The opioid overdose epidemic and the traffic death epidemic share the same supply chain, the same users, and increasingly the same toxicology reports. The enforcement system has no roadside test for it.

## Kill Test
✅ Genuinely surprising: The parallel trajectories between fentanyl overdose deaths and fentanyl in crash toxicology have never been mapped together. The 26% → 89% lab prevalence curve in DUI cases is a powerful, undercovered data point from the brand-new 2025 toxicology survey.
✅ Novel data: Cross-referencing the 2025 NSC-ADID toxicology lab survey with the FARS enhanced toxicology study and the California NPS study creates an original three-layer analysis.
✅ Not covered: No existing Crash Report article focuses on fentanyl's specific trajectory in DUI/crash toxicology.

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: 2025 NSC-ADID Toxicology Lab Survey (Journal of Analytical Toxicology, 2025)
- URL: https://academic.oup.com/jat/article/50/1/bkaf085/8255971
- 80 US forensic labs surveyed (2024 survey, published 2025)
- **Fentanyl prevalence in top 15 DUI drugs reported by labs:**
  - 2016: 26% of labs
  - 2021: 70% of labs
  - 2024: 89% of labs (71 of 80 labs)
- Amphetamine/Methamphetamine now #1 most reported drug in DUI cases (91 of 80 labs — some split them)
- Novel benzodiazepines reported by 22 labs (28%)
- 51% of labs practice stop-limit testing (stop drug testing if BAC ≥ threshold)
- 39% report unconfirmed screening results
- Only 16% fully conform to all recommendations
- Urine removed as recommended matrix (doesn't show current impairment)
- Gabapentin promoted to Tier I

### Source 2: FARS Enhanced Toxicology Study (PubMed, 2025)
- DOI: pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41401642
- 138,505 fatally injured drivers (2013-2017 vs 2018-2022)
- Alcohol: 37.1% → 37.9% (FLAT)
- Any nonalcohol drug: 39.9% → 55.0%
- Marijuana: 14.4% → 26.4%
- Stimulants: 9.3% → 25.1% (170% increase)
- Depressants (includes opioids): 9.1% → 13.7%
- ≥2 nonalcohol drugs: 13.6% → 19.2%
- ≥3 drugs: 2.0% → 5.8%
- 42% increased odds of detecting any nonalcohol drug after FARS enhancement

### Source 3: California NPS Crash Study (Frontiers in Toxicology, 2025)
- URL: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/toxicology/articles/10.3389/ftox.2025.1572324/full
- 1,000 roadway crash victims in LA and Sacramento (Jan-Jul 2024)
- 290 positive for traditional drugs or NPS (29%)
- 17 cases had novel psychoactive substances (1.7%)
- Fentanyl detected in 9 of 17 NPS-positive cases
- para-Fluorofentanyl (fentanyl analog) was 2nd most common NPS (4 detections)
- Bromazolam (designer Xanax) most common NPS (7 detections)
- 16 of 17 NPS cases tested NEGATIVE for alcohol
- Standard immunoassay panels cannot detect NPS

### Source 4: CDC Fentanyl Overdose Data
- URL: https://blogs.cdc.gov/nchs/2023/05/03/7338/
- Fentanyl overdose death rate: 5.7 per 100K (2016) → 21.6 per 100K (2021) = 279% increase
- Methamphetamine overdose rate quadrupled in same period
- 2023-2024: overdose deaths began declining (~15% nationally) while fentanyl in DUI cases continued rising

### Source 5: NHTSA Traffic Fatality Data (2025)
- 36,640 traffic deaths in 2025 (6.7% decline, lowest since 2019)
- Rate: 1.10 per 100M VMT

## Original Contribution
Three-trajectory mapping:
1. CDC fentanyl overdose deaths: tripled 2016-2021 (5.7 → 21.6 per 100K)
2. Forensic lab fentanyl prevalence in DUI: tripled 2016-2024 (26% → 89% of labs)
3. FARS depressant detection in crash fatalities: 51% increase 2013-2022 (9.1% → 13.7%)

The curves track. The overdose epidemic IS a traffic safety epidemic. Same drug, same supply chain, different venue. Fentanyl is now so pervasive that 89% of forensic labs list it among their top drugs in DUI cases — up from about 1 in 4 labs just 8 years ago.

## Limitations
- Lab prevalence (% of labs reporting fentanyl in top 15) ≠ driver prevalence. A lab reporting fentanyl as "top 15" could mean 5% of their cases or 50%.
- FARS depressant category includes all opioids, benzos, and other depressants — not fentanyl-specific.
- The FARS 2018 enhancement confounds detection improvements with real prevalence changes.
- The 89% figure is from 80 labs that volunteered — potential selection bias toward higher-volume labs.
- Overdose deaths began declining in 2023-2024 while DUI prevalence continued rising — the curves may diverge.

## Strongest Counterargument
Detection ≠ impairment. Fentanyl's half-life is 3-12 hours but metabolites persist longer. A driver with trace fentanyl metabolites who used 18 hours ago may test "positive" without being currently impaired. The 89% lab figure captures presence, not causation. The California NPS study found fentanyl analogs at concentrations "lower than those reported for overdose and death cases" — meaning some crash victims had sub-impairment levels that happened to be detectable.

## Actionable Insights
1. If you're pulled over and the officer smells nothing and the breathalyzer reads 0.00, you could still have a lethal amount of fentanyl in your system. There is no roadside instant test.
2. If a family member was killed in a "sober" crash, ask whether toxicology included fentanyl and its analogs. Many labs don't test for it unless alcohol is negative.
3. The Tier I recommended testing list now includes fentanyl at 1 ng/mL confirmation. Ask your state's forensic lab if they meet this standard.
