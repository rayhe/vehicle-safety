# Research: Cannabis Has Overtaken Alcohol in Fatal Crash Toxicology

## Angle
NHTSA's newly published study on "Alcohol and Drug Prevalence Among Seriously or Fatally Injured Road Users" shows cannabis has surpassed alcohol as the most commonly detected impairing substance in fatal crashes — 25% cannabinoids vs 23% alcohol. 56% of fatally/seriously injured road users tested positive for at least one impairing substance. 32% of fatalities had 2+ drugs.

This is Dale Impactor III's beat — toxicology and impairment data.

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely surprising? YES.
- For decades, alcohol was the dominant impairment factor. Cannabis overtaking it is a paradigm shift in traffic safety.
- Most people still associate impaired driving with alcohol. This data undermines that assumption.
- The cross-tab with FARS vehicle-level drug rates is novel — nobody's mapped which vehicles have the highest drug-positive rates.

## Primary Sources
1. **NHTSA study:** "Alcohol and Drug Prevalence Among Seriously or Fatally Injured Road Users" (published Dec 2025)
   - 56% positive for alcohol or impairing drug
   - Cannabinoids: 25% (most prevalent)
   - Alcohol: 23%
   - Stimulants: 11%
   - Opioids: 9%
   - Poly-drug: 18% of serious injuries, 32% of fatalities
   - Source: 7 Level 1 trauma centers nationwide
   - URL: nhtsa.gov (referenced in Drive Sober press release)

2. **NTSB Safety Research Report:** "Alcohol, Other Drug, and Multiple Drug Use Among Drivers"
   - Confirms growing cannabis/drug impaired driving problem
   - Identifies 5 safety needs including cannabis-specific countermeasures

3. **NHTSA FARS data** (our fars_output.js, 2014-2023):
   - Overall drug-positive rate: ~9.6% (vs alcohol 15.3%)
   - BUT: FARS data has known drug testing gaps (covered in our cannabis-impairment-blind-spot article)
   - Highest drug% vehicles: Oldsmobile Alero 18.4%, Buick Park Avenue 16.6%, BMW M5 15.6%
   - NHTSA/NTSB trauma center data captures what FARS misses

4. **NHTSA early estimates Jan-Sep 2025:**
   - Fatalities down 6.4% (27,365 vs 29,245)
   - Rate: 1.10 per 100M VMT (down from 1.19)
   - "Drive High, Get a DUI" campaign alongside "Drive Sober or Get Pulled Over"

## Novel Contribution
- Cross-tabulating the NTSB's national finding (cannabis > alcohol) with FARS vehicle-level drug rates
- The FARS drug% is known to be an undercount (testing gaps) — but even the undercount shows certain vehicles with drug rates approaching or exceeding their alcohol rates
- The vehicles where drugs match or exceed alcohol are the leading edge of the shift
- Actionable: NHTSA can't do BAC-equivalent for cannabis. No breathalyzer exists. This is an enforcement gap.

## Key Stats for Article
- 25% > 23% (cannabis > alcohol in trauma center study)
- 56% of fatally injured tested positive for something
- 32% of fatalities had 2+ drugs
- FARS vehicle-level: Suzuki Grand Vitara (drug 12.7% > alc 11.4%), Nissan NV200 (drug 12.7% > alc 11.8%), Oldsmobile Alero (drug=alc at 18.4%)
- No BAC-equivalent exists for THC
- NHTSA acknowledges: "There is no measure for marijuana that correlates with impairment"

## Limitations
- NTSB/NHTSA trauma center study is a sample, not census
- Cannabinoid-positive ≠ cannabis-impaired (THC metabolites persist for weeks)
- FARS drug testing varies by jurisdiction and year
- Vehicle-level drug rates in FARS reflect reporting, not actual impairment

## Strongest Counterargument
Cannabis presence does not equal cannabis impairment. Unlike BAC, THC metabolites can linger for weeks. A positive cannabinoid test in a fatal crash victim might reflect use days earlier, not impairment at time of crash. The 25% figure likely overstates actual cannabis impairment at time of crash.
