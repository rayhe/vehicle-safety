# Research: The Compact SUV Safety Lottery

## Angle
America's most popular vehicle segment — compact crossover SUVs — has a **14.4x death rate spread** between its safest (Mazda CX-5 at 0.12 per 100M VMT) and deadliest (Jeep Cherokee at 1.73) entries. Impairment rates are nearly identical (17.6%–20.5%), so this isn't a driver behavior story. It's a vehicle engineering story that consumers can't see from a brochure.

## Kill Test
- Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES — this is the single most popular vehicle segment in America and nobody has ranked them by actual death rate from FARS data. IIHS star ratings don't capture this spread.
- Novel angle? YES — the 14.4x within-class spread is a novel FARS cross-tabulation. Impairment rate controls make it a genuine apples-to-apples comparison.
- After 117 articles, is this surprising? YES — there are individual model articles (CX-5, RAV4, Cherokee) but NO head-to-head class comparison.

### Key findings:
1. **14.4x gap** between CX-5 (0.12) and Cherokee (1.73) — same class, same price range
2. **Impairment is a non-factor** — ranges from 17.6% to 20.5%, negligible variance
3. **Honda CR-V is NOT the safest** despite universal recommendation — 4.4x the CX-5's rate
4. **Ford Escape at 0.95** — the only compact SUV approaching sedan-level death rates (avg 1.67)
5. **Jeep Cherokee at 1.73** — deadlier than the AVERAGE SEDAN despite being an SUV
6. **Top 5 are Japanese/Korean** (CX-5, RAV4, Forester, Sportage, then GM's Terrain)

## Original Contribution
A ranked death-rate comparison of 13 compact crossover SUVs from FARS data, controlled for impairment rates, revealing a 14.4x safety spread within the same vehicle segment. This cross-tabulation has not been published before.

## Limitations
- FARS only captures fatal crashes — injury rates may tell a different story
- Fleet estimates based on sales data + assumed fleet life, introducing ±15% uncertainty
- Death rate includes all parties, not just occupant deaths (some SUVs may be aggressors)
- Model year mix differs — older models like Cherokee (KL from 2014) may skew data
- Driver demographics beyond impairment (age, urban/rural) not controlled

## Strongest Counterargument
The fleet age composition differs: Cherokee has older average fleet age (2014+ production only ended 2023), while CX-5 arrived in 2012 and had continuous platform updates. Older vehicles naturally have higher death rates because they lack newer safety tech. The comparison may partially reflect platform age rather than inherent design quality.

However, the RAV4 also dates to 2013 (current gen) and scores 0.19 — proving that a similar-age platform can achieve dramatically better outcomes. The Cherokee's 1.73 rate exceeds even some vehicles from the 2000s.
