# Research: NHTSA Complaint-to-Investigation Funnel

## Hook / Self-Critique
**Angle:** In FY2025, American drivers filed 73,099 Vehicle Owner Questionnaires (defect complaints) with NHTSA. The agency opened 33 investigations. That's 1 investigation per 2,215 complaints — a 0.045% investigation rate.

**Challenge:** Is this genuinely surprising after 355 articles? We've covered NHTSA's enforcement weakness (paper tiger), unfixed recalls (1 in 5), and Ford's botched repairs. Is the complaint funnel different enough?

**Verdict:** YES. Existing stories cover the END of the pipeline (enforcement, completion). This covers the BEGINNING — the intake bottleneck. The specific FY25 quarterly metrics haven't been published. The quarterly VOQ trend (+26% Q1→Q4) is fresh data. Proceed.

## Original Contributions

### 1. Complaint-to-Investigation Ratio
73,099 VOQs / 33 investigations = 1 investigation per 2,215 complaints = 0.045% investigation rate.

For every 2,215 Americans who take the time to formally report a vehicle defect to the federal government, NHTSA opens ONE investigation.

### 2. Full Pipeline Math
73,099 complaints filed
→ 33 investigated (0.045%)
→ 992 recalls issued (most manufacturer-initiated, only ~1 NHTSA-mandated)
→ 30.78M vehicles affected by recalls
→ 65.8% completion rate
→ ~10.5M vehicles in FY25 alone NEVER get fixed

### 3. VOQ Trend Analysis
FY25 quarterly complaints: 16,055 → 18,427 → 18,421 → 20,196
26% increase Q1→Q4. Americans are filing MORE complaints. Investigations are flat (10→8→5→10).

### 4. The Denominator Problem
~290 million registered vehicles in the US
~40,000 annual road deaths
73,099 complaints in a year = roughly 1 complaint per 4,000 registered vehicles
33 investigations = 1 investigation per 8.8 million registered vehicles

## Limitations
- VOQs include duplicates, frivolous complaints, and user error reports — not all represent genuine defects
- Many recalls are manufacturer-initiated and don't originate from complaints; the 992 recalls aren't all downstream of the 73,099 complaints
- Investigations are resource-intensive and NHTSA's budget hasn't kept pace with fleet growth
- A single investigation can lead to recalls affecting millions of vehicles, so raw count understates impact
- Some VOQs are about existing recalls, not new defects

## Strongest Counterargument
NHTSA would argue the system works BECAUSE manufacturers self-police. The 992 recalls were overwhelmingly manufacturer-initiated. The 33 investigations represent the worst failures that manufacturers didn't catch themselves. A high investigation count would actually signal a broken system — it would mean manufacturers aren't doing their jobs. The low count could mean manufacturers are doing better, not that NHTSA is doing worse.

The counterargument has force: if your smoke detector catches fires 99% of the time, the fire department showing up once a year isn't evidence of a failing fire department.

But the counterargument collapses when you look at re-recalls (Ford's 387K botched repairs) and completion rates (34.2% never fixed). The manufacturers aren't catching everything. And when they do catch it, a third of the time the fix never happens.
