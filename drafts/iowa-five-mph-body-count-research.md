## Key Research: Speed Limit → Fatality Relationship

### 1. IIHS — Farmer (2017)
- **Citation:** Farmer, C.M. (2017). "Relationship of traffic fatality rates to maximum state speed limits." *Traffic Injury Prevention*, 18(4), 375-380.
- **Finding:** For every 5 mph increase in the maximum state speed limit, there was a **4% increase in fatalities**
- **Scope:** All 50 states, 1993-2013
- **URL:** https://www.iihs.org/topics/speed

### 2. AJPH — Friedman, Hedeker, Richter (2009)
- **Citation:** Friedman et al. (2009). "Long-Term Effects of Repealing the National Maximum Speed Limit." *American Journal of Public Health*, 99(9), 1626-1631.
- **Finding:** 3.23% increase in fatalities on all roads; **9.10% on rural interstates**; effects persistent 6+ years
- **Scope:** All states that raised limits after 1995 NMSL repeal
- **Body count:** ~12,545 excess deaths estimated from speed limit increases (1995-2005)

### 3. PMC ARIMA — Farmer, Retting, Lund (2006)
- **Finding:** 6 mph increase → 3.23% more fatalities all roads, 9.10% rural interstates
- **Key insight:** "persistent impact of increased speeds on road deaths was observed despite coinciding with tremendous improvements in road design, vehicle safety, mandatory seat belt and child restraint use"

### 4. NCHRP Study
- **Finding:** 55→65 mph = 3% more crashes, **24% increase in fatality likelihood per crash**, 28% overall fatality increase
- **Note:** Fatality LIKELIHOOD per crash increases faster than crash frequency — physics

### 5. AAA Foundation (2023)
- **Finding:** Higher limits = increased crash frequencies, minimal travel time savings
- **Key stat:** Speed a factor in ~29% of US traffic fatalities (NHTSA)

## The Math for Iowa
- 5-year average: 337 deaths/year total, 231 rural
- IIHS rate: 4% per 5 mph → 337 × 0.04 = **~13.5 extra deaths/year**
- Rural-specific (Friedman): ~231 × 0.09 = ~21 (but this is for a 10 mph change, so /2 = ~10)
- **Conservative estimate: 10-14 extra deaths per year**
- Over a decade: **100-140 additional lives**

## The Physics
- Kinetic energy = ½mv². Speed from 55→60 = 19% more kinetic energy at impact
- Stopping distance at 60 mph is ~22% longer than at 55 mph
- Reaction-time distance: at 55 mph = 121 ft; at 60 mph = 132 ft (11 ft more)
- A 5 mph difference is the gap between a near-miss and a fatality

## The Counterargument (and why it's wrong)
- Iowa letter writer: "today's vehicles are considerably safer than vehicles in the 70 mph era"
- **Rebuttal (from PMC study):** "persistent impact of increased speeds was observed DESPITE tremendous improvements in road design, vehicle safety, mandatory seat belt use"
- Vehicle safety gains are offset by speed increases — this has been proven repeatedly
- Vehicles are safer → we drive faster → we die at the same rate = the **Peltzman Effect**

## Actionable Takeaway
- If you drive Iowa's rural two-lane roads: add 2 seconds to following distance
- The time savings: at 60 vs 55 mph over a typical 30-mile rural commute = ~2 minutes 52 seconds saved
- Each minute of time savings costs Iowa roughly 4-5 lives per year
