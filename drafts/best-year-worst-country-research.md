# Research: America's Best Year Is Other Countries' Worst

## Angle
The NSC just announced 2025 US traffic fatalities dropped 12% to 37,810 — the biggest single-year decline in recent history. Headlines celebrate. But the CDC published a study (MMWR, Naumann et al.) comparing US traffic deaths to 27 other high-income countries during 2013-2022, and the results are devastating: while other countries cut their death rates by ~20%, America's went UP 22.5%. Even after 2025's celebrated decline, the US death rate (~11.3 per 100K) is still more than double the median of peer nations.

The celebration IS the story. America's best year would be a national emergency in Sweden.

## Novel Contribution: The Excess Death Calculation
If the US had the median overall traffic death rate of the 27 peer countries (~5 per 100K in 2022 based on the reported trends), applied to the US 2025 population (~335M):
- Expected deaths at peer-country rate: ~16,750
- Actual 2025 US deaths: 37,810
- Excess deaths: ~21,060
- That's 21,000 Americans per year killed by the gap between US and peer-country road safety

Even after the celebrated 12% decline:
- US 2025 rate: ~37,810 / 335M × 100K = ~11.3 per 100K
- Still ~2.3x the median of peer nations
- The "best year" rate would still rank dead last among all 28 countries studied by CDC

- Pedestrians 50-100% more likely to die when struck by SUV/pickup vs passenger car
- Taller, heavier vehicles with poor visibility are a key US-specific factor

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely novel? YES. The CDC study was published in MMWR but the mainstream coverage treated it as a policy paper. Nobody has combined the 2025 NSC decline data with the international comparison to show that America's "best year" is still catastrophically bad by international standards. The excess death calculation — 21,000 Americans per year killed by the gap — is an original contribution. The FARS vehicle fleet data connecting vehicle size to the international divergence adds specificity our site is known for.

## Strongest Counterargument
The US is a fundamentally different country than European peers: larger, more spread out, more car-dependent, with different speed limits, different urban design, and a history of suburban sprawl. Comparing per-capita rates without adjusting for VMT (vehicle miles traveled) is misleading because Americans drive far more than Europeans. The IRTAD data uses population denominators, not VMT. If Americans drive 2-3x as many miles per capita, the per-mile risk gap narrows substantially (though the US is still worse).
