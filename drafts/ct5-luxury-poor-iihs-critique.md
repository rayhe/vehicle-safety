## 🔍 Critic 1: General Editor — 8.5/10

- **Headline**: Excellent. The three-sentence construction ("POOR on Four… Five Stars… Same Car.") builds a cognitive dissonance trap the reader can't scroll past. Strongest headline in weeks.
- **Lede**: The opening sentence (39 words) works as a slow windup — dealer brochure styling vs. unchanged structure — before the bolded IIHS bomb drops. The "but they did not touch the structure" clause is the knife twist. Effective.
- **Structure**: Clean flow: styling-vs-substance hook → crash test detail → NHTSA contradiction → FARS historical data → behavioral data → competitors → actionable advice. Each paragraph escalates. Nothing feels redundant.
- **Pull stat**: "4.1×" is punchy and the label is clear. Good choice over the raw death rate (1.32 per 100M VMT), which is harder to intuit.
- **Word count**: 483 — within the 300-500 target.
- **Pacing weakness**: The second paragraph (side impact test details) runs to 80+ words as rendered including the Jermakian quote. It's the densest paragraph in the piece and the mechanical detail about B-pillar crush, airbag failure, windowsill contact, and rear-seat trauma all hits in one breath. Splitting this or trimming the biomechanics would improve pacing.
- **"Clautomotive Desk"**: Standard byline. Fine.
- **Deduction**: -0.5 for the P2 density. -1.0 for a factual error in the competitors paragraph (see Legal Accuracy below) — the Audi A6 is NOT cheaper than the CT5, making "Every one of them cheaper" false. This weakens the closing argument.

## ⚖️ Critic 3: Ethics Reviewer — 8.5/10

- **Fair treatment of GM**: GM's five-star NHTSA defense is presented ("GM fired back by citing its five-star NHTSA rating, which is technically legitimate") — the phrase "technically legitimate" acknowledges the claim's validity while signaling its insufficiency. Fair, but on the border of dismissive. The article doesn't explore WHY IIHS and NHTSA diverge beyond the disclaimer — different crash modes, speeds, dummies. Body text readers may come away thinking NHTSA is incompetent or captured, which isn't the point.
- **CTS → CT5 impairment data**: Using predecessor impairment data as an implicit indictment of current CT5 buyers is an ethical stretch. The article doesn't say "CT5 buyers are drunk drivers," but the rhetorical proximity — placing impairment data two paragraphs after current test results — creates that inference. The disclaimer notes the CTS/CT5 distinction, but disclaimers are read by 5% of readers.
- **"they did not touch the structure"**: This is presented as fact but is an inference, possibly from the Jermakian quote. If GM did make structural changes that were simply insufficient, the framing is unfair. The Jermakian quote says results "weren't important," not that no changes were made.
- **Self-congratulation**: ✅ None. The article doesn't position itself as heroic for uncovering this.
- **Proportional advice**: The "What this means for you" section is reasonable — check both agencies, look at headlights by trim, inform rear-seat passengers. Not alarmist.
- **Deduction**: -0.5 for the "didn't touch the structure" claim being unsourced. -0.5 for the CTS impairment data being placed to create guilt-by-association with CT5 buyers without explicit caveat in the body text. -0.5 for not explaining the IIHS/NHTSA methodological difference in the body (leaving readers to assume one agency is wrong rather than that they test different things).

## ⚖️ Critic 5: Legal Accuracy — 7.5/10 ❌ BELOW THRESHOLD

- **IIHS "POOR" ratings claim**: The article states POOR on "moderate overlap front crash, side impact, headlights, and front crash prevention." This needs verification against the actual IIHS results page. The freep.com source [1] is cited. Assuming the source is accurate, this is fine — but the article should ideally cite IIHS directly rather than solely through a newspaper intermediary.
- **NHTSA 5-star claim**: Cited directly [2] with a link to nhtsa.gov. ✅
- **FARS data**: Cited generally [3] as "FARS, 2014–2023." The specific 1.32 rate, 4.1× multiplier, and competitor rates are derived from FARS data but the article doesn't show the calculation or link to the specific query. Acceptable for this format, and the disclaimer notes uncertainty.
- **🔴 FACTUAL ERROR — "Every one of them cheaper than a CT5"**: The Audi A6 starts at approximately $64,100 (2026 MSRP per Edmunds). The CT5 starts at $48,600. The A6 is ~$15,500 MORE expensive than the CT5. Claiming "Every one of them cheaper than a CT5" is factually false. This is a significant accuracy failure that could undermine credibility. The original competitors list (Mazda CX-5 $28K, Subaru Crosstrek $31K, BMW X1 ~$40K) are all cheaper — the A6 breaks the pattern.
- **"they did not touch the structure"**: Unsourced engineering claim. Not directly stated by Jermakian or in the cited freep.com article (which says results "weren't important for GM," a different claim about priorities vs. actions). This is a liability risk — GM could dispute it.
- **"essentially the same platform"**: Both CTS and CT5 use GM's Alpha platform. This is factually defensible but not cited.
- **Impairment claim ("over a quarter")**: Cited as FARS data. The 25.9% figure from FARS for CTS is accurate per the research notes. ✅
- **Deduction**: -1.5 for the Audi A6 price factual error. -0.5 for the unsourced "didn't touch the structure" claim. -0.5 for no direct IIHS citation (relying solely on freep.com intermediary).

## 🔬 Critic 6: Research Rigor — 8.5/10

- **Original contribution**: ✅ Cross-referencing same-day IIHS results with FARS historical death rates for the predecessor model is a novel analytical move. No other outlet reporting the CT5 IIHS results (freep.com, etc.) pulled the CTS FARS data. The 4.1× Audi A4 comparison, C-Class double, IS +50% comparisons provide unique analytical depth.
- **Methodology transparency**: The disclaimer explains the CTS-as-proxy limitation, VMT uncertainty, and IIHS/NHTSA methodological differences. Good. The body text, however, doesn't flag that the 1.32 rate is for the CTS, not the CT5 — the transition ("FARS data suggests which answer to trust: the CTS, the CT5's predecessor") is clear but could be clearer about the analytical leap.
- **Strongest counterargument at full strength**: The article presents GM's NHTSA defense ("technically legitimate") but doesn't engage with WHY the methodological divergence exists — that NHTSA and IIHS test fundamentally different scenarios. A reader might conclude one agency is simply wrong rather than that they're measuring different things. The disclaimer addresses this but disclaimers are footnotes.
- **Limitations**: ✅ Present in disclaimer. CTS ≠ CT5, VMT uncertainty, different test methodologies.
- **Verifiability**: FARS death rates traceable to FARS bulk data. IIHS results traceable via freep.com (no direct IIHS link). NHTSA rating directly linked.
- **CTS impairment data relevance**: Using CTS impairment rates (25.9%) as evidence about the CT5 is analytically risky. The CT5 may attract a different buyer demographic. The article doesn't caveat this in the body — the paragraph reads as "CT5 buyers are dangerous drivers" without stating that this is predecessor data with unknown transferability.
- **Deduction**: -0.5 for the CTS impairment data being presented without body-text caveat. -0.5 for not explaining IIHS/NHTSA methodological difference in body. -0.5 for the Audi A6 price error (a research failure).

## Required Revisions Before Re-scoring

### 🔴 BLOCKING — Audi A6 Price Error
The article claims "Every one of them cheaper than a CT5, and every one passed." The Audi A6 starts at ~$64,100. The CT5 starts at $48,600. The A6 is approximately $15,500 MORE expensive.

**Fix options:**
1. Remove the Audi A6 from the cheaper-than-CT5 framing: "In that same IIHS batch, a $28,000 Mazda CX-5 earned Top Safety Pick+. So did Subaru's Crosstrek Hybrid at $31,000 and the BMW X1. Vehicles that cost half as much passed tests the CT5 failed."
2. Or restructure: keep A6 in the TSP+ list but drop the "cheaper" claim: "In that same IIHS batch, vehicles ranging from a $28,000 Mazda CX-5 to a $64,000 Audi A6 earned Top Safety Pick+. Price didn't predict safety — engineering did."

### 🟡 RECOMMENDED — Source the "didn't touch the structure" claim
Either cite a source confirming no structural changes, or soften to "the structure that IIHS tested clearly hadn't kept pace" or similar evidence-based language.

### 🟡 RECOMMENDED — Add body-text IIHS/NHTSA methodology note
One sentence explaining why two agencies can diverge: "The two agencies test different crash scenarios at different speeds with different dummies" — currently only in the disclaimer. Promotes reader understanding.

### 🟢 OPTIONAL — Add direct IIHS citation
Add the IIHS results page URL as a fourth reference. Currently relying solely on freep.com as intermediary.
