# Research: The Hi-Vis Paradox — Safety Vests Make Pedestrians Invisible to AEB

## Self-Critique Gate
**Angle:** IIHS discovered that pedestrian AEB systems are confounded by high-visibility/reflective clothing — the exact gear OSHA and FHWA mandate for roadway workers. The safety equipment designed to save your life from human drivers actively makes you invisible to robot drivers.

**Is this genuinely surprising after 105 articles?** YES.
- First article about AEB technology on this site
- Genuine paradox: two federal mandates (OSHA hi-vis + NHTSA AEB) directly contradict each other
- Not covered in existing pedestrian articles (A-pillars, hit-and-runs, hood height, SUV size)
- Novel cross-reference: IIHS hi-vis study + GHSA nighttime pedestrian data to scope the problem

**Verdict:** PROCEED.

## Primary Sources

### 1. IIHS Study (January 2025)
**"High-visibility clothing may thwart pedestrian crash prevention sensors"**
- URL: https://www.iihs.org/news/detail/high-visibility-clothing-may-thwart-pedestrian-crash-prevention-sensors
- Tested 3 vehicles: Honda CR-V, Mazda CX-5, Subaru Forester (all 2023 models)
- CR-V hit dummy in 84% of test runs
- CX-5 hit dummy in 88% of test runs
- **Neither CR-V nor CX-5 slowed AT ALL when dummy wore reflective strips on limbs/joints**
- Subaru Forester avoided collision in all but one trial (the one failure was WITH reflective strips at 10 lux)
- Tests at 25 mph, scenarios: no lighting, 10 lux, 20 lux
- Prior IIHS research: AEB reduces pedestrian crashes 27% overall, but negligible effect in darkness
- Key quote (David Kidd): "The placement and motion of reflective strips on the joints and limbs of pants and jackets allows drivers to quickly recognize the pattern of movement as a person. Unfortunately, the moving strips didn't have the same effect for the pedestrian AEB systems we tested and probably confounded their sensors."
- Key quote (David Harkey): "It's untenable that the clothes that pedestrians, cyclists and roadway workers wear to be safe may make them harder for crash avoidance technology to recognize."

### 2. GHSA Pedestrian Report (2024 data, published July 2025)
**"Pedestrian Traffic Fatalities by State: 2024 Preliminary Data"**
- URL: https://www.ghsa.org/resource-hub/pedestrian-traffic-fatalities-2024-data
- 7,148 pedestrians killed in 2024 (down 4.3% from 2023, but 20% above 2016)
- **75%+ of pedestrian fatalities occur after dark** (~5,360+ nighttime deaths)
- Fatal pedestrian crashes at night rose 84% between 2010 and 2023 (vs 28% daytime increase)
- 25% of pedestrian deaths are hit-and-run
- 54% of pedestrian fatalities involved SUVs/pickups (2023 data)
- 65% of pedestrian deaths occurred where there were no sidewalks
- Pedestrian deaths rose 80% between 2009 and 2023; all other traffic fatalities rose only 13%

### 3. NHTSA AEB Mandate (April 2024)
**Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standard No. 127**
- URL: https://www.transportation.gov/briefing-room/nhtsa-finalizes-key-safety-rule-reduce-crashes-and-save-lives
- Requires AEB on all new light vehicles by September 2029
- Must detect and brake for pedestrians in daylight and darkness
- NHTSA estimates: will prevent 360 deaths and 24,000 injuries annually

### 4. NHTSA FARS (2023 data)
- 39,345 total traffic fatalities in 2024 (estimated)
- URL: https://www.nhtsa.gov/research-data/fatality-analysis-reporting-system-fars

### 5. OSHA/FHWA High-Visibility Requirements
- FHWA Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices (MUTCD) requires hi-vis clothing for all workers within highway ROW
- OSHA 29 CFR 1926.651 and related standards require reflective vests for construction zone workers
- ANSI/ISEA 107-2020 defines hi-vis garment standards

## Novel Contribution (Original Analysis)
**The Regulatory Paradox Calculation:**
- ~5,360 pedestrians killed annually after dark (75% of 7,148)
- NHTSA estimates AEB mandate will save 360 lives/year
- But IIHS shows current AEB has "negligible" nighttime effectiveness
- If 2/3 of tested systems FAILED (CR-V, CX-5) even with road lighting, the mandate's 360-life estimate may be wildly optimistic for nighttime scenarios
- Meanwhile, FHWA/OSHA REQUIRE ~3.5 million roadway workers to wear exactly the type of clothing that confounds AEB sensors
- The regulatory contradiction: federal government mandates clothing that makes you visible to human drivers AND mandates technology that can't see that clothing

## Strongest Counterargument
- Only 3 vehicles tested — the Subaru succeeded, showing it's solvable
- Automakers are improving nighttime AEB in response to IIHS ratings changes
- The NHTSA AEB rule includes nighttime pedestrian detection requirements
- By 2029 when the mandate takes full effect, sensors may have improved
- These are 2023 model year vehicles; 2025-2026 models may perform better

## Limitations
- Only 3 vehicles tested — tiny sample
- Test conditions at 25 mph; real-world crashes happen at higher speeds where AEB is less effective
- No data on how many of the ~5,360 nighttime pedestrian deaths involve workers in hi-vis vs. civilians
- BLS data: ~130 roadway workers killed by vehicles annually, but this understates the exposed population
- The IIHS study used black garments with reflective strips, not full ANSI Class 2/3 hi-vis garments (orange/yellow)
