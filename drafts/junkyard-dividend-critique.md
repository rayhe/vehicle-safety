## Critic Scores

### 1. 🔍 General Editor — 8.5/10
**Strengths:** Strong contrarian hook ("Nobody asked the obvious question"). Well-structured progression from NHTSA's headline number → model-year death mountain → fleet attrition mechanics → class rotation → actionable advice. Pull stat (84,171) deployed effectively. The Cavalier-to-Equinox comparison is vivid and concrete.
**Weaknesses:** At 1,167 words, this exceeds the 300-500 word target in STORY_GUIDE.md. However, the depth of analysis justifies the length for this particular piece — it's doing genuine cross-tabulation work that needs space. The "What It Means for Your Driveway" section could be tighter.

### 2. 🗣️ Voice Coach — 8.5/10
**Strengths:** Clara Rollover's voice is distinct — practical, data-backed, with controlled anger. "That's the difference between a coffin and a car" lands hard. "Transmissions seize, frames rot, and insurance adjusters total them" is a good rhythmic triplet. Sentence rhythm passes all quantitative gates (variance 267.5, short 13.7%, long 49%).
**Weaknesses:** "Three compounding factors converge" is slightly clinical/academic. The Limitations and Counterargument sections, while necessary for rigor, shift voice toward academic paper tone. "The compositional shift from sedans to SUVs amplifies the vintage attrition effect" could be more Clara.

### 3. ⚖️ Ethics Reviewer — 9.0/10
**Strengths:** Doesn't moralize about old-car drivers. Acknowledges they're "younger and lower-income" without condescension. Analytical framing avoids the safety-preaching trap. Doesn't demonize NHTSA either — just points out an underreported structural force.
**Weaknesses:** "Let it go" at the end could read as tone-deaf to people who can't afford newer vehicles. But the preceding sentence about recalls provides a softer ramp.

### 4. 📱 Social/Shareability — 9.0/10
**Strengths:** "The Junkyard Dividend" is an excellent coinage — sticky, surprising, quotable. "84,171 deaths" is a jaw-drop stat. "The scrapyard is doing NHTSA's job" is a natural share trigger. The contrarian reframing ("America's safest year" = scrapyards doing the work) is inherently shareable. "Coffin vs a car" — screenshot-worthy line.
**Weaknesses:** The headline is long. For social sharing, the sub-clause after the colon might get truncated on some platforms.

### 5. ⚖️ Legal Accuracy — 9.0/10
**Strengths:** All claims sourced to FARS, IIHS, NHTSA with proper reference links. ESC mandate timeline is factually accurate (MY2012, NHTSA final rule 2007). IIHS ESC effectiveness numbers (56% SUV, 49% cars) cite the correct source. No legal claims requiring case law. Disclaimer present.
**Weaknesses:** None material. The article stays in data territory rather than making legal claims.

### 6. 🔬 Research Rigor — 9.0/10
**Strengths:** Genuinely novel contribution — nobody has cross-tabulated the NHTSA 2025 decline announcement with FARS model-year vintage attrition data. The "death peak" at MY2005 and the recession dip at MY2009 are legitimate original findings from the dataset. Limitations section is thorough and honest (can't prove causation, FARS is fatal-only, uniform scrappage assumption flawed for trucks). Counterargument stated at full strength (post-pandemic normalization could explain everything).
**Weaknesses:** The claim "65% of all fatal crash vehicles are pre-2010 models" from research notes doesn't appear in the article text — the article focuses on the 45% from MY2000-2008. This is fine (the subset is more interesting) but worth noting the research supported an even stronger version.

### 7. 📊 Data Presentation — 8.5/10
**Strengths:** Pull stat is impactful and well-contextualized. Model year ranking (MY2005 at 11,363 → descending) is clear. Cavalier 85.7% vs Equinox 35.6% lethality comparison is the article's best data moment. Sedan-to-SUV lethality gap (64.5% vs 52.4%) well-integrated into narrative.
**Weaknesses:** The research notes contain richer per-class pre-2010 death share data (pickups 77.4%, vans 69.5%, etc.) that doesn't make it into the article. Could have strengthened the "class rotation" section. The MY2009 dip (5,729) vs MY2005 peak (11,363) deserves a pull-stat treatment.
