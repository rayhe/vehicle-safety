# Research: The Rear Center Seat Nobody Tests

## Angle
Hyundai recalls 47,749 Konas for a rear center seatbelt buckle that may not restrain occupants. The buckle was manufactured by Joyson Safety Systems using worn stamping dies kept past their lifespan. But the bigger story: the rear center is the most neglected seating position in vehicle safety. No crash test puts a dummy there. No vehicle has a center rear airbag. IIHS's new rear-seat test puts the dummy behind the driver, not in the center. This is the ghost seat of automotive safety.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Hyundai Kona Recall — July 17, 2026 (NHTSA 26V452)
- 47,749 vehicles: 47,733 model-year 2026 Kona, 16 model-year 2025 Kona Electric
- Defect: rear center seat belt buckle may fail to properly restrain occupant in crash
- Supplier: Joyson Safety Systems (Korean supplier, successor to Takata's assets)
- Cause: "inadequate inspection controls" — stamping dies used beyond their lifespan
- No crashes, injuries, or incidents reported
- Fix: replace rear center seatbelt buckle assembly free of charge
- Hyundai advised owners not to use rear center seat until repair

### 2. IIHS Rear Seat Safety Research
- In vehicles from model year 2007+, belted rear-seat occupants face 46% higher fatal injury risk than front-seat occupants (IIHS)
- This is NOT because rear got less safe — front got better (crash tensioners, force limiters, advanced airbags)
- Less than half of new vehicles have advanced restraint systems in rear seat
- IIHS updated moderate overlap test places dummy behind driver — NOT in center position
- The 5th percentile female Hybrid III dummy represents a small woman or 12-year-old child

### 3. NHTSA Research (Kahane 2017)
- Rear center 3-point belt reduces fatal injury risk by 58% in cars, 75% in SUVs
- In 1960s-70s: rear center was 37% safer than front; outboard rear 26% safer
- The disparity has nearly evaporated for adult passengers
- Seatbelts saved 20,443 lives in 2019; 457,578 lives 1968-2019

### 4. Rear Seatbelt Usage Rates
- IIHS 2017 study: only 74% of rear passengers buckle up vs 91% in front
- Unbelted rear occupants are 8x more likely to be injured, 2x more likely to die
- An unbelted rear passenger behind a belted driver increases driver fatality risk by 137% (Bose et al., 2013)
- Half of all people fatally injured in crashes are unrestrained (IIHS VP Jessica Jermakian)

### 5. Submarining Problem
- In IIHS updated tests, multiple vehicles show rear dummy submarining (lap belt slides off pelvis onto abdomen)
- Cadillac XT6: POOR rating
- Audi Q5, Lexus RX: MARGINAL
- Honda Pilot, Hyundai Palisade, Jeep Grand Cherokee: POOR
- The rear CENTER seat belt geometry is typically different from outboard positions — narrower mounting points, potentially higher submarining risk

## Original Analysis

### The Testing Gap
No crash test protocol anywhere in the world puts a test dummy in the rear center seat. IIHS's new test puts the dummy behind the driver. Euro NCAP has tested rear occupants since 2015 — also not in center. The rear center position is literally untested in any standardized crash evaluation.

### The Engineering Hierarchy
Safety engineering investment flows: Driver → Front passenger → Rear outboard → Rear center. The rear center gets:
- No airbag (no vehicle offers one)
- Often no pretensioner or force limiter
- In older vehicles, often just a lap belt
- Different belt geometry due to floor-mounted anchor vs door-mounted
- Narrower seating position with hard armrest intrusion zones on both sides

### The Joyson Pattern
Joyson Safety Systems (formerly Key Safety Systems, which acquired Takata) is now the supplier behind this buckle failure. Stamping dies used past their lifespan = quality control degradation. This is the same company that inherited Takata's airbag business after the largest automotive recall in history.
