# Research: America's Roads Go Dark at Night. The Death Toll Is Predictable.

## Thesis
25% of vehicle miles happen at night, but 50% of driver fatalities and 76% of pedestrian deaths occur in darkness. A Cochrane systematic review found street lighting reduces fatal crashes by 66%. America has chosen not to install it on millions of miles of road — and 20,000+ people die in darkness every year.

## The Novel Cross-Tab
Nobody has done this math publicly: take the FARS "Dark - Not Lighted" fatality count, apply the Cochrane meta-analysis crash modification factor (RR 0.34 for fatal crashes), and calculate how many deaths are attributable to the *absence* of lighting infrastructure. This isn't driver behavior — it's a policy choice.

## Key Data Points

### FARS Lighting Condition Data (2022)
- Total fatalities: 42,721
- ~50% of all fatal crashes occur between 6 PM and 6 AM (IIHS/FARS)
- 18,000+ nighttime fatal crashes in 2022 (AAAM)
- 76% of pedestrian fatalities occurred in dark conditions (2022, NSC/FARS)
- Only 25% of vehicle miles traveled (VMT) occur at night (AAA/FARS query)

### The Infrastructure Gap
- 4.19 million miles of public roads in the US (BTS/FHWA)
- The majority of rural roads have no street lighting whatsoever
- Rural roads: ~19% of VMT but ~43% of fatalities (already covered in our rural article — this is the *lighting* dimension)
- 70% of rural fatal crashes occur in dark conditions (FARS)

### Cochrane Systematic Review (Beyer & Ker, 2009)
- DOI: 10.1002/14651858.CD004728.pub2
- 17 controlled before-after studies
- Pooled risk ratio for fatal crashes with street lighting vs day-time control: **RR = 0.34 (95% CI 0.17-0.68)**
- That's a 66% reduction in fatal crashes
- Total crashes: RR = 0.68 (43% of area-controlled) to 0.45 (55% reduction with area control)
- Injury crashes: RR = 0.68-0.78

### FHWA Crash Modification Factor
- CMF Clearinghouse: lighting installation CMF = 0.72-0.80 for total nighttime crashes
- That's a 20-28% reduction in nighttime crashes from adding lighting

### Cost-Benefit
- DOT Value of Statistical Life (VSL): $13.2 million per fatality (2023)
- LED street light cost: ~$150-300 per unit, ~$1,500 installation per unit
- ~33 lights per km (~53 per mile) for standard spacing
- Cost per mile: ~$54,000-$80,000 for installation
- If lighting prevents even 5,000 of the ~20,000 dark-condition deaths: 5,000 × $13.2M = $66 billion in prevented losses per year
- The entire US street lighting inventory costs ~$10-15 billion per year to operate

### The Counterargument
1. Alcohol is the real driver of nighttime deaths (75% of drunk driving fatalities occur at night) — but lighting doesn't fix impairment. Counter-counter: even controlling for alcohol, the fatality rate per mile is 3x higher at night (NHTSA)
2. Light pollution concerns — LEDs with proper shielding address this
3. Cost of lighting every rural mile is prohibitive — but targeted intersection and high-fatality corridor lighting is cost-effective
4. Drivers compensate by driving faster on lit roads — the "risk compensation" argument. Cochrane data shows net benefit is still massive.

### The "Three Multiplier" Framework
Night driving compounds three independent risks:
1. **Visibility deficit** — human eyes see ~5% as well at night; headlights illuminate 250-500 ft
2. **Behavioral overlay** — fatigue, alcohol, and drug impairment cluster at night  
3. **Infrastructure absence** — the road itself provides less information (no edge markers, no lighting, worse signage reflectivity)

These multiply, not add. A slightly impaired driver on a well-lit urban road is manageable. That same driver on a dark rural two-lane with no edge markers is dead.
