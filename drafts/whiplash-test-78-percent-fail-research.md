# Research: IIHS Whiplash Test — 78% of Compact SUVs Failed

## Angle
The old IIHS whiplash test (20 mph only) was retired in 2022 because nearly every car aced it. Real-world data showed people in "well-rated" vehicles were still getting whiplash at alarming rates. IIHS redesigned the test — added 30 mph impact, sled rig with articulated-spine dummy, actual seat removed from vehicle. Result: only 4 of 18 compact SUVs (22%) earned Good. The industry was optimizing for the test, not for actual protection.

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES.
- Novel contribution: Cross-referencing IIHS test results with FARS fatality data reveals the blind spot — FARS captures deaths, not injuries. A vehicle can have a great fatality rate and still wreck your neck in the most common crash type.
- The Tucson paradox: Consumer Reports top pick, FARS rate of 0.34 (excellent), but POOR on whiplash protection. Safety ratings paint incomplete pictures.
- 78% failure rate on a test that should measure protection from the most common crash injury in America.

## Primary Sources

### 1. IIHS New Whiplash Prevention Test (February 2026)
- 18 small SUVs tested
- Good (4): Audi Q3, Hyundai Ioniq 5, Subaru Forester, Toyota RAV4
- Acceptable (9): Chevrolet Equinox, Honda CR-V, Volvo XC40, Buick Encore GX, and others
- Marginal (2): Nissan Rogue, BMW X1
- Poor (3): Ford Bronco Sport, Hyundai Tucson, Mazda CX-50
- Source: IIHS (iihs.org), reported widely (Jalopnik, MotorTrend, Consumer Reports, AutoBlog)

### 2. IIHS Head Restraint Ratings and Insurance Claim Rates
- IIHS study: vehicles with good head restraint ratings had 11.2% lower injury rates in rear-end collisions
- Females and young-to-middle-age drivers saw greatest reductions
- Source: iihs.org

### 3. NHTSA FARS Data (2014-2023)
- Tucson: 669 deaths, rate 0.34/100M VMT — very low for segment
- RAV4: 914 deaths, rate 0.19 — lowest in segment (and earned Good)
- CR-V: 2072 deaths, rate 0.53 (Acceptable)
- Rogue: 968 deaths, rate 0.35 (Marginal)
- FARS doesn't capture injuries — only fatal crashes
- Source: NHTSA FARS database

### 4. Whiplash Injury Statistics
- Whiplash costs $8+ billion annually in the US (per IIHS/insurance data)
- Most common injury in US auto insurance claims
- Rear-end collisions are most common crash type (~29% of all crashes per NHTSA)
- Source: Insurance Institute for Highway Safety, NHTSA

### 5. Test Methodology Details
- Previous test: 20 mph rear impact simulation, tested whole vehicle
- Nearly every car aced it by 2022 → test retired
- New test: Seat removed from vehicle, mounted on sled rig
- Fully articulated spine dummy (not standard HIII)
- Impacts at 20 AND 30 mph
- Measures: head-to-spine relative movement, cervical vertebrae curvature, head contact time, energy absorption
- Key quote from Marcy Edwards (IIHS senior research engineer): "We don't fully understand the mechanisms that cause whiplash injuries, but our research makes us confident that reducing these types of movements and stresses should cut down on whiplash injuries in the real world."

## Specific Failure Modes (per IIHS/reporting)
- **Tucson:** Head restraint pushed dummy's chin DOWN toward chest (opposite of intended)
- **CX-50:** Seat let dummy slide backward and upward, undermining head restraint entirely
- **Bronco Sport:** "Particularly poor support for head and spine." Long delay before head contacted restraint. High velocity difference between pelvis and head.
