# Research: NHTSA Five-Star Delay — Automaker Lobby

## Angle
NHTSA delayed its updated 5-star safety rating program from 2026 to 2027 model year after the Alliance for Automotive Innovation lobbied for more time. The changes — requiring blind-spot warning, blind-spot intervention, lane-keeping assist, and pedestrian AEB as standard features to earn 5 stars — were first proposed in March 2022. Automakers had 4+ years of notice. Meanwhile, IIHS independently tightened its own Top Safety Pick criteria on schedule. The contrast: a private nonprofit raised the bar on time; the federal safety regulator caved to industry.

## Key Data

### NHTSA Delay (Sources: Reuters Sept 19 2025, Carscoops, Autoblog, Jalopnik Jul 2026)
- NHTSA announced changes to 5-Star NCAP in 2024
- Changes first proposed/floated March 2022
- Alliance for Automotive Innovation (GM, Ford, Toyota, VW, Hyundai, Nissan, Kia, BMW, Honda, JLR, Mitsubishi, Mazda) lobbied in April 2025
- Alliance claimed: NHTSA failed to publish finalized pedestrian crash test procedures
- Alliance also claimed: schedule didn't allow time for vehicle design changes; few vehicles would qualify
- NHTSA caved September 2025 — delayed to 2027 model year
- Delayed features: blind-spot warning, blind-spot intervention, lane-keeping assist, pedestrian AEB
- Also delayed: AEB standard requiring stops from 62 mph (automakers called "unrealistic")
- Alliance separately asked Trump admin to reconsider 2029 AEB requirements
- Ford spokesperson: "80% of recalls are resolved through software-only updates"

### IIHS (did NOT delay — tightened on time)
- 2026 TSP+: now requires good rating in pedestrian front crash prevention
- 2026 TSP+: requires good or acceptable in updated vehicle-to-vehicle 2.0 front crash prevention (higher speeds)
- 2026 TSP/TSP+: front crash prevention must be STANDARD equipment (new for 2026)
- 2026 TSP: requires good rating in moderate overlap front test (raised from "acceptable")
- IIHS President David Harkey: "This year, we're asking automakers to make excellent protection for back seat passengers the norm"

### Pedestrian Deaths (Sources: GHSA, CDC/MMWR, FARS)
- 2009: 4,109 ped deaths (12.1% of all traffic fatalities)
- 2013: 4,779 (14.5%)
- 2022: 7,605 (17.8%) — 40-year high
- 2023: 7,380 (18.0%) — slight decline but proportion still rose
- 2024: 7,148 (preliminary, down 4.3%)
- 2009→2023: +80% pedestrian deaths vs +13% all other traffic deaths
- US pedestrian death rate 2013→2022: +50% (1.55 to 2.33 per 100K)
- 27 other high-income countries: median -24.7% in same period
- US has HIGHEST pedestrian death rate among all 28 high-income countries examined
- 75%+ of pedestrian deaths happen at night
- 65% at locations without a sidewalk
- 25% are hit-and-runs

### FARS Context (vehicle-safety.org data)
- ~40,000+ annual US traffic deaths
- Ford: 53 recalls in 2026, leading all automakers for 3rd straight year (vs Chrysler's 19)

## Original Contribution
1. Timeline math: NHTSA proposed changes March 2022. Delay announced September 2025. That's 3.5 years of notice before the original 2026 deadline. Now delayed to 2027 = automakers get 5+ years total.
2. The IIHS/NHTSA split: both set out to require the same technologies. One is a private nonprofit insurance-industry group that raised its bar on schedule. The other is the federal government, which folded.
3. Body count framing: 7,148 pedestrians killed in 2024. The delayed features specifically target pedestrian detection and avoidance.

## Strongest Counterargument
NHTSA genuinely hadn't published finalized pedestrian crash test procedures. Automakers can't test to a standard that doesn't exist in procedural form. This is a legitimate administrative failure — you can propose a standard, but if you don't publish the test method, manufacturers literally can't comply. The Alliance's complaint wasn't "we don't want to do this" — it was "tell us exactly how you're going to test us."

## Limitations
- FARS data is 2014-2023; can't directly measure lives lost from a one-year delay
- The 5-star program is voluntary (ratings, not mandates); automakers can sell cars without 5 stars
- Pedestrian AEB wouldn't eliminate all pedestrian deaths — many occur at night, high speeds, etc.
- The delay is one year, not cancellation; features should still be required starting 2027 MY
- But: the Alliance also asked to delay the 2029 mandatory AEB rule, suggesting the lobby continues
