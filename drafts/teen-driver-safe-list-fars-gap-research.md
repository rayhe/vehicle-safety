# Research: IIHS/CR Teen Driver "Safe" List vs. FARS Fatality Data

## Angle
The IIHS and Consumer Reports just released their 2026 recommended vehicles for teen drivers (June 5, 2026). Cross-referencing these "safe" picks with FARS fatality data reveals a gap: 6 of 15 recommended sedans have above-average real-world fatality rates. The Honda Accord — the most iconic "safe family car" — kills at 2.1x the sedan average.

## Key Data (FARS 2014-2023)

### Teen-Recommended Cars vs. FARS
| Car | Deaths | Rate/100M VMT | Sedan Avg | Ratio |
|-----|--------|---------------|-----------|-------|
| Honda Accord | 7,102 | 3.07 | 1.46 | 2.10x |
| Honda Civic | 6,553 | 2.25 | 1.46 | 1.54x |
| Nissan Sentra | 2,571 | 2.13 | 1.46 | 1.46x |
| Toyota Corolla | 4,945 | 1.85 | 1.46 | 1.27x |
| Hyundai Sonata | 1,887 | 1.56 | 1.46 | 1.07x |
| Hyundai Elantra | 2,407 | 1.50 | 1.46 | 1.03x |

6 of 15 recommended sedans exceed the segment average fatality rate.

### Contrast: The SUV recommendations
SUVs on the teen list are dramatically safer in FARS data:
- Mazda CX-5: rate 0.12 (12x safer than Accord)
- Subaru Crosstrek: rate 0.08 (38x safer)
- Toyota RAV4: rate 0.19 (16x safer)
- Honda CR-V: rate 0.53 (6x safer)

### Novel Finding
The gap between crash-test performance and real-world outcomes is widest for affordable sedans. The Honda Accord passes every IIHS test with flying colors. It also has 7,102 deaths in FARS — the third-highest body count of ANY vehicle in America. The cars on this list that are cheapest (median $4,700-$8,300) tend to have the highest real-world death rates.

### Original Contribution
Cross-tabulation of the 2026 IIHS/CR teen driver recommendation list against FARS per-model fatality rates. Nobody has run this specific comparison. The finding: crash-test ratings predict crashworthiness in controlled conditions, but not real-world fatality rates, which are dominated by vehicle size/weight, fleet age, and driver demographics.

### Limitations
1. FARS data spans 2014-2023 and includes ALL model years — not just the specific years IIHS recommends (e.g., 2013+ Accord)
2. Older model years of these vehicles lacked the safety tech IIHS now tests for
3. Fatality rates reflect who drives these cars — cheap sedans attract younger, riskier drivers
4. IIHS specifically recommends certain model years with specific safety features; the FARS rate is fleet-wide
5. The comparison is apples-to-oranges by design — that IS the point (crash tests ≠ real-world outcomes)

### Strongest Counterargument
IIHS would correctly argue: "Our ratings evaluate how well the car protects you in a crash and avoids crashes. The FARS rate reflects exposure, demographics, vehicle age, and conditions outside anyone's control. A 2022 Accord with AEB and modern airbags is fundamentally different from a 2005 Accord." This is fair. The IIHS recommendations specify model years for this reason. But parents buying used don't always get the specific year recommended — they get what's affordable — and the FARS data captures what actually happens to these models in the aggregate.

### Actionable Takeaway
If you're buying a teen their first car: the SUVs on the IIHS list have dramatically lower real-world fatality rates than the sedans. A used Mazda CX-5 ($13,100 median) has a 0.12 fatality rate vs. a used Honda Accord ($8,300 median) at 3.07. That $4,800 price gap buys a 25x reduction in fatality rate. If your budget forces a sedan, the Kia Forte (0.40), Subaru Impreza (0.52), and Ford Fusion (1.23) have the lowest real-world rates on the list.
