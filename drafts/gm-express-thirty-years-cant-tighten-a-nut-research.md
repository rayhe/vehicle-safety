# Research Notes: GM Express/Savana — 30 Years, 4 Recalls, and the FARS Data Nobody Checked

## News Hook (June 29, 2026)
- **NHTSA Recall 26V399:** 26,541 units of 2025-2026 Chevrolet Express and GMC Savana recalled for loose nut in steering gear assembly
  - Supplier sensor malfunction let 51 steering assemblies leave factory with hand-tight nuts
  - GM knows the 51 serial numbers but can't trace which vans got them → all 26,541 must be inspected
  - No crashes, injuries, or fatalities reported
  - 10 "incidents likely related" per GM
  - Owner notifications mailing August 10, 2026
  - Source: NHTSA, CarBuzz (Jun 29, 2026), Autoblog (Jun 29, 2026)

- **NHTSA Recall 26V345 (earlier June 2026):** 232 units of 2026 Express/Savana for gear selector defect
  - Incorrectly manufactured steering column assembly gave false tactile gear selection feedback
  - Driver could think van is in Park when it isn't → unintended vehicle motion
  - Supplier: Nexteer Automotive, built Feb 11-16, 2026
  - Source: autoevolution, GM Authority

- **Context:** The Express/Savana has been in continuous production since 1996 (model year). Same generation for 30 years. Oldest single-generation vehicle still sold new in America. Outlived its electric successor (BrightDrop, which failed).

## Original Analysis: Recall Frequency vs. Death Rate
The Express/Savana has had 4 recalls since January 2025. Yet its FARS death rate is 2.7x lower than the Ford E-350 (0.92 vs 2.51). The E-350 gets far fewer headlines.

Key insight: recall frequency measures manufacturing quality control. Death rate measures vehicle design safety. These are INDEPENDENT variables. A van can have perfect assembly (no recalls) but terrible crashworthiness. Or, like the Express, it can have sloppy assembly but solid fundamental design.

The Express steering box has been the same since 2017. The design works. The loose nut is a quality escape — 51 parts out of tens of thousands. That's a 0.19% defect rate in the relevant production run.

## Limitations
- FARS data covers 2014-2023, so the specific 2025-2026 recalled vans aren't in this dataset
- Fleet estimates are approximate (industry sales data × average vehicle life)
- The Express's low death rate may partly reflect commercial fleet driving patterns (lower average speed, more urban, professional drivers)
- Recall compliance rate is unknown — some recalled vans may never be fixed

## Strongest Counterargument
"A loose nut in a steering box can kill someone, and four recalls in 18 months suggests systemic quality problems at GM's Wentzville plant." This is valid. The 51 defective assemblies aren't nothing — one undetected loose nut at highway speed on a 5,600-pound van is a potential multi-fatality event. The FARS data shows aggregate safety, not individual recall risk.

## Actionable Takeaway
1. If you own a 2025-2026 Express or Savana: check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls immediately
2. If you're choosing a commercial van: don't let recall headlines drive the decision. The FARS death rate for the Express (0.92) is lower than the Grand Caravan (1.33), Town & Country (1.26), and dramatically lower than the E-350 (2.51). The Ford Transit (0.14) is the segment's safety leader.
3. The Express's 30-year-old design has a better safety record per mile than most minivans. The problems are in assembly, not architecture.
