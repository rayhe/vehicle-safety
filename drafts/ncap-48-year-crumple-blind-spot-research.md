# Research: NHTSA Rated Cars on Crumpling for 48 Years — Never Asked If They Tried Not to Crash

## Journalist: Vin Wreckage (Existential Dread Columnist)
## Kicker: Existential Dread

## Angle
For 48 years (1978–2025), NHTSA's consumer-facing star rating system — the one on every new car sticker — rated vehicles exclusively on crashworthiness: how well the structure protected occupants AFTER impact. It never tested whether the car tried to PREVENT the crash. In 2026, NHTSA finally added crash-prevention tests to NCAP. One car has passed. ONE.

## Self-Critique Gate
**Proposal:** The US government's car safety rating system spent nearly 5 decades optimizing for crumple zone quality while ignoring whether cars tried not to crash — while pedestrian deaths climbed 50%.
**Challenge:** Is this genuinely surprising? Yes — most people assume 5-star safety means "safe" holistically, not "this car crumples well." The gap between consumer perception and what stars actually measure is a genuine blind spot. The timing data (IIHS rated AEB in 2012, NHTSA 2026 = 14-year gap) is original analysis.
**Verdict:** Proceed.

## Primary Sources (3+ required)

### Source 1: NHTSA NCAP Program History
- Launched 1978 under Joan Claybrook (first consumer crash test program worldwide)
- Originally: 35 mph frontal barrier test only
- 1996: Added side impact test
- 2003: Added rollover resistance rating
- 2011: Enhanced with side pole test, improved dummies, overall score
- **2026: First ADAS tests added** — pedestrian AEB, lane keeping, blind spot warning, blind spot intervention
- Tests are pass/fail, not star-rated
- Results currently self-reported by manufacturers; independent NHTSA lab verification starts 2027
- Source: NHTSA NCAP history, Dori Slosberg Foundation timeline, Sidley Environmental Brief

### Source 2: Pedestrian Fatality Data (FARS / GHSA / CDC)
- US pedestrian deaths rose **50% from 2013–2022** while 27 other high-income countries saw a 24.7% DECLINE (CDC MMWR, 2025)
- 2022: 7,508 pedestrian fatalities (near-record)
- 2023: 7,314 pedestrian fatalities
- 2024: 7,148 pedestrian fatalities (4.3% decline, still 20% above 2016)
- 2025 H1: 3,024 pedestrian fatalities (10.9% drop from 2024, but still 2.5% above 2019)
- US has highest pedestrian death rate among high-income countries for ages 15-64
- Source: GHSA annual reports, FARS encyclopedia, CDC MMWR Vol 74 #8

### Source 3: AEB Effectiveness Studies (IIHS / PARTS / AAA)
- AEB reduces rear-end crashes by 49-52% (PARTS study, 2024, 168 models, 21.1M crash reports)
- AEB reduces serious injuries by 53% (same study)
- For pickups specifically: 43% crash reduction, 77% serious injury reduction (IIHS, 2025)
- AEB prevents ~83% of simulated rear-end crashes (CIRP/CHOP study)
- AAA caveat: AEB fails at high speeds and intersections; 83% of 2022 vehicles already equipped
- Pedestrian AEB: newer systems detect pedestrians; effectiveness data still emerging
- Source: IIHS news releases, PARTS/CODOT study, AAA study

### Source 4: IIHS and Euro NCAP Already Rating Crash Prevention
- IIHS began rating Front Crash Prevention (AEB) systems in 2012-2013
- Euro NCAP required AEB for 5-star ratings starting 2014
- NHTSA NCAP didn't test crash prevention until 2026
- **Gap: 14 years** between IIHS rating AEB and NHTSA including it in consumer star ratings
- During this gap: roughly 97,000+ pedestrians killed on US roads (2012-2025)
- Not all preventable by AEB, but the technology was available and rated elsewhere — just invisible in the sticker shoppers see

### Source 5: Tesla Model Y — First (and Only) to Pass
- 2026 Tesla Model Y built on/after Nov 12, 2025
- Passed all 4 new ADAS tests + all 4 traditional crashworthiness tests (8/8)
- Tests: pedestrian AEB, lane keeping assistance, blind spot warning, blind spot intervention
- No other vehicle has passed as of May 2026
- Meanwhile: NHTSA is still investigating Tesla's FSD for low-visibility crashes
- Source: NHTSA announcement, CarBuzz, Automotive Testing Technology International

### Source 6: "Starflation" Problem
- Consumer Federation of America (2018/2020): Most vehicles receive similar star ratings
- "Starflation" reduced consumer ability to differentiate between safe and less-safe vehicles
- A 2015 Nissan Versa and a 2015 Volvo XC90 could both be "5-star" — one had AEB, the other didn't
- Stars measured structural protection, not technology
- Source: Consumer Federation of America report (2020)

## Original Contribution
1. **Timeline cross-reference:** Mapping NCAP's crashworthiness-only era (1978-2025) against pedestrian fatality trends reveals the rating system was perfecting crumple zones during the exact period pedestrian deaths were climbing
2. **Gap calculation:** 14 years between IIHS rating AEB (2012) and NHTSA including it in star ratings (2026). During that window, ~97,000 pedestrians died on US roads.
3. **Consumer perception disconnect:** A "5-star" car from 2020 and a "5-star" car from 2026 have the same rating. One can see a pedestrian and brake. The other can't. The stars never differentiated.

## Limitations
- Cannot directly attribute pedestrian deaths to absence of ADAS star ratings (correlation ≠ causation)
- AEB was becoming standard voluntarily (20 automaker commitment in 2015, achieved by 2022) independent of NCAP rating
- AEB effectiveness drops significantly at night, in intersections, and above 40 mph (AAA)
- NHTSA's 2026 ADAS tests are self-reported by manufacturers; independent verification begins 2027
- Euro NCAP and IIHS use different test protocols — direct comparison has limits
- Pedestrian deaths driven by multiple factors (vehicle size, speed, road design, phone distraction) — not just AEB absence

## Strongest Counterargument
Star ratings were designed to measure structural crashworthiness — a fundamentally different question from crash avoidance technology. Adding ADAS tests to a crash-test program risks confusing consumers by conflating "this car protects you in a crash" with "this car avoids crashes." A car with excellent crumple zones and no AEB may still be the right choice for a buyer who wants structural integrity. The voluntary market solved the AEB problem faster than regulation would have: 99%+ of new cars now have AEB standard. NCAP was never the constraint.

## Actionable Takeaway
- Check whether your current car has AEB: look in owner's manual or check IIHS.org/ratings for your specific model year
- If car shopping: IIHS Top Safety Pick+ requires front crash prevention with AEB — use that rating, not just NHTSA stars
- Know what your stars actually mean: 5 stars = good structural protection in a crash, NOT "this car is maximally safe"
- Check VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls — 7M vehicles currently have unfixed backup camera recalls alone
