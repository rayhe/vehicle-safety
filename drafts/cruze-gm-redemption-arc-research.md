# Research: The Chevy Cruze Redemption Arc

## Angle
The existing "cavalier-gm-lineage" story tells the tragedy: Cavalier (2.43 rate) → Cobalt (5.10 rate, ignition switch scandal). It ends at the disaster. This story tells the untold third act: the Cruze (0.63 rate) — an 8x safety improvement that made it safer than the Civic, Corolla, and virtually every compact sedan in America. Then GM killed it anyway.

## Self-Critique Gate
**Proposed angle:** GM's budget compact went from America's deadliest sedan to one of its safest in two generations, and nobody noticed.
**Challenge:** Is this just a sequel to the Cavalier story? 
**Verdict:** Proceed — the Cavalier story ends at the Cobalt. The Cruze data is unexamined and the comparison to competitors is genuinely surprising. The discontinuation adds a tragic irony dimension.

## Primary Data (FARS 2014-2023)

### The GM Compact Arc
| Vehicle | Deaths | Fleet | Rate/100M VMT | Lethality | Impairment |
|---------|--------|-------|---------------|-----------|------------|
| Cavalier | 1,225 | 437,500 | 2.43 | 0.857 | 22.4% |
| Cobalt | 1,540 | 262,500 | 5.10 | 0.808 | 22.4% |
| Cruze | 638 | 875,000 | 0.63 | 0.563 | 20.2% |

- Rate improvement: Cobalt → Cruze = **8.1x safer**
- Rate improvement: Cavalier → Cruze = **3.9x safer**
- Lethality drop: 0.857 → 0.563 (occupant survival rate went from 14% to 44%)
- Combined body count across all three: **3,403 deaths** from one model slot

## Novel Contribution
Original cross-tabulation: Cruze vs competitor death rates, with impairment normalization showing the gap is purely structural. No published analysis has compared the three-generation GM compact sedan arc with competitor benchmarking.

## Limitations
- Fleet estimates from industry sales data, not registration counts
- VMT estimates from NHTS averages, not actual odometer data
- FARS captures only fatal crashes; the Cruze's injury crash performance is unknown
- Cruze's newer model year distribution (2011-2019) vs competitors with longer histories may bias rate comparisons
- Discontinued vehicles accumulate older fleet, potentially inflating death rates as vehicles age

## Strongest Counterargument
The Cruze's low rate may partly reflect fleet age composition. It was produced 2011-2019, so its fleet skews newer than the Civic or Corolla (which include models from the 1990s-2000s in the fleet). Newer vehicles have better crash avoidance technology (AEB, lane departure warning) that reduces crash frequency independent of structural design. The comparison isn't fully apples-to-apples without controlling for model year.
