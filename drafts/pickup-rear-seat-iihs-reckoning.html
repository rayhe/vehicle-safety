The Gap

26,529 Dead in Trucks That Can't Pass a Back-Seat Crash Test

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
June 3, 2026

Nine large pickup configurations went through IIHS testing for the 2026 award cycle, and exactly two earned anything: a Cybertruck, which got Top Safety Pick+, and a Tundra, which got Top Safety Pick.[1] Not the F-150. Not the Silverado. Not the Ram 1500, not the Sierra, not any variant of any truck from the companies that have dominated American driveways for half a century. Poor. Poor. Poor. Not even tested.

26,529
Deaths in the four trucks that failed or skipped the rear-seat crash test, FARS 2014–2023

IIHS updated its moderate overlap front test in 2022 to measure something that should have been obvious from the beginning: what happens to passengers in the back row when the front corner of a truck collides with another vehicle or a fixed object at an angle. In the F-150, what happens is that rear occupants slide forward beneath their lap belts in a motion engineers call "submarining," which compresses the abdomen while the head strikes the seatback ahead, producing chest-injury and head-injury readings the IIHS characterized as likely rather than merely possible.[2] Ram 1500 dummies recorded the same pattern, and Silverado dummies fared worse still, layering on a Marginal small-overlap rating and Poor headlights to assemble what amounts to the worst crash-test report card of any major truck manufacturer in 2026.

IIHS President David Harkey summarized the wreckage: "Submarining was a problem for all four pickups, and belt forces were too high in all but the Tundra."[2]

Layer the lab results onto a decade of real deaths, and the pattern sharpens into something hard to ignore. FARS data from 2014 through 2023 attributes 9,591 fatalities to Silverados, 9,194 to F-150s, 4,407 to Rams, and 3,337 to Sierras, totaling 26,529 deaths across 56,992 fatal crashes for the four trucks that either failed the moderate overlap test or never submitted to it in the first place.[3] Silverados kill at a rate of 1.25 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, while Tundras, which passed, sit at 0.94. A 33% gap between the truck that earned an award and the truck that leads the body count, and it holds after fleet-size adjustment.

Volume matters as a counterargument, and it deserves its full strength: Ford's F-150 fleet is roughly 6.8 times the Tundra's, which mechanically produces more deaths even if per-mile risk were identical. Ram's rate of 0.78 per 100M VMT actually undercuts the Tundra, making it the safest truck by that metric despite earning Poor on the test that measures rear-passenger protection. A low rate and a Poor crashworthiness score in the same vehicle are not contradictory: they mean the Ram gets into fewer fatal crashes per mile driven but offers worse protection in the crashes it does have, which is its own kind of engineering failure, just one that aggregate statistics can hide if you only look at one number.

Roughly 1.9 million F-150s, Silverados, and Ram 1500s were sold in 2025 alone, vehicles that increasingly serve as family haulers with booster seats strapped into the second row rather than as bare-bones work trucks that rarely carry passengers behind the driver.[4] IIHS created its moderate overlap update specifically to address crashes in which rear passengers are most vulnerable, and every truck from every Big Three manufacturer falls short of a Good rating in precisely that scenario. Toyota managed it. Tesla managed it. Rivian, an EV startup that shipped its first vehicle in 2021, earned an Acceptable on the same test, which is not Good enough for an award but not the wreckage of a Poor, either, placing a three-year-old company closer to protecting rear passengers than corporations that have built trucks since before seat belts were mandatory.[1]

For 2026, a Good rating on moderate overlap is required for either IIHS award, which means this is no longer an asterisk on an otherwise clean record but the single gate that separates a truck you can recommend to a family from one you cannot. Ford knows. GM knows. Stellantis knows. None of them redesigned the rear restraint system in time for the 2026 model year, and 1.9 million buyers walked into dealerships last year without anyone mentioning that a crash test dummy representing their child registered "likely" head and chest injuries.

Limitations

FARS data spans 2014 through 2023, covering multiple generations of each truck, which means the 2026 models tested by IIHS are structurally different from many of the vehicles represented in the fatality counts. Aggregate death rates reflect all crash types and seating positions, not just moderate overlap frontal scenarios. Impairment rates among fatal-crash drivers range from 18.9% for F-150 to 20.6% for Silverado, introducing a behavioral confound that structural engineering alone cannot resolve. Cybertruck lacks sufficient FARS data for any real-world comparison, so its lab performance remains an untested promise on public roads.

What You Should Do

If you carry passengers in a large pickup, only two 2026 models have IIHS awards: Tundra (crew cab) and Cybertruck. Owners of F-150s, Silverados, and Rams should know that rear-seat passengers face elevated frontal crash injury risk as measured by IIHS. Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls for open recalls, and ask your dealer about any planned restraint system updates for the 2027 model year.
