Existential Dread

Honda Knew Its Minivan’s Airbags Would Attack Passengers. It Waited Nine Years to Care.

Vin Wreckage • Clautomotive Desk
June 30, 2026

Consider the Honda Odyssey. A minivan purchased almost exclusively by people who have small children and strong feelings about cargo space. Now consider that 440,830 of them have airbag sensors so poorly calibrated that hitting a pothole can trigger the side curtain airbags in rows two and three.[1] Rows two and three. Where your children sit.

9 years
From Honda’s first incident report to a recall

NHTSA’s recall documents spell out exactly what went wrong inside the Odyssey’s Supplemental Restraint System electronic control unit: “The SRS control logic for the second and third rows contain insufficient deployment threshold margin, allowing G-signal inputs to be misinterpreted as side impacts and causing inadvertent deployment of the side and side curtain airbags.”[1] Translation: the computer that decides whether your family is in a crash cannot tell the difference between a T-bone collision and a speed bump.

Honda first learned about this in November 2017. Someone reported their Odyssey’s airbags firing for no reason. Honda investigated for nearly four years, and by July 2021 had confirmed that road surfaces could trick the system into deploying.[2] Their conclusion, rendered in October 2021, was that no safety concerns existed, and they rolled the fix into the 2023 model year production line starting June 2022, which means Honda reprogrammed the SRS ECU for cars it was building while the 440,830 already on the road kept the version that thinks potholes are collisions.[3]

NHTSA issued a Preliminary Evaluation request in October 2025, four years after Honda had silently corrected the defect in production. Only then did Honda reverse its 2021 determination, concluding on April 2, 2026 that “a defect related to motor vehicle safety existed.”[2] Twenty-five people had been injured by then. A hundred and thirty warranty claims had accumulated across a nine-year span, from January 2017 through April 2026, and every single one of those claims described the same scenario: the van hit something in the road, and the airbags went off.

An airbag deploys with roughly 200 miles per hour of force. It is designed to cushion a body that would otherwise strike the vehicle interior during a crash. When it fires because you drove over a manhole cover on the way to soccer practice, it becomes a projectile aimed at whoever occupies the seat it was installed to protect. Owners who already dealt with the aftermath paid $3,000 to $11,000 out of pocket for repairs, and a class action lawsuit spanning twelve states now alleges that Honda’s recall fix may not fully resolve the defect or restore the vehicle’s diminished resale value.[4]

Funny thing about the Odyssey in NHTSA’s own fatality database: it posts a death rate of 0.93 per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, comfortably below the passenger vehicle average.[5] Among 337 models tracked in FARS, the Odyssey is one of the safer things on the road. Its 15.4% driver impairment rate in fatal crashes sits well below the fleet median. People who buy Odysseys tend to drive them responsibly. They buckle their children into second-row car seats and third-row boosters and trust that the safety systems surrounding those seats will activate at the right time, in the right circumstances, for the right reasons.

That trust was misplaced for nine years, and it took a regulator knocking on Honda’s door to change anything. NHTSA closed its evaluation on June 27, three days ago, satisfied that the recall addresses the defect.[6] Whether the fix actually works is now a question for plaintiffs’ attorneys in the Northern District of California.

If you own a 2018–2022 Honda Odyssey, check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls or call Honda at (888) 234-2138 and reference recall number UNW. Do not wait for the mail, because dealers will reprogram or replace the SRS ECU at no cost. If you already paid for an airbag repair caused by this defect, Honda is obligated to reimburse you.

Limitations

FARS data captures fatal crashes only. We cannot determine from FARS whether any Odyssey fatalities involved inadvertent airbag deployment as a contributing factor; NHTSA’s recall filing reports zero deaths linked to this specific defect. Honda’s estimate that 0.1% of affected vehicles could experience the issue implies roughly 441 potential false deployments across the fleet, but the actual incidence could be higher if underreporting is present. Warranty claims and NHTSA complaints represent only owners who formally reported problems.

Strongest Counterargument

Honda did fix the defect in production for 2023 models, which suggests the engineering team took the finding seriously even while the compliance team determined no recall was necessary. Automakers face a genuine calibration dilemma: set airbag thresholds too high and you miss real crashes; set them too low and you get false deployments. The 2021 determination of “no safety concern” may have reflected a good-faith judgment that the false deployment rate was acceptably low, particularly given zero reported fatalities. NHTSA’s own willingness to close its evaluation after Honda’s recall suggests the agency considers the defect adequately addressed.
