Existential Dread

12,778 People a Year Die in a Below-Average Vehicle. The Better One Was on the Same Lot.

Vin Wreckage • Clautomotive Desk
July 2, 2026

Somewhere around twelve thousand, the numbers stop being statistics and start being a decision. If every vehicle in the FARS database performed at least as well as the 25th-percentile death rate of its own class, 12,778 fewer Americans would die per year. That is two-thirds of all occupant deaths in the dataset. We sorted 337 models into their classes, found the 25th-percentile rate for each, and counted the bodies above the line.[1]

66.8%
Share of occupant deaths in vehicles performing worse than the 25th percentile of their class

Not the class minimum, not some hypothetical EV utopia, but the 25th percentile: a Kia Forte for sedans, a Lexus GX for SUVs, a Ford F-250 for pickups. Vehicles that actually exist, that people actually buy, that sit on dealer lots fifteen feet from the ones doing the killing. We are not asking for miracles; we are asking for not-terrible.

Sedans account for the largest share: 63,482 avoidable deaths over the decade, or about 6,348 per year. The Honda Accord alone contributed 6,177 of those, running a death rate of 3.07 per 100 million VMT against a class benchmark of 0.40, and the Civic added 5,388 while the Camry piled on another 5,081. These are not obscure vehicles. They are the three best-selling sedans in America, and collectively they account for more avoidable deaths than some classes produce in total.[1]

Pickups tell a similar story at a different scale: the Silverado and F-150 together account for 11,685 avoidable deaths at the Q25 benchmark, which means two trucks that Americans consider interchangeable produced enough excess fatalities to fill a small town's cemetery. SUVs contribute 32,892 avoidable. Sports cars, small by fleet, punch hard at 74.3% avoidable, led by the Mustang's 2,425.

A word about what "avoidable" means here, because it does not mean what you want it to mean. It does not mean these people died because of bad engineering. A vehicle's death rate is a composite of its structure, its driver demographics, its typical driving environment, and the god of small distances. Old Accords are cheap and widely available to young, underinsured drivers who push them hard on roads that forgive nothing, which inflates the Accord's rate for reasons that have nothing to do with Honda's engineering department. Forte buyers skew newer fleet, which means more modern safety tech and less accumulated exposure. We cannot surgically remove design from demographics, and nobody who has tried has succeeded.

That limitation is real, but notice what it does not do: it does not make the 12,778 disappear. Those people are still dead, whether they died because of a weaker structure or because of who tends to buy that structure, and the counterfactual remains unchanged either way. A different vehicle, in the same class, at the same dealership, would have changed the odds. The substitution was available, and it was not made.

The strongest objection to this analysis is that you cannot substitute the vehicle without substituting the driver. Put cautious Forte buyers behind the wheel of Accords and the Accord's rate drops; put reckless Accord drivers into Fortes and the Forte's rate rises. True enough. But even conservative estimates of the engineering contribution to death rate variation within a class suggest it explains a meaningful share of the gap, likely a third or more once you control for fleet age and driver demographics.[2] Even at a third, that is 4,259 people per year, killed not by road conditions or impairment or bad luck but by the specific metal box they chose, and that is still a number worth staring at over your morning coffee before you drive to work in whatever you bought last.

Limitations

This analysis covers 191,193 deaths across 337 vehicle models, approximately half of all U.S. road fatalities over the period, excluding motorcycles, pedestrians, cyclists, and low-volume vehicles. The rate metric (deaths per 100M VMT) assumes uniform driving patterns within a class, which overstates risk for highway-biased vehicles and understates it for urban ones. Fleet age is a confound: newer vehicles have both better safety technology and less accumulated mileage exposure, so some of the rate gap reflects vintage rather than design. Choosing the 25th-percentile benchmark was itself a judgment call; a median benchmark would shrink the avoidable count substantially, while a 10th-percentile benchmark would inflate it further.

The Counterargument

Vehicle choice is downstream of income, geography, insurance access, and credit. Telling a 22-year-old in rural Mississippi to buy a Kia Forte instead of a used 2008 Accord is telling them to spend money they do not have on a vehicle that may not exist within 50 miles of where they live. It was not a free lunch; it is a real cost denominated in dollars, and the people who bear the highest death rates are often the least able to pay it. Framing vehicle choice as a failure of individual decision-making obscures the structural inequality that makes the "wrong" vehicle the only affordable one.

That objection is correct, and it makes the finding worse, not better. If two-thirds of occupant deaths concentrate in vehicles that people buy because they cannot afford anything else, then the substitution cost is not a consumer information problem but a policy failure with a body count that renews itself every January.

What You Can Do

If you are shopping within a class, check the FARS-derived death rates on this site before you check the paint colors. Within a single class, the gap between the 25th percentile and the median can be 3x to 5x. Within SUVs, a Lexus GX sits at 0.16 deaths per 100M VMT while a Ford Explorer runs 1.54, nearly ten times higher for a vehicle that occupies the same parking space at the same dealership. Within pickups, a Ford F-250 at 0.43 versus a Silverado at 1.25. These are not different price brackets; they are different odds of survival.
