# Research: The Crossover SUV Lethality Gap

## Angle
Subcompact crossovers (Nissan Kicks, Toyota C-HR, Buick Encore, Ford EcoSport) are classified as "SUVs" but their occupants die at sedan-like rates when crashes turn fatal. The FARS lethality ratio (occupant deaths / fatal crashes involving the vehicle) reveals a 2.3x spread within the SUV class.

## Kill Test
**Is this genuinely newsworthy?** YES. No existing NHTSA or IIHS report cross-tabulates occupant lethality ratio within the SUV class. Buyers choose subcompact crossovers thinking "SUV = safe" but the data shows their crash survivability resembles sedans. After 119 articles, this is a fresh cross-tabulation.

**Novel contribution:** FARS occupant lethality ratio (deaths/crashes) segmented within the SUV class, showing the bottom of the SUV class offers no better crash protection than midsize sedans.

## Primary Sources (3+)

### 1. FARS Data (2014-2023) — Occupant Lethality by SUV Model
- Nissan Kicks: 0.739 lethality (339 deaths / 459 crashes) — 73.9% of fatal crashes kill the Kicks occupant
- Toyota C-HR: 0.690 (109 / 158)
- Buick Encore: 0.609 (341 / 560)
- Ford EcoSport: 0.606 (86 / 142)
- **vs. midsize/large SUVs:**
- Audi Q5: 0.322 (47 / 146) — only 32.2% kill the Q5 occupant
- Mercedes ML-Class: 0.325
- GMC Acadia: 0.343
- Toyota Land Cruiser: 0.347
- BMW X3: 0.358
- Spread: 0.739 / 0.322 = 2.3x within the same "SUV" class

### 2. IIHS Vehicle Size & Weight Study (2025)
- Weight benefits plateau at ~4,000 lbs
- Below that threshold: each 500 lbs = 17-point reduction in driver death rate
- Above that threshold: no additional benefit for the driver, and partner vehicle death rate increases
- Subcompact crossovers typically weigh 2,800-3,300 lbs — well below the benefit threshold
- Quote: "What this analysis shows is that choosing an extra-heavy vehicle doesn't make you any safer, but it makes you a bigger danger to other people" — Sam Monfort, IIHS senior statistician

### 3. IIHS Side Crash Test Results
- Midsize SUVs performed significantly better than small SUVs in updated side crash test (82% more energy)
- Side impacts account for 23% of passenger vehicle deaths

## Methodology
Lethality ratio = (vehicle occupant deaths in FARS) / (total FARS crashes involving the vehicle)
- FARS only records fatal crashes (at least 1 death per crash)
- Low lethality means the vehicle's occupant survived while someone else died
- High lethality means the vehicle's occupant is likely the one who died
- This is a proxy for crashworthiness + mass protection

## Strongest Counterargument
Subcompact crossover owners may drive differently, in different environments (urban, lower speed), and face different crash partners than full-size SUV owners. The lethality difference could partially reflect driver demographics and driving context, not just vehicle protection. Additionally, FARS only captures fatal crashes — a subcompact crossover might prevent many crashes from becoming fatal in the first place (via active safety tech), leaving only the worst crashes in the dataset.

## Limitations
- FARS captures fatal crashes only (36,000/yr vs ~6.7M total crashes)
- Lethality ratio conflates vehicle protection with driving environment, speed, and crash partner mass
- Fleet estimates use VMT approximations, not actual registrations
- Small crossovers tend to be newer models with more active safety tech — could bias toward fewer total crashes in FARS
- Some models (EcoSport, C-HR) have smaller sample sizes
