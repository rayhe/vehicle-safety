# Research: IIHS Tested the Wrong Seat

## News Hook (June 17, 2026)
IIHS released first-ever commercial vehicle safety ratings. 9 vehicles tested for 5 driver-protection criteria. 5 of 9 failed.

Source: https://www.autoblog.com/news/americas-biggest-pickups-just-got-their-first-iihs-safety-check-and-some-failed
Source: https://www.iihs.org/news/detail/lets-prioritize-safety-in-the-vehicles-that-power-our-economy
Source: autoevolution.com (June 17, 2026)

## IIHS Results — First Commercial Vehicle Ratings

### 5 Criteria Tested
1. Front airbags
2. Side airbags
3. Seatbelt pretensioners
4. Seatbelt force limiters
5. Seatbelt reminders (IIHS standard: 90+ seconds, loud + visible; federal standard: 4-8 seconds)

### PASSED all 5 (4 vehicles)
- 2026 Chevrolet Silverado 3500HD (Class 3 pickup)
- 2025 Ford F-350 SuperCrew (Class 3 pickup)
- Chevrolet BrightDrop 400 (cargo van)
- Ram ProMaster 2500 (cargo van)

### FAILED (5 vehicles)
- Ram 3500 — failed seatbelt reminder
- Ford T250 Transit — failed seatbelt reminder
- Mercedes-Benz Sprinter 2500 — failed seatbelt reminder
- Rivian Delivery 500 — failed seatbelt reminder
- Chevrolet Express 2500 — failed BOTH seatbelt reminder AND force limiters (worst performer)

### Key Context
- IIHS persistent reminders increase belt usage 30%
- Federal rules: 4-8 seconds of chime. IIHS standard: 90 seconds minimum
- 6,535 killed in 2023 in crashes involving heavy/medium trucks or light vans (16% of all US road fatalities)
- This number doubled over past 7 years
- 160,000+ injuries in similar crashes in 2023
- Most people who die in truck crashes are OTHER road users (IIHS VP David Kidd)
- Future phases: headlights, AEB, and expansion to Class 4-6

## Original Analysis — The Wrong Seat

### The Core Irony
All 5 criteria IIHS tested protect the OCCUPANT of the commercial vehicle. But FARS data shows the occupant is NOT the one who typically dies in these crashes.

### The Argument
When a Ford Transit is in a fatal crash, the Transit driver survives 69% of the time. Someone else dies.

IIHS tested whether Transit drivers have seatbelt reminders. Important, sure. But the people who actually die in Transit crashes are pedestrians, cyclists, and occupants of smaller vehicles.

The technologies that would save the MOST lives are:
- AEB (crash avoidance — prevents the crash entirely)
- Front-end design (height, aggressiveness)
- Side underride guards (on semis)

IIHS's own David Kidd said: "most people who die in truck crashes are other road users." Then they tested... driver seatbelts.

### Strongest Counterargument
IIHS started with the easiest, cheapest checks (is this equipment present? yes/no) because it takes months to set up crash and track testing for vehicles of this weight class. Occupant protection was the low-hanging fruit. AEB track testing and crashworthiness testing are planned for later phases. You have to start somewhere.

### Limitations
- FARS data groups all Silverados/Sierras/Rams together (can't isolate 3500 HD specifically in most cases)
- "Other-kill rate" counts fatal crash involvements vs occupant deaths — it's a proxy, not a direct "who died" mapping
- Some "other" deaths include pedestrians, cyclists, and passengers in the commercial vehicle (not just opposing vehicle occupants)
- The IIHS plans AEB and headlight tests later — they know occupant protection is only part of the picture
