# Research: CT5 Five Stars, Four Poors

## Angle
The 2026 Cadillac CT5 simultaneously holds a NHTSA 5-star overall safety rating and "poor" IIHS ratings in four categories. Same car. Two U.S. safety rating systems. Contradictory conclusions. GM's corporate response literally weaponized the 5-star rating to dismiss the IIHS results.

## Novel Contribution
This isn't just "two systems disagree" — it's about how the disagreement creates a consumer information crisis. The two systems test differently (barrier weight, speed, scoring methodology — relative vs absolute), and automakers exploit the gap.

### Source 4: NHTSA Star Rating Methodology
- Stars are RELATIVE within class and ±250 lb weight band. Not absolute.
- NHTSA's own website: "A vehicle can have a high star rating, but still have a safety concern."
- 98% of all new cars receive either 4 or 5 stars (Center for Auto Safety critique)
- URL: https://www.nhtsa.gov/equipment/car-seats-and-booster-seats/ease-use-ratings

### Source 5: IIHS vs NHTSA Test Methodology Differences
- IIHS updated side crash test: 4,200 lb barrier at 37 mph (simulates midsize SUV impact)
- NHTSA side test: lighter barrier, lower speed
- IIHS scores against absolute performance thresholds (good/acceptable/marginal/poor)
- NHTSA scores relative to other vehicles in same class/weight
- IIHS is insurance-funded (wants to reduce claims); NHTSA is government-funded (regulatory capture concerns)

### Source 6: FARS Data (Cadillac context)
- Cadillac CTS (predecessor platform): 265 deaths, 1.32/100M VMT
- Cadillac Seville: 391 deaths, 3.89/100M VMT  
- Cadillac ATS: 55 deaths, 0.36/100M VMT
- For comparison — Lexus IS: 218 deaths, 0.87/100M VMT; BMW 3 Series: 1,237 deaths, 2.73/100M VMT
- CT5 too new for meaningful FARS data, but Cadillac sedans generally have moderate-to-high fatality rates

## Strongest Counterargument
NHTSA 5-star has been the US government's flagship safety rating since 1994. It covers frontal crash, side crash, AND rollover — and it's based on probability of injury. The CT5 genuinely performs well in the specific tests NHTSA runs. IIHS uses newer, more demanding test protocols that go beyond federal requirements. The "disagreement" isn't contradictory — it's measuring different things at different thresholds.

## Limitations
- CT5 too new for FARS fatality data (CT5 launched 2019, data through 2023)
- We're comparing test performance, not real-world outcomes
- NHTSA has updated its program since the Center for Auto Safety critique
- IIHS is insurance-industry funded and has its own institutional incentives

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy after 499 articles? YES. We've never done a piece directly comparing the two rating systems on a single vehicle, and GM's response — weaponizing the 5-star to dismiss the IIHS results — is a corporate communication case study. The fact that a luxury sedan can simultaneously hold the highest and lowest possible safety ratings is genuinely paradoxical.

## Actionable Takeaways
1. Never rely on a single rating system — check BOTH NHTSA and IIHS
2. NHTSA stars are relative (4-star sedan could be safer than 5-star truck in different class)
3. IIHS tests are more demanding — if shopping for a sedan, the IIHS side impact test is more representative of real-world SUV-hits-sedan crashes
4. GM specifically redesigned the CT5's styling without improving its crash structure — a $46K luxury car with a cosmetic facelift
