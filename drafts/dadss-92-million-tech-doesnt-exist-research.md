# Research: $92 Million and 18 Years Later, the Tech That Was Supposed to End Drunk Driving Doesn't Exist

## Journalist: Dale Impactor III (impairment/toxicology beat)

## Angle
Congress mandated (Section 24220, Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act 2021) that all new cars must have passive alcohol/impairment detection technology by November 2024. NHTSA missed the deadline. After 18 years and $91.7 million spent on a single nonprofit (ACTS/DADSS program since 2008), NHTSA told Congress in February 2026 that "there are no in-vehicle technologies in production that can measure BAC or BrAC above 0.08 g/dL passively." The extension pushes to November 2027, but NHTSA's own language is indefinite: "We'll let you know when it can be done."

## Primary Sources

### 1. Section 24220 of Public Law 117-58 (Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act)
- Signed Nov 15, 2021
- Requires NHTSA to issue FMVSS requiring "advanced drunk and impaired driving prevention technology"
- Deadline: 3 years from enactment (Nov 15, 2024)
- Extension clause: up to 3 additional years → Nov 15, 2027
- Technology must work "passively" — no directed breath, no breathalyzer-style interaction
- Must "prevent or limit motor vehicle operation if an impairment is detected"

### 2. NHTSA Congressional Report (February 2026)
- "There are no in-vehicle technologies in production that can measure BAC or BrAC above 0.08 g/dL passively"
- "The current DADSS breath sensor requires directed puff of breath toward the sensor and would therefore not be considered passive under BIL"
- "The technology has not yet been implemented on production vehicles offered for sale to the public"
- Open-ended timeline: "NHTSA will continue to provide annual reports to Congress"

### 3. USAspending.gov — NHTSA → ACTS Cooperative Agreements

- Total: $91,687,500 across 22 transactions

- ACTS members: 15 automakers — the same companies that will be regulated

## The False Positive Math (Original Contribution)
- ~280 million registered vehicles in the US (FHWA)
- ~227 million licensed drivers (FHWA)
- Average trip frequency: ~3.2 trips/day per driver (NHTS)
- That's ~726 million vehicle start events per day
- At 99.9% accuracy: 726,000 false lockouts per day
- At 99.99% accuracy: 72,600 false lockouts per day
- At 99.999% accuracy: 7,260 false lockouts per day
- Even at "five nines" accuracy, 7,260 sober drivers per day are immobilized
- For context: ~30 drunk driving deaths per day
- The technology would have to reach six nines (99.9999%) to drop false lockouts below the daily drunk driving death count
- No passive biometric system in any field has achieved this accuracy at scale

## The Structural Conflict
- NHTSA pays ACTS → ACTS is funded by 15 automakers → those same automakers are the regulated parties
- Public-private partnership where the regulated industry controls the research timeline
- 2013 DOT announcement: "NHTSA and ACTS are contributing a combined total of $6,539,400" (industry matching)
- 18 years of funding, no production-ready technology
- Compare: airbags took ~10 years from mandate to universal adoption. ABS ~15 years. ESC ~8 years from mandate to requirement.

## The Cost-Per-Life Math
- $91.7M spent / 0 lives saved by DADSS technology = undefined (no technology deployed)
- ~10,000+ alcohol-impaired deaths per year × 18 years = ~180,000+ deaths while DADSS was in development
- Ignition interlocks: ~$100/install, 67% reduction in recidivism, ~80% non-compliance (covered in article #284)
- If every convicted DUI offender had an interlock: ~$47M/year × 67% reduction = thousands of lives saved
- The DADSS money could have funded ignition interlock subsidies for every convicted offender in America for multiple years
