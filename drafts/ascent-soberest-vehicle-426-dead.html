Sobriety Report

The 10 Soberest Vehicles in Fatal Crashes Are All Family Cars. 4,079 People Died in Them.

Dale Impactor III • Clautomotive Desk
July 13, 2026

Run the FARS toxicology database through a ranking algorithm and sort every vehicle by impairment rate. At the very bottom sits the Subaru Ascent. Just 8.2% of Ascent drivers in fatal crashes tested positive for alcohol or drugs, against a national average of roughly 20%.[1] Below the Ascent: nothing. This is the soberest vehicle in America.

8.2%
Ascent impairment rate in fatal crashes. National average: ~20%.

Four hundred and twenty-six people died in one.

Widen the lens and the ranking stops being about a single Subaru. Nine other vehicles cluster near the bottom, all below 16% impairment, all with 200 or more tested drivers: Land Cruiser at 8.9%, Ford Transit at 13.6%, Nissan Quest at 14.5%, Dodge Grand Caravan at 15.3%, Honda Odyssey at 15.4%, Lexus GX at 15.4%.[1] Every one is a family hauler: three-row crossovers, minivans, and premium SUVs built for car-pool lanes and college move-in weekends, the kind of vehicles that come pre-loaded with Cheerio crumbs and orthodontic copay anxiety. Combined body count across the 10 soberest models: approximately 4,079 dead, with an average impairment rate of 13.4%.

Those are not impairment fatalities. Those are sober parents in sober vehicles running sober errands who ran out of luck, engineering, or both, and whose toxicology reports read cleaner than most job applicants’.

Flip to the other end and the demographics invert completely. Buick Park Avenue hits 31.7% impaired, Chevy C/K pickup lands at 28%, and the Audi A3 clocks 27.1%.[1] Impairment tracks demographics rather than engineering: old and cheap on one end, young and aspirational on the other, which nobody needed a federal database to confirm. What the Ascent ranking actually proves, and what the industry has not reckoned with, is that sobriety does not track survival.

NHTSA estimates impaired driving accounts for roughly 31% of all traffic fatalities, which means 69% of people who die on American roads are sober.[2] For Ascent occupants, that number is 91.8%: sober, strapped in, doing nothing wrong that a blood test can measure, and still dead at the scene. If behavior were the whole story, minivans would be indestructible and three-row Subarus would never appear in a coroner’s report. When the driver isn’t drunk and isn’t high and still dies, what remains is crash structure, vehicle mass, impact speed, and road design, fixable problems that require money and the political appetite to admit that “just don’t drink and drive” covers barely a third of the dead.

What You Can Do

If you’re shopping for a family vehicle, impairment rates will not help you choose one because your family almost certainly isn’t driving impaired. Look at IIHS crash test ratings and per-VMT fatality rates instead.[3] The Ascent’s 0.78 deaths per 100 million miles is quite low, but check the vehicle you’re replacing at nhtsa.gov/recalls before assuming the car keeps you safe because you do.
