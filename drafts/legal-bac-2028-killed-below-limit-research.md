# Research: 2,028 Killed by Legally Sober Drivers in 2024

## Article #316 — Dale Impactor III — Sobriety Report

## Angle
In 2024, 2,028 people were killed in crashes where a driver had a BAC of .01 to .07 g/dL — below the legal limit in 49 states. These drivers were legally permitted to drive. Utah is the only state with a .05 BAC limit. NHTSA's own study proved Utah's law reduced fatal crashes by 19.8%. The NTSB recommended .05 nationally in 2013 — 13 years ago. A peer-reviewed meta-analysis estimates 1,790 lives per year would be saved if all states adopted .05. Zero states have followed Utah.

## Kill Test
- Is this genuinely surprising? YES — most people don't know 2,028 people died from "legal" drivers
- Novel angle on data? YES — connecting the 2,028 low-BAC number to Utah effectiveness data and the 1,790 lives/year estimate. Nobody has drawn this triangle.
- Distinct from existing Crash Report articles? YES — checked 313 existing articles, none dedicated to .05 BAC / low-BAC death toll

- No impact on alcohol sales, tax revenues, or tourism

## Novel Calculation
- 2,028 deaths at BAC .01-.07 in 2024
- If .05 BAC were national law, the .05-.07 BAC range would be captured
- The meta-analysis estimate of 1,790 lives/year aligns with this — these are approximately the lives in the .05-.07 window
- 13 years × ~1,790 = ~23,270 people who might be alive if NTSB's 2013 recommendation had been adopted
- That's a medium-sized American city's worth of preventable deaths

## Limitations to Acknowledge
- The 2,028 includes all BAC .01-.07, not just .05-.07; a .05 law wouldn't capture .01-.04 deaths
- NHTSA imputes BAC values when not reported — introduces uncertainty
- Utah has unique demographics (lower baseline drinking) — effect might be smaller elsewhere
- Deterrent effect (people choosing not to drive) may matter more than direct arrests at .05-.07
- Correlation vs causation: Utah's improvement could partly reflect other concurrent changes

## Strongest Counterargument
The .05 BAC threshold is effectively a "two-drink limit" for many people. Criminalizing behavior at this level could overwhelm courts, disproportionately affect lower-income drivers who can't afford rideshare, and set a precedent for even lower limits (.02, .00). The hospitality industry argues it would deter dining out and hurt restaurants. AND: police enforcement bandwidth is already stretched — cops are arresting fewer DUI offenders at .08 than they should; lowering the threshold they can't meet makes the problem theoretical rather than practical.

## Actionable Takeaways
1. Check whether your state legislature has a .05 BAC bill pending
2. The "two-drink" mental model is wrong — impairment begins below .05 for many people
3. If you're driving after any alcohol: rideshare. Not because it's illegal, but because the FARS data says you're 3-5x more likely to be in a fatal crash at .05 than at .00
4. Utah residents: your law is working — 19.8% fewer fatal crashes, no impact on alcohol sales
