# Research Notes: NHTSA Called AVs a "Danger" — 23 Days After Gutting Crash Tracking

## News Hook (July 9, 2026)
NHTSA Administrator Jonathan Morrison published a letter calling autonomous vehicles a "danger to the general public" for interfering with first responders. Key quotes:
- "This is unacceptable. To state it bluntly: an AV that cannot safely interact with first responders is a danger to the general public."
- Documented "a clear pattern" of AVs driving "directly into active emergency scenes, blocked the paths of ambulances and firefighters, or failed to recognize and respond to basic safety conditions like flashing lights, flares, smoke, fire, and traffic cones."
- "We expect you to prioritize first responder interactions and will schedule meetings with driverless automated driving system developers by month's end"
- Emergency scenes called "neither rare nor extreme edge cases"
- "NHTSA has already been active in this space and will continue to exercise our enforcement authority"

Source: NHTSA letter via CarBuzz, July 9, 2026

## Context: The Data Gap
NHTSA gutted its Standing General Order (SGO) for AV crash reporting on June 16, 2026 — just 23 days before this letter. Our article #? covered this: "NHTSA Tracked Every Robotaxi Crash for Four Years. On June 16, It Stops."

The SGO (issued June 2021) required:
- Every AV crash reported within 24 hours
- All manufacturers/operators of ADS + Level 2 ADAS
- Built the only comprehensive AV crash dataset in existence

The amended version (effective June 16, 2026):
- Gutted reporting triggers, timeline, and scope
- No more mandatory 24-hour reporting
- Narrowed what counts as a reportable crash

## Data Points
- 16 Tesla crashes into first responder/road maintenance vehicles (NHTSA data)
- 273 ADAS-related crashes documented by NHTSA — 69.6% involved Tesla
- 130 ADS crashes reported (Waymo, Cruise, Transdev, others)
- Tesla FSD: 363,000 vehicles recalled for running red lights (26V-__, July 2026)
- 29 million Tesla vehicles under investigation for FSD (NHTSA, July 2026)
- 14 crashes, 10 injuries, 23 people hurt — six cases of running red lights at same Maryland intersection
- Waymo: claims 35,000 first responders trained, 50K+ emergency interactions/week in CA alone
- NHTSA: "at least 11 [ADS crashes] involved a vulnerable road user"

## The Paradox (Original Contribution)
NHTSA simultaneously:
1. Dismantled the crash reporting system that documented AV problems (June 16)
2. Called AVs dangerous based on data that system collected (July 9)
3. Promised "enforcement authority" without the data infrastructure to enforce

The 23-day gap is the story. You can't regulate what you stopped measuring.

## Counterargument (at full strength)
The SGO amendment wasn't a full repeal — some reporting still exists. AV companies argue that self-reporting through telematics is more comprehensive than the SGO framework anyway. Waymo's 50,000+ weekly emergency interactions with a near-zero incident rate suggests the system works most of the time. The letter may represent a regulator working through normal channels, not contradicting itself.

## Limitations
- We don't have the full text of Morrison's letter, only press coverage
- The "23 days" timeline is approximate based on SGO amendment effective date
- The 273 ADAS crashes figure is from earlier NHTSA data release, not necessarily current
- Tesla's dominance in crash stats may reflect better telematics reporting, not worse safety

## Actionable Insight
If you share the road with driverless vehicles: know your emergency rights. In every state, impeding emergency vehicles carries fines of $100-$1,000 and possible jail time. AVs aren't exempt from traffic law, but you can't cite software. If an AV blocks an ambulance near you, document it (dashcam, timestamp) and file with NHTSA at nhtsa.gov/report-a-safety-problem.
