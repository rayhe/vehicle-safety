Ford Has Recalled the Expedition’s Seat Belts Three Times. The Chemistry Was Wrong the Whole Time.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
June 4, 2026

A seat belt pretensioner has one job. When you hit something, a small pyrotechnic charge fires in the first ten milliseconds, yanking the belt tight against your body before the laws of physics turn you into a projectile. The charge sits dormant for years, waiting for the worst moment of your life, and then it performs. That is the entire point of the device. Ford has now recalled the Expedition and Navigator’s pretensioners three times in 28 months because the charge keeps performing when nobody asked it to.[1]

Owners describe what happens next in remarkably consistent language across NHTSA complaints: a percussive bang, the belt cinching with enough force to feel like a fist to the sternum, then acrid smoke curling from somewhere behind the B-pillar trim.[2] No crash. No warning, beyond an airbag malfunction light that may or may not flicker before the detonation. The belt locks rigid, unable to retract or extend. Your pretensioner, which exists to protect you in the most violent fraction of a second you will ever experience, has instead decided to fire during your Tuesday commute and leave you strapped into a seat with a belt that no longer functions as designed.

Recall math tells its own story here, and it is not a story about a company that understood what it was dealing with.

Recall one landed in February 2024: NHTSA campaign 24V099, covering 77,574 Expeditions and Navigators from model years 2018 through 2020, all built within a nine-month production window ending June 2019.[3] Ford’s root cause theory pointed to moisture: water from the HVAC drain migrating into the pretensioner assembly, corroding the squib pins where the bridge wire welds to the pyrotechnic detonator. Fix the drainage, replace the affected pretensioners, problem solved. Eleven people had already reported injuries by then, mostly torso bruising from the rapid tightening, the kind of injury that comes from a safety device attacking you while you are sitting in traffic on a Wednesday afternoon.

Thirteen months later came recall two: campaign 25V197 arrived in March 2025 because Ford’s Critical Concern Review Group noticed something uncomfortable: vehicles outside the original recall population were blowing their pretensioners, too.[4] Production windows stretched from nine months to twenty-two, and affected vehicles climbed to 105,250. Consumer Reports noted dryly that while Ford initially blamed a water leak, “the problem may be with the pretensioners themselves.”[5] Seventeen more dealer reports followed. Vehicles that had already been serviced under the first recall might still need work under the second. Scope crept wider, production windows crept longer, and the root cause crept further from certainty.

Then came recall three: campaign 26V344, filed June 3, 2026, covering 419,967 vehicles, spanning model years 2018 through 2022 and a production window that stretches sixty-five months from May 2017 to October 2022.[1] Affected population: 5.4 times larger than the first recall and 4.0 times larger than the second. Ford has finally identified what it believes is the real root cause, and it was never primarily about moisture: a specific combination of propellant and stabilizer inside the pretensioner degrades in high-heat environments, producing a corrosive byproduct that triggers the open circuit and the inadvertent detonation.[6]

Propellant chemistry was the problem all along. Not drainage. Not weld quality. A chemical mixture sealed inside a pyrotechnic device that Ford bolted behind the seat belt retractor of every full-size SUV rolling off the Kentucky Truck Plant for five and a half years. A new micro gas generator propellant and stabilizer combination resolves the issue, per Ford’s testing from January through May 2026.

But the fix is not available yet. Interim notification letters go out the week of June 8. Actual remedy parts will not reach dealerships until the window between August 31 and September 4, 2026.[6] Three months. In that window, 419,967 Expeditions and Navigators will accumulate an estimated 1.3 billion vehicle-miles-traveled, based on the Expedition’s average annual VMT. At this model’s FARS fatality rate of 2.31 per 100 million VMT, roughly 30 of those vehicles will be involved in a fatal crash during the exposure period.[7] Not all of those crashes will involve defective pretensioners engaging or failing to engage. But every occupant in every one of those crashes is now strapped into a seat where the belt may or may not do what it was engineered to do in the first ten milliseconds.

What this recall expansion ratio reveals is not unique to Ford or to pretensioners, but the 5.4x growth from first recall to third is a particularly vivid illustration of a systemic NHTSA pattern: manufacturers scope recalls narrowly, test their initial hypothesis, discover the hypothesis was wrong, and widen. Repeat until the chemistry catches up with the bureaucracy. NHTSA closed its preliminary evaluation after the first recall, noting it “reserves the right to take additional action if warranted by future circumstances.”[2] Future circumstances arrived twice, and the root cause changed entirely.

Limitations: FARS data covers 2014 through 2023; 2018-2022 model year Expeditions contribute relatively few fatalities (33 total for those years in FARS), which means the per-model-year rate is estimated from the broader Expedition fleet. Our 1.3 billion VMT exposure estimate assumes average driving patterns and does not account for vehicles already parked or owners who voluntarily stop driving. Because the pretensioner defect does not cause crashes but rather compromises protection during them, quantifying excess risk requires assumptions about crash distribution that FARS does not directly provide.

Strongest counterargument: Ford identified the root cause, developed a new propellant formulation, and expanded the recall to cover every possibly affected vehicle rather than waiting for more complaints to trickle in. A three-month delay is a parts supply constraint, not indifference. And the defect produces a warning (the locked belt) that is impossible for an occupant to miss, meaning drivers know their protection is compromised before any crash occurs. All true. But this counterargument assumes that each of the 419,967 owners will notice the airbag light, recognize its meaning, correctly interpret a locked belt as a pretensioner failure rather than a jammed mechanism, and then choose not to drive until August. That is a lot of assumptions about human behavior in exchange for three months of patience.

What to do: Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls or call Ford at 866-436-7332, referencing recall 26S34. If you own a 2018-2022 Expedition or Navigator, you are in the population regardless of whether you received letters for the earlier recalls. If your belt locks or your airbag light illuminates, do not drive the vehicle. Your pretensioner has likely already fired. You cannot reset it yourself, and the belt will not protect you in a crash.
