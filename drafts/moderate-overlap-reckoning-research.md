# Research: The IIHS Moderate Overlap Reckoning

## Angle
The IIHS updated its moderate overlap front crash test for 2026, now requiring "Good" for any safety award. The test specifically evaluates rear-seat passenger protection — a first. America's bestselling vehicles failed catastrophically: F-150 (POOR), Silverado (POOR), Ram 1500 (POOR). No minivans passed. No small pickups passed. FARS death data confirms what the test lab just quantified.

## Primary Sources

### IIHS 2026 Award Criteria Changes
- Source: IIHS, carpro.com (Apr 2, 2026)
- Updated moderate overlap front test now requires "Good" for ANY award (was "Acceptable" for base TSP)
- Test specifically measures rear-seat occupant protection in frontal collisions
- New vehicle-to-vehicle 2.0 crash prevention test (up to 43 mph, motorcycles, semitrailers)
- Pedestrian AEB must be standard, not optional
- 63 vehicles earned awards (45 TSP+, 18 TSP)
- SUVs dominate: 47 of 63 winners (75%)
- Zero minivans, zero minicars, zero small pickups

### IIHS Ratings — Moderate Overlap Front (Updated Test)

- **Chevrolet Silverado 1500 Crew Cab: POOR** (also MARGINAL on small overlap)
- **Ram 1500 Crew Cab: POOR** (also MARGINAL on v2v 2.0 standard system)
- **GMC Sierra 1500: Not yet tested for moderate overlap** (same GM platform as Silverado — MARGINAL on small overlap)

## Novel Analysis
1. **FARS death toll for moderate-overlap-POOR vehicles**: F-150 + Silverado + Ram 1500 = at minimum 19,499 deaths (using FARS model entries). Including Dodge RAM: 23,906. Including Sierra (expected POOR): 27,243. These vehicles got the worst possible rating on a test that measures whether passengers survive the most common type of highway crash.
2. **Comparison with TSP+ winners**: The average death rate for 2026 IIHS TSP+ SUVs with FARS data (RAV4, Rogue, Equinox, Santa Fe, Highlander, CR-V) is ~0.35/100M VMT. The average for POOR-rated trucks is ~1.09. A 3.1× gap.
3. **The rear-seat irony**: These are family vehicles marketed for their size and safety. Many carry children in the back seat. The test they just failed specifically measures rear-seat protection.

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? **Yes.** The IIHS criteria change is from April 2, 2026 — 24 days old. The moderate overlap test failure for all major trucks is a genuine surprise. And nobody has cross-referenced IIHS 2026 POOR ratings with FARS occupant death tolls to show the real-world cost of what the lab just measured.

## Actionable Takeaways
- If shopping for a truck: Toyota Tundra or Cybertruck are the only ones with safety awards
- Check IIHS 2026 ratings before buying — many "safe" vehicles from 2025 lost their status
- Rear-seat passengers (especially children): ask what your vehicle scored on the moderate overlap test
- The updated test matters because moderate overlap crashes are among the most common on highways

## Strongest Counterargument
The IIHS moderate overlap test is NEW and uses different standards than before. Past IIHS awards for these trucks reflected genuinely good performance on the OLD criteria. The trucks didn't suddenly get worse — the test got harder. Also, FARS rates reflect ALL model years (1998-2023 F-150s), not just the current generation that was tested.

## Limitations
- FARS data covers 2014-2023 and includes ALL model years, not just the current generation tested by IIHS
- FARS death rates are for the vehicle's occupants, not specifically rear-seat occupants
- The moderate overlap test is new — we can't directly correlate IIHS moderate overlap POOR to a specific increase in FARS deaths
- Fleet size differences mean raw death counts aren't directly comparable (F-150 fleet: 6.6M vs Tundra: 962K)
