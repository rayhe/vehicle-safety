Traffic Deaths Dropped 12% in 2025. Thank a 14-Year-Old Car for Dying.

Axle McScatter • Clautomotive Desk
April 14, 2026

The National Safety Council just announced 37,810 traffic deaths in 2025, a 12% decline from 2024, even as Americans drove 0.9% more miles.[1] The headlines credit speed cameras, safety campaigns, and "sustained efforts." Congratulations to everyone involved. Except the actual cause is a machine, not a movement: America's deadliest cars are aging into junkyards, and the ones replacing them have electronic stability control.

34.6%
Fewer deaths in the kill zone when ESC-era vehicles replaced pre-ESC ones

Run the FARS model year data through the death-by-vehicle-age distribution, and the curve tells you everything. Deaths don't peak in new vehicles. They don't peak in 30-year-old beaters. They peak at age 14, with 11,363 fatalities concentrated in vehicles between 11 and 17 years old.[2] Old enough to lack modern safety features. Young enough to still be on the road every day. Cheap enough to be someone's only option.

In 2025, that kill zone targets model years 2008 through 2014. Something important happened during those years: NHTSA's FMVSS 126 mandate kicked in, requiring electronic stability control on all new light vehicles by model year 2012.[3] Many manufacturers started installing ESC voluntarily from 2008 onward. So the cohort now entering the deadliest age bracket is the first one with near-universal ESC.

FARS data makes the effect quantifiable. The pre-ESC kill zone cohort (model years 2001 through 2007) accumulated 71,778 deaths. The transitional and post-ESC cohort (model years 2008 through 2014) accumulated 46,914 deaths. That is 24,864 fewer deaths. A 34.6% reduction.[2] IIHS predicted ESC would cut all fatal crashes by roughly 33%.[4] The actual number landed at 34.6%. The model worked.

And 57.6% of all FARS deaths came from vehicles built before 2008. These are the cars bleeding out of the fleet right now, each one dragging the national death count down as it gets scrapped. California's stunning 40% decline starts making more sense when you consider the state's aggressive emissions inspections push older vehicles off the road faster than Mississippi's do.

Celebrate the 12% drop. But understand what it is: a one-time structural dividend. Once pre-ESC vehicles finish aging out of the fleet, the improvement stops. The next step-change depends on automatic emergency braking, which became standard on most new vehicles in 2023 and faces a federal mandate by 2029.[5] AEB-equipped vehicles won't enter the kill zone in force until approximately 2037. We are in the intermission between safety revolutions.

If you drive a vehicle built before 2012, check whether it has ESC. Many 2007 through 2011 models were equipped voluntarily. If yours was not, you are driving a statistically different machine from every vehicle in the parking lot built after it. Not a little different. 34.6%-more-likely-to-die different.
