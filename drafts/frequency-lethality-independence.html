Crash Frequency Explains 3% of Whether You’ll Die

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
April 18, 2026

The Ford E-350 van shows up in 72.1 fatal crashes per 10,000 registered vehicles, making it the most crash-prone vehicle in the FARS database by a wide margin. But only 41% of those crashes actually kill someone inside it. Meanwhile, the Saturn S Series barely appears at all—10.5 crashes per 10,000 fleet—and 92% of its crashes are lethal.

r² = 3.3%
Crash frequency explains this much of whether a crash kills you. Engineering explains most of the rest.

Run a Pearson correlation across 272 vehicles with fleets exceeding 100,000 and at least 50 recorded crashes in FARS, and the number comes back at r = 0.18.[1] Square that for the coefficient of determination: 0.033. Crash frequency—how often a vehicle model appears in the federal fatal crash database—explains 3.3% of the variance in crash lethality, which is the probability that any given crash will produce a death. The remaining 96.7% comes from somewhere else entirely, and that somewhere is predominantly structural engineering, vehicle mass, and occupant protection design.[2]

This matters because every standard vehicle death rate—deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, the metric NHTSA and insurance companies rely on—multiplies frequency by lethality into a single number.[3] A car with a death rate of 3.0 could be crashing constantly but keeping people alive, or crashing rarely but killing almost everyone involved. Those two failure modes demand radically different interventions. One is a driver behavior problem. The other is an engineering indictment.

The Four Quadrants

Mapping 272 vehicles across both axes creates four distinct safety archetypes, and the residents of each quadrant are not who you'd guess.

Crash Frequently, Survive: The Toyota Tundra crashes 30.6 times per 10,000 fleet—more than double the overall average—but its 41% lethality means occupants walk away from the majority of fatal-crash scenarios. The Honda Odyssey, a vehicle marketed almost exclusively to families hauling children, crashes at 25.8 per 10,000 with 43% lethality. Both carry high death tolls (the Tundra logged 1,223 deaths over the decade) because they're everywhere, but the crashes themselves are survivable at above-average rates. Mass and modern crumple zone engineering are doing the work that driver behavior isn't.[2]

Crash Rarely, Die: The Buick Park Avenue shows up in just 6.3 crashes per 10,000 fleet, one of the lowest frequencies in the database, and yet when those crashes occur, occupants die 88% of the time. The Ford Escort appears at 2.5 per 10,000—statistically invisible—but maintains the same 88% kill rate when it does crash. These are not reckless-driver vehicles. Impairment rates among Park Avenue drivers sit at 24.3%, and Escort drivers at typical levels. The cars themselves are the problem. Built before modern crashworthiness standards, small, light, structurally compromised by age, with twenty-year-old airbag systems and no electronic stability control.

Worst of Both Worlds: The Chevrolet S-10 crashes at 86.4 per 10,000 fleet AND kills at a 76% clip, producing a combined danger score of 65.2—the highest in the dataset. The Nissan Maxima (88.8 frequency, 66% lethality) and Chevrolet Cobalt (72.6 frequency, 81% lethality) form the rest of this grim podium. The Cobalt is particularly damning because 81% lethality means four out of five times this car appears in the FARS database, someone in it died, and it appears often enough to accumulate one of the highest body counts in its segment.[4]

Best of Both Worlds: The Kia Telluride crashes at 1.4 per 10,000 fleet with 36% lethality. The Tesla Model 3 at 1.3 per 10,000 with 43% lethality across a 1.575-million-unit fleet. These vehicles are both rarely involved in fatal crashes and relatively survivable when they are. Newer design, active safety systems, and in the Telluride's case, sheer mass built on a modern unibody platform.

Methodology

Crash frequency = total FARS crashes / estimated registered fleet × 10,000. Crash lethality = total FARS deaths / total FARS crashes. The Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient was computed across all 272 qualifying vehicles (fleet >100,000, crashes ≥50) without controlling for vehicle class, age, or driver demographics. Inputs: NHTSA FARS 2014–2023 bulk data, estimated fleet sizes derived from cumulative U.S. sales data, VMT estimates from the National Household Travel Survey.[1][5]

Limitations

FARS captures only crashes with at least one fatality. Non-fatal crash frequency data (the 6.7 million annual police-reported crashes) is not included, so the "frequency" axis measures only fatal-crash involvement, not total crash involvement. Fleet size estimates carry approximately ±15% uncertainty for low-volume models. The lethality metric includes all occupants and other crash participants, not exclusively occupants of the subject vehicle. VMT estimates are class-level from NHTS, not model-specific, which smooths out behavioral differences between, say, Corvette owners and Camry owners.

The Best Case Against This Analysis

The low correlation could be an artifact of confounders rather than proof of mechanical independence. Old, cheap sedans cluster in the "rare but lethal" quadrant partly because their elderly or low-income owners drive fewer miles (low frequency) while the car's outdated structure ensures fatalities when collisions do occur (high lethality). Modern SUVs cluster in the "safe" quadrant because suburban families drive predictable routes at moderate speeds (low frequency) in vehicles with advanced crash protection (low lethality). Vehicle age and owner demographics could be independently driving both axes, making the r² = 3.3% finding a reflection of socioeconomic confounding rather than a clean decomposition of vehicle safety into two separate engineering dimensions. This analysis cannot fully separate the two explanations with the available data.

What You Should Actually Check

If you're shopping for a car, a single death-rate number tells you almost nothing about why a vehicle is dangerous. Find the FARS data. Separate the two questions: does this car show up in fatal crashes often (exposure/behavior) or does it kill its occupants when crashes happen (engineering/design)? A high-frequency, low-lethality vehicle like the Tundra has a driver behavior and exposure problem that won't follow you if you change your driving patterns. A low-frequency, high-lethality vehicle like the Park Avenue has a structural engineering problem that no amount of defensive driving will fix. One of these problems is yours to solve. The other one was decided in a factory decades before you bought it.
