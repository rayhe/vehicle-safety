Trim Watch

A Plastic Clip Worth Pennies Has Generated Three Million Ford Explorer Recall Notices

Mia Crumplezone • Safety Engineering Desk
July 6, 2026

Between 2021 and 2025, Ford issued three separate recalls for the same defect on the Ford Explorer: plastic retention pins that fail, allowing exterior trim to detach at highway speed. First roof rail covers in 2021 (616,967 units), then A-pillar trim in 2024 (1,889,110 units), and finally B-pillar trim in 2025 (492,145 units).[1] Combined total: roughly three million recall notices for a part that costs less than a dollar.

Ford Explorer retention-pin recall notices, 2021–2025

Two different suppliers built these parts: JAC Products manufactured the roof rail clips while ABC Technologies molded the pillar trim, which rules out a single-vendor quality escape.[2] Ford's design specification for the retention system itself is the common thread across all three failures.

NHTSA “strongly advised” Ford on the A-pillar recall after accumulating 14,000 warranty reports and 568 consumer complaints, but Ford initially resisted. When it finally issued Recall 24V-040, it estimated only 5% of recalled vehicles would actually need repair, roughly 94,000 fixes across 1.9 million notices.[1] Whether that ratio reflects overcautious regulation or undertested design is an open question nobody at Ford has publicly addressed.

Ford's broader recall volume amplifies the pattern, with 51 recalls covering 12+ million vehicles in H1 2026 alone.[3] In June, the Bronco joined the roster with 36,046 units recalled for fender flares that detach because tooling “did not meet” Ford's own standards.[4] Meanwhile, JD Power named Ford its number-one mass-market quality brand for 2026, and both facts remain simultaneously, uncomfortably true.

FARS data frames the stakes differently: Ford Explorers account for 3,797 deaths in the 2014–2023 dataset at 1.54 per 100 million vehicle miles traveled.[5] Zero recorded fatalities link to trim detachment specifically, but a roof rail cover traveling at 70 mph becomes a multi-pound projectile aimed at whatever follows. Risk flows backward: not to the Explorer's occupants, but to everyone in their wake.

Strongest counterargument: Ford's willingness to recall three million vehicles for a cosmetic defect with no reported injuries suggests a lower action threshold, not worse engineering. Other automakers may tolerate identical failure rates without issuing recalls at all. Ford's numbers look terrible precisely because the company acts on marginal defects that competitors ignore.

That argument deserves respect and has a clear limit. When the same failure mode recurs across three model spans, two suppliers, and three trim locations over four years, you've moved past isolated quality escapes into a design philosophy that consistently underestimates clip loading at highway speeds. Ford chose plastic retention pins as its standard attachment method for Explorer exterior trim. Three million notices later, the B-pillar remedy still isn't expected until November 2026.[1]

What to do: Own a 2011–2019 Explorer? Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls, because all three recall windows overlap on those model years. If your A-pillar or B-pillar trim shows visible gaps or wobble, don't wait for the dealer letter and schedule service now. Driving behind any Explorer on the highway, an extra car length costs you nothing given that three million vehicles got recalled for parts that were designed to stay attached and didn't.
