# Research: The 13-Quarter Decline — VMT Paradox

## Angle
Every prior sustained decline in U.S. traffic fatalities was driven by Americans driving less (recessions, gas crises). The current 13-quarter decline (Q2 2022–Q2 2025) is happening while VMT is INCREASING. That's historically unprecedented and suggests structural safety improvement, not just economic regression.

## Primary Source
NHTSA Crash•Stats DOT HS 813 756 — "Early Estimate of Motor Vehicle Traffic Fatalities for the First Half (January–June) of 2025"
URL: https://crashstats.nhtsa.dot.gov/Api/Public/ViewPublication/813756

## Key Data Points

### The Four Great Declines (from NHTSA Figure 1)
1. **1981 Q2 → 1983 Q4** (11 quarters): Recession-driven. Gas prices spiked, VMT contracted.
2. **1990 Q4 → 1993 Q2** (11 quarters): Early '90s recession. VMT growth stalled.
3. **2006 Q2 → 2010 Q2** (17 quarters): Great Recession. VMT dropped ~3.6% peak-to-trough. Longest sustained decline on record.
4. **2022 Q2 → 2025 Q2** (13 quarters, ongoing): VMT is INCREASING throughout. H1 2025 VMT up 0.8% (12.1B miles) vs H1 2024.

### H1 2025 Numbers
- 17,140 estimated deaths (down 8.2% from 18,680 in H1 2024)
- Fatality rate: 1.06 per 100M VMT (lowest since H1 2014's 1.01)
- Q2 2025 alone: -10.2% decline (acceleration from Q1's -5.9%)
- June 2025: -12% year-over-year (steepest monthly decline)
- 13th consecutive quarterly decline

### Rate Trajectory (full-year)
- 2014: 1.08 (pre-spike low)
- 2019: 1.11 (pre-COVID)
- 2020: 1.34 (COVID spike begins)
- 2021: 1.38 (COVID peak — highest since 2006)
- 2022: 1.34
- 2023: 1.26
- 2024: 1.20
- 2025 H1: 1.06 (below pre-COVID, approaching 2014 low)

### The 11 States Getting Worse
Hawaii (+47.8%), Kansas (+21.2%), Idaho (+18.6%), Massachusetts (+15.1%), Wyoming (+10.2%), Montana (+9.8%), New Hampshire (+7.5%), West Virginia (+6.7%), North Dakota (+5.6%), Utah (+2.7%), Illinois (+1.0%). Rhode Island unchanged.

### The Stars
D.C. (-73.1%), Connecticut (-32.5%), Delaware (-26.6%), Mississippi (-25.7%), Maryland (-23.5%), Kentucky (-21.8%), Nebraska (-20.5%)

## Original Contribution
Cross-reference of all four major decline periods with contemporaneous VMT data to demonstrate this is the first where driving volume increased throughout. Prior declines were demand-side (fewer miles → fewer deaths). This one appears supply-side (better cars, better infrastructure → fewer deaths per mile).

## Counterargument (at full strength)
The decline could be regression to the mean after the COVID spike. The 43,230 deaths in 2021 were so extreme that ANY normalization looks like a decline. If the "true" baseline was ~36,000 (the 2019 level), then getting back to ~34,300 (annualized 2025) is only ~5% real improvement over 6 years — modest, not historic. The 13-quarter streak is partly an artifact of how far the COVID spike pushed things up. Counter-counter: the rate (1.06) is now BELOW the pre-COVID rate (1.11), and approaching the 2014 floor (1.08 full-year, 1.01 H1). So even accounting for COVID regression, something real has happened.

## Limitations
- H1 data only for 2025; H2 typically has more deaths (summer/holidays)
- State-level projections use regional inflation methodology — small states (Hawaii, North Dakota, Wyoming) have wide effective confidence intervals
- VMT data from FHWA is itself an estimate based on traffic counters
- We don't have a causal mechanism identified (ADAS? enforcement? fleet age? behavioral change?) — just correlation
- The 2024 full-year numbers are still projections themselves

## Strongest Counter-Argument
The structural improvement thesis relies on VMT increasing while deaths decrease. But VMT increases could be compositional — more miles on safer roads (interstates) rather than more miles on rural roads where most deaths occur. If the VMT increase is highway-heavy, the per-mile risk decrease is overstated because those miles were already safe. Without VMT breakdown by road type, we can't be sure the "same roads got safer" vs "people shifted to already-safe roads."
