The Gap

Nobody Crash-Tested the Vehicles That Kill 6,535 People a Year. IIHS Finally Did.

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
June 20, 2026

A seatbelt force limiter costs about twelve dollars. It is a small mechanical device, roughly the size of a cigarette lighter, that lets the belt yield a controlled amount during a crash so your rib cage absorbs the deceleration over milliseconds instead of all at once. Every Honda Civic sold since 2006 has one. The 2026 Chevrolet Express does not.

5 of 9
Commercial vehicles that failed IIHS's first-ever safety evaluation last week

On June 17, the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety released the results of something that should have happened decades ago: safety evaluations for heavy-duty trucks and cargo vans.[1] Not crash tests, mind you. IIHS didn't even bother sending these vehicles into a barrier yet. It simply checked whether nine of the most popular commercial vehicles in America have five basic safety features that passenger cars have treated as table stakes since the Obama administration. Airbags. Pretensioners. Force limiters. A seatbelt reminder that lasts longer than a sneeze.

Five of nine failed.

Both the Silverado 3500HD and Ford F-350 SuperCrew passed everything. So did the Ram ProMaster and the Chevrolet BrightDrop, which is an electric delivery van that didn't exist three years ago and already outperforms platforms with three decades of development behind them. Ford's Transit, the Mercedes-Benz Sprinter, Rivian's Delivery 500, and Ram 3500 all failed the seatbelt reminder test, because apparently a four-to-eight-second chime is all federal law requires, and four major manufacturers decided that was good enough for the people driving their products twelve hours a day.[2] IIHS data shows persistent reminders increase belt usage by 30 percent, so that eight-second beep isn't just lazy engineering; it is a measurable contributor to unbelted deaths.

Consider the Express, which GM has been building on essentially the same platform since 1996. A human born the year the Express debuted can now legally get a mortgage, have a midlife crisis, and buy a Corvette. After thirty years, it still lacks driver-side seatbelt force limiters. IIHS flagged it as the worst performer in the entire evaluation, failing on both the force limiter and seatbelt reminder criteria. This is not an oversight from a struggling startup. This is General Motors, the largest American automaker, selling a vehicle in 2026 that cannot meet a safety bar set by a $12 part that has been standard in economy sedans for twenty years.

The Soberest Drivers in the Deadliest Vehicles

What makes this regulatory vacuum particularly grotesque is who it kills. Pull the FARS toxicology data for 2014 through 2023 and sort by vehicle class, and commercial vans sit at the bottom of the impairment table: 18.1 percent of fatally crashed van drivers tested positive for alcohol or drugs.[3] Compare that to sports cars at 22.5 percent, sedans at 20.4, pickups at 20.1. Van drivers are, by a significant margin, the most sober people dying on American roads.

That distinction matters because it shifts the causal frame. When a 23-year-old wraps a Camaro around a telephone pole at 2 AM with a blood alcohol content of 0.18, the proximate cause is obvious and the vehicle's crashworthiness is almost beside the point. But when a stone-cold-sober HVAC technician in a fifteen-year-old Express hits a median barrier at highway speed and the seatbelt crushes his sternum because there is no force limiter to meter the load, the failure belongs to the machine. And to the regulatory framework that exempted it from the standards that would have prevented exactly this outcome.

FARS captures the cost in bodies: Ford's E-350 alone accounts for 776 deaths in the FARS dataset at a rate of 2.51 fatalities per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, which is higher than the Honda Accord, the Toyota Camry, or the Ford F-150. GM's Express contributed another 475 deaths across the decade. Its twin, the GMC Savana, added 166.[3] These are vehicles purchased overwhelmingly by commercial fleets, driven overwhelmingly by working professionals, involved overwhelmingly in sober crashes. Among Express drivers in fatal crashes, just 12.7 percent tested positive for any impairment, which is less than half the rate for Corvette drivers.

What This Actually Means for You

If you drive a commercial van for work, run your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls today. Separately, ask your fleet manager whether the company's purchasing spec includes the IIHS's five-criteria checklist: front airbags, side airbags, pretensioners, force limiters, and a persistent seatbelt reminder. If they don't know what a force limiter is, that tells you something about how your employer values your chest cavity.

If you manage a fleet, IIHS just handed you a free procurement filter. BrightDrop 400 and Ram ProMaster passed every criterion, while Transit and Sprinter did not. No Express should appear on any fleet order written after June 17, 2026, unless the buyer is comfortable defending that decision in a wrongful death deposition, because a plaintiff's attorney will absolutely cite these results.

IIHS plans to add headlight evaluations, automatic emergency braking tests, and eventually expand to Class 4 through Class 6 vehicles.[1] But those phases are future promises. What matters now is simpler and more damning: in 2026, you can buy a brand-new American commercial vehicle that lacks a safety feature your grandmother's Corolla had. Nobody tested these vehicles because nobody had to. And 6,535 people died in 2023 in crashes involving them.

Sixteen percent of all traffic deaths. Zero percent were crash-tested before this month.

Limitations

FARS captures only fatal crashes, which are a fraction of the roughly 6.7 million annual U.S. crashes. A vehicle with a low fatality rate might still produce high injury rates that this data cannot see. Our impairment percentages reflect tested drivers in fatal crashes, not all crashes or all driving; FARS toxicology testing rates also vary by state and jurisdiction, which can bias the sample. IIHS examined five occupant-protection criteria and did not include crash tests, so passing this checklist does not mean a vehicle is "safe" in the comprehensive sense. Fleet VMT estimates used in FARS rate calculations carry approximately ±15 percent uncertainty for lower-volume models.

Strongest Counterargument

Commercial vehicles are heavy. Mass is itself a crash-protection mechanism, and the Express's 0.92 fatality rate per 100 million VMT is actually lower than many passenger sedans. It is possible that the vehicle's sheer bulk compensates for the absence of force limiters in many crash scenarios, particularly low-speed collisions where occupant kinematics are less violent. If you look only at per-VMT death rates, the Express is not an outlier. This is a fair point, and it means the safety-equipment gap may matter most in high-delta-V crashes where the belt load becomes the critical variable. But the counterargument concedes the premise: the Express survives on weight, not engineering. That is a strategy, not a standard.
