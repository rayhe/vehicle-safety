# Research: Two-Fleet America

## News Hook
- **NHTSA 2025 data**: US traffic deaths fell 6.7% to 36,640 — lowest since 2019
- **Rate**: 1.10 per 100M VMT (lowest since 2014), 15th consecutive quarterly decline
- **February 2026 preliminary (NSC)**: 1.01 per 100M VMT — approaching sub-1.0 for first time in years
- **Everyone celebrating**. The headline is: "roads are getting safer."

## Thesis
The national rate of 1.10 describes nobody's actual driving risk. America has three distinct vehicle fleets, and the rate gap between them is 6.9:1. The "decline" is real, but it's concentrated in the safe fleet. For the 13.4% of vehicles with fatality rates above 2.0, the national milestone is a statistical fiction.

## FARS Three-Fleet Analysis (2014-2023 data, 337 models)

### Safe Fleet (rate < 1.0 per 100M VMT)
- **241 models** (71.5% of all models)
- **63.6% of registered fleet** (109M vehicles)
- **28.5% of all deaths** (54,501)
- **Effective rate: 0.40/100M VMT** — already well below 1.0
- **Lethality: 0.509** (51% of fatal crashes kill the driver)
- Dominated by: modern SUVs/crossovers, newer pickups
- Top examples: RAV4 (0.19), Ram 1500 (0.13), Equinox (0.36), Rogue (0.35)

### Middle Fleet (rate 1.0-2.0)
- **59 models** (17.5%)
- **23.0% of fleet** (39.5M vehicles)
- **33.3% of deaths** (63,644)
- **Effective rate: 1.30/100M VMT**
- **Lethality: 0.547**
- Mixed: sedans, older SUVs, some pickups

### Danger Fleet (rate > 2.0)
- **37 models** (11.0%)
- **13.4% of fleet** (23M vehicles)
- **38.2% of deaths** (73,048)
- **Effective rate: 2.75/100M VMT** — 2.5x the national 1.10
- **Lethality: 0.647** (65% crash-to-death conversion)
- **Death share / Fleet share ratio: 2.85x**
- Dominated by: sedans (21 models, 51,477 deaths)
- Top death vehicles: Honda Accord (3.07, 7,102 deaths), Honda Civic (2.25, 6,553), Toyota Camry (2.03, 6,328), Nissan Altima (2.88, 4,787), Chevy Impala (5.0, 3,774)

### The Gap
- Safe fleet effective rate: 0.40
- Danger fleet effective rate: 2.75
- **Gap: 6.9x**
- National rate (NHTSA 2025): 1.10
- Safe fleet is **already 64% below** the celebrated national rate
- Danger fleet is **150% above** it

## Novel Contribution
Cross-tabulating FARS per-model fatality rates against fleet share to show that the national VMT-based fatality rate is a weighted average of three statistically distinct populations. The "improvement" being celebrated is driven by the safe fleet (which was already safe) diluting out the danger fleet (which hasn't moved). For a driver in a rate-3.07 Accord, the national rate hitting 1.0 changes nothing about their personal risk.

## Strongest Counterargument
The danger fleet includes many older vehicle generations (2000s-era Accords, Civics, etc.) whose newer versions would land in the safe fleet. The high-rate models are being retired and replaced by safer successors. So the danger fleet IS shrinking naturally. But: these vehicles are still on the road, still being sold used, and still killing people at 2.75x the national rate. The decline has a shelf life.

## Limitations
- FARS rates are 10-year averages (2014-2023), not current-year snapshots
- We cannot show the time trend of the gap (is it widening or narrowing?)
- Fleet size estimates use public sales data, not actual registration counts
- VMT per vehicle is estimated, not measured (±15% uncertainty for low-volume models)
- Some "high rate" vehicles include very old fleets (Impala, Cobalt) whose remaining road population is unclear

## Self-Critique Gate
Is this genuinely surprising after 234 articles? **Yes.** The specific three-fleet decomposition with effective rates has not been computed before. The news hook (1.10 declining toward 1.0) is current. The finding that the danger fleet's effective rate is 6.9x the safe fleet's — and that 21 of 37 danger-fleet vehicles are sedans — challenges the "SUVs are the problem" narrative. The article names specific vehicles most readers own or know someone who owns.
