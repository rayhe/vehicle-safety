The Soberest Drivers in America Are Dying in the Same SUV That Just Failed Its Crash Test

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
May 31, 2026

Only 8.9 percent of Toyota Land Cruiser drivers involved in fatal crashes tested positive for any impairment, according to FARS toxicology data covering 2014 through 2023. That is the second-lowest impairment rate of any vehicle with a meaningful sample, behind only the Subaru Ascent at 8.2 percent.[1] The national average hovers around 30 percent. Land Cruiser owners, statistically speaking, are careful, sober, responsible adults who happen to drive a vehicle with a fatality rate of 6.27 per 100 million VMT, which makes it the second-deadliest SUV in the FARS database, slotted behind the discontinued Chevrolet Tracker.

6.27
Deaths per 100M VMT for the Land Cruiser, behind only the Chevy Tracker (7.83) among SUVs

Two days ago, the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety published its moderate overlap frontal crash test results for the 2026 Toyota Land Cruiser. Its verdict: "marginal." Submarining caused the failure, a phenomenon where the lap belt rides upward over the pelvis and digs into the abdomen during a frontal collision, creating what IIHS describes as a high risk of abdominal injury.[2] The rear occupant dummy showed elevated risk across head, neck, and chest measurements too. Toyota charges $57,345 for this vehicle. A good moderate overlap rating is now required for both IIHS TOP SAFETY PICK and TOP SAFETY PICK+. Neither IIHS award is available to it.[3]

Before anyone reaches for the obvious explanation: the FARS fatality rate is inflated by older models. Deaths concentrate in the 2001 through 2008 model years, when 182 of the Land Cruiser's 343 total fatalities occurred, peaking at 33 deaths for model year 2005 alone. These were heavy, tall, body-on-frame trucks with high centers of gravity and no mandatory electronic stability control. They rolled, and people died. After 2014, the numbers improve dramatically: 51 deaths across four model years, reflecting ESC mandates and modern structural engineering. So yes, the 6.27 rate is partly an echo of an older, more dangerous vehicle.

That concession makes the IIHS result worse, not better. If the old Land Cruisers killed people through rollover physics that modern engineering has largely solved, the new Land Cruiser is introducing a completely different failure mode: passive restraint geometry that lets the lap belt slide above the pelvis during the kind of frontal impact that represents a substantial share of real-world fatal collisions. Toyota fixed the 2003 problem and shipped a 2026 problem, and it is a restraint design issue that should have been caught in development. To compound the situation, Toyota recalled 82,000 vehicles on May 28 for a software defect that blanks the driver's 12.3-inch display on startup, including the 2024 and 2025 Land Cruiser, the Lexus GX, the Lexus UX, and the Mirai.[4] A vehicle that cannot display warning indicators cannot comply with federal safety standards. Two distinct safety failures for the same nameplate in the same week.

Impairment data deepens the paradox because it eliminates the easiest scapegoat. When a Mustang posts a fatality rate of 6.02 per 100M VMT, you can point to driver behavior as a contributing factor, and the data supports that: muscle cars attract younger, more aggressive, sometimes impaired drivers. Land Cruiser owners skew the opposite direction entirely. Only 6.7 percent tested positive for alcohol, and 2.9 percent for drugs. Among the 417 drivers in FARS fatal crashes, 380 were sober. Their vehicle killed them anyway, and the sober-driver-adjusted fatality rate of approximately 5.71 per 100M VMT still exceeds the total rate (impaired and sober combined) of nearly every modern crossover SUV on the market: the RAV4 at 0.19, the CR-V at 0.53, the Forester at 0.26, the Equinox at 0.36.

Consider the Subaru Ascent as a control case. It has the lowest impairment rate in FARS at 8.2 percent, and its fatality rate is 0.78 per 100M VMT, roughly eight times lower than the Land Cruiser's. Both vehicles attract sober, family-oriented buyers, but the difference is engineering: the Ascent is a unibody crossover with a low center of gravity and modern crash structure. Across most of the FARS period, the Land Cruiser was a body-on-frame truck that weighed over 5,500 pounds and stood tall enough to make rollover a plausible outcome in single-vehicle crashes. When you control for driver behavior, the vehicle itself is the independent variable that explains the gap.

A caveat: the Land Cruiser's fleet size in FARS is estimated at 43,750, which is small. Rate estimates for low-volume vehicles carry wider confidence intervals, and a swing of 30 to 50 deaths across the decade would meaningfully change the rate. Read that 6.27 figure as directionally alarming rather than metrologically precise. Its platform cousin, the Lexus LX, shows only 9 deaths on a similarly small fleet and a rate of 0.16, but that sample is too tiny to draw engineering conclusions from. What the data does tell us with confidence is that Land Cruiser drivers are not the problem, the vehicle was, and in at least one measurable dimension, the new one still is.

What You Should Do

If you own a pre-2010 Land Cruiser, your vehicle predates mandatory electronic stability control. Its high center of gravity and body-on-frame design made it statistically more dangerous in the era when most of these deaths occurred. If you are shopping for a 2026 Land Cruiser, be aware that rear occupants face elevated injury risk in moderate overlap frontal crashes due to the lap belt submarining issue identified by IIHS. At $57,345, Toyota does not buy you a passing grade on the crash test that now determines IIHS safety awards. Check whether your 2024 or 2025 model is affected by the display recall at nhtsa.gov/recalls. And if you want a family SUV with a sober-driver profile and a fatality rate that reflects good engineering rather than just good intentions, the Subaru Ascent (0.78) and Forester (0.26) start at roughly half the price.
