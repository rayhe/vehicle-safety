The Jeep Grand Cherokee Failed Twice in 20 Years. The Data Shows Exactly When.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
March 29, 2026

Two design choices. Two decades apart. Both visible in the federal death count if you know where to look. Chrysler's flagship SUV accumulated 1,161 fatalities between 2014 and 2023 in FARS data, with model-year breakdowns that read like a timeline of Chrysler's worst engineering decisions.[1]

121 → 64
Deaths dropped 47% between MY2004 and MY2005, the year Chrysler moved the fuel tank forward of the rear axle

Start with the fuel tank. From 1993 through 2004, every Grand Cherokee rolled off the line with a plastic fuel tank mounted behind the rear axle. In a rear-end collision, this arrangement turned the vehicle into something uncomfortably close to a Ford Pinto tribute act. By 2013, the Center for Auto Safety had tallied 487 deaths in 349 fatal fire crashes involving these models by 2013. Chrysler's own FARS analysis showed the Grand Cherokee was 21 times more likely to experience a fatal rear-impact fire crash than a Ford Explorer.[2]

NHTSA told Chrysler to recall 2.7 million vehicles. Chrysler said no. Then it said yes, sort of, agreeing to recall 1.56 million of the oldest models. The fix? Installing a trailer hitch. Chrysler's own former VP of Engineering, Francois Castaing, testified under oath that trailer hitches do not protect the fuel tank.[2] One year after the recall, the completion rate stood at 12%. NHTSA calculated it would take Chrysler 4.7 years just to produce enough parts.

Our FARS model-year data captures the before and after. The 2004 model year, the last Grand Cherokee with the behind-axle tank, logged 121 deaths during the 2014–2023 observation window. The 2005 model, the first year Chrysler relocated the tank forward of the axle in the WK redesign, logged 64. That is a 47% reduction between consecutive model years. Deaths continued falling: 43 for MY2006, 28 for MY2007.[1]

15 → 93
Deaths jumped 520% between MY2013 and MY2014, the year the monostable electronic shifter debuted

Then Chrysler found a new way to kill people. For 2014, Chrysler gave the Grand Cherokee a ZF 8-speed automatic with a monostable electronic gear selector. Unlike a traditional shifter with a physical "Park" detent, this one returned to center after every input. A small dashboard indicator was the only confirmation the vehicle was in Park. Drivers walked away from running vehicles they believed were parked. The vehicles rolled.[3]

In June 2016, actor Anton Yelchin was crushed between his 2015 Grand Cherokee and a brick pillar in his own driveway. He was 27. FCA recalled 1.1 million vehicles worldwide, including the 2014–2015 Grand Cherokee, 2012–2014 Dodge Charger, and 2012–2014 Chrysler 300. At least 41 injuries had already been reported.[3]

The FARS data is stark. Model year 2013 logged 15 deaths during the observation window. Model year 2014 logged 93. That is a 520% increase for consecutive years of the same vehicle.[1] Model year 2015 came in at 81. After the recall and software update, model year 2016 dropped to 32.

Impairment does not explain any of this. Grand Cherokee drivers tested at 20.8% for any substance and 16.3% for alcohol, both within a percentage point of the fleet average.[1] These are not reckless-driver deaths. They are engineering-decision deaths.

The math, transparent

MY2004 deaths (121) vs. MY2005 deaths (64): (121 - 64) / 121 = 47.1% reduction. MY2013 deaths (15) vs. MY2014 deaths (93): (93 - 15) / 15 = 520% increase. The overall death rate of 0.51 per 100 million VMT puts the Grand Cherokee below the SUV class average, which makes the model-year spikes more notable, not less. The aggregate number looks acceptable because good years dilute the bad ones.

What this does not prove

FARS model-year totals capture all causes of fatal crashes, not just fuel tank fires or rollaway incidents specifically. We cannot isolate the exact proportion attributable to each defect. Sales volume also plays a role: MY2004 was the final year of the popular WJ generation, and MY2014 was the first year of a refreshed model with higher sales. Both factors inflate total deaths independent of any design defect. Without per-model-year registration data (which FARS does not include), we are attributing causation to design changes where the confound of sales volume exists. The observation window also biases older model years, which have accumulated more years of exposure.

The strongest case against this analysis

The MY2004 peak could simply be a volume story. It was the last year of a popular generation being sold alongside the new WK, often at steep discounts, pushing unit sales higher than surrounding years. Similarly, the MY2014 spike coincides with a mid-cycle refresh that boosted sales significantly. If Chrysler sold 60% more MY2014 Grand Cherokees than MY2013s, a proportional death increase would be expected even without a shifter defect. We do not have the exact sales-by-model-year figures to test this alternative hypothesis against FARS.
