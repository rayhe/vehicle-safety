# Research Notes: Cadillac CT5 IIHS Failure + CTS FARS Impairment Data

## Angle
The 2026 Cadillac CT5 just failed FOUR IIHS tests — the worst results for any Cadillac in memory. GM pointed to its perfect 5-star NHTSA rating as a defense. But FARS data on its predecessor, the CTS, reveals a deeper problem: the CTS had the highest impairment rate (25.9%) of any luxury sedan in the dataset. One in five CTS drivers in fatal crashes was drunk. GM replaced that car with one that can't pass a side-impact crash test.

## IIHS CT5 Results (July 2026)
- **Moderate overlap front crash test: POOR** — rear passengers at extreme risk, head/chest/neck forces dangerous, dummies moved too far forward
- **Side impact crash test: POOR** — B-pillar crushed into occupant compartment, driver's head moved past side curtain airbag and hit windowsill, 4,200 lb barrier at 37 mph (simulating SUV T-bone)
- **Headlights: POOR** — adaptive LED scored Poor, standard LED scored Marginal
- **Vehicle-to-vehicle front crash prevention: POOR** — AEB system failed under updated criteria
- Seat belt reminders: Marginal
- Pedestrian crash prevention: Acceptable
- The CT5 earned ZERO top-tier ratings in any test category

## NHTSA CT5 Results (for contrast)
- Frontal crash: 5/5
- Side crash: 5/5
- Rollover: 5/5
- Perfect scores across the board

## Original Contribution
Cross-referencing the CT5's July 2026 IIHS failure with the CTS's FARS impairment data reveals a compounding vulnerability: the replacement for the most-impaired luxury sedan in America just proved it can't protect occupants when they crash. No other analysis has drawn this connection.

Additionally: the CTS death rate (1.32) is 2.1x the Mercedes C-Class (0.63), 3.7x the Cadillac ATS (0.36), and 4.1x the Audi A4 (0.32). GM's own entry-level Cadillac was 3.7x safer than its mid-level Cadillac.

## CT5 Product Details
- MSRP: $48,600 (2026 Premium Luxury)
- Introduced 2020 as CTS replacement
- Architecture: Alpha platform (shared with Camaro)
- 2026 was a styling refresh, no structural changes
- No 2027 planned; 2028 redesign is first chance to address IIHS findings

## Counterargument
The CTS and CT5 are different physical vehicles with different engineering. CTS impairment data reflects the driver population for a discontinued car, not the CT5's current buyer base. Additionally, NHTSA's 5-star methodology is federally mandated and has been the gold standard for decades; IIHS is insurance-industry-funded and has changed its criteria to be more demanding. The CT5's NHTSA scores are legitimate safety markers.

## Limitations
- FARS data is 2014-2023; the CT5 was only on sale from 2020, so limited FARS history exists for the CT5 itself
- The CTS/CT5 are sequential products but not identical vehicles
- Impairment rates reflect the driver population, not the vehicle's contribution to crashes
- IIHS and NHTSA test different crash scenarios; neither is "wrong"
- Fleet estimates use NHTS VMT data with ±15% uncertainty for low-volume models
