# Research: America's Best-Selling Trucks Fail at Protecting Rear-Seat Kids

## Angle
Cross-reference IIHS rear-seat crash test failures with FARS fatality data to reveal a systemic problem: the three best-selling American trucks all fail at protecting the occupants most likely to sit in the back seat — children.

## Kill Test
- **Novel?** Yes. Nobody has cross-tabulated IIHS rear-seat test results with FARS death counts to compute family exposure risk across the entire American truck fleet.
- **Genuinely surprising?** Yes. Most buyers assume bigger = safer. These trucks protect drivers well but fail the dummy that simulates a 12-year-old.
- **Data-backed?** Yes. IIHS test ratings, FARS death counts, fleet sizes, sales volumes.

## Novel Contribution
**Family Exposure Calculation:**
- Big 3 American truck fleet: 18.9 million vehicles on the road
- ~75% are crew cab (family configuration): ~14 million trucks
- All of them have rear seats that FAILED the IIHS test for a 12-year-old dummy
- In 2024 alone, 2+ million new ones were sold
- The only half-ton that passes: Toyota Tundra (159,528 sales vs 1.69M for the failing three)
- Ratio: For every Tundra sold, 10.6 trucks that fail the rear-seat test are sold

## Limitations
- FARS captures fatal crashes only — not injuries
- FARS death counts don't distinguish seating position (we can't separate rear-seat deaths from driver deaths in the aggregate)
- IIHS test uses a dummy simulating a 12-year-old, not an actual child in a car seat
- IIHS notes the back seat is still the safest place for young children (due to airbag risk in front)
- Fleet estimates have ±15% uncertainty
- Crew cab percentage is estimated from industry data, not exact

## Strongest Counterargument
Pickup trucks have lower per-VMT death rates than many sedans (F-150 at 1.04 vs Accord at 3.07). Their mass advantage in multi-vehicle crashes provides significant protection. The IIHS rear-seat test measures one specific crash scenario at 40 mph — it doesn't mean rear-seat passengers are broadly at high risk in all crashes. And FARS data doesn't tell us what percentage of pickup deaths were rear-seat passengers.
