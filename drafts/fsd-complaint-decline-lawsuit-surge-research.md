# Research: FSD Complaint Decline / Lawsuit Surge

## Angle
NHTSA complaints about Tesla FSD/Autopilot are declining rapidly — phantom braking complaints dropped from 300 to 3. The regulatory system interprets this as mission accomplished and is closing investigations. But the litigation system is telling the opposite story: lawsuits are surging, exposure is $14.5B, and a driver was just charged with manslaughter. Same technology. Three accountability channels. Three different verdicts.

## Original Contribution
First systematic comparison of the regulatory, civil, and criminal accountability channels for Tesla's autonomous driving technology, showing that complaint-frequency decline can mask ongoing lethality when the regulatory channel measures symptom reports while courts measure outcomes (deaths).

## Primary Sources

### 1. NHTSA Investigation Closures (2026)
- **Phantom braking (PE22-004):** Closed July 2026. 695,000 Model 3/Y vehicles. Complaints declined from 300 (when opened) to 45 (2024), 19 (2025), 3 (2026 YTD). Software updates in early 2022. NHTSA cited "low demonstrated hazard."
  Source: Reuters, July 2, 2026
- **Steering loss:** Closed June 27, 2026. 376,241 Model 3/Y (2023 MY). Tesla recalled 376K in early 2025 (OTA software update). NHTSA closed in view of recall.
  Source: Reuters, June 27, 2026
- **Smart Summon:** Closed April 2026. 2.6M vehicles. ~100 crashes, 0 injuries, 0 fatalities. "Low incident occurrence and low incident severity."
  Source: Electrek, April 7, 2026
- **Steering wheel detachment:** Closed April 2026. 120,089 Model Y. Closed without manufacturer action.
  Source: Reuters, April 28, 2026

### 2. NHTSA FSD Engineering Analysis (Still Open)
- Upgraded March 2026 from preliminary evaluation. 3.2M vehicles.
- 9 crashes, 1 fatality (Johna Story), 1 injury
- Focus: FSD degradation detection failure — system can't tell when cameras are blinded by sun glare, fog, dust
- Engineering analysis is typically the last step before a recall
  Source: Reuters/Electrek, March 2026

### 3. Civil Litigation
- **Johna Story settlement:** First known FSD pedestrian death (Nov 2023, Arizona highway, 71-year-old). Tesla Model Y in FSD mode. Settled quietly June 2026. Terms undisclosed.
  Source: Bloomberg via Autoblog, June 29, 2026
- **Waller v. Tesla class action (No. 4:26-cv-05350-KAW):** Filed June 4, 2026, N.D. California. Covers HW1-HW3 vehicles (2017-2023). 5 counts: breach of warranty, KCPA, fraud by misrepresentation, fraud by omission, unjust enrichment. Anchored on Tesla Q1 2026 earnings admission: "Hardware 3 simply does not have the capability to achieve unsupervised FSD." $14.5B combined exposure. 27-state class, arbitration opt-outs only.
  Source: Electrek, June 29, 2026
- **Martha Avila wrongful death:** Filed June 23, 2026, Harris County District Court. 76-year-old killed inside her home when Tesla Model 3 allegedly on Autopilot crashed through brick wall at high speed. June 19, 2026 incident. Seeking >$1M plus punitive damages.
  Source: People.com, Fox26 Houston, June 2026
- **Prior Autopilot verdict:** $243M federal jury verdict (referenced in Autoblog)
- **DOJ criminal probe** into Tesla's self-driving claims (referenced in Electrek)

### 4. Criminal Prosecution
- **Michael Butler charged with manslaughter:** 44-year-old driver in Avila crash, charged by Harris County. Told police car was on Autopilot. No signs of intoxication. Tesla's VP Ashok Elluswamy posted on X that "driver manually overrode self-driving by pressing accelerator all the way to 100%."
  Source: Fox26 Houston, July 2, 2026

## Key Numbers for Article
- 99%: Decline in phantom braking complaints (300 → 3)
- 4: Tesla probes NHTSA closed in 2026
- 3.2M: Vehicles in the still-open FSD engineering analysis
- $14.5B: Combined lawsuit exposure
- $243M: Largest Autopilot jury verdict
- 1: Pedestrian deaths (Story case, settled)
- 1: Grandmother killed inside her own home (Avila case)
- 1: Manslaughter charge filed

## Strongest Counterargument
NHTSA's complaint-based system is working as designed. Software updates that reduce phantom braking from 300 incidents to 3 represent real safety improvement. Litigation inherently lags — cases reaching court now reflect incidents from years prior. The two channels aren't contradicting each other; they measure different time horizons. And the FSD engineering analysis (3.2M vehicles) IS still open, showing NHTSA hasn't abandoned oversight.

## Limitations
- Settlement terms undisclosed; can't quantify Story case outcome
- NHTSA complaint counts are self-selected, not representative of all incidents
- "Lawsuit exposure" ($14.5B) is plaintiff-estimated, not adjudicated
- Fleet size growth could explain some litigation increase independent of technology risk
- Avila crash cause still under investigation; Autopilot engagement not yet confirmed by data
