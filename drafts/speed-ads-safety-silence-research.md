# Research Notes: Speed Ads, Safety Silence

## Angle
IIHS study (May 12, 2026) found 43% of U.S. vehicle ads emphasize speed/performance while only 8% mention safety. Safety-themed ads collapsed from 11% to 3% between 2018-2022. Meanwhile, 11,288 people died in speed-related crashes in 2024. The U.S. is virtually alone among developed nations in not regulating auto advertising content for safety.

## Journalist
Dale Impactor III — behavioral/cultural data story

## Kill Test
Is this genuinely newsworthy? YES.
- Novel IIHS study released May 12, 2026
- Quantifies something everyone suspects but nobody measures: the auto industry sells speed while people die from speed
- The safety-ad collapse (11% → 3%) is a genuinely surprising finding
- U.S. regulatory gap vs. UK is concrete and actionable
- Original contribution: cross-referencing ad trends with FARS speed fatality data creates a timeline nobody has published

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: IIHS Study (May 12, 2026)
- "Speed, safety, and selling automobiles: a content analysis of television and digital advertising in 2018, 2020, and 2022"
- Lead author: Amber Woods, IIHS Research Scientist
- 1,500+ TV ads, 1,000+ internet/social media ads analyzed
- 10 coders from UVA media studies department
- Results weighted by ad spending (Nielsen Ad Intel data)
- Key findings:
  - 43% of vehicle ads highlighted speed, maneuverability, traction, stopping or power
  - Speed emphasized 2x+ as often as safety
  - Only 8% of ads highlighted safety (overall study period)
  - Safety ads: 11% (2018) → 3% (2022)
  - Speed ads: 14% (2018) → 19% (2022)
  - Traction ads: 20% (2018) → 38% (2022)
  - SUV performance ads: 28% (2018) → 45% (2022)
  - Sedan speed ads: 47% in 2020
  - Less than 1 in 10 traction ads also coded for safety
- URL: https://www.iihs.org/news/detail/more-car-ads-promote-vehicle-performance-despite-dangers-of-speed

### Source 2: FARS Speed-Related Fatalities
- 2023: 11,775 deaths (29% of all motor vehicle fatalities)
- 2024: 11,288 deaths (29% of all road deaths)
- Consistent ~29% share of all fatalities for years
- URL: https://www.iihs.org/topics/speed

### Source 3: NHTSA 2025 Data
- 36,640 total traffic deaths in 2025 (6.7% decline from 2024)
- 1.10 fatalities per 100M VMT
- Speed remains ~29% of all fatal crashes
- URL: https://www.nhtsa.gov/

### Source 4: UK Advertising Standards
- UK ASA/BCAP Code prohibits ads encouraging dangerous driving
- Explicit restrictions on messages about power, acceleration, handling unless clearly safety-related
- "Speed should not be the dominant message" — UK regulatory standard
- US has no comparable federal regulation; relies on broadcaster self-regulation

### Source 5: IIHS ISA Study
- Study found 60% of U.S. drivers accept advisory speed warnings
- 50% accept accelerator pedal resistance
- Only 21% accept intelligent speed limiters
- Shows cultural attachment to speed vs. safety
- URL: https://www.iihs.org/topics/speed

## Novel Contribution
**The vanishing safety ad and the constant death toll:**
- Between 2018-2022, automakers cut safety-themed advertising from 11% to 3%
- During that same period, speed-related crash deaths remained flatlined at ~11,000-12,000/year (~29% of all fatalities)
- The industry is literally increasing speed marketing while decreasing safety messaging, against a backdrop of unchanged death rates
- No other consumer product class with this kill rate could advertise this way
- Comparison: tobacco advertising was banned. Alcohol ads require "drink responsibly." Car ads showing 160mph drifts get a fine-print disclaimer about closed courses.

## Limitations
- Correlation between advertising themes and driver behavior is not proven causally
- IIHS study covers 2018-2022; more recent trends may differ
- Ad impact is difficult to isolate from other cultural factors (Fast & Furious franchise, social media car culture, etc.)
- The study weighted by ad spending, which may skew toward expensive campaigns that don't represent overall messaging

## Strongest Counterargument
- Automakers might argue that performance advertising actually promotes vehicle capability and control, not reckless driving
- Disclosure: "Professional driver on a closed course" appears in most performance ads
- Some traction/maneuverability features are genuinely safety-relevant (AWD in snow, ABS stopping power)
- The ad spending drop in safety could reflect that safety features became standard and thus less of a differentiator — not that the industry stopped caring

## Actionable Takeaway
- Buyers: prioritize IIHS safety ratings over marketing claims about performance
- Parents: recognize that SUV ads increasingly sell speed, not family safety
- Policy: support IIHS's upcoming 2027 requirement for ISA and anti-speed tech in Top Safety Pick+
- Culture: treat speed advertising with the same skepticism we learned to apply to tobacco and alcohol marketing
