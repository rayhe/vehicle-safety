# Research: 14 EVs Recalled, 9,931 ICE Deaths

## Hook
On July 14, 2026, Hyundai and Kia recalled exactly 14 EVs (6 Ioniq 5s, 7 EV6s, 1 EV9) for a battery cell defect that caused zero fires, zero crashes, zero injuries. The defect was discovered after hearing about failures in "other unnamed brands" using the same SK On battery design.

Meanwhile, Hyundai-Kia's ICE fleet appears in FARS with 9,931 combined deaths (2014-2023).

## Original Calculation
- 14 EVs recalled for theoretical fire risk with zero incidents
- 9,931 ICE deaths across the same manufacturer group (10 years of FARS data)
- **Ratio: 709 deaths per recalled EV** — for every EV they pulled off the road, their ICE cars killed 709 people
- ~993 deaths/year from Hyundai-Kia ICE fleet → 70.9 annual deaths per recalled EV
- The Hyundai Veloster alone (8.54 per 100M VMT, 598 deaths) kills at a rate that makes the theoretical battery fire risk look like a rounding error

## Counterargument
The recall system works as designed — catching defects BEFORE they cause harm is the point. The comparison is unfair because recalls address manufacturing defects, not inherent crash risk. Nobody "recalls" a car for being small and light.

**Rebuttal:** That's precisely the asymmetry worth examining. The regulatory apparatus treats a theoretical battery anomaly in 14 vehicles as urgent enough for a formal recall, while the demonstrated, quantified, annual death toll of specific ICE models is treated as the natural order of things. The system defines "defect" as a deviation from design — but if the design itself kills at 8.54 per 100M VMT (Veloster), maybe the definition needs work.

## Limitations
- FARS data is 2014-2023; EV fleet was small during most of this period
- EV death data is thin because EVs haven't been in significant numbers long enough
- Comparing recall counts (a point-in-time action) to cumulative deaths (a 10-year sum) inflates the ratio
- Battery fires and crash deaths are different failure modes — not directly comparable
- The 14-vehicle recall is a quality control catch, not a condemnation of EVs
