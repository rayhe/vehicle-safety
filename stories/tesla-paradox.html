The Tesla Model 3 Is 60x Safer Than the Honda Accord. The Model S Drives Like a Corvette.

Gemini • Clautomotive Desk
March 2, 2026

The Tesla Model 3 has an estimated fatality rate of 0.05 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled. The Honda Accord — America’s default sedan, the car your parents said was “safe” — sits at 3.07. That’s a 60-fold difference. The Model Y is even lower at 0.03. By this metric, Teslas aren’t just safe cars. They’re in a different universe.

0.05
Model 3 estimated deaths per 100M VMT — 60x lower than the Accord

Fleet size helps explain some of this: Tesla’s estimated fleet is newer, and newer cars are safer. The sales-as-fleet-proxy methodology also favors vehicles with recent high sales and low cumulative road-years. But even granting generous caveats, the magnitude is remarkable. The Model 3’s 92 total deaths across a decade of data, against an estimated fleet of 1.575 million vehicles, is an extraordinary ratio.

Now for the uncomfortable part. The Tesla Model S — the $80,000+ performance sedan that launched the brand — has an impairment rate of 24.0%. Nearly one in four Model S drivers in fatal crashes tested positive for alcohol or drugs. That’s higher than the Camaro (23.0%), higher than the Challenger (22.5%), and within spitting distance of the Corvette (26.2%).

The Model 3? A pedestrian 20.1%. The difference is the buyer. The Model S is the Tesla you buy because you want 0–60 in 2.1 seconds. The Model 3 is the Tesla you buy because you want to stop paying for gas. One attracts performance chasers with champagne budgets. The other attracts commuters. The data doesn’t judge, but it does keep score.

Source: NHTSA FARS 2014–2023. Estimated rates use sales-as-fleet proxy; Tesla’s recent market entry amplifies favorable fleet-age effects. See methodology for caveats.
