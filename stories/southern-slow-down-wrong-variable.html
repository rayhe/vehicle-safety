Operation Southern Slow Down Ends Today. Florida Has the Lowest Speeding Death Rate in America.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
July 18, 2026

Five states spent this week pulling over speeders. Florida, Alabama, Georgia, South Carolina, and Tennessee deployed extra patrols for Operation Southern Slow Down, a nine-year-old campaign built on the premise that speed kills.[1] Speed does kill, but in Florida it accounts for 11% of traffic fatalities, the lowest share of any state in the nation.[2] By Florida’s own narrower definition, 6%.[1]

11%
Florida’s speeding-related share of traffic deaths. National average: 29%.

The state anchoring a multi-state anti-speeding operation has the smallest speeding problem in America, and Tennessee, another participant, sits at 19%. The national average is 29%, dragged upward by Vermont at 54% and Alaska at 50%.[2] Operation Southern Slow Down runs in the region where speed is least relevant to who lives and who dies on the highway.

Compare that to the variable nobody is patrolling: vehicle choice. FARS data shows a 94x death-rate gap within the SUV class alone, from the Chevrolet TrailBlazer at 2.83 deaths per 100 million VMT to the Tesla Model Y at 0.03.[3] Across all types, the Ford Mustang posts a death rate 170 times higher than the Model Y. No enforcement campaign can close a gap that wide because it is baked into the crash structure and the engineering decisions made years before anyone touched a pedal.

The strongest counterargument is pragmatic: traffic stops catch more than speeders, surfacing suspended licenses, open warrants, and impaired drivers along the way. But last year’s campaign produced 7,000 tickets and eight arrests in Florida, all aimed at a factor behind roughly 411 of the state’s 3,738 annual road deaths.[1] The other 3,327 died from causes no speed trap addresses. Q1 2026 fatalities hit 0.99 per 100 million VMT, the lowest quarterly rate in a decade, and that progress came from fleet turnover and crash engineering, not from a week of radar guns.[4]

Speed is a choice, and so is which car you buy. One carries a 94x mortality spread within a single vehicle class, yet only the other gets an annual multi-state enforcement campaign. Look up your vehicle’s FARS death rate at vehicle-safety.org and decide which variable deserves more attention.
