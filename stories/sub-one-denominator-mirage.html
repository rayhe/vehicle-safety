The Fatality Rate Just Dropped Below 1.0. You Should Not Be Celebrating.

Vin Wreckage • Clautomotive Desk
July 13, 2026

NHTSA dropped its Q1 2026 numbers last week, and the press releases practically wrote themselves: 0.99 fatalities per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, the lowest quarterly reading since 2014.[1] Sub-one. Magic digit. Government officials used words like “encouraged.” Traffic safety advocates called it a “milestone.” Nobody mentioned the 7,770 bodies.

86
People killed per day on U.S. roads in Q1 2026. One every 17 minutes. That is what 0.99 looks like.

A 0.99 is a fraction, and fractions have two parts. The numerator is carnage: 7,770 people who left for work, or school, or the grocery store and never came back. Now the denominator: roughly 786 billion vehicle miles traveled between January and March. When you divide a four-digit tragedy by a twelve-digit abstraction, you get something that fits neatly into a press release. You get something that starts with zero.

Americans drove roughly 29.8 billion more miles in 2025 than in 2024, and the early data suggests 2026 is following the same trend.[2] Every additional billion miles inflates the denominator. If you held VMT constant at 2019 levels and applied 2025’s death count of 36,640, the rate would be 1.21, not 1.10.[2] [3] The rate didn’t fall below 1.0 because America solved road safety. It fell below 1.0 because America drove 300 billion more miles.

Then there’s seasonality. Q1 is always the gentlest quarter: cold weather keeps motorcyclists off the road, shorter days reduce the most lethal exposure window, and holiday travel season is over. NHTSA’s own 2025 data showed the decline stalling by summer: July dropped just 1.7% year-over-year, August just 2.5%.[4] Celebrating a Q1 rate as a trend is like weighing yourself the morning after a stomach flu and calling it a diet.

Meanwhile, the fleet is doing the real work. FARS data from 2014–2023 shows that 64% of fatalities involved vehicles built before 2010[3] — cars that predate mandatory electronic stability control, that lack automatic emergency braking, that crumple in ways their manufacturers could have fixed a decade earlier. Those cars are rusting into scrapyards at the natural rate of fleet turnover: roughly 6–7% of the vehicle population per year, replaced by SUVs and crossovers with higher hood heights and better crash structures. Fatality rates are falling because the deadliest cars are dying of old age. Nobody made a policy decision. Nobody innovated. Entropy did it.

That 0.99 is real. It is also, genuinely, the least interesting number NHTSA published last week. But the interesting number is 86. Eighty-six people per day, every day, for ninety days. One every seventeen minutes. The rate could drop to 0.50 tomorrow if Americans drove twice as many miles. Would that be progress?

What you should do: Check your vehicle’s model year. If it’s pre-2012, it likely lacks electronic stability control, side curtain airbags that meet current standards, or any form of automatic emergency braking. Your single most effective safety upgrade is not a dashcam or a better tire. It is a newer car. Run your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls regardless. And the next time someone tells you the roads are getting safer because the rate dropped below 1.0, ask them how many people that 0.99 represents. The answer is 86 a day.
