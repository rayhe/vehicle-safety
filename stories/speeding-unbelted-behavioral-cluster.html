Half the Speeders Killing Themselves Aren't Wearing Seatbelts. NHTSA Runs Two Separate Campaigns Anyway.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
July 21, 2026

NHTSA runs two marquee enforcement campaigns every year. "Click It or Ticket" goes after seatbelt scofflaws. "Speeding Slows You Down" targets lead-foots. Separate budgets. Separate ad buys. Separate press conferences. Separate slogans. FARS 2024 data says they're chasing the same driver.[1]

50%
Speeding drivers in fatal crashes who were also unrestrained

Among passenger vehicle drivers in fatal crashes with known restraint use, half of those who were speeding were also unbelted. For non-speeding drivers, the number is 21%. That is a 2.4-to-1 ratio, and it is not a statistical fluke across a thin dataset. FARS logged 11,288 speeding-related fatalities in 2024, 29% of all 39,254 traffic deaths.[2]

Add alcohol and the cluster tightens. Among speeding drivers aged 21 to 54 in fatal crashes, 43% or more were also alcohol-impaired at BAC 0.08 or above. Under 21, it drops to 33%, which still means a third of teenage speeders who died were also drunk. The behavioral triad of speeding, not buckling up, and drinking isn't three independent dice rolls. It's one risk profile expressing itself across three measurable dimensions.[3]

Geography makes it worse. Vermont's speeding-related fatality share is 54%. Florida's is 11%. A five-to-one difference within the same country, under the same federal safety framework. Some of that gap is road type and weather. Vermont has mountain passes and black ice; Florida has flat interstates and sunshine. But enforcement culture matters too. Florida runs aggressive speed enforcement on I-95 and I-4. Vermont's state police cover 600 miles of interstate with roughly 330 troopers.[4]

NHTSA's own July 2026 campaign launch acknowledged the decline. Speeding-related fatalities fell an estimated 11% in 2025 to 10,035 deaths, back to pre-pandemic levels last seen in 2019. Administrator Jonathan Morrison stood in Lebanon, Tennessee with a NASCAR team owner and a victim advocate to announce it.[5] Progress. Real progress. Except the campaigns still treat the behaviors as isolated. "Click It or Ticket" runs in May. "Speeding Slows You Down" runs in July. If your enforcement calendar handles them as separate problems, your funding model does too. And the data says the person doing 95 on the interstate without a seatbelt is also the one at 0.10 BAC.

This is not a call for a single unified campaign. Seatbelt laws and speed enforcement have different legal frameworks, different officer protocols, and different judicial outcomes. But FARS is screaming something that the campaign structure refuses to hear: these behaviors cluster in the same individuals. A stop for speeding is a seatbelt check. A seatbelt violation is a reason to look for impairment. The data supports a sequential enforcement model where one observed behavior triggers assessment for the others. Several states already do this informally. None do it as a codified protocol with data-driven thresholds.

A caveat: FARS only captures fatal crashes. The behavioral cluster might look different at lower severity. And correlation is not causation. Speed, non-use of restraints, and alcohol may all spring from a common root variable — risk tolerance, age, rural driving culture, or something FARS doesn't code — rather than directly producing each other. The directional insight holds regardless: stopping one behavior creates an enforcement opportunity to screen for the others.

What this means for you: Wear your seatbelt. Always. If you routinely drive 10+ over the limit, know that FARS data puts you in a statistical cohort where half the people who die in crashes like yours weren't wearing one. The seatbelt won't slow you down. It might keep your funeral off the FARS dataset. And if you see someone weaving through traffic unbelted, give them room. In fatal crashes, that behavioral profile clusters hard with alcohol impairment.
