Drunk Trucking Surged 26% in 2024. Nobody Else Got Worse.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
July 8, 2026

NHTSA released its 2024 FARS annual overview in April, and buried in Table 6 on page 8, between neatly formatted percentage signs and bureaucratic line items arranged to suggest orderly progress, sits a number the agency's own press release chose not to mention: 224 alcohol-impaired large truck drivers were involved in fatal crashes in 2024, up from 178 in 2023, a 26-percent jump that added forty-six more drunk operators behind the wheel of vehicles that weigh 80,000 pounds fully loaded.

+26%
Year-over-year increase in alcohol-impaired large truck drivers in fatal crashes, 2023 to 2024

Every other vehicle category moved the opposite direction. Passenger car impaired drivers fell 5 percent, light trucks dropped 1.8 percent, and motorcycles declined 3 percent, bringing the total down by 357 drivers across all vehicle types for a 3-percent overall improvement that the government was eager to celebrate.[1] Large trucks stood alone as the single category going backwards, their impaired-driver share ticking quietly from 3 to 4 percent of all truck drivers involved in fatal crashes.

Forty-six additional drunk truckers sounds trivially small when you stack it against 11,367 total impaired drivers nationwide. But a drunk person piloting a Camry and a drunk person commanding a Peterbilt 579 present fundamentally different threats to everyone else on the road, because physics doesn't grade on a curve and mass always wins the argument when 80,000 pounds meets 3,500 at highway speed. In 2024, large truck crashes killed 5,340 people, and of those deaths, 3,753 belonged to occupants of the other vehicle while another 667 were pedestrians and cyclists who never had a choice in the matter.[1] For every trucker who died in a multi-vehicle crash, roughly four people in the smaller vehicle did not survive the encounter, a ratio baked into the weight differential that no amount of crumple-zone engineering in a sedan or minivan can meaningfully overcome.

The injury data tells a story of escalation rather than the steady improvement NHTSA would prefer to narrate. People injured in large truck crashes climbed 5 percent to 161,201 in 2024, with occupants of other vehicles accounting for an 8.2-percent surge that put an additional 8,809 people in emergency rooms, operating tables, and rehabilitation wards they never expected to visit. Single-vehicle large truck injuries spiked 12 percent in a single year.[1] That last number matters because it strips away the usual excuses about car drivers cutting off truckers or merging recklessly into blind spots; when the crash involves only the truck and the truck's driver, the problem is the trucker.

The timing makes the data politically awkward, because Transportation Secretary Duffy chose the very press event announcing these figures to brag about "cracking down on unqualified truckers driving big rigs," with the FMCSA administrator standing beside him on stage, both men celebrating the overall 4.3-percent decline in traffic deaths as though every vehicle category were participating equally in the good news.[2] The random alcohol testing rate for CDL holders sits at 10 percent, where it has collected dust since 2016, while the random drug testing rate runs five times higher at 50 percent because FMCSA raised it in 2020 when industry drug positives crossed 1 percent.[3] Nobody has applied the same logic to alcohol, and nobody standing at that podium volunteered an explanation for why not.

The strongest case against alarm is statistical, and it deserves to be stated at full strength rather than dismissed: 224 is a small count, a 26-percent increase on a base of 178 could represent year-to-year noise rather than a genuine inflection, and FARS impairment figures include imputed BAC values where testing data is unavailable, meaning the apparent change could partly reflect differences in testing compliance across vehicle categories rather than real behavioral shifts. All fair. But "wait for more data" carries a different weight when the vehicle in question outweighs a family sedan by a factor of twenty, because the cost of being wrong about noise versus signal is not an academic embarrassment but a trail of crushed passenger compartments along interstate medians. One year of 26-percent growth in any other safety metric would trigger an immediate NHTSA investigation. This one got six cells in a table nobody read aloud at the podium.

What you can do: The FMCSA Drug & Alcohol Clearinghouse at clearinghouse.fmcsa.dot.gov lets employers query whether a CDL holder has violated federal drug or alcohol regulations before making a hiring decision. If you manage commercial drivers, query it before you hand someone the keys to a rig that weighs as much as twelve Honda Civics. FMCSA can raise the 10-percent random alcohol testing threshold administratively, the same way they raised the drug threshold six years ago. They haven't. That decision should bother anyone who shares a highway with an 18-wheeler.
