The Safest Sedan in America Has a 67-Death Blind Spot

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
July 6, 2026

By every aggregate measure, the Toyota Prius is the safest sedan you can buy. FARS puts its fatality rate at 0.55 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, 3.4 times lower than the Corolla, 4.1 times lower than the Civic, 5.6 times lower than the Accord.[1] Its impairment rate is the lowest of any major sedan at 16%. Across 787,500 registered vehicles, the Prius averages 49.5 deaths per year. A model with that record should have a clean model-year distribution: steady, unremarkable, flat. It doesn’t.

2.98×
2010 model year deaths vs. adjacent-year average

Sort FARS fatalities by model year and the 2010 Prius breaks formation. Sixty-seven deaths over the 2014–2023 study window, against 19 for the 2009 vintage and 26 for the 2011. A 2.98x spike that vanishes the moment you step to either side.[1] Walk farther into the Gen3 era and the numbers settle: 23, 20, 16, 23 for model years 2012 through 2015.[1] Only 2010 jumps.

Run the same check on every compact sedan sold that year and the anomaly isolates further. Civic 2010 shows a 1.35x bump over its neighbors, Corolla 2010 runs 1.44x, Sentra 2010 hits 1.52x.[1] Whatever happened to the 2010 Prius, it happened to the Prius alone.

What happened was two brake recalls filed three years apart for the same model year. NHTSA Campaign 10V-039, filed February 2010, covered 148,549 vehicles for an ABS software flaw that increased stopping distances on rough or slick roads.[2] Toyota reprogrammed the ECU, declared the problem solved, and moved on. Then in June 2013, Campaign 13V-235 landed: 87,060 of those same 2010 Priuses needed their brake booster pump assemblies replaced because nitrogen gas was leaking into the brake fluid and reducing hydraulic pressure.[3] For at least three years between the two recalls, some 2010 Priuses were driving around with a software patch on top of a hardware defect nobody had found yet.

Correlation is not causation, and FARS does not code crashes by contributing defect. Volume matters too: the 2010 model year was the debut of the Gen3 platform, and Toyota likely sold more 2010 units than 2009s, which were the tail end of Gen2. But 2011 was also Gen3 with comparable sales, and it logged 26 deaths, not 67.

A generation change explains some of the gap between 2009 and 2010, but it does not explain the gap between 2010 and 2011.

If you still drive a 2010 Prius, look up both recall campaigns at nhtsa.gov/recalls with your VIN. Confirm both the ABS software update and the brake booster replacement are marked complete. If you're shopping used Priuses and the 2010 model year shows up at a good price, ask for the recall completion letter before signing. Every other Gen3 vintage is statistically unremarkable, and this one isn't.
