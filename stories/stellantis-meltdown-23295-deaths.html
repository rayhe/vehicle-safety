Body Count

Stellantis Vehicles Have Killed 23,295 People. The Company Might Not Survive to Fix the Problem.

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
July 5, 2026

Stellantis N.V. has a market capitalization of roughly $18 billion.[1] It also has a body count of 23,295 across its American vehicle portfolio, accumulated over the FARS reporting window of 2014 through 2023.[2] That works out to approximately $773 per corpse, if you are the kind of person who does that math. I did.

23,295
Stellantis occupant deaths across 36 models, FARS 2014–2023

The parent company of Dodge, Jeep, Chrysler, Ram, and Fiat accounts for 12.2% of every fatal crash recorded in the FARS database across 36 distinct models. It places third among automaker conglomerates, behind General Motors at 51,687 deaths and Ford Motor at 37,931. Toyota sits in fourth at 23,275, a margin of twenty deaths that would barely register on a single holiday weekend. Stellantis holds the bronze by the thickness of a bumper sticker.

On February 6, 2026, Stellantis disclosed a €22.2 billion writedown and cancelled its shareholder dividend.[3] Shares dropped 23.69% in a single session. A securities fraud class-action lawsuit followed, alleging the company had concealed the scope of its deteriorating trajectory from investors throughout the class period.[4] The stock, which traded above $27 in early 2024, closed at $5.81 on July 3, 2026, roughly eighteen inches from its 52-week low of $4.91. Carlos Tavares resigned as CEO in December 2024. The company killed its entire plug-in hybrid lineup, axed the Ram EV program, and reintroduced V8 engines into multiple product lines, which is a bit like a hospital shutting down its oncology ward and reopening the smoking lounge.

So much for the balance sheet, which brings us to the fleet.

On June 9, 2026, Stellantis recalled 1,076,999 Jeep Wranglers and Gladiators, model years 2021 through 2025, because their electric hydraulic power steering pump wiring can overheat and ignite the vehicle.[5] Seventy-two fires. One injury. Fires occur with the ignition off. Park it in your garage, go to bed, wake up to smoke. NHTSA’s official guidance to the owners of a million Jeeps: park outside and away from structures. Stellantis told customers it “anticipates a solution no later than July.” July is here.

What makes the Wrangler recall interesting beyond the sheer count is the investigation timeline. Stellantis began looking into steering-pump fires in May 2023. By April 2024 it closed its own investigation, citing a “low rate of occurrence.” Four months later, more fires forced it to reopen.[5] Fourteen additional months of forensic analysis followed before the company determined in late May 2026 that the defect “posed an unreasonable risk to safety,” which is NHTSA’s statutory language for “we should probably stop setting garages on fire.” Total elapsed time from first known fire to recall: three years and one month.

Simultaneously, the company faces a separate recall of 419,000 Jeeps for airbag defects, older Stellantis vehicles remain under Takata “do not drive” orders, and a 2025 global campaign covered roughly 700,000 vehicles for additional fire risk across multiple brands.[6] Stellantis accounts for approximately 20% of all vehicles subjected to “park outside” fire advisories since 2020, a distinction it shares mainly with Hyundai and GM.[7]

Break down the FARS body count by brand and a pattern emerges. Five vehicles carry the heaviest loads:

Those five models alone account for 11,610 deaths, nearly half the total portfolio. At 4,407 occupant deaths, the Dodge Ram carries the single heaviest load, placing it among the deadliest nameplates in the entire FARS dataset regardless of manufacturer. Below them, you find the Dodge Dakota at 1,237 deaths, the Jeep Grand Cherokee at 1,161, the Jeep Liberty at 801, the Chrysler 200 at 772, and the Chrysler 300 at 751. Discontinued models like the Dodge Neon (602 deaths) and Chrysler PT Cruiser (653) still generate fatalities because their fleets remain on American roads, aging without modern safety equipment and increasingly without manufacturer support.

Stellantis and Toyota sit so close in the body count that rounding could swap them. But the comparison obscures an important asymmetry in how each conglomerate fills its showrooms. Toyota sold 2.33 million vehicles in the United States in 2024.[8] Stellantis sold approximately 1.4 million.[9] Nearly identical death totals from a substantially smaller fleet means more deaths per vehicle sold. Run the division and Stellantis’s FARS death toll relative to its annual US sales volume is 67% higher than Toyota’s. It is a crude metric, since FARS spans ten years and sales fluctuate annually, but it controls for the otherwise obvious objection that Toyota simply sells more cars.

A company in financial distress is a company making trade-offs, and trade-offs in automotive engineering have body counts. Stellantis has already cut headcount, idled plants, abandoned electrification programs, and reorganized its North American operations under interim leadership. The question for consumers is whether recall remedies, engineering resources, and quality control survive the same austerity measures being applied to dividends and development budgets. When Stellantis closed its own Wrangler fire investigation in April 2024 because the fire rate was “low,” fifty-one fires had already been documented; by the time of the recall filing, the count had reached seventy-two. The rate was not low; the threshold for caring was.

Counterargument, and it deserves its full weight: FARS data covers 2014 through 2023, a period that includes many discontinued Stellantis models and predates current safety improvements. Several high-death-count vehicles in the table (Grand Caravan, Town & Country, Dakota, Neon, PT Cruiser) are no longer in production, and their remaining road fleet shrinks every year. Stellantis’s current lineup includes vehicles with competitive IIHS ratings, and the company’s recall volume, while staggering, at least represents defects being identified and addressed rather than ignored. A smaller automaker will naturally have fewer total deaths than GM or Ford regardless of per-vehicle safety performance, so the absolute body count comparison has real methodological limits. If Stellantis survives its financial crisis and maintains its recall commitments, the death toll should decline as older vehicles age out of the fleet.

Those are legitimate points, and they deserve the weight I just gave them. But “our most dangerous cars are discontinued” is a defense that only works if the company also stops setting the current ones on fire. As of this writing, 1,076,999 Wrangler and Gladiator owners have been told to park their vehicles outside while Stellantis finalizes a remedy it has been working on for three years. The company’s stock price is $5.81, and its FARS body count is 23,295. One of those numbers is going in the right direction.
