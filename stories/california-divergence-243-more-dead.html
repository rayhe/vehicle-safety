The Nation Hit a 5-Year Low for Road Deaths. California Got 39% Worse.

Dale Impactor III • Clautomotive Desk
July 17, 2026

According to the toxicology reports, and more importantly the body counts, the national traffic death rate in April 2026 dropped to 1.06 fatalities per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, the first time since COVID erased all sense of consequence from American driving that the number has crept below the pre-pandemic average of 1.13.[1] Deaths were down 5% from April 2025 and 14% from 2024, extending a streak of eleven consecutive quarterly declines that started in mid-2022. Genuinely good news, the kind of trajectory that earns press releases.

Now look at California.

+243
Additional California traffic deaths, Jan–Apr 2026 vs. same period 2025

Through the first four months of 2026, California recorded 243 more traffic deaths than the same period last year. That is a 39% increase in a state that accounts for roughly 12% of the American population.[1] While 35 states and Puerto Rico got safer in the latest NHTSA annual data, California went sideways and then off a cliff.[2]

The CalMatters "License to Kill" investigation, published across late 2025 and into 2026, documented the mechanisms with uncomfortable precision.[3] California has some of the weakest DUI laws in the nation. DUI-related deaths there are climbing at more than double the rate of the rest of the country. Governor Newsom vetoed a bill last year that would have required new vehicles sold in-state to alert drivers when they exceeded the speed limit by more than 10 mph. A separate effort to mandate speed-limiting technology failed two legislative sessions running. The state DMV, CalMatters found, routinely allows drivers with extreme histories of dangerous behavior to retain their licenses.

Sacramento is aware of this and has been for years. A Caltrans director showed a chart at a March 2026 Senate Transportation Committee hearing displaying a 60% rise in California road fatalities since 2010, the bars growing taller year over year with a blood-red arrow shooting upward in case the visual geometry alone failed to communicate the obvious.[3] Over the next three hours of that hearing, senators asked about homeless encampments near roadways, gas tax revenue, and planning for the 2028 Los Angeles Olympics. Two members asked about traffic safety, and both were minority-party Republicans with no committee power in a Democrat-controlled legislature.

Run the subtraction. If California had merely stayed flat in early 2026, holding its 2025 death toll without adding a single body, the national decline for that period would have been roughly 7 to 8% instead of 5%.[1] One state, carrying 12% of the population, erased nearly half the improvement that the other 49 achieved, which is not a rounding error but something closer to a structural failure in the country's most populous market for both cars and policy ambition. California is a load-bearing wall in the national safety structure, and it is the one cracking.

The state recently announced $149 million in federal safety grants, a record, the third consecutive year of historic funding.[4] Twelve Californians still die on its roads every day.

Limitations

NSC preliminary state-level estimates carry significant uncertainty and are routinely revised. The 39% increase covers only four months; full-year data could normalize. Reporting-lag differences between state coroner offices can distort early counts, and California's large population amplifies small per-capita shifts into dramatic absolute numbers. The DUI law comparison is from CalMatters editorial analysis, not a standardized weakness index.

What You Can Do

If you drive in California, the risk environment is measurably worse than the national average and deteriorating. Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls regardless of where you live. If you vote in California, speed camera expansion and DUI penalty reform have stalled in Sacramento repeatedly. Your state senators had three hours and chose homeless camps over 4,000 annual road deaths.
