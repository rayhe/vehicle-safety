The Gap

Consumer Reports Ranked Lincoln the Fourth-Safest Brand in America. The Federal Body Count Disagrees.

Vin Wreckage • Clautomotive Desk
July 17, 2026

In February 2026, Consumer Reports unveiled its first-ever Safety Verdict, ranking 29 automotive brands on crashworthiness, braking, handling, control ergonomics, and standard safety tech.[1] The top four: Mazda, Genesis, Acura, Lincoln. Lincoln landed above Toyota, Honda, Subaru, and Volvo, which is the kind of ranking that makes you wonder who did the counting. This is Ford's luxury division, the brand whose parent company has recalled 19.5 million vehicles in the past twelve months, more than every other automaker combined.[2] That sounded strange, so we ran the FARS data, and what came back was uncomfortable.

7.30 vs. 3.66
Lincoln's brand-level FARS death rate compared to Acura's, the brand ranked one spot above it

FARS doesn't grade new cars in a lab. It counts corpses. Dead people on real roads across ten years of real driving. Between 2014 and 2023, Lincoln's fleet accumulated 1,137 deaths at a rate of 7.30 per our VMT-normalized index.[3] Acura, ranked third in CR's verdict, logged 862 deaths at 3.66. Mazda, the top-ranked brand, came in at 4.23 with 1,432 deaths across a much larger fleet. Lincoln's real-world death rate across the full fleet is nearly double Acura's and 72% higher than Mazda's, which is not a rounding disagreement between two safety methodologies. Consumer Reports said Lincoln was their peer; the federal data said otherwise.

The gap persists even when you strip out legacy sedans and compare only the modern crossovers that Lincoln and Acura are both currently selling at comparable price points to overlapping demographics in the same suburban dealership corridors. Lincoln's MKC: 0.79 deaths per 100 million VMT, while Acura's comparably sized RDX clocks 0.13. That's a sixfold difference for vehicles occupying the same showroom tier, the same price bracket, the same parking lot at the suburban pediatrician. Consider the Lincoln Aviator, the specific vehicle that iSeeCars identified as carrying 23.4 times the industry-average recall rate, the single most-recalled nameplate in America.[2] Its FARS rate sits at 1.15. The Acura MDX, its closest competitor in luxury three-row SUVs, manages 0.30, nearly four to one.

Why does Lincoln test well and die badly? Because Consumer Reports evaluates what rolls off the assembly line. FARS measures what happens after 50,000 miles, after three recall notices, after the seat-belt pretensioner that was supposed to get replaced sits in a glovebox envelope for two years because the dealer is an hour away and nobody died yet. CR's methodology explicitly includes standard safety equipment, and Lincoln loads every model with adaptive cruise, blind-spot monitoring, and automatic emergency braking at no extra cost, which earns high marks on paper. But the CR reliability survey tells the rest of the story: Lincoln ranked twentieth out of twenty-six brands, with a predicted reliability score of 40.[4] Acura ranked seventh at 54. Safety features only work as long as the vehicle does, and Lincoln's vehicles have a documented, quantifiable, recall-drenched habit of not working for very long after they leave the showroom floor.

The Lincoln Aviator's projected lifetime recall rate versus the industry average (iSeeCars, 2026)

Strongest objection first: FARS includes older vehicles. Lincoln's Town Car, discontinued in 2011 with a lethality ratio of 0.805 (four out of five fatal-crash involvements produced a dead body), still populates the fleet in livery services and senior garages across the country. Its death rate of 0.86 anchors the brand average upward, and that's fair enough as a criticism. But Acura's fleet includes the original Integra and the RSX, cheap sport compacts from the 2000s with lethality ratios above 0.77, driven hard by demographics that skew young and aggressive. Those vehicles drag Acura's average up too. Half. Acura's brand average, weighed down by the same legacy-fleet problem Lincoln faces, still lands at half of Lincoln's rate.

So what should you actually do with this information? If you trust Consumer Reports, trust all of it: the Safety Verdict that ranked Lincoln fourth and the reliability survey that ranked it twentieth, because those two numbers are not contradictory. They measure the same brand at different stages of its life, and the second stage is the one where people actually die. If you're cross-shopping a Lincoln Corsair against an Acura RDX, the sticker prices overlap around $43,000 to $48,000, which means you're paying roughly the same money for two radically different odds of coming home. At 0.13, the RDX's FARS rate barely registers, while the Corsair doesn't appear individually in FARS, but its platform sibling, the MKC, runs 0.79, which is six times higher. Lincoln's interior will be nicer, probably, and Acura's survival rate will be nicer, definitely. Choose accordingly. Or don't. The morgue doesn't check your Consumer Reports subscription.
