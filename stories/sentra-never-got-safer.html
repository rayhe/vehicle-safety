Every Competitor Got Safer. The Nissan Sentra Got Worse.

Mia Crumplezone • Clautomotive Desk
July 15, 2026

Between model years 2015 and 2019, Toyota rebuilt the Corolla on an entirely new platform, stiffened its body, rewired its crash energy management, and watched its exposure-adjusted FARS death count drop 46%. Hyundai pulled off something similar with the Elantra, delivering a new architecture with a stiffer body and 13% fewer deaths. Honda redesigned the Civic around an ultra-high-strength steel passenger cell and shed 11%.

Nissan's Sentra, competing in exactly the same segment against exactly the same buyers, went up 63%.[1]

+63%
increase in the Sentra’s exposure-adjusted death count from MY2015 to MY2019, while every competitor declined

How We Got That Number

FARS data covers 2014 through 2023, which means a 2015 model year vehicle had nine years to accumulate fatal crashes in that window while a 2019 had only five. You would therefore expect roughly 56% of the earlier count just from reduced time on the road, before any engineering improvement shows up at all. Anything below that baseline means the car got genuinely safer across generations; anything above it means something went backwards, because even standing still should produce a decline.

On a per-year-of-exposure basis, MY2019 Sentras killed at a rate of 34.6 deaths per year compared to 21.2 for MY2015 vehicles. During those seven model years from 2013 through 2019, Nissan's B17-generation Sentra rode the same platform with the same crash structure geometry and the same rear torsion beam, receiving only cosmetic refreshes and infotainment updates while Toyota, Honda, and Hyundai were redesigning their structural bones.[3]

What Changed Everywhere Except Yokohama

Toyota's TNGA platform widened the Corolla's stance, dropped its center of gravity by 10mm, and introduced multi-link rear suspension that keeps tires planted during the kind of emergency lane changes where an older torsion-beam car would rotate and roll.[2] Honda rolled out its Sensing suite of automatic emergency braking, lane-keep assist, and adaptive cruise across the 10th-generation Civic lineup, making it standard or available on every trim by model year 2019. Hyundai's third-generation Elantra brought hot-stamped ultra-high-strength steel into its roof structure and redesigned how frontal impact energy routes through the A-pillars.

Nissan, across those same years, added Apple CarPlay and tweaked the grille. Neither of those things absorbs a frontal offset impact, and neither redirects crash energy away from the occupant cell.

Five Recalls in Fifteen Months

When Nissan finally replaced the B17 with the B18-generation Sentra for model year 2020, the redesign brought better IIHS scores and a new platform, but it also brought a manufacturing quality record that raises its own set of questions.[4][5][6] In November 2025, Nissan recalled 41,797 Sentras because misaligned locator pins in glass molds at its Aguascalientes II plant in Mexico caused windshield air bubbles that obscured driver visibility (campaign 25V807). By January 2026, another 6,988 Sentras were swept into a 26,432-vehicle recall after improperly welded door strikers threatened to let doors swing open at highway speed (26V023). March 2026 brought a front seat frame welding failure where seats could come loose under crash load (26V145), and April added another seat frame campaign with failing hook brackets. Then on July 13, 2026, Nissan recalled 946 more vehicles because a supplier tooling error at the same Aguascalientes II plant left driveshafts unseated, causing loss of drive power and rollaway risk after parking (26V410).

Windshields that delaminate, doors that unlatch at speed, seats that break free in a collision, and driveshafts that walk out of the transmission all trace back to the same facility that built the B17 for seven years. Nissan changed the platform, but the manufacturing discipline at the plant that produces nearly every Sentra sold in North America has not kept pace with the engineering ambitions of the redesign.

Strongest Case Against This Analysis

Sales volumes vary by model year, and FARS does not track per-model-year registrations. If Nissan sold substantially more MY2019 Sentras than MY2015s, the higher death count could partly reflect a bigger fleet rather than a more dangerous car, and compact sedan sales were lumpy across this period as buyers migrated to SUVs and reshuffled each model's volume year to year. Without per-model-year registration data to normalize against, this analysis measures directional divergence between segment competitors rather than absolute per-vehicle risk; the direction is unambiguous, but the magnitude could shift with fleet data we don't have.

FARS also captures only fatal crashes, so the 2,571 Sentra deaths across 2014 through 2023 represent a fraction of total Sentra-involved crashes. Annualized rates here divide cumulative deaths by years of exposure in the FARS window, which does not account for fleet attrition (older model years lose units to scrappage), newer-car driving intensity (recently purchased vehicles accumulate more VMT annually), or shifting demographic patterns among the buyers attracted to each successive model year.

What You Should Do

If you own a 2020 through 2026 Sentra, run your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls today, because five recalls in fifteen months means the probability of at least one open campaign on your specific vehicle is not trivial. If you are shopping for a used compact sedan in the 2015 through 2019 range and comparing a Sentra against a Corolla or Civic from the same years, the engineering trajectory data shows that the Corolla and Civic were actively getting safer across those model years while the Sentra's platform stood still, and that gap compounds as vehicles age because structural improvements don't degrade the way infotainment features do. For the 2020-and-newer Sentra, the platform is genuinely better designed on paper, but whether Aguascalientes II is building it to spec is a separate question the recall record keeps asking.
