Body Count

GM Killed Seven Sedans. Those Sedans Have Killed 7,370 People Since.

Clara Rollover • Clautomotive Desk
July 14, 2026

Between 2005 and 2011, General Motors discontinued seven models: the Cobalt, Cavalier, Grand Prix, G6, LeSabre, Century, and HHR. Corporate called it portfolio optimization; FARS calls it 7,370 deaths.

7,370
Deaths in discontinued GM models, 2014–2023. Every victim was driving a car GM had already stopped making.

Every one of those fatalities occurred in a vehicle already out of production. The youngest Cobalt on the road in 2023 was 13 years old; the youngest Cavalier was 18. These aren't cars — they're rolling fossils with airbag software from the Bush administration, and NHTSA says only 37% of vehicles that old ever get their recall repairs done.[1]

Cobalt leads the body count at 1,540 deaths and a fatality rate of 5.10 per 100 million vehicle miles traveled. For reference, the Toyota Corolla, which is still in production and still receiving crash-avoidance updates, sits at 1.85. That's a kill rate 2.8 times higher, in a car that hasn't seen a design improvement since Obama's first term. Corolla's rate benefits from newer model years still on the line. That's the point.[2] Cobalt was also ground zero for GM's ignition switch scandal, a defect linked to 124 confirmed deaths and a recall that landed in 2014, four years after the last Cobalt rolled off the line.[3]

GM isn't alone in this. Across all manufacturers, 12 discontinued models produced 11,907 deaths in the same FARS window. Dodge's Dakota added 1,237, and Mercury's Grand Marquis tacked on 1,153. Ford's Crown Victoria, beloved by police departments and taxi fleets, contributed 881. But GM owns 62% of the total orphan body count, because GM discontinued more cheap sedans than anyone else and replaced them with nothing in the same price bracket.

NHTSA's recall completion reports quantify the decay: a brand-new Camry recalled the year it's sold gets fixed 88% of the time. Recall that same Camry when it's 11 years old, and the completion rate drops to 37%.[1] Between 2015 and 2024, another 2.6 million vehicles (industry-wide, not just GM) had airbag-deployment defects that remained unrepaired, roughly 22% of the total recalled population.[4] Orphan fleets sit at the worst end of that curve: old enough that owners skip the recall, cheap enough that nobody tracks the VIN, and discontinued long enough that dealers have moved on.

Every one of these seven GM models predates the 2012 ESC mandate. None shipped with automatic emergency braking or forward collision warning. The safety technology froze the day the assembly line stopped, and nothing will ever unfreeze it. If you're shopping for a used car under $5,000, check safercar.gov before you check Craigslist. That Cobalt might be cheap for a reason.

What to do: If you drive any of these seven GM models, enter your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls. A recall repair costs you nothing, and it might save your life.
