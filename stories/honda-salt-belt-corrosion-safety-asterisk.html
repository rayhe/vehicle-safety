Honda’s Safest Vehicles Have an Asterisk. It’s Your Zip Code.

Vin Wreckage • Clautomotive Desk
July 1, 2026

The Honda Pilot kills at 0.29 deaths per 100 million vehicle miles traveled, making it one of the safest SUVs ever sold in America by FARS data, and the Ridgeline holds the lowest fatality rate among pickup trucks while the Acura MDX sits near the top of the luxury crossover class. Not marketing claims, but body counts.

Unless you live in Ohio, Michigan, or any of 21 other states where Honda just recalled 880,514 of these vehicles because their rear suspensions may corrode until the wheels come loose.[1]

880,514
Pilots, Ridgelines, Passports, and MDXs recalled in 23 salt-belt states for rear suspension corrosion

NHTSA Recall 26V367000 covers vehicles sold in 23 states and D.C. that salt their roads, from Connecticut to Wisconsin.[2] A Pilot bought in Palo Alto does not have this problem, while an identical Pilot purchased in Pittsburgh might lose a rear wheel on I-376 because its subframe was exposed to fundamentally different road chemistry.

The defect is mechanical, old-fashioned, and entirely preventable: a supplier called F&P Georgia manufactured rear subframes with coating specs that fell short of standard. Paint peeled, road salt found exposed metal, and corrosion ate through the mounting points for the rear control arms, which Honda says can cause wheel misalignment, loss of stability, and brake failure.[3]

Honda identified a paint quality difference in December 2021, and its supplier improved the process by August 2022. But the first field report of premature corrosion arrived September 2025, and Honda did not formally declare a safety defect until May 28, 2026, four years from bad paint to official defect.[4]

Credit where earned: zero injuries, zero deaths, zero warranty claims, and Honda estimates only 1% of recalled vehicles actually carry the defect, making this a proactive recall caught before catastrophe.[5]

But this is also Honda's third corrosion recall affecting rear suspension and subframe components, following earlier campaigns on the CR-V and older Ridgeline, and a recurring defect in the same structural area from the same supply chain stops being bad luck at some point and becomes a procurement pattern.[4]

The uncomfortable finding extends beyond Honda: a FARS death rate measures a national average across all 50 states, and it cannot tell you whether your specific car in your specific climate is the car the data describes. That 0.29 was calculated across the entire country, including 27 states where the subframe coating was never challenged by salt, and no safety rating currently discloses this geographic asterisk.

Owners in the 23 affected states will receive notification letters starting July 7, 2026, and dealers will inspect the rear subframe and install a reinforcement kit at no cost. If you drive a 2016–2022 Pilot, 2017–2023 Ridgeline, 2019–2023 Passport, or 2014–2020 MDX in a state that salts its roads, check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls before the letter arrives.
