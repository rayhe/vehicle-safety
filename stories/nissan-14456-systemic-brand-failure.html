Every Nissan Sedan Is Above the Death Rate Median. Every Single One.

Vin Wreckage • Clautomotive Desk
July 5, 2026.

Nineteen Nissan models appear in the federal fatality database, and thirteen of them rank above the death-rate median for their vehicle class.[1] Chance predicts roughly half; Nissan hits 68%, which means fourteen thousand four hundred fifty-six people climbed into a car whose brand had already tilted the odds against them.

7.5×
The Nissan Maxima's death rate versus the sedan class median — the single deadliest sedan in the entire FARS dataset

Start with the sedans, where the Maxima kills at 5.11 per 100 million vehicle-miles traveled against a class median of 0.68, making it the single deadliest sedan in the entire FARS dataset. The Altima sits at 2.88, the Sentra at 2.13, and the Versa at 0.90. All four Nissan sedans rank above the class median with zero exceptions and a combined body count of 9,624.

Demographics explain some of it, as Nissan's buyers skew younger and lower-income and the Altima anchors rental fleets with high-turnover drivers. But Toyota's Corolla and Camry sell to overlapping buyers at overlapping price points and sit at the 63rd and 66th percentiles respectively, while every single Nissan sedan clusters in the worst quartile of its class.

SUVs offer no shelter either, because the Xterra runs at 4.0 times the SUV class median, the Pathfinder at 2.7 times, the Kicks at 2.0 times, and the Armada, Murano, and Juke all land above the line.[1] Only the Rogue, Nissan's volume crossover with 2.2 million registered, lands exactly at the median, making their best-seller their only safe car and leaving you to draw your own conclusions about what motivates the engineering when commercial survival depends on it.

The recalls compound the picture further, with four separate safety campaigns landing in 2026's first six months alone, culminating in a July 3 Sentra driveshaft recall for sudden power loss and rollaway risk.[2] That cadence comes from a company projecting a $4.2 billion annual loss,[3] and IIHS underlined the pattern when the redesigned Kicks earned "Poor" in the moderate overlap front test last year.[4] A single model failing a crash test generates a news cycle, but thirteen of nineteen models ranking above the death-rate median somehow generates nothing.

Limitations: FARS captures fatal crashes only, so Nissan could outperform in non-fatal survivability and this data would not reflect it. Fleet and VMT estimates carry ±15% uncertainty that widens for low-volume models, and we cannot fully isolate build quality from road type or fleet age effects.

What you should do: If you own a 2025 Sentra, check nhtsa.gov/recalls for the driveshaft campaign now. If you are shopping any Nissan sedan, pull the IIHS rating for your exact model year before signing. Cross-shopping a Nissan crossover against a Honda HR-V (death rate 0.13) or Subaru Crosstrek (0.08) deserves the same scrutiny, because the sticker prices are close but the survival odds are not.
