One Brand, Two Vehicles, a 13× Death-Rate Gap

Axle McScatter • Clautomotive Desk
July 17, 2026

I ran the numbers on every Mazda in the FARS database. Then I stared at them for a while, because a single brand should not contain a vehicle at 0.12 deaths per 100 million VMT and another at 1.63. Most of that gap traces to older generations of the Mazda3, but the 10-year blended rate is still what shows up when you do the math, and it paints a picture that Mazda’s brand ranking never mentions.

13.6×
Death-rate gap between the CX-5 and the Mazda3, same brand, same showroom

On July 9, the IIHS awarded the redesigned 2026 CX-5 its highest honor: Top Safety Pick+.[1] A day later, Consumer Reports confirmed what the crash-test footage already showed: Mazda is the safest overall brand in America.[2] Nobody mentioned the Mazda3.

That omission matters. Over the FARS reporting window (2014–2023), the CX-5 accumulated 162 fatalities across an estimated fleet of 1.05 million vehicles. In that same decade, the Mazda3 accumulated 491 fatalities from a fleet one-quarter the size, producing three times the death toll from dramatically fewer registrations.[3] Per mile, the Mazda3 lands at 1.63, slotting it between the Hyundai Elantra (1.50) and the Volkswagen Jetta (1.71) in the compact-sedan class. If you want a compact sedan with a lower FARS rate, the Kia Forte (0.40) and Chevy Cruze (0.63) are where the data points.[3]

Toxicology does not explain the split. Mazda3 drivers test alcohol-positive 17.0% of the time in fatal crashes, versus 14.2% for CX-5 drivers.[3] A three-point spread in impairment does not produce a thirteen-fold spread in death rate. At least three confounders likely dominate: mass (the CX-5 outweighs the Mazda3 by roughly 500 pounds, and heavier vehicles protect occupants better in crashes[4]), ride height (crossovers redirect collision energy differently than sedans in multi-vehicle crashes), and buyer demographics (the population purchasing a $26,000 sedan skews younger and drives differently than the population purchasing a $31,000 family crossover). Badge matters less than body style, weight class, and who sits behind the wheel.

One encouraging trend emerges from the model-year data. Peak lethality belongs to model years 2008 (64 deaths) and 2010 (62 deaths). By model year 2019, annual deaths drop to five, just six for 2020 and eight for 2021.[3] Mazda’s engineering genuinely improved with the current generation, and a comparison isolating only 2019+ vehicles from both lines would almost certainly narrow the gap substantially. But a blended 10-year rate does not know which generation you own, and neither does the person scanning a brand ranking before signing a used-car lease.

Caveats: blended 10-year rates penalize any nameplate that sold heavily in older, less safe generations, and comparing a CX-5 (on sale since 2013, no pre-unibody baggage) to a car that dates to 2004 inflates the gap. FARS captures only fatal crashes; a Mazda3 might have excellent injury-crash outcomes invisible here. Fleet and VMT estimates for lower-volume vehicles carry roughly ±15% uncertainty, meaning the true Mazda3 rate could range from about 1.39 to 1.87. None of this data isolates road type, geography, or driver age.

What to do with this: if shopping used Mazda3s, the 2019 redesign is your floor. If you own a 2008–2013 model, run your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls and confirm your side-curtain airbags were never part of a Takata inflator campaign. If you want a compact sedan and safety is the priority, the Kia Forte at 0.40 and the Chevy Cruze at 0.63 outperform the Mazda3 by a wide margin in the federal fatality data. And if you want to stay in the Mazda family, the CX-5 and CX-30 (both at 0.12) are among the safest vehicles in the American fleet, period.
