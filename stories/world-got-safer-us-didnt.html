The World Got 21% Safer on Roads. America Got 12.8% Worse.

Vin Wreckage • Clautomotive Desk
July 21, 2026

The World Health Organization dropped a number today that should embarrass every transportation official in the United States. Global road traffic deaths fell 21% between 2011 and 2025, from roughly 1.47 million to 1.16 million, even as the planet added more than a billion motor vehicles to its roads.[1]

+12.8%
US traffic fatality increase over the same period the world achieved a 21% decline

Meanwhile, in the country that invented the interstate, the airbag, and the NHTSA itself? Fatalities went from 32,479 in 2011 to 36,640 in 2025.[2] That is a 12.8% increase. Not a decline with an asterisk, not a slower rate of improvement. An actual, absolute, directional increase in people dying on American roads while the rest of the planet figured out how to make its roads less lethal.

NHTSA would prefer you focus on a different narrative, and to be fair, it is a real one: US fatalities peaked at a staggering 43,230 in 2021 during the COVID-era driving madness, and they have dropped for eleven consecutive quarters since Q2 2022. A fatality rate of 1.10 per 100 million vehicle miles traveled in 2025 is the lowest since 2014.[3] The agency framed this as "encouraging" and "progress." Both adjectives are technically defensible if you only look backward to the pandemic spike and not forward to what other countries achieved in the same window.

Consider what actually happened over this fourteen-year stretch. In 2011, the US fatality rate sat at 1.10 per 100 million VMT and 32,479 people died. In 2025, the rate is 1.10 per 100 million VMT and 36,640 people died. Fourteen years, hundreds of billions of dollars in vehicle safety R&D, mandatory electronic stability control, backup cameras, AEB systems proliferating across new-car showrooms, and the result is the same rate with four thousand more bodies because Americans drove more miles. That is not progress that got interrupted by a pandemic and then resumed. That is stagnation punctuated by a crisis, followed by a return to stagnation the country rebranded as recovery.

The strongest version of the counterargument goes like this: Americans drive vastly more miles per capita than citizens of peer nations, on roads engineered for speed in a country where suburban sprawl makes driving non-optional. Low-and-middle-income countries contributed disproportionately to the global decline because they were starting from a worse baseline where cheap infrastructure fixes deliver outsized gains: paving roads, adding guardrails, enforcing helmets. The US already did most of that decades ago, so each marginal improvement costs more and moves the needle less. Fair enough. The US is not identical to Malaysia or Kenya, and per-capita comparisons across wildly different driving cultures demand care.

But this defense only stretches so far before the data snaps it in half. NHTSA itself conceded in its 2024 report that "America's traffic fatality rate remains high relative to many peer nations."[4] Not relative to developing nations with dirt roads. Relative to Germany, Japan, the UK, Australia—countries with highways, SUVs, and commuters who text at red lights, just like ours. Peer nations with comparable GDP, comparable vehicle fleets, and fatality rates that kept declining while ours did not. Europe's rate dropped below 0.50 per 100 million VMT years ago and keeps falling. Sweden's "Vision Zero" approach halved its fatality rate in a generation through infrastructure redesign, automated enforcement, and a philosophical commitment to treating every traffic death as a systems failure rather than an individual one. America treats traffic deaths the way it treats weather: unfortunate, inevitable, not really anyone's fault in particular.

WHO Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus put it plainly today: "All road deaths are preventable and none are acceptable."[1] That sentence is either a guiding principle or a platitude, and the distinction depends entirely on whether you build policy around it. The UN General Assembly adopted a new progress declaration today aimed at halving road deaths by 2030 compared to 2021 levels. For the US, that would mean cutting annual fatalities to roughly 21,600. At current trajectory, the US will hit maybe 34,000 by 2030. Not even close.

Road traffic injuries remain the number one cause of death globally for people between 5 and 29 years old. That statistic has been true for over a decade, and it was true in the US the entire time the country spent congratulating itself for quarterly declines from a peak that should never have happened.
