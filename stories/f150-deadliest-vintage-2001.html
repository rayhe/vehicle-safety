The 2001 Ford F-150 Has Killed More People Than Any Other Vintage in America

Rex Driverton • Clautomotive Desk
July 13, 2026

Somebody has to be number one. In the NHTSA Fatality Analysis Reporting System, which records every traffic death on American soil, the model year 2001 Ford F-150 holds the single deadliest vehicle-vintage slot in the entire database: 672 deaths across the 2014–2023 decade.[1] Second place is the 2004 Chevrolet Silverado at 663. Third is the 2007 Silverado at 604. All five of the top five are full-size pickups. A sedan does not appear until the eighteenth slot.

Across all model years, the F-150 nameplate has killed 9,194 people, second only to the Silverado at 9,591.[1] One F-150 occupant dies every 9.5 hours. Nine hundred nineteen per year.

What makes MY2001 specifically lethal is volume meeting age. Ford sold more 10th-generation F-150s (1997–2003) than any previous generation, and by 2014 those trucks were 11 to 17 years old, priced under $8,000, and driven hard by people who could not afford newer metal.[2] No electronic stability control. No side-curtain airbags. That generation alone accounts for 3,316 FARS deaths, 36 percent of the nameplate’s entire toll.

Ford’s per-mile death rate of 1.04 per 100M VMT sits only slightly above the fleet median of 0.92.[1] An unremarkable rate. But on 6.56 million registered vehicles, unremarkable rates produce remarkable body counts. Compare: the Dodge Ram kills at 0.78, 25 percent lower. If the F-150 matched the Ram’s rate, an estimated 2,295 people in this dataset would still be alive. Impairment? 18.9 percent, below the fleet average. You cannot blame the drivers. This is a volume problem wearing a hard hat.

What You Can Do

If you are shopping for a used F-150, avoid model years 2001 through 2004. Those four vintages alone carry 2,415 FARS deaths and predate every modern active safety system. Look for 2015 or newer, after the aluminum-body 13th generation brought structural improvements and available automatic emergency braking. Check your VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls. And remember that the most popular vehicle in America is not the same thing as the safest.

Limitations

FARS fleet death rates rely on estimated vehicle miles traveled, not odometer readings, introducing roughly ±15% uncertainty. Model-year-level death counts include all occupant and non-occupant fatalities attributed to that vehicle in a fatal crash, not exclusively driver deaths. Comparing across nameplates with different fleet age distributions (the F-150 has been sold since 1948, the Ram 1500 nameplate since 2019 under that brand) is inherently imprecise. Newer model years have fewer exposure years in the 2014–2023 window.

Strongest Counterargument

A 1.04 rate is not exceptional. Most of the F-150’s body count comes from sheer fleet size, not engineering failure. No automaker controls what happens to a 20-year-old truck on its fifth owner with a zeroed-out maintenance budget. MY2001’s 672 deaths reflect a massive production run aging out of the safe zone, not a defect Ford could have recalled away.
