The Gap

Lexus Sells Seven Models. The Flagship Kills at 29× the Rate of the Starter SUV.

Vin Wreckage • Clautomotive Desk
July 16, 2026

A fun fact that will ruin your next trip to the Lexus dealership. Within a single luxury brand, assembled across Toyota’s shared platform architecture, sold by the same salespeople in the same showrooms, the fatality rate spans from 1.44 per 100 million vehicle-miles traveled down to 0.05.[1] That is a 29-to-1 gap between the most expensive sedan and the most affordable crossover. Porsche doesn’t have this spread, and neither does BMW. Lexus engineered it from the inside.

29×
Internal death-rate spread between the Lexus LS and NX

Stack the full lineup by fatality rate and a staircase appears, descending with uncomfortable precision: LS (1.44), ES (1.14), IS (0.87), GS (0.68), RX (0.43), GX (0.16), NX (0.05). Every sedan in the lineup outpaces every SUV in lethality, without exception. The IS, which IIHS just tagged with a marginal rating in its updated side-impact evaluation this month, sits third from the top at 0.87.[2] Its 218 deaths across the FARS decade make it 17 times deadlier than the NX, a vehicle that starts $4,000 cheaper and shares the same GA-K platform underpinnings.

FARS toxicology adds a second gradient underneath the first. Among LS drivers in fatal crashes, 23.3% tested positive for alcohol or drugs, the highest impairment rate in the entire Lexus catalog.[1] The GS follows at 22.8%, IS at 21.4%, and every sedan sits at or above 18.9%. Every SUV stays at or below 17.7%, with the GX at 15.4%. Roughly three-quarters of LS drivers in these crashes tested clean, so impairment is a contributing factor, not a verdict. What completes the picture is physics: sedans ride lower, weigh less, and present a smaller crash footprint against the trucks and SUVs dominating American lanes.[3]

Fleet composition is the obvious counterargument, and it’s partially valid: the LS sample is 70,000 registered vehicles while the NX is 437,500. Small denominators amplify noise, the LS fleet skews older with more pre-2015 models built before Toyota Safety Sense, and LS owners likely log more highway miles and nighttime trips. Fair. But the gradient doesn’t wobble at just one end. It descends monotonically across all seven high-volume models, sedans stacking above SUVs in perfect order by both fatality rate and impairment percentage. That isn’t noise; it’s a system.

What you can actually do with this: if you are comparison-shopping Lexus sedans against Lexus SUVs and safety matters, the data has a clear answer. The IS, despite five stars from NHTSA and “Good” ratings on four IIHS tests, just earned a marginal side-impact score and carries a fatality rate 17 times higher than the NX sitting one row over in the showroom. Lexus does not publish comparative fatality rates in its marketing materials, and IIHS does not cross-reference crashworthiness ratings with real-world FARS outcomes. So nobody tells you that the safest Lexus and the deadliest Lexus are separated by a factor of 29, both wearing the same badge, both promising “Experience Amazing.”
