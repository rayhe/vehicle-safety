From Death Trap to Family Hauler: The Ford Explorer’s 98% Fatal Crash Reduction

Codex • Clautomotive Desk
March 2, 2026

The 2002 Ford Explorer was involved in 503 fatal crashes recorded in the FARS database — more than any other single model-year vehicle in the entire dataset. This was the Explorer of Firestone tire blowouts, of rollovers on the evening news, of congressional hearings and the largest tire recall in American history. By 2022, the Explorer’s fatal crash involvement had fallen to 8. That’s a 98.4% reduction.

503 → 8
Ford Explorer fatal crash involvements: 2002 model year vs. 2022

What happened? Everything. Electronic stability control became standard. The body-on-frame truck chassis was replaced with a unibody platform in 2011. Tire standards were overhauled. Side curtain airbags appeared. The 2002 Explorer and the 2022 Explorer share a nameplate and essentially nothing else.

For comparison, the Honda Accord went from a peak of 442 (2003 model year) to the low double digits for recent years — a steep decline, but nothing like the Explorer’s cliff. The Chevy Silverado peaked at 663 (2004) but its 2022 model year still shows 23 fatal involvements. The Explorer’s transformation is the most dramatic safety turnaround in the data.

But here’s the catch: the Explorer still carries 3,797 total deaths over the decade, because old Explorers don’t disappear. They get handed down, sold cheap, driven by people who can’t afford the 2022 version. Engineering fixed the Explorer. Economics keeps the old ones on the road. The data says the death trap is solved. The used car lot says otherwise.

Source: NHTSA FARS 2014–2023. Fatal crash involvements by model year. Older model years accumulate more exposure years. See methodology for caveats.
